## 2 The Transcendental Deduction Gets Underway

### §1 Introduction: What's the Problem?

In both editions of the Critique, the Transcendental Deduction opens as follows: 'When jurists speak of rights and claims, they distinguish in a legal matter the question concerning right (quid juris) from the question concerning fact (quid facti), and since they require a proof of both, they call the first, the one that is supposed to confirm a claimed right, the Deduction' (B116). Dieter Henrich has argued that we should take the legal language seriously. Kant self-consciously appropriated a certain genre of legal writing—the Deduktionsschrift—for his argument.[^1] The need for such a writing always comes in the face of a challenge: a doubt has been raised about somebody's claim to an acquired right: e.g., the right to inherit an estate, to hold a certain office or to exercise sovereignty over a certain territory. The task of the jurist is to write a deduction or legal brief that will vindicate the client's claim. Kant proposes to write a deduction on behalf of the pure concepts of the understanding, because he imagines that they claim for themselves the right to relate a priori to objects and that this claim has, or can, become the object of a dispute. In this spirit, he says:

> But among the many concepts that make up the very mixed fabric of human knowledge, there are some that are destined for use a priori, completely independent of experience, and their right to this always requires a deduction, because proofs from experience do not suffice for the legality of such a use, and yet we must know how these concepts relate to objects that, however, they do not take from any experience. (A85/B117)

The category of cause and effect is supposed to illustrate the point. It raises an expectation in the understanding that, as Kant puts it, 'after A, there will be set something quite different, B, according to a rule' (A91/B122). But we can give no reason a priori why appearances must conform to this expectation, because, as Hume and others before him had pointed out, we can conceive or imagine, without any inconsistency, that A will not be followed by B. For all we know, the category of cause and effect is without any object whatsoever. The continuation of the passage reads as follows:

[^1]: Dieter Henrich, 'Kant's Notion of a Deduction and the Methodological Background of the first Critique' in Kant's Transcendental Deduction, ed. Eckart Förster (Stanford: Stanford University Press, 1989) 29–47.

> For it is plain that objects of sensible intuition must conform to the formal a priori conditions of sensibility from the fact that otherwise they would not be objects for us. But that they must also conform to the conditions that the understanding requires for the synthetic unity of thought, this conclusion is not so easy to see. For perhaps appearances could be so constituted that the understanding would find that they do not at all conform to the conditions of its unity and that everything lies in such confusion that, for example, in the sequence of appearances there is nothing that . . . corresponds to the concept of cause and effect so that this concept is thus quite empty, null and without meaning. (A90/B122–123)

Precisely because the category of cause and effect is not a pure form of sensibility, appearances need not conform to it. Indeed, they need not conform to any other category either. They might be in such confusion as to defeat our understanding. This worry makes a transcendental deduction of the categories absolutely necessary.

In sum, two claims about the categories press upon us the need for a transcendental deduction: first the claim that they are 'destined for use a priori, completely independent of experience'; second, that they are not pure forms of sensibility, like space and time. I will have more to say about the categories in §2d of Chapter Three. This much suffices for now both to motivate the transcendental deduction and to address a natural question arising from Chapter One, namely how the Deduction and the Duisburg Nachlaß might be related.

Kant seems not to have a full complement of categories in the Duisburg Nachlaß. He discusses only the three categories of relation. But he already characterizes these three in the way he would later characterize all twelve in the programmatic remarks from the Critique that I just quoted. He calls them 'concepts of the understanding' in R4675, R4679, and R4680 (17.652.10, 17.664.20, 17.665.5); he says in R4674 that they are 'the conditions a priori known in the [sc. thinking—AL] subject' and are therefore required for the exposition of appearances (17.646.7–10)—which means that they are 'destined for use a priori, independent of experience'. He also explicitly denies in R4680 that they are forms of sensibility (17.665.18–19). It should already follow in the Duisburg Nachlaß that the categories require a transcendental deduction. For could we not doubt whether appearances conform to them and hence whether they are 'empty, null and without meaning'?

The Duisburg Nachlaß does not use the word 'deduction'. But it does use legal language not unlike that of the Critique. We have already noted the use of the word 'Titel' and its connotations. We saw in §2e of Chapter One that Kant used this word in conjunction with the word 'Disposition' and its associated forms. Thus the legal question under discussion in the Duisburg Nachlaß seems to be: how may the mind rightfully take command over the manifold? The manifold at issue is not the mind's creation; it is given to us under the conditions of our sensibility. These conditions are powerless to impose anything on it; all they can do is make it possible as such. It could thus escape our command altogether, as Bohemia escaped that of the Hapsburg Emperor in 1618. What gives our mind 'title' over appearances? The answer is not brute military force, because no Wallenstein can impose order at the mind's bidding. If any is forthcoming, it can only take the form of a legal argument. Thus we seem to be on our way to the idea expressed in the passage at A90/B122–123 of the Critique quoted above: unless we can show that the mind has legal title to the manifold given to it under the forms of our sensibility, appearances may be in such confusion that they conform to none of its expectations.

We can also see that the Duisburg Nachlaß has a rough strategy for putting this worry to rest. It appeals to three 'titles of the understanding': the functions or actions of the mind that enunciate a priori the three rules or universal laws that are supposed to govern the way appearances relate to one another. Thus the Duisburg Nachlaß has something reasonably precise to say about what it means for concepts of the understanding—at least the three under discussion—to relate a priori to objects. They do so by giving the functions of the mind the basis of a statement a priori for the general rules we discussed in §1c in Chapter One. The concepts of the understanding relate a priori to objects just insofar as these rules do, and these rules relate a priori to objects in just the same way that the enunciation of a proposition in classical geometry relates to its ekthesis, namely as a general rule applies to a special, representative case. It is not clear how the details would work. But we have enough to suggest that the Duisburg Nachlaß already recognizes the need for a transcendental deduction and that it provides one in outline.

Having said that, the need for a transcendental deduction is not recognized in the Duisburg Nachlaß in such radical terms as those in the passage I quoted from A90/B122–123 in the Critique. This is plain if we imagine that the exposition of appearances in the Duisburg Nachlaß should come up short. In that case, the three relational concepts of the understanding would not relate to appearances a priori. But it would not follow that they are 'empty, null and without meaning'. For they would still relate a priori, by means of apperception, to the thinking subject. In the Duisburg Nachlaß, a question arises about the legal title of our understanding to command over appearances, but not about its title to command over the mind itself. Kant takes that for granted. We saw the textual evidence in §1d of Chapter One. We also saw the philosophical reasons: unless the thinking subject is a thinking substance that knows itself as such, it will be relations all the way down—a consequence that Kant rejected in the mid-1750s and apparently still rejected in the mid-1770s.

From the standpoint of the Duisburg Nachlaß, it would be scandalous if the argument to justify the mind's title to appearances should collapse: we claim the right to think, reflect, and try to learn the truth about them. But the programmatic remarks from the Critique quoted above indicate that such a collapse would be far worse than scandalous: it would call into question our right to think and reflect at all. That worry could not have occurred to Kant until he had reason to doubt the assumption in the Duisburg Nachlaß of the mind's title to itself. This suggests that the push for a transcendental deduction, designed to address the radical worry just indicated, went hand in hand with the idea animating the section of the Critique on the Paralogisms.

The focus of the Paralogisms is rational psychology, a pure doctrine of the soul elaborated by reason, without appeal to experience, solely from the thought that 'I think'. The rational psychologist knows that this thought can accompany all our other thoughts and therefore assumes that it can reveal truths about the thing that thinks it. But under critical scrutiny, this 'I think' is supposed to prove quite empty. My effort to reflect on it to discover what truths it might reveal about me and thinkers like me will set it just out of reach, because it will force me to affix another 'I think' to whatever conclusion I draw, and thus I can ask all over again what is revealed by this 'I think' (B404). Considerations like these lead Kant to say in the first Critique that the 'I think' is purely formal: it conveys no information about any object; it has no special content of its own distinct from that of the representations it accompanies. It is nothing more than the consciousness I have of my own thoughts. Hence, it is a mistake to think that we might use the 'I think' to learn about any object, much less about the single, simple, thinking substance alleged to be our soul. Fallacy is thus the basis of all inferences in rational psychology. Kant concludes: 'Not the slightest gain is made, then, in respect to the knowledge of myself as object through the analysis of the consciousness of my self in thought as such. The logical elaboration of thought as such is falsely held [in rational psychology] for a metaphysical determination of the object' (B409).

The perspective of the Duisburg Nachlaß on these things is very different from that of the Critique. It does not treat apperception as a purely formal condition of all thought expressed by an empty 'I think', but rather as the consciousness that reveals the mind to itself as a thing that thinks. It allows the mind to apply to itself and its representations the three relational concepts: Substance, Cause, and Whole. By implication, the Duisburg Nachlaß assumes that it is possible to do what the Critique rules out, i.e., elaborate a metaphysics of the thinking subject, just by reflecting on the 'I think'. It is thus significant, but not surprising, that we found the sketch of a rational psychology along these lines at the end of R4684. The presence of a rational psychology in the Duisburg Nachlaß shows that, even if the exposition of appearances should fail and the mind should thereby lose title to appearances, it would not have immediately followed for Kant in the mid-1770s that the concepts of the understanding are 'empty, null and without meaning'. I will not speculate about what led him to treat apperception as a purely formal condition of thinking that cannot yield any insight into the nature of the mind. But once he had taken that step, then, given the role played in the exposition of appearances by such insight, the need for a transcendental deduction, to allay the worry expressed at A90/B122–123, would have impressed itself on him with a special urgency.

We have thus found an important parallel between the Duisburg Nachlaß and the Transcendental Deduction in the Critique of Pure Reason: each offers a legal argument to vindicate the claim of the pure concepts of the understanding—whether three or twelve in number—to apply to appearances a priori. But the doubt addressed by the legal argument in the Critique is much more radical. This is because, by 1781, the foundation of the legal argument mounted in the Duisburg Nachlaß had given way: Kant had abandoned the thinking subject. It was now relations all the way down, which meant the collapse of the exposition of appearances, as conceived in the mid-1770s. Whence in turn the doubt that the categories might well be 'empty, null and without meaning'. We will explore in the rest of this book how Kant went about addressing this more radical doubt.

### §1a Introduction: What is the Scopos of the Transcendental Deduction?

'It may be as well to explain in advance the kind of study this is, so that knowing beforehand the road, as it were, which we are to travel, and understanding which part of it we are on, we may walk the more easily, and not adopt unwittingly a misconception of the project.'[^2] These are words one would like Kant to have written at the beginning of the Transcendental Deduction, along with the appropriate follow-up. In fact, they are by Aristoxenus, the student of Aristotle, at the beginning of Book Two of his Elements of Harmonics. He was moved to write them because of his awareness that the subject matter of the science he was proposing to set forth, music theory, was a matter of controversy and his endorsement of the lesson that Aristotle himself apparently drew from the failure of Plato's storied public lecture on the good. This is a lesson that Kant himself would certainly have considered important too. According to Aristoxenus, Aristotle used to relate the following account of what happened to those who attended Plato's lecture; the translation of this passage (with modifications), like that of the passage with which I opened this subsection, is by Andrew Barker:

[^2]: Aristoxenus, The Harmonics of Aristoxenus, 30.10–16 as ed. by Henry Macran (Oxford: Clarendon Press, 1902). For the translation see Greek Musical Writings, volume 2, ed. and trans. Andrew Barker (Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, 1989) 148.

> They all came, he [sc. Aristotle—AL] said, supposing that they were going to acquire one of the things which people commonly consider good, such as wealth, health, strength—in general, some astounding happiness or other. But when the discourse turned out to be about the mathematical sciences, about numbers and geometry and astronomy, and in the end to claim that the good is One, it seemed to them, I imagine, altogether contrary to their expectations, so that some of them pooh-poohed the business, while others dismissed it out of hand. And why? Because they did not know in advance what the subject was . . . .[^3]

[^3]: Aristoxenus, 30.16—31.4. For the translation, see Barker, Greek Musical Writings, 148.

Aristoxenus could also have asked: and whose fault was that? It is hard to imagine that Aristotle did not ask the question—or at least invite his own listeners to consider it. The answer was obvious enough: Plato had only himself to blame. Had he more carefully advertised ahead of time the 'scopos' or aim of his lecture, his listeners would not have arrived with the wrong expectations, and so nobody would have been disappointed.

Kant was very careful to explain at the outset why a Transcendental Deduction of the categories is necessary. So he has, to that extent, motivated the undertaking. But he has said nothing direct or explicit that would give us an idea in advance what kind of argument it will be, what road we are to travel and what part of it we are on right now. In short, he has given us no guidance about its 'scopos'. If I were a contemporary of Kant, confronted with the Critique for the first time, I might have lots of quite different expectations.

Kant's mismanagement of reader expectations is an obstacle to understanding the Transcendental Deduction. But it can be overcome by collecting clues in Kant's other writings: in the Duisburg Nachlaß and earlier writings on metaphysics—in later writings as well. Those clues suggest the following brief and programmatic characterization of the 'scopos' of the Deduction—and with it, the lay-out of my book.

I will focus on the Transcendental Deduction in the second edition of the Critique. Following Dieter Henrich,[^4] I take the B-Deduction to be a single argument in two steps. The first step plays out from §15 to §21; the second step, from §22 to §26. I take the 'scopos' of the whole first step to be thinking as such—as it discloses itself to itself. I will devote two chapters to it. This chapter will examine §15 through §17. The next chapter—Chapter Three—will examine §18 to §21.

I take the 'scopos' of the second step of the B-Deduction—and indeed that of the Deduction as a whole—to be cosmology. This can be seen, I will argue in §1a of Chapter Four, if §26 of the B-Deduction is read in light of §36 of the Prolegomena and the early metaphysical writings I discussed in §2c of Chapter One. My claim could seem provocative, because metaphysics is something that ought to be excluded from the Deduction as a matter of principle. For we learn from the preface to the first edition that the Deduction is supposed to test the faculty of reason 'with respect to all cognitions to which it may aspire independent of all experience and thereby settle the question of the possibility or impossibility of a metaphysics as such' (AXII). No part of metaphysics can be a resource for the Deduction, precisely because the task of the Deduction is to help determine whether it has a future or not. Indeed, an important difference between the Deduction and the Duisburg Nachlaß is precisely that the metaphysics of the thinking subject will now be put on hold. But the cosmology sought by the second step of the B-Deduction is no part of the metaphysics whose future is in question. It is purely formal—a cosmology of experience, as I call it. By this I mean an argument that treats experience as a (sensible) world, i.e., as a unified whole of appearances (with appropriate qualifications to guard against antinomies), and that tries to establish its conditions of possibility by showing that its unity comes from laws legislated to appearances by the understanding through its categories. I will devote Chapter Four to this argument and the fifth and final chapter to what I take to be related business: Kant's attempt in the second half of §24 and §25 to resolve a paradox he associates with empirical self-knowledge. I take the solution of the paradox to turn on the possibility of empirically knowing my place in the world, as determined by the cosmology of experience.

[^4]: Henrich, 'The Proof-Structure of Kant's Transcendental Deduction' in Kant on Pure Reason, ed. Ralph Walker (Oxford: Oxford University Press, 1982) 66–82

### §2 Opening Gambit: §15 and §16

We can see that thinking is the scopos of the first step of the B-Deduction, if we note to start with that §17 plays a crucial role by stating the 'supreme principle of all use of the understanding'. Kant has already characterized the understanding as the 'spontaneity of knowledge', i.e., as the 'faculty for thinking the object of sensible intuition'[^5] (A51/B75). This is by contrast with sensibility, which he characterized as the 'receptivity of our mind for receiving representations insofar as it has in any way been affected' (A51/B75). Thought is the special activity of the understanding. The 'supreme principle of all use of the understanding' secured in §17 of the B-Deduction is the principle that governs all our thinking. Thinking is thus the matter of concern to the B-Deduction from §15–§16, just to the extent that these sections prepare the way for §17. It continues to be the matter of concern in §18 and §19, which elaborate the significance of the supreme principle for judgement. §20 and §21 bring the first step of the Deduction to its conclusion. Thus the whole first step of the B-Deduction focusses on thinking and anything that can help us understand its nature and the way that it works. To get a fix on the details we must determine what §15 and §16 can contribute to the elaboration of the supreme principle in §17. We must therefore figure out what Kant means by 'synthetic unity of apperception', because the 'supreme principle of the understanding' in §17 explicitly says that such unity is essential to all our thinking. Before we can ask how or why, though, we must understand what it is. But before we can even do that, we must first figure out what a manifold is. For the 'supreme principle of all use of the understanding' says that 'every manifold of intuition stand under the original synthetic unity of apperception'[^6] (B136). The ambition of this section of the current chapter is to sort out these preliminary notions.

### §2a What is a manifold?

The first thing to say about manifolds is that every intuition has one. This is implied by the opening lines of §15 and stated explicitly at A99. Kant never spells out what he means, but the word itself suggests he is making an assumption, namely that intuitions are always complex representations. Thus a manifold is just a bunch of different representations that collectively make up a given intuition. We may be tempted to think that a manifold is more specifically a bunch of untutored sensations or raw sense data. For such things characteristically lack organization or structure, and this is in keeping with Kant's subsequent call for the manifold to be 'combined'—assuming, of course, that combination is just a matter of organizing whatever is unorganized. Dieter Henrich seems to be thinking in this way and goes so far as to speak of Kant's 'Datensensualismus'.[^7] But, at least for the purpose at hand, Kant cannot have anything so specific in mind. For he says quite plainly that every intuition has a manifold. His claim is not restricted to empirical intuitions; it extends to pure intuitions as well (A99, B137 fn., B160–161). Moreover, it extends to both the pure intuition of space and the pure intuition of time. Kant says studiously little about manifolds as such. This may be deliberate; certainly it is due to the argument's high level of abstraction. We can see a bit better what he has in mind, if we consider his long standing use of the word.

[^7]: Henrich, Identität und Objektivität (Heidelberg: Carl Winter Universitätsverlag, 1976) 17ff., 21.

This can be documented from passages in Part Two of the Beweisgrund of 1763. The word of interest appears on the very first page of Part Two, in the heading of the first section of the First Reflection: 'the unity in the manifold of the essences of things proven from the properties of space'. As the reference to space in the heading suggests, this section of the First Reflection is devoted to geometrical examples—all of them drawn from the geometry of circles. The first example is that of Proposition 35 in Book Three of Euclid's Elements, a special favourite of Kant's, which states that, for any two line-segments intersecting at a point within the circumference of a circle, the rectangle formed by the sections of the one segment is equal to the rectangle formed by the sections of the other. The second example, another special favourite of Kant's, is Galileo's so-called law of chords, which depends on the Euclidean theorem just mentioned. The law of chords states that, in a 'vertical circle', i.e., in a circle whose diameter is perpendicular to the horizon, balls rolling along planes variously inclined to the diameter will reach the circumference of the circle in the same time, if they start from rest at the place where the plane meets the diameter.[^8] Both these examples involve a rule that applies to a certain 'manifold'. The manifold in question is that of space. Space has a manifold, because it has infinitely many continuous, homogeneous parts: they can be divided, as far as you wish; their boundaries may be set out any way you wish. The excitement value of these two examples is that, because of the relevant rule, we find that this manifold exhibits a certain 'unity' or 'fitting together' (Zusammenpassung) (2.93.19).

[^8]: See Proposition Six in Day Three of the Discorsi.

The examples in this section of the Beweisgrund might lead us to think that 'manifold' is a word that Kant specially reserved for geometry (and kinematics) in the 1760s—or perhaps mathematics more generally: maybe a number is a manifold of units. But he goes on in the next section of the Beweisgrund to use it of bodies insofar as they are material and subject to the laws of motion. He speaks of the laws of motion just as he did of the Euclidean theorem and the law of chords in the previous section: the laws of motion apply to all bodies just as the Euclidean theorem applies to all line segments that intersect within the circumference of a circle. Just as we find unity exhibited in the manifold of Euclidean space due to the geometry of the circle, so we find 'unity and order in the infinite manifold of the [sc. physical—AL] universe . . . ' (2.99.13–14). The physical universe here seems to be the sum-total of all matter.

The passages from the Beweisgrund we just considered suggest that, around 1763, anything would count as a manifold so long as it is many in some respect. That is the significant point of contact between the 'manifold' homogeneous, continuous parts of Euclidean space and the 'manifold' of physical matter subject to the laws of motion. It is true, of course, that the manifolds Kant singles out for consideration in these sections all exhibit 'unity'. This might suggest that unity was another distinguishing feature of a manifold in the 1760s. But this inference should be resisted for a couple of reasons. First, the stated purpose of the First Reflection of Part Two of the Beweisgrund is to show that 'from the unity perceived in the essences of things, the existence of God can be inferred a posteriori' (2.92.6–7). Manifolds that lack unity are not useful to the project. So it is no surprise that Kant does not insist upon them. Second, Kant says repeatedly of the manifolds of immediate interest to him that the unity we discover in them is always a surprise or even a wonder: we continue to be astonished at their unity long after it has been discovered. Indeed, who would have thought that any such unity would be discovered among the homogeneous, continuous parts of Euclidean space? This suggests that the default assumption is that manifolds do not exhibit unity at all. Finally, Kant gives other examples of physical phenomena that lack unity in the relevant sense, but that one suspects might equally well count as manifolds.

For example, at the beginning of the second section of the First Reflection, Kant cites biological examples, notably the claws of cats—domestic cats and big cats like lions. The thing he wants to stress is that there is no natural or necessary unity here that flows directly from laws governing material things—like the laws of motion. We could not deduce from the laws of motion that cats have claws, much less retractable claws, as we can deduce, say, the inverse-square law and the universal gravitational pull that all particles of matter have on one another. This is not to deny that there is a certain 'agreement, order and utility' (Übereinstimmung, Ordnung und Nutzen [2.96.14]) in the arrangement of parts on a cat's paw. But until we learn otherwise, there seems to be no other way to understand this except as the effect of a wise choice by the creator of cats: it is better for a cat's chance at bagging prey if its claws always remain sharp, and there is no better way to ensure that they will remain sharp except to make them retractable (2.96.18–23). Retractable claws, as found in all species of felines,[^10] are manifold, but lacking in unity—at least that necessary unity we find in the geometrical and physical examples.[^11]

[^10]: Cheetahs do not have retractable claws. But though they are a species of felid, they are not a species of feline. I do not know whether Kant was aware of cheetahs.
[^11]: Since it seems clear that retractable claws among all the felines do have some kind of unity, just by virtue of being retractable and found among all the felines, it may be useful—as a way to avoid confusion—to characterize their unity as 'contingent' and thereby distinguish it from the unity that prevails in manifolds insofar as they are governed by natural laws and/or mathematical principles. For Kant calls the latter sort of unity 'necessary'. The use of 'contingent' here is in keeping with other biological examples Kant gives later in Part Two (2.106.5–107.22).

Now it may well be significant that Kant neglects to make explicit use of the word 'manifold' in his discussion of the biological examples. But I do not see what would prevent him. They seem to be no less manifold than the other examples he discusses. I will venture to say that Kant reserves the word 'manifold' for the examples that do exhibit the unity of interest to him for shock value. These are precisely the manifolds that are so manifold we expect not to find any unity in them, like the manifold of space. In that case, Kant could have used the word 'manifold' for things that have neither unity nor even the 'agreement, order and utility' of retractable claws in feline predators. If that is right, the word could apply even to what I will call 'Euclidean litter': all those continuous, homogenous parts of space that, because of their construction and haphazard arrangement relative to one another, do not specify any one theorem like that which so fascinated Kant from Book Three of Euclid's Elements.[^12]

[^12]: This conjecture is confirmed by a passage in the Enquiry concerning the Certainty of the Principles of Natural Theology and Morals, a work published in 1764, one year after the Beweisgrund. The context of the passage is the claim that we find only a few simple, unanalysable concepts in mathematics and innumerably many in philosophy. Speaking more specifically about metaphysics, Kant says 'one immediately anticipates that one will inevitably arrive at simple concepts in the course of breaking concepts apart . . . and that there will be uncommonly many of these simple concepts, since it is impossible that universal cognitions of such great manifoldness [von so großer Mannigfaltigkeit] should be put together out of only a few foundational concepts' (2.280.15–20). Here the word 'manifoldness' is used of a great multiplicity of cognitions where there is no thought or assumption that anything holds them together or unites them somehow. The only thing Kant is stressing here is that they are unbelievably many. That fact is supposed to justify the inference that the primitive, simple concepts out of which they are composed are appropriately numerous.

The use of the word 'manifold' in the Beweisgrund is significant not just because it documents Kant's earlier understanding of the word, but also because it helps to establish Kant's later understanding of the word as well. This can be seen from the fact that Kant reprises some of the examples from the Beweisgrund in the Critique of Judgement of 1797, i.e., in the First Section of the Analytic of the Teleological Power of Judgement (§62). For he continues to use 'manifold' as he did in 1763: namely of things that are many in which we expect not to find unity, but that arouse our wonder when we do. Thus referring to the properties of the circle—and here he is once again thinking of Proposition 35 in Book Three of the Elements (5.362.28–363.2)—he speaks of the 'manifold rules (from a principle) that arouse wonder . . . ' (5.364.29–30). This passage is interesting, because it applies 'manifold' to rules themselves. In context, we can see that the rules in question are all those subsidiary laws—like Galileo's law of chords—whose proof rests ultimately on Kant's favourite Euclidean theorem in Elements, Book Three.[^13] What makes these rules 'manifold' is only that they are numerous—or anyway, more than one in number. What makes them special and wonderful in Kant's eyes (and not his alone) is that, contrary to what we would expect at first, they rest on a single principle, namely Proposition 35 from Book Three of the Elements. But this fact about them is not a consequence of their being 'manifold'.

[^13]: Kant really does seem to have been enamoured of Proposition 35 in Book Three of Euclid's Elements. It is used as an example not only in the passages I have mentioned from the Beweisgrund and the third Critique, but also in the Prolegomena (§38, 4.320ff.) and the Enquiry concerning the Certainty of the Principles of Natural Theology and Morals (2.278.26–31).

The pattern of Kant's use of the word 'manifold' for more than thirty years after the Beweisgrund indicates that it had no special, technical meaning for him: it was just an all-purpose term for anything capable of having magnitude, number, internal complexity, or multiple variants. The question raised by this is what, if anything, the word could not apply to. God is supposed to be internally simple and numerically one, according to the Beweisgrund, but even he is 'manifold' with respect to his attributes and the effects they produce in creation. Kant never explicitly uses the word to express this idea, but there is nothing in the Beweisgrund that would have prevented him from doing so.

When we consider, in light of this, the relevant parts of the Critique of Pure Reason, it seems natural to resist using 'manifold' for concepts, because the word is so closely associated with the deliverances of sensibility and because of the sharp distinction between sensibility and the understanding. But it is worth noting that, in the famous passages at the beginning of the Transcendental Logic, where this distinction is introduced, manifolds are not mentioned at all. The relevant consideration is that sensibility is the mind's receptive capacity through which an object is given in intuition, whereas understanding is the mind's spontaneity by means of which an object is thought through concepts (A50–52/B74–76). We know from the passages I cited earlier that every intuition has a manifold. But we may well wonder now whether there is any reason not to say that concepts are, or have a, manifold. The surviving lectures on logic tell us that any given concept has both an extension (Umfang) and an intension (Inhalt). The extension of a concept is all those lower concepts that fall under it, as Felid, Viverrid, and Mustellid inter alia fall under Carnivore. The concept's intension is all those higher concepts contained within it, as Carnivore contains Mammal and Animal.[^14] Concepts like Carnivore are 'manifold' with respect to both their intension and extension, though Kant never says as much in the Critique.

But, for one thing, Lambert does: at least with respect to what Kant would call the intension of a concept. This is in the context of his discussion at the beginning of the Anlage zur Architectonik about Leibniz' project of analysing concepts into their 'innere Merkmale'. If a concept has more than one such Merkmal, perforce it 'contains' something manifold. Only simple concepts do not, he says.[^15] This is just because simple concepts are unanalysable. They are, however, the exception. Thus Lambert is willing to apply 'manifold' to concepts. This nicely illustrates the point: concepts composed out of other concepts are manifold in the relevant sense. If we want to know what we are saying or thinking when we use such concepts, we must review their 'manifold' and determine what they contain by way of part-concepts (Teilbegriffe). As for Kant, we should remember that he is willing, as late as 1797, to use 'manifold' for all those 'rules' whose demonstration rests on Proposition 35 from Elements, Book Three. Kant is sometimes willing to say, even in the first Critique, that a concept serves as a rule, namely for thinking appearances (A106). If concepts are rules at all, why should they be any less eligible for the word 'manifold' than the rules considered in our passage of the third Critique?

The answer to this question, I think, is that Kant is indeed willing to speak of 'manifold concepts', as at B143 where he speaks of 'manifold given representations (whether they be intuitions or concepts)'.[^18] But he is also doing his best to avoid confusion. 'Manifold' is a word more strictly used for intuitions than concepts. Every intuition has a manifold just by being an intuition. Every concept has both extension and intension, but we may not infer that it is, or has a, manifold just by being a concept. This will sound paradoxical until we see how Kant understands the principle governing all concepts, i.e., the 'supreme principle of all use of the understanding', as laid out in §17 of the B-Deduction. For now it is enough to say that every concept, as concept, is best understood as a special case of that self-consciousness Kant calls 'pure apperception'. The pure apperception associated with our understanding is supposed to be distinctive in that it does not come packaged with a manifold of its own. It is such that 'in the representation, "I think", nothing at all manifold is given' (B138). Kant says that we can hypothetically imagine an understanding for which this is not true, but such an understanding would be radically different from ours. As special cases of the apperception associated with our understanding, concepts do not as such have manifolds. That they are manifold in the way we have seen must reflect the fact that they express the different ways the manifold of intuition can be taken up by pure apperception. This is not the place to go into the details. The important thing for now is just that the word 'manifold' applies to intuitions in what we might call a primary sense, whereas it applies to concepts—if at all—in a derivative sense.

I see no reason not to think that the word 'manifold' is used for intuitions in the Critique in the same way it was used for the examples, both physical and geometrical, in Part Two of the Beweisgrund. Intuitions are, or have a, manifold just like space. Space is, or has a, manifold because of its homogeneous, continuous parts, which may be divided or set apart from one another as we please. An intuition is, or has a, manifold, because of its internal complexity, which lends itself to, or invites, many different construals.[^19] The manifold of an intuition can be construed in different ways, just as space can be marked off into regions in different ways.

The way we construe the manifold of an intuition is not given to us with the manifold itself. I will try to flesh this out in what follows. For now the thing to say is just that our construals of a manifold will always be the work of the mind's spontaneity, i.e., they will always be an act of the understanding. But what the mind's receptivity offers us with a manifold of intuition is an opportunity to exercise this spontaneity in lots of different ways. That means there is one significant difference between the use of the word 'manifold' in the Critique and its use in Part Two of the Beweisgrund. I said earlier that the word is used in the Beweisgrund for 'shock value', i.e., to play up the surprise and wonder we feel when we discover unity in a manifold so manifold that we would never have expected it. There can be no such shock, and hence no such value in using the word thus, in the Critique, because unity is never simply discovered in a manifold of intuition. It cannot be discovered, because it is not there.[^20] It is always the effect of construing the manifold; construing the manifold is always an exercise of the mind's spontaneity. Spontaneity is something entirely up to us. As long as we exercise it, there will be some kind of unity in the manifold of intuition. No surprise there. It follows, as a result, that the philosophical weight of the word 'manifold' has shifted. Kant uses it in the Critique to play up something quite different, namely the fact that the source of our manifolds and the source of their unity are not the same. This is not to say that, having digested the critical philosophy, we will be any less amazed by Proposition 35 in Elements, Book Three. As I indicated, Kant continues to marvel at it in the Analytic of the Teleological Power of Judgement in the third Critique. It is just that the source of this wonder will have to be understood differently.

Having discharged this preliminary, we may now proceed to the synthetic unity of apperception.

### §2b What is combination? What is synthesis?

By offering us a manifold, intuitions are supposed to make a fundamental contribution to knowledge. But the manifold all by itself is not yet knowledge as such. How could it be? It is nothing more than an opportunity to exercise the spontaneity of our understanding in different ways. It is an opportunity to think. But an opportunity to think is not yet a determinate thought. Unless we have determinate thoughts, we will have no knowledge. I suggested earlier that to exercise our spontaneity on the manifold is an effort to construe it. The two terms Kant himself uses for this are 'combination' (Verbindung) and 'synthesis' (Synthesis) (B130).

I propose now to consider a couple of examples so that we may have a more concrete idea of what Kant has in mind. The value of such examples is that they can help us clarify what he says and stimulate us to think through the implications. In retrospect, it seems like obvious bad judgement on his part to announce in the preface to the first edition of the Critique that he would be dispensing with examples and 'elucidations in concreto' (AXVIII).[^21] But I acknowledge that examples are a hazard sometimes. They can misdirect if we read too much in to them or make too much out of them. My strategy for dealing with this is to use examples that are genuinely Kantian—ones that can be documented from Kant's other writings.

[^21]: It is interesting that Kant reports, in this passage of the preface to the first edition, that he felt the need to provide examples, as he first started writing the Critique and that examples occurred to him. He says he decided to leave them out so as not to bloat the book. He also says at AXIX that too many examples can make it hard for the reader to get a sense of the whole. He has a point if one considers Lambert's Neues Organon, parts of which have such a surfeit of examples that one is not sure whether one is coming or going. On the other hand, the contrast between the Critique of Pure Reason and the Critique of Judgement is striking. The Critique of Judgement is full of examples. Perhaps this is a sign of recognition that the first Critique could have done with more, or perhaps it reflects the nature of the topic: reflective judgement. Unlike determining judgement, which proceeds from the universal to the particular, reflective judgement starts with the particular and seeks the universal.

The first example I propose is that of empirical pattern recognition. It might be objected that, even if this example is genuinely Kantian, it is not relevant to the deduction, because it will take us into the field of empirical psychology. But that would be too quick. The second step of the B-Deduction explicitly takes up perception and its conditions of possibility. It claims at B162 that these conditions have their origin in the understanding, which opens the way to seeking concepts for the things we perceive, as when we ask: what is this? The asking and answering of such questions is empirical pattern recognition. I can find no reason to think that reflecting on empirical pattern recognition to shed light on elements of the B-Deduction is wrong. That is why I feel free to invoke it here as my first example of combination or synthesis. The second example I will invoke is very different: I will develop it out of an assumption Kant seems to make about the greater certainty of mathematics over philosophy in the Enquiry concerning the Certainty of the Principles of Natural Theology and Morals—a work from 1764.

The value of having two such different examples is that, against the backdrop of their differences, they may reveal something important about all cases of combination or synthesis. As we learn from Part Two of the A-Deduction, synthesis comes in lots of different flavours. I could have picked other examples. In fact, I will discuss two others in detail in the chapter to come: the 'association of ideas' through reproductive imagination and judgement. We will see that the central question of §18 and §19 of the B-Deduction is what makes the difference between them. The reason I picked the two examples of synthesis that I will discuss in this chapter is that I can show they were of long-standing interest to Kant and that they can help us understand why he represents all synthesis in §15 of the B-Deduction as a form of 'self-activity' (Selbsttätigkeit) exercised by the understanding.

Here is one example of empirical pattern recognition as a form of combination of the manifold. Suppose you are at the summit of a high hill overlooking countryside in every direction for miles around. Straight ahead, but far in the distance, you see a whitish patch moving against the green. If you are sufficiently interested, you will wonder what it is and will try out conjectures. Some will be outlandish; you will probably not bother with them. But that may still leave a number you find plausible. Perhaps it is a flock of sheep moving through a meadow, or perhaps a herd of goats, or perhaps a herd of cattle. How you decide will be up to you, according to your sense of the likelihoods, given what you take to be your present circumstances. What you have been given is this view of a whitish patch moving against a green backdrop. Even that characterization of it may over-interpret things, since perhaps we would do just as well to characterize it as a greenish backdrop moving behind a white patch. But either way, it is a manifold of intuition in precisely the sense indicated earlier: it has many parts that can be grouped together in different ways. Just for that reason, it is equivocal. It can be construed in different ways, but it will not construe itself. That is entirely up to you, as an exercise of your intellectual spontaneity. This may involve taking into consideration things you remember seeing on your way up the hill, or things you remember reading about agriculture and livestock in this region. You will have to exercise your spontaneity even to decide which of these things, or which other things, to take into consideration. Once you have decided how to use your spontaneity, the result will involve treating an otherwise equivocal manifold in such a way that it is no longer equivocal, i.e., it will place a certain interpretation on it. This is one of the understanding's contributions to your knowledge. It is a form of what Kant more usually calls 'combination'.

Kant seems to have had a long-standing interest in empirical pattern recognition. This is suggested by the way my own example resonates with a remark he makes in Dreams of a Spirit-Seer of 1766. The context of the remark is a discussion of how states of mind altered by illness or circumstance can lead people to construe what they see and hear in different ways. The example Kant gives, which he takes to be the one most intimately familiar to his reader, is this: 'When, after having slept soundly, one regards, in a state of ease approaching drowsiness, the various filaments of the bed curtains or the bedclothes [der Bettvorhänge oder des Bezuges], or the small flecks in a nearby wall, one easily makes out human faces and the like' (2.346.31–36).

Another passage in the same vein can be found in the Collins anthropology lectures from the Winter Semester 1772/1773. Kant is here reported as saying: 'The mind is capable of images just so long as it has occasion to [Veranlassung]; it constantly paints [mahlen], and since there is nothing closer to a person, also nothing that so preoccupies the senses of a person, than people, the mind thus paints nothing but people, even if only a stick [covered in] moss is there. But we often paint camels and dragons in the clouds' (25.77.5–10). This is certainly empirical pattern recognition. Collins makes a brief, but suggestive, report about how Kant understood it to work: 'Whoever considers something unfamiliar for the first time does not yet have an image of it. One must first run through it [durchlaufen], as it were; and of great things, one cannot make for oneself a proper image' (25.77.1–4).

This talk of 'running' or 'going through' came up when we discussed exposition in §1c of Chapter One. But, as I indicated there, such language points ahead to the same sort of talk about the synthesis of apprehension in the Critique (A98–100; B160–161). Collins does not report Kant as making the point, so important in the passages from the Critique on this subject, that the thing we 'run through' is manifold. But it is implied, because, unless the thing is manifold, we need not go through it: we would get it all in an instant. Nor does Collins report any remark by Kant about time. But the need for time is also implied, because the mind cannot run through anything manifold in no time at all. That both a manifold and time are needed in the 'run-through' essential to empirical pattern recognition—or 'image formation', as Kant himself calls it here—is indicated in yet a third passage: this one can be found in the lectures on metaphysics designated as L1.

We considered an important passage from L1 in §1c of Chapter One. But it is not relevant for present purposes. The one that is relevant here can be found at 28.235–236. I would like to postpone discussing it in detail until §2a in Chapter Four. For now, let me just point out that the issue in this new passage from L1 is the same as that in Collins: what goes into forming an image of something we have never before directly encountered? But Collins is short and perfunctory, while L1 elaborates useful examples at length. Kant appears to have gone out of his way to pick examples that he regarded as more than usually manifold: for instance, the highly ornamented interior of St. Peter's in Rome. The point he was apparently trying to make was that the more manifold the manifold, the more effort and presumably the more time required to run through it all. He seems to have thought that there might be limit cases where so much time and effort would be required that the effort to form an image might be defeated. This may shed light on the otherwise cryptic remark at the end of the Collins report I quoted above: 'of great things, one cannot make for oneself a proper image.' It is as if such things are too manifold for our own good.

An observation about these three passages is in order before pushing on. I said that they indicate Kant's long-standing interest in empirical pattern recognition. I also noted above that pattern recognition must involve concepts: if I recognize that the figure I see in the clouds is that of a camel, I have not just formed an image; I have also formed a judgement that deploys the concept Camel: as when I say, 'That's a camel.' But the three passages quoted above seem to go in opposite directions on concept deployment, in a way suggestive of the distinction Kant will make in the Critique of Judgement between determining judgement and reflective judgement. In the former case, we start with the universal (a concept), and our problem is to find something particular that corresponds to it; in the latter case, we start with something particular, and our problem is to find the universal (concept) that we think suits it best. The passage from the Collins anthropology and the one from Dreams of a Spirit-Seer seem to give us cases of pattern recognition that might be described as acts of reflective judgement: a person forms an image by construing or 'going through' a manifold, and then seeks a concept. What is it I see before me? What is this an image of? Collins is explicit about this, for it reports Kant as saying that 'after one gets the image, one still needs the concept' (25.77.4). My own example of the scene of whitish patches moving against a green backdrop goes along this line: I combine or construe the manifold by running through it, forming an image out of it and then seeking the concept it seems most naturally to fall under. On the other hand, the passage from L1 is more suggestive of determining judgement. The visitor who is bedazzled and bowled over by the interior of St. Peter's already had some concept before entering the precincts, because he presumably knew at a very minimum that he would be visiting St. Peter's. That means he already had the concept Cathedral, or perhaps the concept of St. Peter's, or perhaps that of Papal Splendour. The problem, for this person, is to form a workable image to fit his concept.

I do not mean to suggest here that Kant already possessed, in the 1760s and 1770s, a distinction that he would not formally elaborate until the Critique of Judgement in the 1790s. But I do think he was already thinking in the early years about issues that naturally invite some such distinction. If we ask whether issues that invite such a distinction are relevant to thinking through what may count as combination or synthesis, I think the answer must be, yes. For one thing, combination or synthesis will always be involved in what Kant will later call determining judgement. That much is uncontroversial. But they must also be involved in reflective judgement, even if Kant does not precisely put it this way in the Critique of Judgement. For the problem of reflective judgement is to 'go through' a manifold, without the guidance of a concept ready to hand, with the hope of finding—by whatever means—a concept that fits the bill. On that score, judgements of empirical pattern recognition are indeed cases of reflective judgement. So too judgements about historical events. It seems obvious that these are activities of the mind that involve combination or synthesis: indeed, inevitably so. It should not be a surprise to us then if, in retrospect, we can think of combination or synthesis, as presented in Kant's earlier writings, in terms of the later distinction between determining judgement and reflective judgement. For we can either start with a concept and work up, by combination of a manifold, an image of a special case, or we can start, by combination of a manifold, with an image or some other kind of representation of something particular and then cast about for a general concept.[^22]

[^22]: I would like to express my gratitude to Andrew Chignell for pointing out to me how suggestive these passages and issues are of the distinction in the Critique of Judgement.

The case of empirical pattern recognition is useful, because it may help us answer a question raised by the opening paragraphs of §15 of the B-Deduction: how is the relation between combination and synthesis understood by Kant?

That the relation must be very close indeed is indicated by the words themselves. The Greek word 'synthesis' and the German word 'Verbindung' (which I have been translating into English as 'combination') overlap in meaning. Kant uses both of them in §15 of the B-Deduction. But he seems to single out 'synthesis' as having a certain nuance: he apparently takes it to designate a special, defining feature of combination. Thus he says that 'all combination . . . is an operation of the understanding upon which we might confer the general appellation "synthesis" in order to indicate thereby that we can represent nothing as combined in the object without having previously combined it ourselves . . . ' (B130). This passage suggests that every combination is a synthesis, but also that if we only spoke of it as combination, we would overlook something important. To speak of combination as such is to insist only on the fact that the relevant operation is carried out by the understanding. To speak of synthesis, however, is to indicate that combination is an act of the understanding that I have carried out on my own behalf. It is to treat combination as a form of 'self-activity' (Selbsttätigkeit). Here I am guided by Kant's own words. He says at the end of the passage I just quoted from B130 that talk of synthesis indicates that 'among all representations combination is the only one that it is not given through objects, but rather can only be brought about by the subject alone, because it is an act of self-activity[^23] [Selbsttätigkeit].'[^24]

[^23]: Here the emphasis is mine.
[^24]: In §1b of Chapter Five, we will consider passages in the Collins anthropology lectures from the early 1770s that testify to Kant's engagement at this time with 'self-activity'. Again, the German word will be 'Selbsttätigkeit'. The context in Collins is working out implications from Stoic moral philosophy.

The example of empirical pattern recognition can help us see more concretely what Kant has in mind. Consider the problem of spotting well camouflaged birds and wildlife in treetop foliage. This is a case along the lines of the passage we just considered from L1. Hence, it follows the paradigm of determining judgement: a person has a certain concept and the problem is to synthesize (by an act of self-activity) a given manifold in such a way as to yield an image of a special case. The so-called Biodôme in Montreal tries to recreate four different ecosystems including a tropical rainforest that has birds, animals, and a dense canopy of tropical trees. Spotting the sloth at the Biodôme is a serious problem, because the foliage is so dense and the animal slow-moving: it mostly just hangs from a branch as high up in the canopy as it can get. The visitor knows it is there, because it is well advertised on signs and brochures you can read going in (and from which you may acquire the concept Sloth, if you did not already have it). But usually what you see—at least to start out with—is just shadows and light. There is an obvious difference between seeing shadows and light and seeing the silhouette of the sloth against the shadows and light. Even if a Biodôme guide helpfully tries to point it out, the shadows and light will not magically align themselves to form the image of a sloth for you. Until you have somehow combined the manifold shadows and light in the right way, you will not see a sloth, no matter how hard you stare in the direction the guide is pointing. Combining the shadows and light in this way is something only you can do for yourself. You are the author of your sloth epiphany. Hence we may take seeing the sloth as an example of synthesis. Synthesis is thus a combination or a construal of the manifold that you cannot expect either the object itself or someone else to graciously perform for you; it is something that falls to you and you alone. In Kant's words from B130, we call combination 'synthesis' in order to indicate that 'we can represent nothing as combined in the object without having previously combined it ourselves.' This is the sense in which combination is a form of self-activity. I can represent as combined in the sloth the various qualities I perceived in him on my last visit to the Biodôme only to the extent that I myself combined them thus in my effort to synthetically construe the manifold shadows and light I saw there.

A second example suggestive of 'self-activity', quite different from the first, can be found in the Enquiry of 1764. The problem of this work, to start with, is to determine how philosophy and mathematics differ. The solution, whose details are not important just now, begins with the claim that there are two ways to reach any given universal concept and thus to state a corresponding definition. One way is to subject a concept we have already to analysis and thus to reduce it to its constituent part concepts. This is the procedure allegedly employed in philosophy. The other way—the procedure allegedly employed in mathematics—is 'through the arbitrary combination [Verbindung] of concepts', i.e., by putting together concepts we already have, as we see fit. That there is a close connection in the Enquiry between 'arbitrary combination' and synthesis is suggested by the appearance of the word 'synthetic' in the section heading over the remark just quoted: 'Mathematics arrives at all its definitions synthetically, while philosophy does so analytically' (2.276.5–6). The reason for thinking that the synthetic combination of concepts at work in mathematics is a form of self-activity is that mathematicians undertake it on their own behalf, namely to suit their purposes. Kant would presumably want to say in the Enquiry that, as a mathematician, I do not represent as combined in a concept anything that I have not already combined in it myself. Indeed, this idea seems to underlie Kant's account in the Enquiry of the greater certainty of mathematics.

Certainty and uncertainty depend on the degree to which we have command over our concepts and can say or determine what they include and what they do not include. As our command diminishes, we will be more likely to fall into error—on two different fronts. Either we could falsely believe that our concept includes something that it does not include, or we could falsely believe that our concept does not include something that it does in fact include. The way that we have arrived at our concept will determine how much command we have over it and hence the degree to which we may be certain about it. If I am a philosopher, I will start with a complex concept that has been given to me and I will try to figure out what it includes by analysis. How far I have pursued the analysis will determine the extent to which I am in command of my concept and its parts. Most of my concepts will be so complex that I can never hope to complete the analysis and thus I will always be more or less uncertain about them. If I am a mathematician, on the other hand, I will ideally start with simple—or, anyway, clear and easy to understand—concepts and I will combine them as I wish. Since I know which concepts I combined, and how I combined them, I will know everything I need to know about the complex concept I end up with. I need not worry that it might include anything of which I am unaware; nor will I be tempted to think that it includes anything that I did not deliberately put in to it. I should be able in principle to vouch for what is there and what not. This is the source of the greater certainty of mathematics in the Enquiry. It may well be a simplistic, over-optimistic view of mathematics.[^25] But it matters, for our purposes, because it is suggestive of the 'self-activity' Kant will later claim for synthesis and combination in §15 of the B-Deduction. I think that the Kant of 1787 may well have been willing retrospectively to accept his story from 1764 as an 'elucidation in concreto' of synthesis, as understood in the B-Deduction. This is what moves me to offer it as a second example to complement that of pattern recognition. Still it is important not to make too much of this.

[^25]: Even if we know with perfect certainty which elements we have arbitrarily combined in our concept, however, we will not be able to predict what the consequences will be. It is important to stress this, as Calvin Normore reminds me, because otherwise the view will turn out to be silly. One interesting example that helpfully illustrates the point and that should have been of interest to Kant is Apollonius' treatise on conic sections. Book Three can be understood to anticipate elements of projective geometry that did not begin to be elaborated until Pascal and Philippe de La Hire in the seventeenth century. It would be crazy to suggest that Apollonius had foreseen such developments, even though, on Kant's account, he was fully aware of all the simple ingredients he had combined in his concepts of the different conic sections.

There are two obvious differences between §15 of the B-Deduction and the business from the Enquiry that I laid out in the previous paragraph. The first and obvious difference is that Kant's remarks about synthesis in the Enquiry apply only to mathematics, whereas the self-activity at issue in the B-Deduction is relevant to all uses of a finite understanding like ours, including those involved in empirical pattern recognition. Second of all, self-activity as such is never taken up for its own sake in the Enquiry. Nor, to my knowledge, is it taken up and elaborated in other writings before the first Critique—not even in the Duisburg Nachlaß. Thus the idea that combination is the activity characteristic of our understanding as such and 'synthetic' by virtue of its 'self-activity' does not receive any direct attention from Kant until the Deduction and—I think—not until the B-Deduction. It may well be presupposed in the A-Deduction, but I have found no explicit statement of it there. If that's right, perhaps one important thing to say about the two versions of the Deduction is that the second version expresses very clearly Kant's recognition that the significance of self-activity needed a more direct treatment.

I offered two examples of synthesis: empirical pattern recognition and mathematical concept formation in the Enquiry of 1764. Though different, they seem to have something in common. They both combine a manifold as the expression of a person's 'self-activity'—Selbstätigkeit. This is spontaneity, but not just any kind. It is not an unscripted eruption of activity on the part of the understanding—the intellectual equivalent to spontaneous combustion or spontaneous generation. It is rather the spontaneity that I exercise on my own behalf such that I represent as combined in the object just those things that I myself combined in it. This conception of self-active spontaneity goes to the very heart of the programme announced in the preface to the second edition of the Critique.

We remember that Kant opens this preface by lamenting the state of metaphysics: it never makes progress; it gropes around blindly. The point is that no genuine science—no body of a priori knowledge of objects—ever finds itself in this state. Kant contrasts metaphysics with mathematics and natural philosophy. His thumbnail portraits of Thales, Bacon, Galileo, Torricelli, and Stahl are supposed to help us understand how these disciplines became scientific. In the case of Thales or whoever first demonstrated the construction of an equilateral triangle on a given line segment, Kant says that

> a light went on, because he found that he was required not to scout out either what he saw in the figure or even its mere concept and, as it were, read off its properties from that, but rather to produce [it] through that which he himself thought into it a priori according to concepts and that which he set out through construction and that, in order to know something a priori with certainty, he must ascribe to the thing nothing but that which follows necessarily from whatever he himself put in it according to his concept.[^26] (BXI–XII)

[^26]: The emphasis is mine.

The physicists and natural philosophers had a similar insight: 'They understood that reason only grasps that which reason itself produces in accordance with its design . . . ' (BXIII). Reason must compel nature to answer its questions and not 'let itself be nannied by nature, as though led about in a halter' (ibid). 'Even physics', he says, 'only owes such an advantageous revolution in its way of thinking [sc. as that brought about by Galileo and Torricelli—AL] to the insight that reason should seek out that in nature which it must learn from it in accordance with that which reason itself put in to it . . . ' (BXIII–XIV).

Kant plainly believes that mathematics and natural philosophy have secured the status of science, so far denied to metaphysics, because they are forms of self-activity in precisely the sense we encountered in §15 of the B-Deduction. Thales (or whoever it may have been), Galileo, Torricelli, and company did not passively take instruction from their object; they recognized as combined in it only that which they themselves combined and invested in it. In other words, they produced a synthesis, i.e., a representation 'that can not be given through objects, but can only be brought about by the subject itself' (B130). This is precisely what Kant characterizes in §15 of the B-Deduction as 'self-activity'. Kant is thus led to ask in the preface to the second edition of the Critique whether metaphysics would fare any better if self-activity of some kind were incorporated in to it:

> I should think that the examples of mathematics and natural science, which became that which they now are through a revolution carried out all at once, are striking enough to [make us] reflect on the essential part of the change in thinking that was so advantageous to them and to [lead us] in this respect at least to try imitating them so far as their analogy with metaphysics as cognition through reason permits. Hitherto one took for granted that all our knowledge must be oriented towards the objects. But all attempts, on this assumption, to determine anything about them a priori whereby our knowledge would be extended have amounted to nothing. Let it one time be tried therefore whether we do not make greater progress in the tasks of metaphysics on the assumption that the objects must be oriented towards our knowledge . . . . (BXV–XVI)

The objects will be oriented towards our knowledge if we follow the lead of Galileo, Torricelli, and company and accept that we will grasp in them only that which we ourselves produce according to our design. That would treat metaphysics as the outcome of a synthesis or self-activity of some kind. Kant goes on, in the next few lines, to compare what he is undertaking in the critique of pure reason to what Copernicus did in astronomy. Those remarks have led readers of the Critique to speak of a 'Copernican turn'. This indicates that the reflections about synthesis as self-activity in §15 of the B-Deduction, however brief and perfunctory, are central to the critical programme in the second edition.

One last remark is in order. We have just seen, in effect, that self-activity can come into play at times when we would not claim to have any knowledge of objects. Not every case of empirical pattern recognition directly results in knowledge as such. When we 'paint' camels and dragons in the clouds, we are engaged in self-activity. But we would not qualify such cloud-painting as knowledge, even though it would not be possible if we did not have some idea what camels and dragons are. This indicates that the programme announced in the preface to the second edition of the Critique will require some further reflection about the conditions under which self-activity yields knowledge and when it does not. That, as we will see in the next chapter, is the concern of §18 and §19 of the B-Deduction. Knowledge takes the form of judgement. Judgement will depend on self-activity. But so will the association of ideas, which does not yield knowledge. §18 and §19 of the B-Deduction are supposed to explain the difference between the association of ideas and judgement. If it succeeds, it will advance the programme and get us to the cases of self-activity that Galileo, Torricelli, and company made possible—and that might, or might not, be possible in any future metaphysics that might step forward as a science.

### §2c What is the relation between synthesis and apperception?

It is easy to see from my two examples in the previous subsection that synthesis, by its very nature as a form of self-activity, depends on something like what Kant calls apperception in §16, i.e., the act of spontaneity that expresses itself in the formula 'I think'.[^27] That, on the face of it, is a provocative claim, because Kant's remarks about apperception in both versions of the Deduction are notoriously Delphic. But I do not intend for my claim to be provocative. Indeed, I think it is a direct implication of what we have learned about synthesis so far.

If synthesis is a combination that only I can carry out for myself, I must really be able to carry it out for myself. That means I should be able to account to myself for what I have done, or at least the result of what I have done. Such accounts as I give myself will vary in content, because I do not always combine the same things in the same way. But they will all have a certain feature in common—a feature that Kant would call 'formal' just to indicate its independence from the content: all my accounts will begin with the expression 'I think' or an equivalent.[^28] As Kant himself puts it, 'the "I think" must be able to accompany all my representations, for otherwise there would be represented in me something that could not be thought, which is as much as to say that the representation would either be impossible or at least nothing for me' (B131–132). If I could not in principle affix an 'I think' to all my representations, I would not be able to account to myself for what I had synthetically combined in the effort to construe a manifold. If I could not account for this, I might just as well have done no combining at all. In other words, the result of my combining would be as good as nothing for me. It would be subjectively impossible, because I would have to deny my own synthesizing the very self-activity that characterizes it as synthetic. This is paradoxical. Therefore, it must be possible for the 'I think' to accompany all my representations, given that they all involve synthetic combinations.[^29]

This idea that synthesis requires a relation to apperception can be illustrated. The best example can be developed from the conception of mathematical certainty in the Enquiry that I discussed earlier. It is the best one, at least for now, just because synthesis in that case is so deliberate. We will consider much less deliberate cases later.

As a mathematician, on Kant's view in the Enquiry, I form and use no concept that is not the result of my own synthetic activity. I thereby represent as combined in the object—whatever this object might be—nothing that I myself did not previously combine in it. I can make this explicit at any moment by stating 'I think that, in the object, there is such and such a combination of such and such elements.' The representation of these elements as combined in the object (in whatever way I have combined them) is the result of my so combining them. Adding the 'I think' serves just to mark this fact. Colleagues might well come along and argue that it would be better or more fruitful to combine the elements I have combined in some other way. Or perhaps they might argue that I should combine elements different from the ones I have combined. I might well be persuaded by them. But even then, I will continue to insist on one thing, namely that I myself at first combined in the object the elements that I have been representing as combined in it. If I wish to emphasize my commitment to this point, I will preface what I have been thinking about the object with an 'I think'. This is enough to suggest programmatically that, even in the Enquiry, synthetic combinations in mathematics should be understood as being related to apperception, as conceived in §16 of the B-Deduction.[^30]

A stimulating 'elucidation in concreto' of my suggestion can be found in the early history of the theory of proportion, if we try to imagine how Kant would have understood it in the Enquiry. I do not know whether Kant was ever aware—or fully aware—of this history. But it would have been an interesting case for him, not just because of the issues I would like to explore on his behalf here, but also because he himself says explicitly (and not unreasonably) in the Enquiry that 'allgemeine Größenlehre' is the foundation of all mathematics (2.282.13–16). By 'allgemeine Größenlehre'—the universal theory of magnitude—I take him to mean the theory of proportion.[^31]

The different competing theories of proportion were developed out of different definitions of sameness of ratio. In Book Eight, Chapter Three of the Topics, Aristotle is a witness to one such definition: sameness of ratio consists in 'having the same mutual subtraction' (158b), as when a given parallelogram is divided by a line parallel to its sides. For then the segments of its base are in the same ratio as the two smaller parallelograms that result from the division of the given parallelogram.[^32]

If the two smaller parallelograms can be subtracted, the lesser P' from the greater P, exactly x₀-times, the two segments of the base can likewise be subtracted, the lesser B' from the greater B, exactly x₀-times. In other words, we have P = x₀·P' and B = x₀·B'. If P' can be subtracted from P x₀-times with a remainder r₀ less than P', then B' can be subtracted from B x₀-times with a remainder s₀ less than B'. In other words, P = x₀·P' + r₀ such that r₀ < P' and B = x₀·B' + s₀ such that s₀ < B'. But if there are remainders r₀ and s₀, and if r₀ can be subtracted exactly x₁-times from P', then s₀ can be subtracted exactly x₁-times from B' so that we have P' = x₁·r₀ and B' = x₁·s₀. If the subtraction is carried out x₁-times with remainders r₁ < r₀ and s₁ < s₀, then r₁ can be subtracted from r₀ and s₁ can be subtracted from s₀: either exactly x₂-times or x₂-times with remainders less than the previous remainders. If there are remainders, the process will continue: either it will never stop (in which case we are dealing with incommensurables), or it will stop, after the last remainders r i and s i go in to the previous remainders exactly x i-times. If, then, A and B are magnitudes of the same kind standing in the ratio of A:B and such that the lesser of the two magnitudes can be successively subtracted from the greater x₀, x₁, x₂, . . . xi -times, and if C and D are magnitudes of the same kind standing in the ratio of C:D and such that the lesser of the two magnitudes can be successively subtracted from the greater y₀, y₁, y₂, . . . y i, then A:B is the same ratio as C:D if and only if x i = yi for all i.

A significant alternative to the definition just stated is the one given by Euclid as Definition Five at the beginning of Book Five of the Elements. Suppose that A and B are magnitudes of the same kind and C and D are magnitudes of the same kind, then A:B is the same ratio as C:D if and only if, for all m and n, where m and n are whole numbers: mA>nB if and only if mC >nD, and mA =nB if and only if mC =nD, and mA <nB if and only if mC <nD. We can find a 'setting out' or 'exposition' of this definition in the proof that Euclid gives for Proposition One in Book Six of the Elements. This is the same proposition as the one I stated earlier, following Aristotle in the Topics, to 'set out' the definition of sameness of ratio in terms of mutual subtraction: two parallelograms under the same height, P and P', are to each other in the same ratio as their bases, B and B'. The Euclidean proof depends on extending the base B of the one parallelogram P so that it is equal in length to some arbitrary multiple mB and on extending the base of the other parallelogram P' so that it is equal in length to some other arbitrary multiple nB'. The proof then considers the parallelograms on the extended bases, P* and P*'. Since these parallelograms are under the same height as the two given parallelograms, P and P', they are the same arbitrary multiples of P and P' as the extended bases B* and B*' are of the two given bases, i.e., P* = mP and P*' = nP'. As P* is greater than, equal to, or less than P*', so its extended base B* is greater than, equal to, or less than the extended base B*' of the other. We can see that this satisfies the requirements of Euclid's stated definition for sameness of ratio. So we conclude that P:P' is the same ratio as B:B'.

In the Enquiry of 1764, Kant would have taken these two definitions of sameness of ratio to state the result of two different syntheses or combinations of shared and different elements. At least one element is common to both: the concept of 'magnitude as such', which Kant explicitly characterizes in the Enquiry as the object of all mathematics (2.282.13–17). They have some other things in common as well. They both presuppose that magnitudes of the same kind can stand in a ratio with each other and that ratios of magnitudes can be compared with respect to size. For some ratios are the same in size; some are greater, and some are smaller than others. But the two definitions are different and differ partly in respect to some of their elements (they also differ in their consequences, as we will see later).

Perhaps Kant would have said that the elements that help distinguish the two definitions turn on the way they each represent the ratio between two magnitudes of the same kind. The one definition represents a ratio in terms of the mutual subtraction of its two terms and thus defines sameness of ratio by comparing the mutual subtraction of one pair of terms and that of another. The Euclidean definition leaves mutual subtraction out of the picture. It represents a ratio as a distribution. This, at any rate, is the helpful suggestion of Augustus De Morgan, the author of the entry on proportion in the Penny Cyclopaedia.[^33] By distribution, he means the placing of one set of magnitudes among those of a second set, once the magnitudes of the second set have been arranged in order. As an example, he invites the reader to consider the distribution of the multiples of 3 among the multiples of 8. He puts the latter in Roman numerals to help make the idea intuitive: 3, 6, viii, 9, 12, 15, xvi, 18, 21, xxiv/24, 27, 30, xxxii, and so on. Then he restates Euclid's definition of sameness of ratio (or proportion) in terms of a distribution. We should understand it, in other words, as the same distribution of the multiples of one magnitude among those of another. De Morgan puts it this way: 'Four magnitudes, A and B of one kind, and C and D of the same or another kind, are proportional when all the multiples of A can be distributed among all the multiples of B in the same intervals as the corresponding multiples of C among those of D. Or whatever numbers m and n may be, if mA lies between nB and (n+1)B, mC lies between nD and (n+1)D.'[^34] In other words, viii and xvi will be in the same ratio as 9 and 18 just in case, if (m)viii lies between (n)xvi and (n+1)xvi, then (m)9 lies between (n)18 and (n+1)18—for all m and n. I do not know whether Kant would have accepted De Morgan's paraphrase of Euclid's definition. But suppose, just for the sake of argument, that he did. Then, from the position staked out in the Enquiry of 1764, he would say that an important difference between our two competing definitions of proportion or sameness of ratio is that the Euclidean definition combines the concept of distribution with the concept of magnitude and related concepts, while the definition to which Aristotle is a witness in the Topics combines the concept of mutual subtraction with these other concepts.

Suppose that I am a mathematician and that I have embraced the definition of sameness of ratio in terms of mutual subtraction. I have therefore combined the relevant elements in the relevant way. (Let it be noted explicitly, before going any further, that Kant gives us no guidance in the Enquiry about the way combinations are carried out in mathematics: whether, for instance, they might have different logical forms, and if so, what they might be.) Suppose, as I say, that I have carried out the relevant combination in the relevant way. I will find myself in disagreement with any colleague of mine who has embraced the Euclidean definition. How does this disagreement play out on Kant's view in the Enquiry?

One thing is certain. My Euclidean colleague cannot come to me and say that my definition overlooks anything in the concept of proportion, for example, the concept of distribution, which figures in her definition. Even if it had not occurred to me that the concept of distribution might be useful for the theory of proportion, I cannot be said to have overlooked it, because it was never a constitutive part of what it means for magnitudes to have the same ratio: i.e., it was never a constitutive part of some concept out there, independent of my combinations and those of my colleagues, the discovery of which would settle our dispute by determining for us what sameness of ratio means. If such a concept existed and if it included distribution, then I would concede that I had overlooked distribution, once it had been brought to my attention; I would then correct my definition accordingly. But that concept does not exist and hence there is nothing to overlook. Kant himself says at the very beginning of the Enquiry, 'The concept that I elucidate [sc. in mathematics by means of a definition—AL] is not given prior to the definition, but rather it first comes about through the definition itself'—i.e., through a synthesis or combination I carry out (2.276.14–15; cf. 2.281.32–35; 2.283.29–30). If my Euclidean colleague insists that I have overlooked something, indeed something essential, I can always refer her to my definition and remind her that it is the result of my combination of the relevant concepts. I combined everything I wanted to combine; I left out nothing that I wanted in. Perhaps I deliberately chose to leave out everything essential to the Euclidean definition, because I had reason to do so (2.291.11–15). But rehearsing all these Kantian lines for the benefit of my Euclidean colleague is equivalent to prefacing my definition with the 'I think': I think that sameness of ratio consists in having the same mutual subtraction. Maybe, in fact, the 'I' in that 'I think' should be printed in italics for emphasis, which suggests that my definition has an essential relation to apperception, just because it is the result of self-activity—and not the expression of a concept out there someplace, lying in wait until somebody comes along and discovers it.

I would like to make one more observation about the mathematical disagreement we have just been imagining. If the two definitions of sameness of ratio we have been discussing are the result of synthetic combinations that people have carried out by acts of self-activity, then it seems that there are two different concepts of the same thing. Each has an essential relation to apperception. But this apparently means that each of two different mathematicians has a proprietary interest in his or her preferred definition. It seems, then, that they—the two mathematicians—must be at logger heads. Kant himself says explicitly at the beginning of the Third Reflection in the Enquiry that synthetic combinations are not, as such, prone to error (2.291.7–8). How could they be? They are just arbitrary combinations of concepts ('arbitrary' is Kant's word). So I have my definition of sameness of ratio and the Euclidean mathematician has hers.

That is indeed the inference, but it does not imply, on Kant's view in the Enquiry, that discussion between the two mathematicians must end in a stalemate. We read at the end of §1 of the First Reflection that 'the business of mathematics is to join and compare given concepts of magnitudes to see what may be inferred from them' (2.278.8–10). Kant himself gives examples of mathematical definitions, but none like mine, where we have two definitions of the same thing and must deliberate between them. Still, his remark in the passage I just quoted suggests that, in the case I am considering, he indicates a way forward: it would be to consider the consequences of the competing definitions.

As a matter of fact, our competing definitions of sameness of ratio have very different consequences. For one thing, the Euclidean definition can be used to give a general proof for 'alternando': if A:B:: C:D, then A:C:: B:D, where A, B, C, and D are all magnitudes of the same kind. That is Proposition Sixteen in Book Five of the Elements. No straightforward general proof of alternando from the definition by mutual subtraction is possible. It would be established case by case, for each of the different kinds of magnitudes: a separate proof for lengths, parallelograms, parallelepipeds, cylinders. Oskar Becker provides the details in a classic paper from the 1930s first published in Quellen und Studien.[^35] One might think that it would be better for a theory of proportion if we did not have to keep proving the same thing for special cases. That would be a reason for preferring the Euclidean definition. On the other hand, one might dislike that definition just because it is so abstract: it is hard to make it intuitive; indeed, it is hard to understand what it means. That might be a reason for preferring the definition by mutual subtraction. Still again, this definition has other costs besides the lack of generality: the book-keeping needed to determine when two ratios are the same can get very complicated when the mutual subtraction must be carried out many times. In short, a rational discussion about the merits of the two definitions can indeed be carried out: it is a question of evaluating their different consequences. That suggests in turn that, for Kant in the Enquiry, such a discussion will ultimately be guided by what the different stakeholders value in a theory. Such values are not dictated by the object as such, which, in this case, is magnitude. Magnitude does not tell us to prefer generality or intuitive plausibility. Because these values are not dictated by the object, they will be expressed by different acts of self-activity. That makes sense: what I value intellectually is up to me. The way I make my values explicit to myself and to others is by prefacing an 'I think' to the acts of self-activity that are my contributions to a discussion like the one I just imagined. Hence, such acts of self-activity will have an essential relation to apperception. Disagreements in mathematics will depend on such a relation, even (indeed precisely) to the extent that they are constructive, stimulating, and so do not degenerate into a: I have my definition; you have yours.

The mathematical case, as presented in the Enquiry, is useful, because it can help us see concretely the relationship between synthesis and apperception, as understood in §16 of the B-Deduction. But we remember that Kant contrasts the case of mathematics in the Enquiry with that of philosophy. This contrast is itself useful. For Kant says in the Enquiry that philosophy does not arrive at its concepts and definitions synthetically. By implication, philosophers are not as such self-active, because self-activity is the distinguishing mark of synthesis. From the perspective of §16 of the B-Deduction, then, Kant's account of philosophy in 1764 gives us a way of seeing what it would be like, if it were possible, to orient ourselves in thought without any relation to apperception. For there can be no such relation where there is no synthesis.

I am presupposing for the purposes of this exercise that, by the time of the B-Deduction, Kant had made a significant revision to his portrait of the philosopher in the Enquiry. This needs argument, before setting out in earnest, because it is well known that he continued to think that philosophers carry out analysis on concepts: this is supposed to be their task and their method. He says so explicitly in the 'Discipline of Reason' at the end of the Critique (B746). But Kant could keep this commitment as late as the 1780s while revising other things he had said about philosophers some twenty years earlier. For nothing in his conception of self-activity in the Critique excludes analysis, whether carried out by philosophers or anybody else. The only thing excluded from the Critique is the fundamental claim in the Enquiry that analysis is a perfectly good way—besides synthesis—of arriving at a concept and indeed the way that ought to be followed by philosophers as such (2.276.5–277.7). That can no longer be the case, if it is true, as Kant says at A77/B103, that

> no concept—as far as its content goes—can arise analytically. Rather the synthesis of a manifold (whether it be given empirically or a priori) first produces a cognition: though this cognition may well be crude and confused to start with and may therefore need analysis, still synthesis is indeed that which in truth gathers the elements and unites them into a certain content. It is therefore the first thing we must attend to, if we wish to evaluate the first origin of our cognition.

Let the philosophers analyse away. They will perform a great service too, if they convert a 'crude and confused' cognition into one that is refined and cogent. That much is compatible with the Enquiry. The significant correction to the earlier work here is that the philosophers will have nothing to analyse if they have not themselves performed a prior synthesis. Synthesis is the origin of all our cognition and hence all our concepts. Hence, there can be no philosophy without it, even if the job of philosophy is to carry out analysis. All philosophers in the Critique—good and bad—carry out syntheses (at least to get started); no good ones do so in the Enquiry.

With that in mind, we may now consider how a disagreement between philosophers will play out in the Enquiry.

Two philosophers engaged in a discussion about the definition of justice are discussing a concept that is given to them both (to the rest of us as well). The question they ask of each other is what the concept contains. They could very well overlook something essential: does it include doing injury to one's enemies or not? They could get the answer wrong, because it is not up to either of them (2.291.18–24). They can get started on an answer by subjecting the concept of interest to analysis. But it is so complicated that it cannot ultimately be analysed into constituent parts that are not further analysable. This will likely be the case for all other concepts of interest to philosophers. Consequently, we can never really be sure what is packed into them. From the standpoint of §16 in the B-Deduction, much of what these concepts contain is as good as nothing for me.

The philosopher, as understood in the Enquiry, is thus set up for a state of thinking that we might have to characterize as subjectively paradoxical—from the standpoint of §16 in the B-Deduction. His thoughts about justice will presumably make use of the concept of justice given to him in whatever way such concepts are given to us. How else is he to think thoughts about justice? But if that concept must remain as good as nothing to him, what is he thinking when he uses it? Can he report to himself or to anybody else that he thinks such and such about justice when he does not know what he is thinking? We can always say on his behalf that expressing half-baked thoughts is a widespread phenomenon, even in philosophy—all too familiar to count as paradoxical. But there is at least a whiff of paradox here insofar as the philosopher thinks whatever he thinks about justice by means of a concept over which he ultimately has so little command. He will face an outbreak of florid paradox if he can get no command over it, while nevertheless reporting his thoughts about justice to us. Even if things never come to such a sorry pass, he will be susceptible to various kinds of intellectual neurosis that are never encountered in mathematics. Indeed, such neurosis—and bad philosophy (as Kant understands it in 1764)—may be an important source of relief from paradox.

For one thing, the philosopher will be susceptible to subreption: 'subreptio' in Latin; 'Erschleichung' in German. The word in either language has a special connotation: that of something unwanted creeping under, or getting past, the defenses we put up. The thing that is unwanted here is a misrepresentation—that of justice or whatever else the philosophers may be discussing. Since they cannot ultimately get a handle on justice, even with a lot of analysis, they are likely to jump the gun and smuggle in to their thinking about it elements that, as much as anything, they would like to believe belong to it.

Kant himself gives an example of an 'Erschleichung' in the Enquiry. It is that of the philosophers' definition of contact. He says that they define contact as 'the immediate, mutual presence to each other of two bodies' (2.288.29–30). Their motivation for introducing the definition is to attack the Newtonian doctrine of action at a distance, as through empty space. The philosophers want to argue that two bodies cannot immediately act on each other, unless they are in contact and hence that action at a distance is impossible. Their definition is supposed to be the basis of their argument. But Kant claims that it is 'erschlichen'—subreptic. For he says it rests on the feeling of resistance to penetration we have when we touch a body (2.288.10–16). That feeling is the effect of the body immediately acting on us: on our fingertips, kneecaps, elbows or the like. We call this contact, which is fine, so far as it goes. The mistake or Erschleichung is to assume, from this familiar experience, that impenetrability or contact is the only way two bodies can be immediately present to each other. That cannot be assumed, because it is precisely the thing in question. The philosophers have thus no case against the Newtonians. They import into their definition of contact what they would like to believe is true of all physical interaction—in the absence of a complete understanding of all the forms such interaction might take. The doctrine of action at a distance might not be true: Kant is not taking sides in this passage of the Enquiry. But he says that it is not undermined by this philosophical definition.

It is obvious, on reflection, that subreption is a kind of synthesis. If a philosopher smuggles into his concept of justice that which he hopes or expects to find there by analysis, he has, in effect, represented as combined in justice just the things he himself has combined in it. He has done so on his own behalf by a synthetic act of self-activity. The problem here is that it has not been owned up to as such. It has been put forward, perhaps from self-deception, as the result of an analysis that has not taken place. Kant does not explicitly characterize subreption as synthetic in the Enquiry. But he could have, had he wished to. He does call our attention to syntheses that occasionally occur in philosophy, but they are not as such subreptic. He says that they are just clarifications of eccentric word use: nominal, rather than real definitions. As an example, he cites Leibniz' notion of a slumbering monad: 'Leibniz imagined a simple substance that had nothing but obscure representations and called it a slumbering monad. He did not explain this monas [sc. by analysis—AL], rather he made it up. For the concept of the same was not given to him [sc. as philosophical concepts typically are—AL]; on the contrary, he invented it himself' (2.277.15–19). Subreption, by contrast, is a distinctively philosophical form of synthesis that purports to give us a real definition. It is always mistaken, because it is the result of the philosophers' peculiar predicament: the difficulty in carrying out the analysis of complex concepts and the natural temptation to mistake what one would like the outcome of the analysis to be with the outcome itself (should it ever come to light). But though always mistaken, subreption is always synthetic.

Kant does not say in the Enquiry how widespread subreption is. But we know that the inaugural dissertation of 1770 takes it to be a significant problem and sets out to expose and correct 'metaphysical errors of subreption', i.e., the hybrid concepts that result when metaphysicians surreptitiously combine pure intellectual concepts and other representations that have been drawn from the senses and sensibility. An example of such a concept might be that of the rational soul as a simple substance—so far so intellectual—with the added concept of corporeal qualities drawn from the senses, e.g., the capacity to occupy space somehow, as when I suppose that my rational soul is somehow present in the space occupied by my body as though by a physical force. I have argued that the discovery of these metaphysical errors was a signal to Kant that metaphysics had to be radically reformed, because he was committed to the traditional idea that metaphysics is concerned with objects of the intellect and not those of sensibility.[^36] But it is also clear in retrospect that this discovery signalled a sort of empowerment by synthesis for metaphysicians—at least if we reconsider, by the light of §16 in the B-Deduction, the portrait Kant had painted of philosophers more generally in the Enquiry of 1764. If metaphysicians synthetically add to their pure concepts of the intellect all sorts of junk drawn from sensibility, and even if they do this on the sly due to bad habits of thought rather than any deliberate policy, they can—at least in principle—be held to account for what they think. If someone were to point out to them, for example, that their conception of the rational soul is tainted with sensible representations of the body, they could not reply defensively that that is 'as good as nothing' for them. After all, this conception was not merely 'given' to them like a piece of baggage packed with who-knows-what and thrust upon an unwary traveller by a stranger at a foreign airport: they cooked it up on their own. It would certainly be harder for these metaphysicians than for the mathematicians of the Enquiry to take stock of what they had done, because they are not fully aware of what they have been doing—that's a given with an error of subreption—and may therefore be uncooperative. But it is in the nature of the case that they should be able in principle to recognize the hybrid concepts they have been using as the fruit of their own synthetic combinations. This follows from the assumption about certainty built on the notions of synthesis and combination in the Enquiry; it also follows from Kant's own efforts in the inaugural dissertation to expose and correct the different errors of subreption to which he believed metaphysics was vulnerable. Those efforts would be practically pointless if it were impossible for metaphysicians to recognize these errors as their own.

The upshot now is that the metaphysicians under scrutiny in the inaugural dissertation have a distinct advantage over the philosophers in the Enquiry, at least the ones who are doing what Kant took to be good philosophy in 1764, when considered retrospectively from the standpoint of §15 and §16 of the B-Deduction. However misguided they may be, their thoughts and commitments are at least in principle under their command. If they are kept honest, they can tell us what they think without any whiff of paradox clinging to their claimed ability to report to us. The Kant of the inaugural dissertation may have to work at breaking down their recalcitrance. But nothing in the nature of the case condemns that work from the outset. There is historical proof of this: Kant himself came to recognize his own errors of subreption, if the account I defended elsewhere of his trajectory towards the inaugural dissertation is right.

This just goes to show that subreptic metaphysicians think thoughts that have a necessary relation to apperception. That is what preserves them from the whiff of paradox, even if it does not preserve them from doing bad philosophy. By contrast, those who do good philosophy, as understood in the Enquiry, think thoughts that are ultimately almost as good as nothing for them. Their thoughts do not have a necessary relation to apperception. Even if analysis can bring to light enough of what these philosophers are thinking to preserve them from a florid case of paradox, they are susceptible to another form of intellectual neurosis—perhaps more pernicious than subreption, which can in principle be cleared up by the right kind of reflection. We can see this if we ask these philosophers a simple question: how are their complex concepts given to them? The Enquiry does not address this question. But a passage in a footnote to §36 in the Prolegomena can be understood to explore a plausible answer—and to expose the pathology that may well accompany it.

The passage I have in mind is about the laws of nature. Kant has just said that these laws and our recognition of them as such must be the fruit of a synthesis carried out by the understanding. If we deny that they are the fruit of a synthesis, we will have little other recourse except to follow Crusius and imagine that 'a spirit [Geist] who can neither err nor deceive originally implanted these laws of nature in us.' What is the result of entertaining Crusius' suggestion? Kant answers as follows:

> But since nevertheless it often happens that deceptive principles sneak in—indeed this very man's system offers not a few examples—the appeal to such a principle [sc. the appeal to such a spirit—AL] seems not especially promising, given the lack of reliable criteria for distinguishing between an authentic origin and an inauthentic one, since one can never know for sure what the spirit of truth or the father of lies might have transmitted to us. (4.319.31)[^37]

Kant's point here can be reformulated to apply to the more general case I have been discussing. If we deny that some, or all, our concepts are the fruit of a synthesis, and if we try a Crusius dodge to explain how they were given to us by saying that they were originally implanted in us by some sort of spirit, then we have surely opened the door to some serious worries. How do we know that the spirit is beneficent and truthful? How do we know that it has not been transmitting all sorts of fakes and cheats to us? Therein lies the path to certifiable craziness. It is a craziness to which the philosopher in the Enquiry is susceptible, if he persists in thinking his concepts are given to him. For he might just as well say that the relevant spirit is a CIA operative doing all the synthetic combining and hence all the thinking for him: the concept he is given by the spirit is the expression of the spirit's self-activity, not his own. But this is nuts. If the state of mind of the philosopher in the Enquiry is not downright paradoxical, it will prove susceptible to intellectual paranoia—at least once he gets around to addressing the obvious question: how are his concepts given to him seeing that they are not the result of his own synthetic combinations?

These considerations present the philosopher whose portrait is painted in the Enquiry with a conundrum. No philosopher who engages in subreption must worry about paradox, but every such philosopher will prove to be engaged in wishful thinking. No philosopher who avoids such wishful thinking will avoid both paradox and paranoia: either that philosopher will claim to entertain thoughts about justice, or what-have-you, by means of concepts over which she has no command; or she may end up having to grant that the thinking of these thoughts is due to a thinker other than herself. That is the predicament of somebody whose thinking is not the effect of self-activity and as such is without an essential relation to apperception—so runs the diagnosis, given §16 in the B-Deduction. Since the mathematician never seems to face this predicament and since, moreover, he is—as a mathematician—engaged in self-active synthesis, it seems natural to suppose that the philosopher might also spare herself these travails, were she just to disregard the pronouncements of the Enquiry and start synthesizing. The spectre of spirits working for the CIA would disappear. So would any whiff of paradox. All her thoughts would have an essential relation to apperception. Then, so long as the philosopher starts synthesizing, it seems not such a big leap to say that we all do, no matter who we are or what our professional profile might be. In that case, it will be natural to accept Kant's claim in the B-Deduction that synthesis, insofar as it has an essential relation to apperception, is the characteristic activity of the understanding as such. I am not suggesting that the ideas I have elaborated here reconstruct the evolution of Kant's thinking on all this from 1764 to the second edition of the Critique. It is not clear to me how this would have played out historically. But I do not hesitate to say that the professional profiles of the mathematician and the philosopher, as sketched in the Enquiry, have given us a useful 'elucidation in concreto' of the way Kant was thinking in the B-Deduction of the relation between apperception and synthesis.

The case of the metaphysician who commits an error of subreption, like those Kant claims to correct in the inaugural dissertation, raises one last question about our 'elucidations in concreto'. From the standpoint of the B-Deduction, the philosopher and the mathematician in the Enquiry give us a picture in black and white. As a mathematician, I am fully in command of my concepts; as a philosopher, I may be completely and paradoxically powerless with respect to them, depending on how closely I adhere to Enquiry strictures about analysis. The metaphysician who commits an error of subreption shows us that there is a spectrum of greys in between. One may well wonder how such a spectrum is possible. But the answer is that we do not now have to be fully in command of our thoughts in order to be ultimately in command of them. It is difficult to conceive of God and the rational soul in a way that is both rigorous and informative; hence it is natural to assimilate into our concepts of these things our thoughts of other things that, in the order of discovery, are better known to us, though less well known in themselves. The history of metaphysics offers many examples of this process of assimilation. But though subreptic assimilation may well be understood as a form of synthesis, along the lines of §15 and §16 in the B-Deduction, it is not often fully aware of itself as such, unlike the form of synthesis that Kant seems to attribute to the mathematician in the Enquiry. This does not imply at least two different things. First of all, it does not imply that the metaphysicians can never arrive at an explicit understanding of what they have been doing intellectually. We saw that the project of the inaugural dissertation is built on optimism that this is always possible. But second of all, it does not imply that those metaphysicians who remain in the dark about their intellectual activity will be consigned to the paradoxical state of the philosopher in the Enquiry. On the contrary, as long as they have been combining for themselves, however subreptically, they will in principle have title to the results. If they have combined in the rational soul all sorts of corporeal qualities they know only through the senses, they will not fail to represent the rational soul in bodily terms even if they are not aware of the smuggling they have engaged in. Here is the place to point out that the notion of 'self-activity' associated with synthesis in §15 and §16 of the B-Deduction requires of me only that I myself previously combine in the object whatever I ultimately represent as combined in it. In other words, I must do the combining myself, but I do not have to be aware of doing so. Indeed, Kant says at A78/B103 that 'synthesis as such is . . . the result of the imagination, an indispensable function of the soul without which we would have no knowledge, but of which we are seldom even conscious.' My having carried out the synthesis ensures that I can reap its fruits, but it does not ensure that I understand exactly how I produced these fruits. If I do subsequently come to understand how I produced these fruits, I may well discover that my understanding of the fruits themselves has to be revised. But that does not mean that the fruits remain out of reach as long as I fall short of this self-understanding. This important and interesting fact can be illustrated by the case of empirical pattern recognition—a case we considered in sub-section 2b.

The metaphysician who commits an error of subreption falls on the lighter end of the grey spectrum we have been discussing, because he can become fully aware of the steps he took to synthesize his concepts. On the darker end of the spectrum lies anybody who has spotted the sloth at the Biodôme or made out constellations among the stars in the night time sky or anything else of the sort. There is no doubt in these cases that, unless I synthetically combine the manifold of shadows and light, I will not spot the sloth, because no one can do this for me. Having carried out the synthesis, I can report to you that I have indeed spotted the sloth. But I will never be able to report exactly which steps I took in synthesizing the manifold—except in the most general, crudest of terms. I might be able to tell you that first my glance fell on the branches roughly four feet in front and below him. Then I directed my glance above and beyond him by as many feet. Then I directed my glance right through him, but didn't spot him—and then when I shifted my weight a bit to my heels, suddenly I pulled him out of the shadows. That might do as a rough account of what happened, but it is not really a history of my synthesis as such. In fact, I will more usually be reduced to telling you that I just stared in the right direction long and hard enough. The mathematician of the Enquiry can tell us, by contrast, exactly which ingredients he put into his concept and in which order: proofs in Euclidean geometry can be understood to give us an explicit record of this. Even the metaphysician who has committed an error of subreption, and who has identified it as such, will have succeeded in uncovering the history of her synthesis. But the sloth-spotter will never really be able to do anything of the kind. And yet the fruit of his synthesis cannot be denied him, when he reports to us: 'I think I saw the sloth and he seemed to be really shaggy.'

### §2d What is the relation between apperception and unity?

We have seen that synthesis, by its very nature, necessarily relates to apperception, because the 'I think' formally expresses the self-activity I have invested in it (my synthesis). But there is another angle from which to view the matter. Kant says that all combination, and hence all synthesis, presupposes unity. This seems to be a robust claim. On the face of it, a weaker claim can be made that has the virtue of being totally obvious: unity is an effect of my having combined a given manifold. Before I started combining, the manifold lacked unity; it exhibited unity after I was done with it. Kant would certainly not deny this totally obvious claim. But he clearly intends something stronger, because he says explicitly at the end of §15 that unity is what makes combination and synthesis possible as such. He puts it this way: 'Combination is the representation of the synthetic unity of the manifold. Hence the representation of this unity cannot arise from combination. Rather it [sc. the representation of synthetic unity—AL] is that which, by being added to the representation of the manifold, makes the concept of combination possible in the first place' (B131). The point here seems to be that the unity in question is not the effect of combination; rather combination is the effect of unity. This reading seems to find confirmation in the first clause of the next sentence where Kant speaks of that unity that 'precedes a priori all concepts of combination'—and that, by so preceding them, makes them possible, or so we presume. The weak claim and the robust claim taken together would seem, on the face of it, to take us in a circle: unity comes from combination, which itself comes from unity. The circle, if there is one, could be either vicious or virtuous. Much will depend on how we take 'unity'—a word that may be said in many ways. At the very end of the paragraph under consideration, which coincides with the end of §15, Kant says that we must seek the unity at issue in that which contains 'the possibility of the understanding even in its logical use' (B131).[^38] We know from §16 that this unity is closely associated with apperception. The question, of course, is how. But that question must start as a grammatical one, because until we understand what Kant is saying, we will not understand what he means.

Throughout §16 and the sections that follow, Kant repeatedly makes use of the phrase 'Einheit der Apperzeption', i.e., 'unity of apperception'. Both 'unity' and 'apperception' are often modified by adjectives. But, for now, that does not matter. What does matter is that 'Apperzeption' is in the genitive, and this genitive is ambiguous. A classicist would say that it could be either an 'objective' genitive or a 'subjective' one.

We can capture the relevant ambiguity in English, if we consider a grammatically equivalent construction like 'the love of the mother'. Without more context, we cannot tell whether this phrase refers to the love felt by the mother or the love felt for the mother by somebody else. The difference corresponds to that between a sentence with 'love' as a transitive verb and 'mother' as its subject—as when we say that 'the mother loves her children'—and a sentence with 'love' as a transitive verb and 'mother' as its direct object—as when we say that 'the children love their mother.' In the first case, we have the English equivalent of a subjective genitive; in the second case, the English equivalent of an objective genitive.

The difference between the two kinds of genitive cannot come into play, however, unless the word that governs the genitive—in English, the word that precedes the preposition 'of'—is related to a transitive verb. In the example case, this is easy to see, because 'love' is the verbal noun of the transitive verb 'love'. But, unlike 'love', neither the English word 'unity' nor the German word 'Einheit' may be considered a verbal noun. They are both abstract nouns formed out of the word for 'one': the English has been taken from the Latin 'unitas', and the Latin word for 'one' or 'single thing' is 'unus'. But, in §16, Kant uses the closely related transitive verb 'vereinigen' or 'unify', as when he writes: 'The thought that . . . representations given in intuition all belong to me is as much as to say that I unify them [vereinigen] in one self-consciousness or can at least unify[^39] [vereinigen] them therein' (B134). It is not unreasonable to take Kant in §16 as treating 'unity' or 'Einheit' as the result or even the act of 'unifying' something, in which case 'unity' or rather 'Einheit' might well be treated, in this context, as a verbal noun for 'vereinigen'. 'Unification' and 'Vereinigung' would be more usual. But nothing would be changed in §16, either in the original German or the English translations, if 'unification' and 'Vereinigung' were everywhere substituted for 'unity' and 'Einheit'. Moreover, 'Vereinigung' (unification) is indeed the word we find in parallel passages in the Prolegomena.

In the Prolegomena, Kant avoids the expression 'unity of apperception'. But when it comes time in §22 to distinguish between the synthetic unity of apperception and its analytic counterpart, he uses the word 'Vereinigung'. 'This unification of [sc. representations—AL] [Vereinigung] in one consciousness', he says, 'is either analytic, through identity, or synthetic, through the putting together [Zusammensetzung] and the coming together [Hinzukunft] of different representations' (4.305.5–9). In §36 of the Prolegomena, he speaks in similar terms to express the idea that the synthetic unity of apperception is the source of the law of connection of all appearances: 'We can take the law of their connection [sc. that of appearances—AL] from nowhere else save from the principles of their connection in us, i.e., the conditions of the necessary unification [Vereinigung] in one consciousness that constitutes the possibility of experience' (4.319.5–10). These passages do not have the relevant genitive, because Kant has replaced 'Apperzeption' with the adverbial phrase 'in einem Bewußtsein' ('in one consciousness'). But they are significant, because they document the equivalence of 'Einheit' and 'Vereinigung', i.e., 'unity' and 'unification', for the purposes of translation. Thus we do indeed find in Kant's expression 'unity of apperception' an ambiguity like the one we found earlier in the expression 'love of the mother'. For it is not clear whether we have a subjective genitive such that apperception effects the relevant unity in something other than itself, or whether we have an objective genitive such that something effects unity in apperception. The solution to the problem will turn on the way apperception relates to the self-activity that seems to be characteristic of all synthesis and hence all thinking.

From the self-active nature of synthesis and the significance of synthesis for all thinking, we would naturally expect that a subjective genitive is required. Indeed, the passage I quoted from B134 two paragraphs ago can be understood in this way. Suppose I say: I think that P, Q, and R. However much P, Q, and R differ, they will share one thing in common: they will all bear the stamp of my authorship as the thinker who thought them. This is because I cannot claim them as my thoughts without having carried out on my own behalf the synthesis that made them possible as such. But having carried out that synthesis, I have unified P, Q, and R. That means, at a minimum, that P exhibits internal unity: it is not just a manifold in its own right; it is a manifold that I have unified and thereby appropriated for myself intellectually. So too Q and R. This much gets us to what I earlier called the 'weak claim': any unity exhibited by a manifold is the effect of combination, which has been carried out by apperception. If there is a new piece here, it is this. P, Q, and R also collectively exhibit unity: namely insofar as they are one and all my thoughts and, in Kant's words, are 'able to stand together in a universal self-consciousness' (B132). This would not be possible just from the fact that I am alive and alert and therefore empirically aware of each of them. I could be empirically aware of P, then Q and then R without being empirically aware of having entertained all three of them. For empirical awareness or 'empirical apperception', as Kant calls it, lacks self-activity: it never does anything—either on its own behalf or on behalf of anything else. It is thus unable to assimilate any of my representations into a 'universal self-consciousness'. That is something only pure apperception can do. To the extent that it does so, the expression 'unity of apperception' is a subjective genitive: pure apperception self-actively unifies every element to which it relates and thereby assimilates them all into a 'universal self-consciousness'—unlike its empirical counterpart.

That much seems easy and expected. But at least as much stress falls in §16 on the objective genitive. For pure apperception has itself been unified: it is itself one and not many. In other words, it exhibits numerical identity and is aware of it. Passages can be found in §16 and elsewhere that explicitly make this claim. In the first paragraph of §16, for example, Kant characterizes pure apperception as 'that self-consciousness that . . . is one and the same[^40] in all consciousness' (B132). In this respect too it must be distinguished from 'empirical apperception', which 'accompanies different representations and is in itself scattered and without any relation to the identity of the subject' (B133). I said before that empirical apperception is the awareness I have by being both alive and alert. I would not have any waking representations without it. It therefore accompanies all my waking representations. But empirical apperception as such, considered in itself, has no unity or numerical identity. It is as multiple and diverse as the multiple and diverse representations I have. This suggests that self-activity goes hand in hand with 'unity' taken with the objective genitive. Indeed, the one seems to be both the necessary and sufficient condition of the other. This is suggested by the following passage from §16: 'The relation [to the numerical "identity of the subject"] does not yet come about from the fact that I accompany every representation with consciousness, but rather from the fact that I add one to the other and am conscious of their synthesis. Thus only insofar as I can combine a manifold of given representations in one consciousness is it possible for me to represent the identity of consciousness in these representations' (B133). This is as we should expect. I cannot be expected to engage in any act of self-activity if my self is fragmented, because a fragmented self cannot act on its own behalf. Parts of it could be acting independently of other parts of it, but no single synthesis could be the result of their activity—not even their joint activity, since it is not clear how they could collaborate. On the other hand, if I myself exhibit the relevant unity or numerical identity and am aware it, I will be aware of myself as self-actively engaged in carrying out a synthesis.

Does Kant imagine that I could not otherwise be aware of my numerical identity? I do not take §16, all by itself, to shut down arguments—of whatever nature they might be—that I possess numerical identity and that all other rational beings do so too. But if we ask how any given one of us comes to be aware of it in his or her own intellectual person, then I take Kant to be offering a challenge: how so, if not by the awareness you have of intellectually acting on your own behalf—as when you engage in a discussion like the one I imagined in the previous subsection between the two mathematicians? If you urge your definition of sameness of ratio on your interlocutor, you do so on the understanding that you have your reasons for preferring to define it—say—as having the same mutual subtraction. That understanding comes bundled with the intellectual awareness that you are not an uncoordinated manifold of conscious beings: you are one and the same thinker of all your reasons for defending the relevant definition. That sort of intellectual awareness is prior to any independent arguments we could mount—of whatever nature they might be—that you and all other rational beings possess numerical identity. So even if such arguments are possible, they will not be what originally alerts me to my numerical identity as the thinker of my thoughts.

All that too is easy enough. But if we take the expression 'unity of apperception' to be an objective genitive, we will face a seemingly outrageous question. 'Love of the mother' is an objective genitive when, for example, we say that the mother is loved by her children. If the expression 'unity of apperception' is an objective genitive, then apperception itself is unified, but we must ask: by whom or by what? Fixing an 'I think' to my representations is an act of self-activity. We have seen at length and with the aid of examples what this means concretely. But we also saw that every act of self-activity is an act of spontaneity. The unifying of apperception itself is not something that can be carried out by external forces. Neither the spirit of truth nor the father of lies can carry it out, otherwise they might as well do my thinking for me. But nor can material nature do so. Material nature is itself a synthesis that we carry out progressively in our attempts to acquire knowledge. That is what Kant is saying in §36 of the Prolegomena. The thought underlying his remarks there will start to come into focus in §3 of this chapter when we discuss §17 of the B-Deduction. For now, though, we may say this. One of the things that can become an object of knowledge for us is the way that we ourselves are affected by natural processes. But acquiring such knowledge would not be possible if we had no intellectual spontaneity, i.e., if it were not up to us where to direct our attention and how to frame the questions we wish to put to nature, in keeping with the spirit of the remarks Kant makes in the preface to the B-edition of the Critique that we discussed at the end of §2b in this chapter. If it is indeed true that our own powers of understanding are up to us to that extent at least and if it is true that these powers express themselves in the act of fixing an 'I think' to our thoughts, then we cannot treat material nature as the subject of 'unifies' and hence as that which unifies pure apperception when the expression 'unity of pure apperception' is taken to be an objective genitive. The natural thing to think, of course, is that the subject we are looking for in the case at hand is pure apperception itself. If that is right, the expression 'unity of apperception' can be an objective genitive only if it is at the same time a subjective genitive. This could happen for the expression 'love of the mother' if the mother loves herself. In that case too, we would have a genitive that is both subjective and objective at the same time. The peculiar thing about the expression 'unity of pure apperception' is that it must be both at once—for the reasons I just indicated. 'Love of the mother' need not be. Even when we take 'unity of pure apperception' as a subjective genitive, as we did to start with, it will turn out, on reflection, to be an objective genitive as well. For if 'pure apperception' is the subject of the verb 'unifies' and if it unifies a manifold, it carries out a synthesis on its own behalf. When it does that, it unifies itself, i.e., it becomes aware of itself as having numerical identity. The upshot, in other words, is that the expression 'unity of apperception' seems to be ambiguous in precisely the same way as 'love of the mother'. But when we try to dispel the ambiguity, we discover that, unlike 'love of the mother', 'unity of apperception' must always be both objective and subjective at the time. That's what makes it special.

Do these considerations help us deal with the apparent circle at the end of §15? As we saw, Kant might be understood to have said there that unity is needed for combination, which in turn is needed for unity. But all combination is synthetic and therefore self-active. We have just seen that self-activity is both the necessary and sufficient condition of the unity of pure apperception, where the genitive is objective and subjective at the same time. An understanding is self-active just in case it exhibits numerical unity and its thoughts 'can stand together in a universal self-consciousness'. The apparent circle at the end of §15 reflects the biconditional.

### §2e Comparison with R4675 in the Duisburg Nachlaß

The line of thinking in §16 of the B-Deduction that we just finished exploring is related to the idea expressed in R4675 in the Duisburg Nachlaß that we discussed in §2d of Chapter One. The relevant passage in R4675 reads as follows: 'The condition of all apperception is the unity of the thinking subject. From thence flows the connection [added: of the manifold] according to a rule and in a whole' (17.651.13–15). This passage too may be read as leading us from unity to unity, by way of combination, in something like the apparent circle suggested by Kant's remarks at the end of §15. For the manifold put together 'according to a rule and in a whole', mentioned at the end of our passage from R4675, exhibits unity. It owes this unity to a certain activity of the thinking subject; it would be impossible for the thinking subject to engage in this activity if it lacked unity of its own.

One important difference between this passage from the Duisburg Nachlaß and §15 of the B-Deduction is that Kant speaks of 'connection' in R4675. The German is 'Verknüpfung' rather than 'Verbindung', the word in the B-Deduction that I have been translating as 'combination'. This could be significant, because I have argued that 'combination' in the B-Deduction connotes self-activity, and self-activity is not stressed in the Duisburg Nachlaß. Neverthless, Kant does indeed use the word 'Verbindung' as an apparent synonym for 'Verknüpfung' in a passage from R4681. For he writes: 'All combinations [Verbindungen] are made by the mind, and the mind combines [verbindet] nothing obiective except for that which is determined from its correlato. Otherwise, representations are indeed put together [zusammengestellt], but not connected [verknüpft], in perception to be sure, but not in concept' (17.668.7–11). It is one thing, though, to treat 'connection' and 'combination' as synonyms, as here, and another thing to treat them both as relating to self-activity. Because that notion is not in evidence in the Duisburg Nachlaß, we would expect unity to play a role in it different from the one it plays in the B-Deduction.

A related observation is that our passage in R4675 mentions unity and apperception, but never the 'unity of apperception'. Unless I am mistaken, this notion—so important to the B-Deduction—is never mentioned in the Duisburg Nachlaß at all. Perhaps the reason is that Kant came to think of apperception differently in the Critique.[^41] All he says explicitly on the subject in the Duisburg Nachlaß is that apperception is 'the perception of one's self as a thinking subject as such' (R4674–17.647.14–15) and that it is 'the consciousness of thinking, i.e., of representations as they are set in the mind' (R4674–17.647.16–17). On the face of it, this is a passive conception. Apperception seems to be just the thinker's taking in or taking note of his or her own thoughts and nature. It must be an immediate, intellectual grasp of myself, as I argued in §1d of Chapter One, because otherwise the story about the exposition of appearances told in the Duisburg Nachlaß will run into difficulties. But such a grasp may well be passive: indeed, that would have been the usual way to think of it among most philosophers of Kant's time and earlier. Leibniz believed that we, as conscious, thinking beings, have apperception and that it expresses the inner activity of our soul. But that was idiosyncratic. Descartes seems to take the insight I have of myself—and indeed all other intellectual insights—as purely passive. The act whereby I give my assent to such insights is active for him, but not the insight itself. I would never deny that the Duisburg Nachlaß acknowledges that thinkers are active: after all, they do connect manifolds 'according to a rule and in a whole'; moreover, they carry out 'functions' in the sense adumbrated in §2e of Chapter One. But it can say this and yet be quite innocent of any more active role for apperception than this. Be that as it may, there can be no ambiguity of genitive in the expression 'unity of apperception' if it never appears in the Duisburg Nachlaß. Thus the Duisburg Nachlaß cannot even begin to elaborate the considerations we reviewed in §2d.

A point related to the observation I just made is that, instead of the unity of apperception, our passage from R4675 mentions the 'unity of the thinking subject'. A further difference with the B-Deduction is that, nevertheless, no ambiguity of genitive can be found here. For the unity in question just seems to be my state of being as a thinker. As long as I endure as a thinking subject, I will be a single, simple substance: I never effect unity in myself, nor do I undergo a process of unification carried out by some agency other than my own.

When Kant speaks of the 'unity of the thinking self' in the Duisburg Nachlaß, he means the metaphysical unity of a single, simple thinking substance. It is ultimately because of such metaphysical unity that a thinking substance can connect a manifold 'according to a rule and in a whole'—with or without the help of apperception. There might be different ways of understanding this claim. But, as I argued in §2d of Chapter One, Kant understood it in terms of a parallel with claims he had made in the inaugural dissertation about God and the world. Thus he thought that the mind as a thinking substance unites a manifold into a whole of appearances in something like the way that God makes a whole world by uniting his creatures. He also thought that as a necessary, but insufficient, condition of producing a world in this way, God must be a single, simple thing, i.e., he must have unity. If there were more than one world-maker, the outcome would not be a single world, but a plurality of them. This is because a divine being cannot connect creatures unless they owe their existence to it; a given creature can owe its existence to only one divine being—not to a consortium. This admittedly carries over to thinking substances only so far. The big difference between a divine being and a human mind is that the former creates its own manifold; the latter passively receives it. Nevertheless, the human mind cannot bring together its manifold into a whole of appearances unless it is a single, simple, unified thinking substance. The reason is not so far different from that in the case of the divine being: it is a matter of title or jurisdiction. Just as a divine being's unity gives it title to do as it pleases with the creatures that owe their existence to it, so a human mind's unity can give it title to exercise its spontaneity over the manifold that has been sensibly given to it. If this mind were internally fragmented, it could be no more conscious of what is given to all its different parts than a properly unified one can be immediately conscious of what is given to other minds. Hence some part of the manifold might be as good as nothing to a mind gone to pieces.[^42] By having unity, then, the mind can have jurisdiction to unite a given manifold of appearances in something like the way that a single divine being can unite its creatures as a world.

In both editions of the Deduction, by contrast, Kant studiously avoids saying anything about the metaphysical constitution of the human mind as such. It could be substance or accident; it could be simple or complex. Instead of talking about what is doing the thinking, he wants to focus on the thinking itself: what it is like to the thinker who engages in it and what sort of constraints it operates under, given its quality of thinking as such. The biggest difference between the Deduction and the Duisburg Nachlaß may be one of perspective. The Deduction takes the perspective of the person who is thinking and asks how the activity of thinking seems to that person as the thinker of those thoughts. The Duisburg Nachlaß does not. The Deduction takes note of the fact that the unity of that person as the thinker of those thoughts is, or can be, discovered by him or her in the very act of thinking those thoughts. The Duisburg Nachlaß assumes that human thinkers have an intellectual intuition of themselves and that this is what reveals to them their spontaneity and simplicity of being, i.e., those qualities and gifts that are the object of the rational psychology in R4684. Intellectual intuition is thus directed at the nature of the thinker, not at thinking as an activity and the way it plays out for the thinker. These things are the concern of the Deduction. No intellectual intuition is required to bring them to light; they come to light through thinking, because this activity is transparent to itself.

The Deduction distinguishes itself from the Duisburg Nachlaß by offering what we might call, following the example of Husserl, a phenomenology of thinking. An effect of this phenomenology is to set up an 'epochē' of the underlying metaphysics: it postpones for another day or chapter the question what, if anything, we can learn from the phenomenology of thinking about the nature of the thinker as such. All we need to know for now is that carrying out a discussion about the best definition of sameness of ratio, for example, comes bundled with an understanding of my commitments as my own and myself as one and the same thinker committed to them. An Epicurean, committed to the materiality, mortality, and complexity of the mind should be able to sign on to this result with as little hesitation as a Leibnizean, committed to a radically different metaphysical psychology. This is as it should be. §16 does not take us very far: all by itself, it cannot settle any metaphysical debates. Nor can the Deduction, even upon completion. For though it teaches us, if the argument is sound, that the pure concepts of the understanding apply a priori only to appearances, further argument is needed to show what significance this result has for understanding the nature of thinking beings. Providing such argument will be the task of the Paralogisms.

Now our passage from R4675 and related passages in the Duisburg Nachlaß show a connection with Kant's early cosmology. That was a point I argued in §2 of Chapter One. I just tried to show in the current subsection of this chapter the respect in which our passage from R4675 differs from §15 and §16 of the B-Deduction. But I do not take these differences to signal a complete rupture either with the Duisburg Nachlaß or the early cosmology that informs it. Rather I take Kant to have continually adapted the ideas he took from his early cosmology in the service of his epistemological programme. He apparently believed that a priori knowledge and thought of appearances depends on converting them into a world-like whole overseen by a single, self-same thinker. One problem of adapting the early cosmology for the purposes of elaborating this idea is that of making due allowance for the finitude of human thinkers. The inaugural dissertation and the Duisburg Nachlaß can both be understood to contribute elements of a solution to that problem. §15 and §16 of the B-Deduction can be understood to make a further adaptation, namely by articulating the fundamental assumption that thinking, as a human activity, discloses itself to the human thinker who engages in it.

I said at the end of §1a of this chapter that the scopos of the first step of the B-Deduction (§15 to §21) is thinking as such and that the scopos of the second step (§24 to §26) is the cosmology of experience. We just found the scopos of the first step, as I characterized it, coming plainly into focus. But if it is true, as I just claimed, that §15 and §16 mark a continuing engagement with the concerns lying behind the passage from R4675 discussed here and in §2d of Chapter One and, beyond that passage, with the inaugural dissertation, then already we find an intimation of the cosmology of experience that will come more sharply into focus in §26 of the B-Deduction (see Chapter Four): that a manifold be taken up by the synthetic unity of pure apperception is the necessary condition for the understanding to legislate laws to nature and convert appearances into a world of experience. The intimation should not be taken as a sign that the first step of the B-Deduction is stepping on the toes of the second. Rather, the thing to think is something like this: if the first step does not bring thinking into focus with a view towards the cosmology of experience to come, the second step will not be able to take the argument to its intended destination.

### §2f Last point: thought as self-activity gives itself logical structure

But now the question is whether thought as such can really claim to have learned anything about itself from §16 of the B-Deduction. The official answer, of course, is yes, because, by the end of §16, we are supposed to have uncovered the principle that will form the basis of what is to come. This is the principle that 'the analytic unity of apperception is possible only under the presupposition of a synthetic unity' (B133–134). For, as Klaus Reich argued, this principle is really the foundation of all thinking as such. What I take to be the upshot of this principle, indeed even as Reich adumbrates it, is this: it tells us, in effect, that the nature of thinking as a self-activity is ultimately the source of its logical structure. The story goes like this.

Up until now, the discussion in §15 and §16 has played itself out solely in terms of synthesis and combination. The results of this discussion must now be applied to thinking as such. The larger context suggests that Kant is proceeding from a certain characterization of thinking, we have not discussed so far, namely that it is 'knowledge through concepts' (B94).[^43] Now all concepts are supposed to have a certain logical form, namely that of universality. Kant says this explicitly in the A-deduction (A106).[^44] He implies as much earlier in the Transcendental Analytic when he says, for example, that 'a concept holds for many' (B93). Thus when I declare that 'Rufus is a black cat', I am applying the concept Cat to a certain individual, but this concept will hold universally of all the members of a certain species of small carnivore. To that extent, I can also use it to make generalizations, as for example when I declare that 'cats always land on their paws.' The next step is to note that, because they are universal in form, all concepts are specifications of the 'I think'. This should strike us as obviously true when one considers first that every time I use a concept like Cat, I am and must be able to use the 'I think' to introduce it in a judgement. Thus if you ask me for my thoughts, I might say, for example, 'I think that cats are hunters of mice and birds.' That cats are hunters of mice and birds specifies what I think. But a deeper point is in play here. To say that a concept is universal in form is to say that it is the same thought we entertain about many different things. This can be seen in the case of the concept Cat, which remains the same thought whether I use it to think of Rufus, Tiger, Izzle, Roy, or any of the other cats I know. It can also be seen from the concepts of higher generality that I use when I make generalizations about cats and other animals. Thus Small Carnivore is more universal than Cat, since it can be used in generalizations about dogs and weasels too. But as such, it will be the same thought no matter which of these species I apply it to. Now the 'I think' is likewise a thought that remains the same no matter how many very different representations we make it accompany. It is the same 'I think' whether I am thinking about cats or dogs. As such, it has what Kant calls 'analytic unity'. The point to register now is that concepts too have analytic unity, just by virtue of their logical form. The difference is that their analytic unity gives a specific determination to that of the 'I think', which would otherwise be totally indeterminate.[^45] Now we are ready to make the last step in the argument and thus arrive at the conclusion Kant has in mind.

The analytic unity of the 'I think' is just the fact that this thought remains one and the same no matter what it accompanies. It is thus equivalent to the numerical identity of the 'I think'. But Kant says that I can become conscious of the numerical identity of the 'I think' only under the following condition, namely that I synthesize a given manifold. 'Only insofar as I can combine a manifold of given representations in one consciousness,' he says, 'is it possible for me to represent to myself the identity of consciousness in these representations' (B133). But given that concepts have analytic unity just by virtue of their logical form, and given moreover that their analytic unity is just a specification of that of the 'I think', the same thing follows for them, namely that I can become conscious of the fact that they are the same thought holding for many only if I synthesize some manifold. Until I have synthesized manifolds that I construe as feline, the thought that Rufus, Tiger, and Izzle are cats and that certain generalizations apply to them and a host of similar animals will be as good as nothing for me. But we know from earlier in §16 that all synthesis presupposes the synthetic unity of apperception. That leads us to Kant's intended conclusion: 'only on the assumption of a synthetic unity of apperception is the analytic unity of apperception possible' (B133–134).[^46] The implication for thinking is this. If all thinking is knowledge through concepts and all concepts are specifications of the analytic unity of apperception, it follows that there can be no thinking at all in the way familiar to us without the synthetic unity of apperception.[^47]

The statement and elaboration of this principle are Kant's best answer to the question about the 'scopos' of the B-Deduction. The B-Deduction—or rather the parts of it we have been examining—is an argument that brackets metaphysics and psychology as such. At least to start out with, it uses thought to disclose the most fundamental formal features of thought to itself. It does so on the basis of the insight that all thinking is based on synthesis and synthesis is a form of self-activity. It leads us ultimately to the recognition that this self-activity is one that imposes a logical structure on itself, i.e., the structure that is exhibited in concepts and their formal relation to one another. Once again, a final contrast with the Duisburg Nachlaß is instructive.

For we have seen that, at issue in the Duisburg Nachlaß, is the numerical identity of the thinking self as a simple substance. This identity is given to me as a brute fact about myself and the underlying metaphysics of my thinking. I am aware of it directly in 'apperception' taken to be some kind of intuitive, yet intellectual, self-awareness. By contrast, the Deduction focuses not on the numerical identity of the thinking self as a simple, immaterial substance, but rather on that of pure apperception (assuming the odd subjective–objective genitive). It denies that apperception is any kind of intellectual intuition, but rather takes it to be the self-activity formally constitutive of all synthesis and hence all thought; and thus it explicitly denies that the numerical identity of apperception, so construed, is a brute given: it is rather something that only reveals itself to me in the activity of synthesis itself. As Kant puts it at B133, 'Only insofar as I can combine a manifold of given representations in one consciousness is it possible for me to represent the very identity of the consciousness in these representations.' Of course, this famous claim ultimately depends on the insight about synthesis as a self-activity that we started with. That insight said that I cannot represent as combined in the object anything that I did not combine in it myself. If we accept that insight, then we must also accept that I cannot be conscious of, or represent to myself in any way, the identity of myself as a thinker, unless I first combine the relevant things, i.e., the things that are given to me as a consciousness. Those things are just the manifold of some intuition that I have received. So precisely because all thinking is a form of self-activity in the relevant sense, my consciousness of my own numerical identity as a thinker is itself a form or a product of self-activity. Once this result is in play, the principle currently under discussion is supposed to follow as an implication: 'the analytic unity of apperception is possible only under the presupposition of some kind of synthetic unity' (B133–134).

To sum up, the difference between the Duisburg Nachlaß and the first Critique is a matter of scopos. In the opening sections of the B-Deduction, Kant remains focused in a very disciplined way on thinking as such, doing his best to eliminate from his account all considerations that are 'extraneous and contrary' to the enterprise as he now understands it. Looking back on the Duisburg Nachlaß, he might have said of his earlier self what Aristoxenus can be understood to say about Archytas: his earlier self had not yet given an acceptable account of thinking as such, because he failed to define the scopos in the right way. That is, he failed to explain (empirical) thought in terms of the only thing relevant to it, which is thought itself.

### §3 Talk of Objects and the Central Argument of §17 of the B-Deduction

§17 supplies the first premise of §20, which completes the first step the Transcendental Deduction. It states that 'the manifold given in a sensible intuition belongs necessarily under the original synthetic unity of apperception, because through this alone is the unity of intuition possible' (B143).[^9] A question is raised thereby about the relationship between §17 and §16, namely whether the one marks an advance over the other. We already knew from §16 that the manifold of intuition had to be brought under the original synthetic unity of pure apperception. Indeed, we already knew that doing so—or the possibility of doing so—is a fundamental principle of the understanding itself: Kant himself says so explicitly in §16—not once, but twice (B134n., B135). This fact makes implausible or hollows out the otherwise natural suggestion, prompted by the very title of §17, that the task of the current section is just to show that the result of §16 is the 'highest principle of all use of the understanding' (B136).

[^9]: There is an alternative statement of the result in §17 itself that reads as follows: 'every manifold of intuition stands under conditions of the original synthetic unity of apperception' (B136).

A clue may perhaps be found in the first paragraph of §17 where Kant sets up a parallel between the principle of the necessary synthetic unity of apperception, as presumably established in §16, and space and time, as treated in the Transcendental Aesthetic. Thus the former principle is supposed to be the supreme principle of the understanding in just the same way that space and time were shown to be the supreme principles of sensibility. This parallel strongly suggests the language and strategy of the section that appeared in the second edition without number between §13 and §15 under the title, 'Transition to the Transcendental Deduction of the Categories'. (For ease of discussion, and following the example of the 'Academy Edition', I will henceforth refer to this section as §14.)

§14 turns on a parallel between the categories and the intuitions of space and time. As pure forms of sensibility, space and time just are the conditions under which something can appear to us. Hence, appearances necessarily agree with them, since otherwise they would be as good as nothing for us. Kant has already said explicitly and unambiguously in §13 that the categories are not forms of sensibility: that is how he got us to feel the urgent need for their transcendental deduction. But now he asks in §14 whether they might not be like forms of sensibility in a certain way. Suppose that they are 'formal' conditions of thinking just as space and time are the 'formal' conditions of sensibility. Then given that all experience includes thinking as well as sensing, it will follow that all appearances necessarily agree with the categories insofar as they are objects of experience. As objects of experience, such appearances will be objects of thought. They can no more fail to agree with the categories as the formal conditions of thinking than they can fail to agree with the pure intuitions of space and time as the formal conditions of sensibility. This leads Kant to say in §14 that he has a 'principle' for the Transcendental Deduction, namely to show that the categories are indeed the formal conditions of all thinking and to that extent conditions a priori of the possibility of experience as such (B126–127). If he can show this, he will have established that the categories relate a priori to all those appearances that are objects of thought and hence objects of experience.

Kant conspicuously says nothing at all about the categories in §17. But the sympathetic resonance of the first paragraph of §17 with §14 suggests that the strategy announced in the latter section has now started to come into play. In other words, it looks as if the point of §17 is indeed to establish in the most general terms the formal condition of all thinking and hence all experience, i.e., the 'supreme principle of all use of the understanding'. This principle is nothing other than the result of §16. Thus we may expect the argument of §17 to be that the understanding cannot think any object unless 'every manifold of intuition should stand under conditions of the original synthetic unity of apperception.' It will not be until §20 that Kant more fully carries out the strategy of §14 and connects this principle stated generally of the understanding as such with the categories.

This observation helps explain how §17 will move us beyond §16, and indeed in the direction of §20. For §17 must introduce talk about objects if any progress is to take place. A concern for objects is what drives the strategy announced in §14; this strategy rests on the idea that objects of thought agree with the supreme principle of the understanding in the same way that objects of the senses agree with the supreme principle of sensibility. Hence, the task of §17 is to show not how thinking and understanding as such are possible: that would indeed reduplicate the result of §16. It is rather to show how the result of §16 may be developed to explain how thought of an object is possible for an understanding such as ours. It is surely symptomatic of the state of the argument that no talk of objects appeared in §15 and §16 and that such talk appears for the first time in §17, as can be seen from a first-pass reading of the second paragraph of §17 where Kant spells out explicitly the argument of this section:

> Generally speaking, understanding is the faculty of knowledge. Knowledge consists in the determined relation of given representations to an object. But an object is that in the concept of which the manifold of a given intuition is united. But now all uniting of representations requires unity of consciousness in their synthesis. Consequently, the unity of consciousness is that which alone constitutes the relation of representations to an object, therewith their objective validity, and consequently that they come to be knowledge; hence, unity of consciousness is that upon which rests even the possibility of the understanding. (B137)

It is easy enough to account for the gross structure of this argument. First, Kant gives a characterization of the understanding. He says that it is 'the faculty of knowledge'. It is striking that he characterizes it now in these terms rather than as before in §16, namely as the 'faculty for combining a priori' (B135). But the explanation must be that the terms of §17 allow Kant to do as he must and could not otherwise, namely introduce talk about objects.[^48] For it might be argued that there can be no talk about knowledge without talk about the objects of knowledge. Next he must say what he takes knowledge to be. Accordingly, he tells us that knowledge 'consists in the determined relation of given representations to an object.' The problem then seems to be to specify how such a relation is possible. We expect Kant to try to argue that no such relation—hence no knowledge—is possible without the principle of the necessary synthetic unity of pure apperception. Suppose he succeeds. Then the result will be: 1. I have knowledge only if my given representations relate to an object; 2. My given representations relate to an object only because I have subjected a given manifold of intuition to the synthetic unity of pure apperception. The question is how he thinks he can get this result. The answer depends on how he conceives of objects, which depends in turn on exploiting insights about concepts familiar to us from §16.

Kant says in §17 that 'an object is that in the concept of which the manifold of a given intuition is united.' It is striking that he characterizes objects here in terms of our concepts of them. No doubt he has two reasons for this. First, and as noted above, the central argument of §17 rests on the characterization of the understanding as the faculty of knowledge: Kant presupposes that we can have no knowledge without concepts.[^49] Second, the move should in principle allow him to connect considerations about knowledge and objects of knowledge with his new-found principle of the necessary synthetic unity of apperception. The strategy is not spelled out in full, but it is surely designed to exploit the conclusion from §16 that 'only on the assumption of a synthetic unity of apperception is the analytic unity of apperception possible' (B133–134). As we saw in §2f of this chapter, this conclusion directly concerns concepts. It turns on the fact that concepts have analytic unity because of their logical form. Because they are universal, concepts remain one and the same thought no matter which individual or set of individuals we apply them to. They specify the analytic unity of apperception, which is itself the paradigm of all concepts. For the 'I think' remains one and the same thought no matter which representation it accompanies. But I cannot become aware of the analytic unity of apperception unless I synthesize a manifold and subject it to the synthetic unity of apperception. The same must hold for the analytic unity of concepts. Therefore, the supreme principle of the use of all concepts must be the synthetic unity of apperception. It is easy to see now that this is supposed to take us directly to objects. Concepts contribute to our knowledge; to that extent, they relate given representations to objects.[^50] But all use of concepts depends on the synthetic unity of apperception. It follows that the synthetic unity of apperception is the principle under which concepts relate given representations to objects and thereby yield knowledge. This seems to give us the result we were anticipating, i.e., numbered propositions one and two stated above.

I said earlier that it is easy to account for the gross structure of this argument. So it is, once we spell it out more fully than Kant himself does. But ease of exposition in this case does not yield greater understanding. The argument does nothing to illuminate Kant's thinking about objects or what it means for a concept to relate given representations to an object in such a way as to generate knowledge. To be sure, these issues arise in the argument—as indeed they must if Kant is to advance the strategy he announced in §14. But it happens so quickly that one may well wonder whether the relevant moves are purely verbal. To help us clarify Kant's thinking, let us consider an argument suggested by remarks he makes in the A-deduction at A104–105 in the section called the 'Synthesis of Recognition in Concepts'.

### §3a A parallel passage in the A-Deduction

At A104, Kant appears to address the problem I just raised. For he stops short and says, 'Here it is necessary to clarify what one means by the expression "an object of representations".' It is natural to expect that the remarks he goes on to make will shed light on §17 of the B-Deduction. But Henry Allison once doubted whether this expectation is reasonable. He energetically argued in the 1983 edition of Kant's Transcendental Idealism against the strategy I am proposing.[^51] In the 2004 edition, he acknowledged a point of contact between the two passages, but also warned against making too much of it. The fundamental reason for his caution was, I think, the main one already articulated in 1983: the notion of object in the two passages, he said, is not the same. Thus he argued in 2004 that, in the passage at A104ff, Kant is concerned with 'the thought of an object corresponding to, and distinct from, our representations', whereas 'in the B-Deduction, the focus is not on an object considered as distinct from our representations but rather on one considered in abstraction from its manner of giveness.'[^52] Allison appears to have softened his stance in his 2015 book on the Transcendental Deduction,[^53] but he still stresses points of divergence: he now says that the passage from the A-Deduction presupposes the Transcendental Aesthetic while §17 of the B-Deduction has put it on hold for the time being.[^54] Whatever the current state of Allison's thinking, the point he made so unequivocally in 1983 has the virtue of being perfectly true on the face of it. For the passage at A104ff does indeed speak of objects 'corresponding to, and distinct from, our representations'. But I think that such talk reflects the rhetorical strategy of the passage at A104ff and that this strategy itself reflects a philosophical position shared with §17 of the B-Deduction: we can put to rest the worry that the argument in §17 of the B-Deduction is purely verbal if we understand what this position is and what motivates it.

That we do indeed find an important parallel between the two passages is already suggested by a point made by Hermann de Vleeschauwer. Any reader will notice that at A104, Kant very suddenly shifts gears. Until now, he had been working up an account of the three 'subjective sources that constitute the foundation a priori for the possibility of experience': these three sources are apprehension in intuition, reproduction in imagination and recognition in concepts (A97). But at A104, Kant begins to speak of objects for the first time. It is natural to ask what moves him to do so. De Vleeschauwer suggests that he is anticipating an objection. Because he has entrenched himself in a discussion of the subjective sources of empirical knowledge, it would seem he can no longer carry out the programme of the Transcendental Deduction, which is to show that the categories relate to objects a priori. Kant cannot focus only on the subjective side of the equation; he must say something about objects if he is not to lose sight of his mission.[^55] De Vleeschauwer's point is instructive, because it recalls the fact that at the outset of §17 we find Kant at the same threshold. He has said nothing specific about the 'three subjective sources' of experience at issue in the A-Deduction. But that is only because the corresponding discussion in the B-Deduction takes place at a level of such sweeping generality: it is supposed to apply to all synthesis no matter what form it takes. But at that level of generality, it covers the same ground as the relevant section of the A-Deduction on the three subjective sources of experience, which are themselves just three different kinds of synthesis. The difference between the relevant part of the B-Deduction and that of the A-Deduction is, among other things, that the former leaves out the details of the latter. That being the case, we should expect to find Kant pressed to make the same move in both. And so he is: just as he feels the need at A104 to introduce talk of objects into the A-Deduction, so too in the argument from §17 of the B-Deduction. Indeed, we saw earlier that unless he introduces talk of objects at this point of the B-Deduction, it is not clear how he can carry out the strategy for a Transcendental Deduction he has announced in §14.

Immediately after having declared at A104 that it is time to clarify what is meant by an 'object of representations', Kant makes an assertion about appearances: 'We said above that appearances themselves are nothing but sensible representations that in themselves must not be regarded in just the same way as objects [Gegenstände] (outside the power of representing).' The internal reference here is apparently to an assertion Kant made just three pages earlier. At A101, he said that 'appearances are not things in themselves, but rather the mere play of our representations that ultimately amount to determinations of inner sense.'[^56] It is a much vexed question how in general Kant conceives of appearances. But it is as clear as one could ever hope how he conceives of them in these two passages: they are nothing but sensible representations. As such, Kant expects them to elicit talk of objects from us. This is plain from the fact that he goes on immediately to ask what such talk could mean. He states this question in the following terms: 'What then does one mean when one speaks of an object [Gegenstand] corresponding to, but also as a result different from, knowledge' (A104). Allison would certainly have stressed in the past that the question presupposes we are talking about objects as those things 'outside the power of representing'; and, he would have argued that such talk is not in evidence in §17 of the B-Deduction. Again: he would be right, as far as that goes. But I would like now to call attention to two more parallels between the two texts—this time parallels of detail.

First, it is clear from the statement of the question at A104, as I just quoted it, that Kant expects us to talk about objects not only as being 'outside of the power of representing', but also as being objects of knowledge. Our argument in §17 is fundamentally about objects in this, the latter sense. But second of all, when Kant characterizes knowledge in §17, he does so in terms that recall the question posed in A104, for he says it is that which consists in the 'determinate relation of given representations to an object.'[^57] 'Given representations' is synonymous with 'sensible representations'. Sensible representations are at issue in our passage at A104, precisely insofar as this text explicitly treats appearances as subjective.[^58] These observations suggest that nothing so far prevents us from thinking of Kant as asking in §17 just as he does at A104: 'What does one mean when one speaks of an object corresponding to, yet different from, knowledge?' I ultimately hope to show that Kant's answer to this question, as stated at A104ff, resurfaces in §17.

Once the question has been stated at A104, Kant goes on—as I understand him—to argue that our talk of objects 'corresponding to, yet different from, knowledge' is problematic or unhelpful without critical reflection. Here I am following the lead of Wolfgang Carl.[^59] That Kant is critical of our talk of objects is suggested by the way that he attempts to spell out, on our behalf, what we could possibly mean when we engage in such talk. Thus he says: 'It is easy to see that this object must be thought of only as something as such = X, because, outside our knowledge, we have nothing that we could set over and against our knowledge, as corresponding to it' (A104). As long as we regard this object as something entirely distinct from anything we know, there is nothing in particular we can say about it: it is just a something or other. The point will seem to turn on an equivocation. Kant apparently represents us as saying or implying that the object of knowledge is other than knowledge and infers from this that we are thereby committed paradoxically to saying that we do not know the object of our knowledge: it is just a something we know not what = X. But on closer inspection, the point seems to be more serious. If we are challenged to show that the object really does correspond to our knowledge, and yet treat it independently of the knowledge we have, we will have denied ourselves the means of answering the challenge. We cannot, as it were, set the object and our knowledge side by side and compare. A challenge to show that the object corresponds to our knowledge is, in fact, a challenge to show that what we say about the object is true. The only way to meet such a challenge is to confront our claims with genuine items of knowledge that have withstood such tests in the past. As T. H. Green puts it at the beginning of the Prolegomena to Ethics, 'Nothing can be known by help of reference to the unknown.'[^60] As Kant himself puts it at A104, 'outside our knowledge, we have nothing that we could set over and against our knowledge, as corresponding to it.' If you ask me whether it is true that a dodecahedron can be inscribed in a sphere, I will first have to review the different items of knowledge that I know have been proved about these two figures (and others). If you tell me that I may not conduct such a review, I will have to ask you how you expect me to answer your question.

Since it is unhelpful to speak of the object independently of knowledge, Kant invites us now to speak of the relation of knowledge to the object. He says that we conceive of this relation as exhibiting necessity of some kind and that we take our justification for conceiving it in this way from our persistent belief that the object is 'over and against' knowledge (dawider). As such, we conceive of it as preventing knowledge from being arbitrary or left to chance (aufs Gerathewohl oder beliebig). We cannot rightfully say anything we please about the object, because it acts as an external brake against falsehood. This is also supposed to mean that if we make more than one true pronouncement about it, they will all necessarily agree with each other. It is not clear how Kant takes us to understand this necessary agreement. But the thought must be that all the true pronouncements that constitute knowledge of an object somehow belong together—indeed necessarily so—just by relating, in the way required for truth, to one and the same object. Does that mean that they form a system of some kind? Who knows.[^61] The key thing seems to be that the necessary relation between each of them and this object goes hand in hand with the necessary agreement of each with all the others.

I think that Kant remains suspicious of our continued talk of the object as 'over and against' knowledge. That is strongly suggested by his remark in the next paragraph that 'something that is supposed to be different from all our representations' is nothing for us (A105). If it is nothing for us, it cannot really be the sort of thing that stands in a relation to our knowledge. Nevertheless, Kant apparently finds something valuable in the things we say about this relation, especially in the idea of a necessary agreement of our different items of knowledge with each other. Again, it is not clear how Kant takes us to understand this agreement. But the rough shape of the idea can be discerned. For it is precisely by some such necessary agreement that we can answer the challenge whether our claims about the object are true: we will test our claims against items of knowledge that have already withstood scrutiny; they will pass the test only if they necessarily agree with—i.e., are somehow corroborated by—the items of knowledge summoned to test them. Kant says, moreover, that this agreement expresses itself as a 'unity'. That is not surprising. The items of knowledge that necessarily agree with one another about dodecahedrons are a unity just by being all about the same thing and, I think, by somehow mutually corroborating each other. Kant is then led to say that this unity among items of knowledge is what 'constitutes the concept of an object'. This too is unsurprising, since by forming a unity, the items of knowledge that necessarily agree about dodecahedrons collectively inform what we think about dodecahedrons and to that extent yield the concept (or theory) of a certain object, namely that of the dodecahedron.[^62]

We will have to examine more closely the cluster of ideas that emerges from Kant's reflections in this extended passage from the A-Deduction—especially what he takes to be the relation between an object of knowledge as such, the relevant concept, and the unity that he associates with both concept and object. But here we would do well first to take stock of as much of the passage as we have seen and consider its general tenor. What follows is a very brief synopsis of the passage, as I understand it, followed in turn by my assessment of its argumentative strategy, followed in the next subsection by a few examples that are designed to help clarify more concretely how Kant is thinking about these matters.

Kant asked us at the beginning of the passage what we mean when we speak of an object 'corresponding to, yet different from, knowledge.' He argued that such talk is unhelpful if we treat the object independently of knowledge. That led him to reconsider the question in light of the things we say about the relation of knowledge to the object. That line of enquiry led him to conclude in turn that we take our concept of the object from the unity among the relevant items of knowledge. The advantage of speaking in these terms is supposed to be that it does not treat the object independently of knowledge. It satisfies the important intuition that we justify our claims about the object, when pressed to do so, by testing them against the unified set of things we already know about the object. At the same time, it is supposed to raise an expectation about the object itself, namely that, as an object of knowledge, it too exhibits unity, indeed precisely that unity which is expressed in the necessary agreement of our items of knowledge about it. This necessary agreement is formally expressed in the special unity constitutive of a concept.[^63] Hence, the object as an object of knowledge is itself constituted by the same unity constitutive of our concept of it. This is, in fact, as we should expect, though the details are unclear, since concepts make an essential contribution to our knowledge, and the object at issue all along has been not just some shadowy object = X we know not what, but an object of knowledge as such. This is what it means to speak of an object 'corresponding to' knowledge, though it comes at the cost of giving up talk about the object as 'different from' knowledge, i.e., as that which can be set over and against it for the purposes of comparison.

As I understand the line of thinking at A104 running through the first two full paragraphs of page 80 in volume four of the Academy Edition, Kant starts from what he takes to be ordinary ways of talking about objects of knowledge: hence the locutions, 'what do we mean when we speak of . . . .' and 'we find that . . . .'[^64] He then subjects the ordinary things we say to critical scrutiny and comes to the conclusion that what we say must be reconsidered, in the way I have indicated. It is, indeed, a peculiar passage. But it distinguishes itself from §17 of the B-Deduction not by doctrine, emphasis, or perspective (as I will argue in due course), but perhaps rather by strategy of argument. The passage at A104ff is some kind of conceptual analysis that might be characterized not too implausibly as a mini-Socratic elenchus. It is not laid out as a dialogue, nor can we easily distinguish in it two different voices—as though it were a dialogue (as we find, say, in the first Meditation where we do easily distinguish the voice of the firmly resolved Meditator and that of his weaker willed, philosophically much more complacent self). Nevertheless, Kant seems to be inviting us to consider what we take to be the object of knowledge and challenging us to defend what we say about it. He seems to be using what he anticipates will be our commitments, arguments, and aporiae to steer us toward the insight he thinks we should get out of the exercise. By contrast, the argument in §17 of the B-Deduction is much more straightforward. However brief it may be, it is presented as a bunch of premises, one after the other, with the conclusion they are supposed to license at the very end. That sort of argument has the advantage that it can be assessed in a straightforward way. What are the premises? Are they all there? Does the conclusion follow from them? But the advantage of the argument in the A-Deduction is that it should ideally be more pedagogically useful. For if it were more felicitously carried out than in fact it is, it could quite easily help us understand what motivates one of Kant's central commitments in the Deduction and elsewhere in the Critique: namely that you cannot say anything meaningful about objects of knowledge if you insist that they have nothing to do with knowledge.

### §3b Examples and elucidations in the interest of assessing the central idea about objects in the parallel passage in the A-Deduction

Since the topic of pedagogy has come up, it is natural to ask for 'examples and elucidations in concreto'. For without them, it is hard to test whether we really understand what Kant is saying. The passage at A104ff is disappointing in this respect, because it offers us very little.[^65] If left to our own devices, however, we will probably find that the most natural examples seem to come from geometry.[^66]

Suppose I claim to know something. If you come along and ask me what it is, you will not be satisfied if I tell you that I know something = X. You will want me to tell you more specifically what it is that I know. If I tell you equilateral triangles, you will still be unhappy until I become a little more expansive. One special way to satisfy you is to ask you to give me a line segment—any line segment will do. I will describe intersecting arcs of two circles with the line segment as their radius and the extremities of the line segment as their respective centres. In accordance with the first proposition of Book One in Euclid's Elements, I will join each of the extremities to the point of intersection. Thus I will have constructed an equilateral triangle, i.e., a triangle whose three sides are demonstrably the same length and whose interior angles are the same size. This should satisfy you—or begin to satisfy you—that I really know what I am talking about. For I have put on display my knowledge of this specific object. I have done so by generating the object itself, and indeed in such a way that there will be no disagreement between us that this is the object at issue. Thus it turns out that the conditions of knowing the object and the conditions of possibility of the object itself (as an object of knowledge) are precisely the same. It also turns out that my knowledge and its specific object are in an immediate relation to each other. For they are both the product of the same operation of the understanding, namely the constructive procedure laid out by Euclid at the beginning of Elements, Book One. Moreover, this relation is necessary. I cannot fail to know equilateral triangles if I have mastered the technique for constructing them; and, I cannot say just anything I please about them either. This necessity manifests itself in the fact that the different elements of my cognition agree with each other, i.e., form some kind of unity: I know equilateral triangles because of the peculiar connection I have established among the items of knowledge involved in Euclid's construction, i.e., the knowledge I have of straight lines, circles and their radii, and the fact that between any two points I can produce a straight line segment. My very concept of the equilateral triangle comes from this unity. The same unity is mirrored in the very structure of the equilateral triangle itself.

The geometrical example is felicitous, because it seems to capture the insights Kant apparently wants to retrieve from us without capitulating to the intuitions he finds aporetic, notably the idea of an object treated as something independent of knowledge. But it works as well as it does, because geometry is a body of knowledge that constructs its object. I cannot really have knowledge of equilateral triangles without knowing how to generate them—in thought at least. I cannot generate them without betraying my knowledge of them. Thus it is natural to think of the object of geometry as internally given—as a byproduct of knowledge itself—and not as something on the outside of knowledge trying to make itself heard, but just beyond earshot.

It is important not to overstate this observation. Construction is an essential part of classical geometry; Kant was sensitive to this fact. But he would not say that all our concepts in geometry are directly drawn from, or depend only on, construction, because that is not true. I do not know everything about parabolas just from having learned from Apollonius how to produce one by cutting any cone in the right way. The construction all by itself does not even tell me, if P be any point on a parabola, whether that point satisfies some condition, much less what that condition might be. Unless I know this, I will not make much progress in the study of parabolas.[^67] Nevertheless, Kant will want to say that geometry is special and relevant here, because the necessary agreement between concept and object is secured by construction at the outset of the enquiry and because, along the way, it may be shored up and take new results within its compass by auxiliary constructions required for more advanced propositions about parabolas, e.g., the problem of constructing a tangent from any given point on a parabola.[^68] Much more might be said about classical geometry and what Kant could reasonably expect from construction and what not, but that will do for now.[^69]

The felicity of the geometrical example just as naturally raises the question whether other bodies of knowledge can be so well accounted for by Kant's critical reflections in our current passages from the A-Deduction. It is much less obvious that the object of physics is internally given in the same way. Here the reply can only be speculative and controversial. I would suggest that Kant took geometry to be paradigmatic for other branches of knowledge, or—at any rate—believed that, in the relevant respects, other branches of knowledge have a lot more in common with geometry than one might think.[^70] Thus physics may be understood to construct the physical world in thought, just as geometry constructs plane and solid figures. Again, it will be important not to overstate the claim. But we can get a more concrete, precise idea of what it might mean by reference to the Universal Natural History and Theory of the Heavens of 1755.[^71] For Kant himself claimed there to have grasped the physical structure of the universe, i.e., why the planets revolve in the same direction on a common plane around the sun at the centre, and why our whole solar system forms a galaxy with other such systems by revolving with them in the same direction on a common plane around a greater central body, and why systems of galaxies can form still greater systems in the same way. He argued that this structure of systems upon systems evolved from an original state of chaos as the effect of randomly scattered material particles exercising upon each other their original forces of universal attraction and repulsion under Newton's laws of motion. Kant can be understood to have used the assumption of these laws and forces as constructive procedures, analogous to those used by geometers to solve constructive problems—like that of inscribing a dodecahedron in a sphere. But instead of constructing a geometrical figure, he used his procedures to construct a world in thought. Upon the success of this construction he staked his claim to have understood why the universe has the physical structure that it does. As in the geometrical examples we were considering earlier, the idea must have been that you cannot fail to understand the reason for this physical structure, if you can show how it was produced. To produce the object in thought is thus to show that you know it or are on the path towards knowing it more completely and, to that extent, the object is internally given to knowledge, not some something or other = X set over and against knowledge.

This is not to say, however, that the universe as such is nothing more than an ideal construction and that we are deluded in thinking it exists outside the relevant theory. The point in our passage from the A-Deduction is just that, as an object of knowledge, it is generated the same way our knowledge of it is, namely from the evidence: Kant is thus an idealist about objects—as objects of knowledge. But nothing we have seen him say so far, nor anything I have said on his behalf, commits him to what I will call weird idealism or scepticism about the external world. We will naturally wonder how idealism about objects of knowledge in our passage from the A-Deduction might be reconciled with unruffled confidence that the external world exists.[^72] Perhaps the answer is just that Kant regards the external world, insofar as it is not yet an object of knowledge, as the object of our curiosity. The difference between an object of curiosity and an object of knowledge aligns with the difference between our recognition that the former has not yet been understood and our claim that the latter has. We strive to convert the one into the other, which suggests that we may regard our ultimate object of curiosity—the whole physical universe outside us—as a kind of regulative ideal of reason. This thought is admittedly undeveloped, but not obviously incoherent. I will return to the nature of Kant's idealism in the passages from the A-Deduction I have been discussing (and their parallels in the B-Deduction) and compare it with that in the Duisburg Nachlaß in §3d of this chapter.

We may now wonder, of course, how the success of constructions in physics could be judged, assuming that the story about classical geometry, suggested in our passages from the A-Deduction, applies to physics at all. Kant will say, from the standpoint of the Critique, that the construction of the physical universe he attempted in 1755 was a failure, because it is vulnerable to antinomies: it makes incoherent assumptions about whether the universe is infinite in space and time. But what about physical constructions that are not vulnerable to antinomies? The seemingly natural thing to say, especially if we deny that Kant is committed to weird idealism, is that we must somehow compare the construction itself with the part of the world it is supposed to model. But in that case, it might seem very hard not to think of the relevant part of the real world outside our minds as something 'set over and against' our purported knowledge of it. If that is what we must say, it would then seem that the ideas I have been attributing to Kant do break down when applied to branches of knowledge other than geometry—and notably to astronomical physics. For we will very naturally permit ourselves to speak about objects of knowledge in the way I claimed on Kant's behalf to be aporetic. But I seriously doubt that this conclusion is forced upon us. 'Comparing' the physical universe, or some part of it, to a Kantian construction is not a simple matter of setting an item of knowledge against something lying beyond or outside knowledge, in the way that we might set a colour tile against towels and shower curtains to see if they match. For the comparison of interest to us will involve first collecting the relevant observations. This in turn will surely presuppose a host of theoretical commitments—to take one simple example: theoretical commitments about how to measure time and distance. Thus it will likely turn out that collecting observations is itself best understood not as a matching of knowledge to thing 'outside and beyond' knowledge, but as an effort that brings together various items of knowledge, i.e., theoretical commitments, to form some kind of unity. How that would work for astronomical physics on my reading of Kant would have to be spelled out of course. I cannot begin to settle the matter here.[^73]

But if I have correctly formulated Kant's position in the passage from the A-Deduction we have been examining, then Kant's position, as I have formulated it, does not break down here after all. The position we have seen Kant taking in A104 is one that could be developed in different ways. For example, one way would be to turn it on its head. Kant has been telling us that the object is not something that we can characterize meaningfully without an appeal to knowledge: there can be no general account of the object of knowledge without recalling some of the things we think we already know about it. The flip side of this idea is to doubt whether we can meaningfully characterize knowledge in general without some kind of reference to the different things that we take to be objects of our knowledge. Given the intimate relation Kant ultimately claims for knowledge and its object, he could just as easily have staked out his position in these terms.[^74] But then there would have been at least one precedent for his view—turned now on its head in this way—in the history of philosophy. If Cornford is right, the view is defended by Plato in the Theaetetus. That dialogue is deliberately aporetic, on Cornford's reading, precisely because Plato is trying to show us how futile it is to articulate a general theory of knowledge without saying anything about the objects of our knowledge, which—for Plato—would be the forms.[^75] But there could be other ways to develop the idea. For example, one might develop it as a doubt about the viability of the post-Gettier project of developing a general theory of knowledge detached from an account of the special objects of our knowledge, e.g., mathematical structures and physical systems. In other words, one might wonder how viable this project is if it is detached from the special sciences and philosophical reflection on them. The interest of recalling these alternative developments of the idea at issue is that it can help spotlight what is distinctive about Kant's story.

There are at least two more things that are distinctive about Kant's story. The first is that it is better and more completely told if it takes 'self-activity' into account. As we saw in §2b of this chapter, self-activity is what distinguishes all acts of the understanding. The understanding is a faculty for combining a priori (B130, B135). But I cannot represent anything as combined in the object that I did not combine in it myself. That I undertook the combining myself is what makes my representation a product of my self-activity. It is what makes my combination a 'synthesis' in Kant's idiosyncratic use of the term. It now seems clear, from what we have learned from our passage in the A-Deduction, that the object of knowledge as such is a synthesis in the relevant sense. Consider again the example of the equilateral triangle. What makes it an object of knowledge on the account we have been considering? The answer must be that it is a combination: at least to the extent that the equilateral triangle is something we put together from certain things given to us (a line segment together with the whole of Euclidean space) following the relevant constructive procedures. But to that banal observation, we might just as well add that, unless I really do construct it, the equilateral triangle might as well be nothing for me. I cannot really expect others to do the constructing for me. Even if I am just reading through Euclid's proof of the first proposition in Book One of the Elements, I am not at all passive: if I am really following along, I am going through the steps of the construction in thought. I understand the proof, if at all, because I can now see for myself how to carry out the construction on my own. No one will credit me with the discovery of this proof, but, by the same token, no one should really credit Euclid for my having learned it from him. He may have given me assistance, but he could not do my learning for me. Nor can my senses be credited with doing my learning for me either: they convey to me a manifold, but do not carry out the combination needed to construe or interpret it in the relevant way. Then last of all, it would be ridiculous to assign any such credit to the object itself, on the pre-elenchic, ordinary way of thinking about what it is. On that view, it is supposed to get credit for preventing me from learning false things about it and for compelling me to say the few true things about it that I have picked up along the way. But how is this possible? By the object's just being there and coyly inviting my intellectual gaze upon it? This just isn't a serious answer. Euclid had to do some hard work just to get credit for assisting me in my learning. Even my senses had to do something to convey an unsynthesized manifold to me. But the object, on the aporetic view, does nothing at all and can scarcely deserve credit for anything except perhaps for avoiding annihilation—and even that accomplishment should perhaps be more rightfully credited to its creator. If, then, I do not undertake the relevant construction myself and on my own account, I will never receive an equilateral triangle as an object of knowledge, i.e., I will never know anything about triangles of this sort. These considerations strongly suggest that the object of knowledge as such—at least in the case of the equilateral triangle—is indeed a product of our intellectual 'self-activity'. This will hold not only for the objects of geometry, but also for those of the other sciences as well, if it is true, as I suggested above, that they too make themselves known to us through some process analogous to geometrical construction.

Now self-activity does not receive explicit mention in Kant's account of the object of knowledge in the passage beginning at A104. That is not especially surprising, since Kant says nothing about self-activity as such in the A-Deduction. This is a theme that seems to get its first explicit treatment in the B-Deduction, like the associated theme of the Copernican turn in metaphysics. Nevertheless, nothing obviously stands in the way of our supplying self-activity to the analysis Kant develops at A104ff. Moreover, the analysis itself seems to require it or—anyway—invite it. That Kant himself explicitly introduces the notion in §15 of the B-Deduction indicates his recognition of its importance.[^76] So, on balance, I think it can and should be called on to convey some of what is distinctive in Kant's account of objects and objects of knowledge, even in the relevant part of the A-Deduction.

But a second feature of this account is also quite distinctive, namely the role played by unity.[^77] So far unity has come into play at two different levels, at least in the examples I gave above. By carrying out the construction of the equilateral triangle, I have produced a certain geometrical figure that exhibits unity just by being a determinate geometrical figure: a representative equilateral triangle that we can recognize as distinguishable from any isosceles triangle among other things. This means that a certain kind of unity—namely synthetic unity carried out on the manifold of an intuition—is constitutive of the object as an object of knowledge. But, in the second place, the unity constitutive of the object as an object of knowledge is at the same time constitutive of the special unity exhibited by my understanding of the object, as expressed in my concepts of it and the judgements I make about it using these concepts. When I construct an equilateral triangle according to the relevant rule, I bring together three lines equal in length in such a way that they enclose a certain area and such that the interior angles of the figure are equal. That is the unity on display in the construction as such. But that very unity is representative of a whole class of geometrical figures that can all be constructed in the same way. Thus the unity on display in my construction is also expressed by the corresponding concept. For the concept conveys my understanding that equilateral triangles in general are Euclidean figures that enclose an area on three sides equal in length and such that the interior angles are equal. This is just as it should be. Given that the object as an object of knowledge is not something other than or different from knowledge, but somehow its internal byproduct, then unity of the object must go hand in hand with the unity of the concept or concepts by which the object is grasped by my understanding. They should, in fact, be the same unity, assuming—as Kant does, of course—that concepts themselves make an essential contribution to our knowledge.

The significance of unity emerges in the continuation of the passage we have been reviewing from the A-Deduction. Here Kant offers in a sketchy way his own example of an object of knowledge. It is interesting to note that the example is a geometrical one: that of the construction of a triangle. Thus we read at A105:

> So we say then [sc. in conclusion and as the upshot of the analysis so far] that we know the object if we have effected synthetic unity in the manifold of the intuition. But this is impossible unless it has been possible to bring forth [hervorbringen] the intuition according to a rule through the sort of function of synthesis that makes possible a concept . . . in which the manifold has been united. Thus we imagine a triangle as an object by being conscious of putting together three lines according to a rule, i.e., a rule according to which such an intuition can always be set forth [dargestellt]. Now this unity of the rule determines every manifold and restricts it to conditions that make the unity of apperception possible; and, the concept of this unity is the representation of the object = X that I think through the indicated predicate of a triangle.

Though the passage is murky, the essential elements seem to be in play. The triangle is an object of knowledge for us to the extent that we can construct it from a manifold given in intuition according to the relevant rule.[^78] By carrying out the construction, we will have effected synthetic unity in the relevant manifold. This unity is the condition of possibility of the triangle, but at the same time, it seems to be the condition of possibility of the concept of triangle or—what comes to the same—my use of the predicate Triangle in a judgement. Once the construction has been carried out and I have both the triangle as an object of knowledge and the concept of triangle, the place left open for the object in our discussion by the capital 'X' has been filled in by something determinate: a specific object of knowledge grasped by a determinate concept.

### §3c The continuation of the parallel passage in the A-Deduction and an assessment of its relevance for the central argument of §17 of the B-Deduction

The first consideration Kant raises immediately after having introduced his example of the triangle is the logical form of concepts. This is an essential consideration, precisely because the issue all along has been knowledge. At the beginning of the passage now under consideration Kant says explicitly that 'all knowledge requires a concept, however incomplete and obscure it may be' (A106). Then he says this: 'according to its logical form, a concept is always something universal that serves as a rule' (A106). From the perspective of the B-Deduction, we recognize this claim as equivalent to the claim that concepts have 'analytic unity', i.e., each concept remains one and the same thought applicable to all the relevant cases, instances or individuals. From the perspective of the A-Deduction, that is what makes every concept a 'universal representation that serves as a rule for thinking appearances' (A106). But now Kant places the emphasis in our current passage at A106 on 'necessity'. That will seem strange, because we would treat universality as a quantifier and necessity as a modal operator. Quantifying over the whole class of something, as when we think discursively and use a class concept like Cat, does not all by itself license an inference that what we have said or thought about cats is a necessary truth. Our puzzlement may ease when we recognize that Kant has in mind necessity of a special sort. It relates to a consideration he raised at the very beginning of his discussion in the A-Deduction of the so-called 'synthesis of recognition in concepts': namely the condition without which 'reproduction in the sequence of representations' would not be possible.

The claim before this discussion got underway had been that, unless I can keep present in my mind the images or representations that are the focus of my attention, they will be as good as nothing for me. Keeping them present in my mind involves reproducing them in my imagination. Now the claim is that reproduction would itself be for naught 'without consciousness that that which we are [now] thinking is precisely the same as that which we were thinking a moment ago' (A103). It turns out this consciousness is just a concept, i.e., a discursive representation whose universality or logical form allows us to recognize the reproduced item for consideration as the same as the item we were considering before. To the extent that the universality of a concept is necessary for reproduction in imagination, it is that without which my mental and intellectual life would be impossible. The necessity at issue here is that of a necessary condition. At A106, in the continuation of the passage we have been discussing, Kant is about to push the argument back as far as it will go and ask after the ground of this necessity. He is about to ask after the necessary condition of the logical form or analytic unity of concepts. The initial push backward takes us to that which, from the perspective of §16 of the B-Deduction, we recognize as the 'analytic unity of apperception', i.e., its numerical identity or the fact of its being one and the same apperception no matter which representation it accompanies (A106–107). But Kant does not leave it at that in this stretch of the A-Deduction. He proceeds to argue that the analytic unity of apperception ultimately rests on the synthetic unity of apperception. Thus he says that 'this unity of consciousness [sc. the numerical identity or "analytic unity" of apperception] would be impossible if the mind could not become conscious in the knowledge of the manifold of the identity of the function through which it [sc. apperception] synthetically combines the manifold into an item of knowledge' (A108). However obscure the mode of expression in this passage, the implication is clear. We recognize it as the conclusion of §16 of the B-Deduction. Since the analytic unity of concepts presupposes the analytic unity of pure apperception, and since the analytic unity of pure apperception presupposes the synthetic unity of apperception, it follows that the synthetic unity of apperception is the ground underlying the analytic unity of concepts. In short, it is the ground underlying all concepts, all thought, and hence all use of the understanding. It is, as Kant would later say in §16 of the B-Deduction, 'the highest point to which we must attach all use of the understanding, even the whole of logic and after that transcendental philosophy' (B134 n.).

In context, the result Kant just reached at A108 has a special significance. For, as we have seen, it is appended to a series of reflections that are supposed to help us understand what an object of knowledge as such is. This strongly suggests that the result just reached can, and is intended to, explain how such objects are possible or how there can be a relation of our given representations to objects (understood in the relevant way) such that we can be said to have knowledge. The thing that makes all this possible is the very same synthetic unity of pure apperception that underlies all our use of concepts. This is, in fact, what we should expect, when all is said and done. For we saw earlier that an object of knowledge is not something other than knowledge, but rather an internal byproduct of knowledge as such. Hence an object so understood and the relation of our given representations to it ultimately presuppose the same unity that makes possible the relevant concept. For, as Kant says at A106, 'all knowledge requires a concept.' But the unity that makes possible the relevant concept is none other than the synthetic unity of pure apperception. That is precisely the point that Kant goes on to make as explicitly as one would like in the concluding paragraph of this section of the A-Deduction:

> The pure concept of this transcendental object (that is really always one and the same = X) is that which can provide all our empirical concepts as such relation to an object, i.e., objective reality. Now this concept can contain no determinate intuition and will thus concern nothing other than that unity that must be found in a manifold of knowledge insofar as it stands in relation to an object. But this relation is nothing other than the necessary unity of consciousness [sc. the numerical identity of pure apperception] and hence also the necessary unity of the synthesis of the manifold through a common function of the mind for combining it in one representation. (A109)

It might be thought that this passage undermines our retelling, on Kant's behalf, of the story about objects of knowledge as such. For it looks as if Kant is suddenly prepared to accept talk here of a something = X after all. But we must not be led astray by the capital letter. Kant is not conceding that an object of knowledge as such is something other than, or different from, knowledge, in the way that he represents us at the beginning of the passage as finding intuitively obvious. Nor is he even saying that there is something all objects of knowledge have in common and that could itself be the object—let's call it 'transcendental' for good measure—of a general epistemological theory. Rather he is using the capital X as a place-holder until we get some knowledge and some determinate objects of knowledge get put on the table. Until that happens, our capital X will keep a place warm for these things and call attention to the condition that will ultimately make them possible. We must not confuse that condition with any specific object or item of knowledge as such. We should certainly not confuse it with the aporetic conception of the object that Kant deflated earlier in the passage. It is very likely a mistake, moreover, to imagine that this condition ever obtains without our having some knowledge or without our given representations relating to something in the way constitutive of knowledge. Nevertheless, we can, by philosophical reflection, clarify in our minds what that condition is without which no knowledge nor any object of knowledge as such is possible. When we do that properly, Kant says we will arrive at the synthetic unity of pure apperception.

That finally brings us back to §17 of the B-Deduction. The key argument of this section is, I think, identical to the argument that we have been reviewing in the part of the A-Deduction called the 'Synthesis of Recognition of Concepts'. The only thing missing from §17 of the B-Deduction is the explicit elenchic-style deflation of what I have been calling the 'aporetic conception of the object'. But that Kant still rejects this conception in the B-Deduction, and along the same lines as we have found in the A-Deduction, is clear from the third paragraph of §17 where he gives a gloss on the key argument of this section.

The thing Kant wishes to stress in the third paragraph of §17 is that the principle of the original synthetic unity of apperception is the 'first pure knowledge of the understanding upon which rests all further use of the understanding' and that, as such, it is 'completely independent of all conditions of sensible intuition' (B137). This leads him to say that space, the 'mere' form of outer intuition, does not itself count as knowledge. It just gives us the manifold of intuition a priori without which knowledge would be impossible. Hence, we need something in addition to the pure form of outer intuition even to have knowledge of objects in space. What we need is the original synthetic unity of apperception. The passage of interest now reads as follows:

> But in order to know something in space, e.g., a line, I must produce it [ziehen] and thus bring about synthetically a determinate combination of the given manifold so that the unity of this operation [Handlung] is at the same time the unity of consciousness (in the concept of a line), and only then and in that way is an object known. Thus the synthetic unity of consciousness is an objective condition of all knowledge, not merely the condition needed for me to know an object, but rather that condition under which every intuition must stand in order to become an object for me. For in any other way and without this synthesis, the manifold would not be united in one consciousness. (B137–138)

The interest of this passage is, first of all, the way Kant conceives of whatever we take to be our object of knowledge: in this case, a certain geometrical figure extended in space, namely a straight line. This object is something outside of, and different from, knowledge no more than the triangle in the example we found at A105. It is the result of a construction: it must be produced or extended from one point to another. Unless I carry out this construction, in thought and/or imagination, I will not have a line segment, nor will I get around to thinking or knowing any such thing. This is just to say that the line, as an object of knowledge, and the knowledge I have of it, are both the result of a synthesis of a given manifold that I must carry out for myself. This synthesis expresses a certain unity: in the first instance, 'the unity of consciousness (in the concept of a line)'. I take this to mean that the unity in question is the analytic unity of the relevant concept and hence a special determination of the analytic unity of apperception. But we know, and Kant himself reminds us at the end of this passage, that analytic unity always presupposes synthetic unity. Thus the implication of rejecting the aporetic conception of the object is to recognize that 'the synthetic unity of consciousness is an objective condition of all knowledge.'

This goes to show that the key argument of §17 in the B-Deduction and the argument we reviewed from the A-Deduction in the section on the 'Synthesis of Recognition in Concepts' are on all the essential points the same. Both arguments appeal to the equivalent of the result of §16 of the B-Deduction. Both arguments reject the 'aporetic conception of the object'. Both ultimately appeal to the original synthetic unity of pure apperception as the principle that underlies the possibility of knowledge and the object of knowledge as such. Still it must not be overlooked that there are two differences between the two versions of the argument.

The first difference is that §17 explicitly calls attention to the significance of 'self-activity' for the argument. This is clear from the passage at B137–138 that I just quoted. For Kant says there that the synthetic unity of consciousness is 'not merely the condition needed for me to know an object, but rather that condition under which every intuition must stand in order to become an object for me'. An intuition will become an 'object for me' to the extent that I have effected the relevant synthesis as an act of 'self-activity'. That synthesis may be nothing more elaborate than the understanding of the construction of an equilateral triangle I get by working through the steps of Euclid's proof of Proposition One in Book One of the Elements. But, as we saw earlier, if I do not actually carry out this synthesis for myself, nothing and no one—not even Euclid—will do it for me. Still, if I really do carry it out, the relevant intuition will become 'an object for me', i.e., something I really do understand and therefore an object of knowledge. Nothing, in fact, can become an object of knowledge unless it becomes an object for me. The 'Fettdruck' or bold print in this passage clearly places the emphasis on 'self-activity'. Though it may well be the case that Kant presupposes self-activity in the A-Deduction and consciously took himself to be doing so, the text as such does not make this explicit. I think that that is one of the weaknesses of the A-Deduction.

The second difference between the two versions of the argument is that the passage from B137–138 of the B-Deduction goes out of its way to point out that space as such is 'not yet any knowledge at all'. It is 'merely' (bloß) the pure form of outer intuition. It yields a manifold a priori for knowledge, but not the synthesis of the manifold that is constitutive of knowledge as such. We noted at the outset that §17 deliberately calls attention to a parallel between the 'highest principle of the possibility of all intuition with respect to sensibility' and the highest principle of the understanding. It is supposed to turn out, by the end of §17, that the principle of the original synthetic unity of pure apperception is the formal principle of the understanding just as space and time are the formal principles of sensibility. We saw what that might mean and entail. But with every parallel, there comes a qualification. The qualification is given in the passage from the third paragraph of §17 we were just considering. The principle of the original synthetic unity of pure apperception is the 'first pure knowledge of the understanding upon which rest all other uses of the understanding', whereas space and so presumably time do not count as knowledge even of things in space and time: at least insofar as they are nothing more than the pure forms of inner and outer intuition respectively. This is not to deny that space and time contribute something to our knowledge—something essential, namely a manifold a priori. But the clear implication of this passage is that this contribution will not come to fruition unless this manifold too is subject to the highest principle of the understanding, i.e., it too—like the manifold of any empirical intuition—must be subject to the original synthetic unity of pure apperception. Until that happens, we will not have any knowledge. This important idea about space and time never comes up in the A-Deduction.

Once one stops to notice this,[^79] it seems reasonably clear that Kant's explicit qualification about space in §17 is pointing ahead to §26 of the B-Deduction and the distinction between space as a form of intuition, which is not itself knowledge of anything, and space as an item of knowledge in its own right insofar as its manifold has been synthesized by the imagination and ultimately subjected to the synthetic unity of pure apperception (B161–162).[^80] But, of course, this important distinction is not made explicit in the A-Deduction either. So the second difference between the argument of §17 of the B-Deduction and that of our extended passage from the A-Deduction is part of a larger difference between the two versions of the deduction. I am inclined to think that the distinction between space as form of intuition and space as an intuition whose manifold has been synthesized is presupposed in the A-Deduction. But like the significance of 'self-activity', it is not made explicit. In my view, this is another significant weakness of the earlier presentation of the argument.

These two differences between the key argument of §17 in the B-Deduction and the argument we reviewed from the A-Deduction are important, but not so important as to diminish the significant agreement between the two arguments. This agreement should put to rest the worry we had about the key argument of §17, namely that, at the crucial moment, it is purely verbal. That moment came when Kant tried to use the result of §16 to state the condition under which alone concepts relate our given representations to objects and thereby yield knowledge. The problem then was to understand what Kant could mean by an object of knowledge in this context. The solution is provided by the argument we reviewed from the A-Deduction. Read in the light of this argument, the key argument of §17 in the B-Deduction turns on the deflation of a highly problematic conception of objects as objects of knowledge and a philosophically interesting alternative. The problem with this argument is not that it is merely verbal. Rather, it is much too concise and elliptical to communicate clearly what makes it work. It is fortunate for us that the relevant stretch of text from the A-Deduction remains on the record. For without it, it would be that much more difficult to make sense of §17 in the B-Deduction.

### §3d How the central idea about objects in §17 of the B-Deduction contrasts with the Duisburg Nachlaß

We may appreciate the significance of §17 of the B-Deduction by contrasting it with the Duisburg Nachlaß. The relevant contrast will come into focus if we begin by noting two points of contact. The first is as obvious as it is striking: both texts turn on a conception of understanding—not in Kant's technical sense, but just in the sense of 'getting' or grasping something—informed by the role of construction in classical geometry. We saw how this is supposed to work for §17 of the B-Deduction in §3b and §3c of this chapter. We saw how it was supposed to work for the Duisburg Nachlaß in our discussion of ekthesis in §1b of Chapter One. There we also noted that the geometrical example in §17 of the B-Deduction had already appeared in a passage from L1, a course of lectures on metaphysics from the late 1770s, related to the Duisburg Nachlaß by its preoccupation with the 'exposition of appearances'. The example is stated in both passages as the claim that I cannot represent, conceive, think, or imagine a line unless I draw one in space. 'Getting' or grasping what a line is depends on constructing one.

The second point of contact can be found in the portentous claim of R4674 in the Duisburg Nachlaß that we discussed in §1c of Chapter One: 'I am the original of all objects' (17.646.5–14). This looks like the insight we have been discussing from §17 of the B-Deduction, because it asserts a certain dependence of the object on me insofar as I think. It comes bundled with the related thought that having an object of thought or knowledge, and hence standing in the relevant epistemic relation to this object, is not something we achieve by direct intuitive insight. The Duisburg Nachlaß allows for the mind to have an intellectual intuition of itself: this is something we saw in §1d of Chapter One. But it explicitly rules out intellectual intuition of any other object. Hence, already in the Duisburg Nachlaß, the object is not something that we can think of as just sitting there, inviting the mind's gaze, as it were. It is rather a kind of constructive problem to be solved. The solution consists in carrying out an exposition of appearances. But that means supplying appearances with unity. For, again, the Duisburg Nachlaß says that we can conceptually represent an object only 'according to its relations', i.e., as having a place and time in the unified spatio-temporal whole Kant calls 'experience'.

Against these two points of contact, the relevant point of contrast now comes into view. For if we adopt the Duisburg line, it will become natural to think of the relation between knowledge and its object in the terms that Kant was to single out as aporetic in the passage at A104ff in the A-Deduction and in §17 of the B-Deduction. An observation I made at the end of §1c of Chapter One makes this claim plausible. I pointed out that Kant's strategy in the Duisburg Nachlaß is to derive the concepts of Substance, Ground, and Whole from the mind's reflection on itself and then to use these concepts to formulate universal laws that are supposed to apply to appearances by exposition. But why should we expect this strategy to work? The three concepts at issue express the mind's engagement with itself. We might well find a way to formulate them as universal laws. But one might worry that the effect of applying these laws to appearances will result in nothing more than a projection on to appearances of the mind's self-understanding. If it is true that such a projection is at the basis of empirical thought, then perhaps empirical thought is a mirage—at least insofar as it purports to be thought about things other than the mind. The worry may be restated in terms of unity. For the effect of applying the relevant universal laws to appearances is to confer on experience a certain unity that comes only from the mind. It is as if I export the unity I encounter in myself by introspection into my thought of the object. But, then, why should I expect the world to exhibit the kind of unity that I discover in myself by introspection? The only way to address such a question is to test the object somehow. But the only means I have for conducting any kind of test will involve the mind's projection of its unity on to appearances. For that, according to the Duisburg Nachlaß, is the necessary condition of all empirical thought of the object: if I deny myself the means of thinking the object, I deny myself the means of testing it. The trouble is that I cannot enlist the mind's projection of its unity on to appearances in the answering of the question at hand. For it is precisely in the nature of this question to make an issue of the mind's unity: we are asking whether the mind really finds its reflection in the Object Itself, lying behind the solution we come up with for the object as an object of thought. Thus the Duisburg line naturally invites us to brood on the aporetic conception of the Object as something lying outside and beyond thought and knowledge—a something that, in the present case, might as well be nothing for me as a thinker and knower.

For present purposes, the interest of §17 of the B-Deduction and the parallel section of the A-Deduction is that these sections seem to preserve much of the Duisburg Nachlaß gambit, except for the two problematic ideas I have signalled: first of all, the idea that the unity of the mind is metaphysically given and, second of all, the idea that thought of an object depends on projecting its unity on to appearances.[^81] Excluding these two ideas puts a brake on the worry that the mind constructs an object and a world for the object to inhabit that is so much in its own image as to be pure fantasy. Since the unity of the mind is as much of a task as the unity of the object, we cannot prescribe in advance how the latter is to be articulated. To be sure, it formally constrains the unity of the object, since we cannot carry out the one task without also at the same time carrying out the other.[^82] But there is no reason to think that the outcome of the task associated with the object has been, as it were, coloured or contaminated by any prior image, conception, or direct grasp we might have of the mind's inner constitution. For none is available: the mind's unity is not given to us in intellectual intuition as a metaphysically brute fact that we can use to flavour our apprehension of the world. This does not mean that we will never wonder what the world is like independent of our take on it. No amount of philosophizing by Kant or anybody else can prevent us from doing that: that is what makes it an object of curiosity. But the way to deal with this concern is always to test what we take to be the object of our knowledge. The merit of the insight driving §17 of the B-Deduction is that, unlike the story of the Duisburg Nachlaß, it does nothing to make all such testing suspect from the start. We will never find a way to test the object unless it is possible for us to entertain thought about it. But though we still say, as Kant had in the Duisburg Nachlaß, that the possibility of such thought rests on the unity of myself as the thinker of my thoughts, we no longer cash this out in such a way as to infect the content of all our thought of the object with thoughts that are really about us as thinking minds. By making the unity of the mind a task that we carry out in the act of thinking and constructing the object, Kant has found a way to cut off the infection at its source.

Finally, I would like to observe that §17 of the B-Deduction is incomplete all by itself. It turns on the idea that understanding or 'getting' something requires ekthesis. But it leaves out the considerations about universal laws that we learned in §1c of Chapter One are essential for putting this idea to work. As we will discover by the end of §3 in Chapter Four, §17 of the B-Deduction is supplemented by §26. §26 of the B-Deduction completes the argument in §17 by tracing the origin of the relevant universal laws back to the understanding and its categories.

It may also be understood as a question about the relationship between mathematical astronomy and observational astronomy. I myself do not yet know how to address these questions in the way they deserve. But I can indicate more precisely the sort of the thing I mean. Consider, for example, Kepler's Laws and their significance for Newton's theory of gravitation (Newton refers to them as 'phenomena' at the beginning of Book Three of the Principia). By the same token, consider Eratosthanes' estimation of the distance between the earth and the sun—also significant for Newton's theory of gravitation, because it is presupposed by his thought experiment, in Proposition Four of Book Three in the Principia, of the moon being brought down to earth. Another important question would be how he thinks the understanding or perhaps reason recognizes which 'phenomena' are relevant—either for mounting a construction, or for confirming or refuting one.
