## 1 The Duisburg Nachlaß

Kant's correspondence includes a short letter from a well-wisher named Bertram. The content of the letter is as harmless as it is uninteresting: Bertram invites Kant to visit his brother's estate. 'Do come', he says, 'because the weather is so beautiful and such travel so beneficial' (10.182). The interest of the letter is entirely exhausted by the date: 20 May 1775. For Kant used the letter to write up ideas, some suggestive of themes later to emerge in the *Critique of Pure Reason*. Similarity of handwriting and train of thought suggests that these notes are part of a whole bundle of papers collectively known as the Duisburg Nachlaß (R4674–4684 in volume 17 of the Academy Edition). It was supposedly Kant's practice to use letters as scrap paper shortly after receiving them.[^1] If so, we can date the Bertram sketches—and probably the bundle as a whole—to sometime in 1775. That would make the Duisburg Nachlaß one of the few pieces of philosophical writing in Kant's own hand to come down to us from the 1770s, and the only extended piece in his hand from the middle of the decade. It offers a rare glimpse of Kant at work on immediate ancestors of central ideas in the first Critique and might therefore be expected to shed light on an important chapter in his philosophical development.

They involve adaptations of metaphysical commitments he held as far back as the mid-1750s.

Two related sets of early Kantian metaphysical commitments will come in to play. One set is concerned with external relations: first, all such relations depend on substance; second, the idea of substance is the ground of all our thought and knowledge of such relations. Kant appeals to this pair of metaphysical commitments in the Duisburg Nachlaß to elaborate and defend his answer to the question how appearances can be made objective or converted into experience. He does so, because he takes experience to be a network of external relations. He is asking, in effect, for the ground underlying this network; he takes this ground to be a certain substance: the I that thinks and the intellectual intuition it has of itself. His problem will be to adapt this first set of early metaphysical commitments to accommodate his account of sensibility in the inaugural dissertation of 1770. His treatment of the problem will be my chief concern in the first section of this chapter.

The second section of this chapter will consider the second set of early Kantian metaphysical commitments taken up in the Duisburg Nachlaß. The second set develops the commitments about external relations of the first set as a cosmology. Kant does not merely conceive of experience as a network of external relations; he conceives of this network as having the scope and structure of a world—one whose 'matter' is the totality of all possible appearances, like the sensible world in the inaugural dissertation, and whose 'form' is a special, overarching unity such that every possible appearance can externally relate to every other appearance in one and the same universal experience. The principle of the matter of the sensible world in the Duisburg Nachlaß is space and time taken to be pure intuitions. The principle of the form of the sensible world is that same thinking substance (and the intellectual intuition it has of itself) to be considered in the first section of this chapter. In the second section of this chapter, we will find that Kant conceives of this thinking substance in cosmological terms, i.e., as relating to the sensible world in ways analogous to those in which he conceived of God as relating to a world as such in the *Nova dilucidatio* of 1755 and to the intelligible world in the inaugural dissertation of 1770.

Let me briefly relate my work to previous studies of the Duisburg Nachlaß. Little has been written about it. It was the object of a critical edition and commentary by Theodor Häring in 1910. Häring argued that Kant already holds in the Duisburg Nachlaß the position of the first Critique: 'The Dissertation, insofar as it is opposed to the KrV, is completely superseded [sc. in the Duisburg Nachlaß—AL]; in principle, all of the fundamental thoughts of the Critique are present.'[^2] In his three-volume work on the transcendental deduction of 1934, Herman de Vleeschauwer accepted Häring's conclusion and the details of his analysis.[^3] I will be arguing in this chapter, by contrast, that Kant makes liberal and imaginative use in the Duisburg Nachlaß of ideas from the inaugural dissertation and much earlier. Häring and de Vleeschauwer overlooked these important points of contact with Kant's earlier writings because they were bent on establishing continuity with the *Critique of Pure Reason*.

The most significant study of the Duisburg Nachlaß is Wolfgang Carl's *Der schweigende Kant* of 1989.[^4] I owe a great deal to this book—beginning with its optimism that sense can be made of the Duisburg Nachlaß and that it can shed light on Kant's development in the mid-1770s. My specific debts to Carl in this chapter I acknowledge along the way. My work distinguishes itself from his, however, by systematically taking account of Kant's earlier metaphysical commitments. Thus I read the Duisburg Nachlaß in a backward-looking way, while he reads it in a forward-looking way. My conclusion will be that Kant was reinterpreting his own early cosmology in the mid-1770s and that an important feature of this reinterpretation was to give it an epistemological twist in part by replacing God with the thinking subject. That point will come into focus in §2d of this chapter.

Another full-length treatment of the Duisburg Nachlaß from the late 1980s is that of Paul Guyer in *Kant and the Claims of Knowledge*.[^5] This study is distinctive in the weight it puts on objective time determination and the significance of this issue for Kant's later development. But Guyer does not take into account Kant's early metaphysics—as I propose to here.[^6] I disagree with him on many points. The most significant one is this. As I will explain in §1d of this chapter, I take Kant in the Duisburg Nachlaß to accept some kind of idealism. Guyer's claim, as I understand it, is that Kant tries hard in the Duisburg Nachlaß to resist idealism and does not finally embrace it until the Critique (though not without some continued hedging in the 'Refutation of Idealism' [B274–279]): I will argue in §3d of the next chapter that Kant's position in the Critique is a new and improved form of idealism that tries to overcome a problematic conception of the object of our knowledge peculiar to his idealism in the mid-1770s.

In a much more recent study, Henry Allison reads the Duisburg Nachlaß as the fruit of Kant's engagement starting in the mid-1760s with what he calls 'meta-metaphysical' questions: those that concern the proper method of metaphysics.[^7] This engagement is supposed to have led first to the distinction in the inaugural dissertation between the principles of sensibility and understanding and then to an effort in the Duisburg Nachlaß to determine how sensibility and understanding cooperate and jointly yield knowledge of objects. I do not dispute the general contours of Allison's reading: no doubt, Kant is interested in the relation between sensibility and understanding in the Duisburg Nachlaß. My view, though, is that the interest of the Duisburg Nachlaß lies in the details of its account of this relation and that these details depend—at least in part—on Kant's continued engagement with first-order metaphysical commitments that he tried to formulate and defend in the mid-1750s. Leaving this engagement out of the account is a missed opportunity.

## §1 The First Set of Metaphysical Commitments: External Relations As Such

### §1a What is an 'exposition of appearances'?

Throughout the first section of Chapter One, I will take as a point of reference the following passage from R4674 of the Duisburg Nachlaß, because it announces central, recurring themes (cf. R4674–17.644.27–645.5):

> X is the object. This can be given a priori through construction, but through exposition (which is something quite other than observation, which has nothing connected a priori with A) we can recognize in the subject conditions a priori under which A in general relates to an object, namely to something real. This object can be represented only according to its relations and is nothing other than the subjective representation (of the subject) itself, but made universal, for I am the original of all objects. (17.646.5–14)

Kant makes a couple of peculiar claims in this passage that will be the focus, in turn, of the next two subsections of this chapter. Here I would like to make and develop a simple observation in part by drawing on related passages elsewhere in the Duisburg Nachlaß. The development will not be as simple as the observation itself, but it will help set the stage for the next two subsections. It will also highlight something the Duisburg Nachlaß has in common with the *Critique of Pure Reason*. The difference between them will come into focus as we move forward.

The simple observation is this. In the passage from R4674 I just quoted, Kant distinguishes three ways a sensible concept (designated by the letter A) can relate to an object (designated by the letter X): construction, observation, and exposition. This three-way distinction is built on a more fundamental two-way distinction: a sensible concept can relate to an object either a priori or a posteriori. Observation is a case of the latter, as when I apply the concept Red to something I see in the Berlin Tiergarten and observe: that (squirrel) is red! By contrast with observation, construction and exposition are both ways a sensible concept can relate to an object a priori.

Construction is the more familiar of the two. Kant does not say much about it in the Duisburg Nachlaß, but the little he does say is consistent with his remarks in the Critique and other later writings. First, it is a technique of geometry. We do not find the word 'geometry' in the Duisburg Nachlaß; we find 'mathematics' instead (R4674–17.645.30). But the one example—only mentioned in passing, never developed in full—is properly geometrical: a triangle in space is the sort of thing that can be constructed, and the size of its angles can thereby be determined (R4674–17.644.5–7), as when we construct an equilateral triangle following the procedure indicated by the proof of the first proposition in Book One of Euclid's *Elements* (R4678–17.662.16–17).

Construction is a 'way of knowing how [a concept—AL] is produced or specified [erzeugt oder specificirt], or what flows out of its specification or what is connected therewith' (R4674–17.644.3–5). We cannot carry out Euclid's procedure for constructing an equilateral triangle without grasping the relevant concept, i.e. without understanding in general terms that an equilateral triangle is a three-sided figure whose three sides are equal. If we do indeed carry out the procedure, we will have specified the concept by supplying a representative example: the characteristic things that are true for this one are true for all possible ones. Of course, the construction or the specification of our concept takes place a priori. Kant says so explicitly in a couple of places in the Duisburg Nachlaß. For example, he says in R4675: 'To determine a priori is to construct' (17.653.6–7; cf. R4675–17.651.26–27). Thus one way we can relate sensible concepts to an object a priori is through construction.

What, then, is exposition? The question is important, because exposition seems to be a focal interest of the Duisburg Nachlaß. Given the little we have seen so far, we might have expected that Kant would be willing to characterize it too as a way of 'determining' concepts a priori—on the assumption that this is just another expression for relating a concept to an object a priori. But then the passage I just quoted from R4675, at the end of the previous paragraph, might be taken to imply that exposition has been squeezed out of the picture by construction, because Kant makes no qualification that would leave a space open for it. They are certainly enough alike that Kant speaks of exposition in R4684—without explicitly using the word—as 'an analogue of construction' (17.670.21). But one important difference between them is the target, for lack of a better word. Construction targets intuition (R4678–17.662.9), whereas the target of exposition is always appearance. This can be inferred from a remark Kant makes in R4678: 'We cannot construct appearances, only intuition. But we must have rules of their exposition' (17.660.27–29; cf. R4674–17.646.15–19).

Further along in R4678, we find mention of 'the agreement of appearances with one another and with the unity of the mind, hence of exposition'[^8] (17.661.25–26). We cannot yet tell why Kant associates exposition with the agreement of appearances or how the unity of the mind comes in to the picture. But it is clear enough that appearances are the target of exposition, while intuition is that of construction. The difference here is that between pure and empirical intuition. Construction goes to work on the former. But exposition must go to work on the latter, because, as Kant writes in R4679, 'empirical intuition is appearance' (17.663.32).

Here we may detect sympathetic resonance with a passage in the Critique from the proof of the general principle of the Analogies of Experience. We found that Kant denies in R4678 of the Duisburg Nachlaß at 17.660.27–29 that appearances can be constructed. But he says in our passage from the Analogies at A179/B222: 'For . . . this cannot be constructed.'[^9] The demonstrative pronoun refers not to appearances as such, but rather to their 'existence' (Dasein): the existence of appearances cannot be constructed. The qualification serves to mark a modal distinction, relevant for our purposes, between the existence of appearances and their mere possibility.

Kant's point in the Analogies of Experience is in part that the possibility of appearances can indeed be constructed: that is what happens when we apply mathematics to appearances. For then we disregard everything empirical in appearances and focus on pure intuition; that is, we consider nothing but their form a priori: 'that which brings it about that the manifold of appearance can be ordered in certain relations' (B34).[^10] Construction is a mathematical technique for describing rigorously the constraints imposed on appearances by their form. The rigour is such as to inject necessity into the mix, but always under a condition. Here is an example. If anything appears to us under the formal constraints distinctive of an equilateral triangle, then we can infer from the relevant construction that this appearance will necessarily have three equal angles that are together equal to the sum of two right angles. But the condition here is that something indeed appear to us having this shape. Unless that happens, the necessity is 'problematic'. It will kick in only if a certain existence has been established, namely that of a triangular appearance with three equal sides; yet existence is beyond the pay grade of construction.

Kant says in the Critique that knowledge of existence requires perception. He characterizes perception in the Postulates of Empirical Thought as appearances (distinct from their form) or empirical intuitions (sensations) of which we are conscious (A225/B272). A perception is a witness to something that happens or obtains somewhere, sometime. That something happens or obtains somewhere, sometime can be taken to indicate that something exists. Moreover, knowledge of existence consists in understanding the way our perceptions relate to one another in time—not as a matter of hypothesis, of course, but as a matter of fact (A225/B272). Kant says that 'when a perception is given to us in a temporal relation with other (albeit undetermined ones), . . . [it will be possible to say a priori under the Analogies of Experience] how it [sc. the so far undetermined perception—AL] is necessarily connected, with respect to existence, in this mode of time, with the given perception' (A179/B222). The interest here is that existence, as an object of experience, is not something isolated. It is existence in relation to still more existence. Our perceptions are a witness to these relations if they are a witness to existence at all.[^11] We acquire knowledge of existence if we can determine what these relations are. But this cannot be constructed.

Now it might be thought that Kant is overstating his case here. He surely cannot deny that the constructive techniques of classical geometry can be used to make predictions about the motions of heavenly bodies: determinations about where these bodies will in fact be relative to one another at future times or retrospective determinations about where in fact they were relative to one another in past times. Ptolemy shows us how to do this in the Almagest. Newton shows us how to do this in the Principia with the help of his theory of universal gravitation. Kant, of course, knows these things. So I take his point to be uncontroversial—at least to start with: Ptolemy and Newton could not put geometry to work in this way without some observations—of risings and settings at specific times, on specific dates. These require perceptions. Perceptions themselves cannot be constructed, because they essentially consist in the very thing that construction leaves out of account: empirical intuitions.

I now take Kant to make a second point. This one is admittedly controversial, but not obviously false: our understanding of the temporal relations among our perceptions, even when it is secured by mixing observations with mathematical inferences, depends ultimately on principles other than those that 'make legitimate the application of mathematics to appearances' (A178/B221), i.e., other than the Axioms of Intuition and the Anticipations of Perception. The principles in play, for these purposes, are—of course—the Analogies of Experience, i.e., the principles of the understanding associated with the relational categories of substance, ground and consequence, and community.

This takes us back to the problem of exposition in the Duisburg Nachlaß. Because Kant's account of construction is so underdone here, the possibility side of the modal distinction we just considered from the Critique does not come clearly into view. But the other side of that distinction does. A passage in R4677 is a good indication: 'Perceptions are not just appearances, i.e., representations of [stricken: its] the appearances, but of their existence.[^12] For example: that there is reality, that it is successive and that it is simultaneous [added: with] other reality' (17.659.14–17). We find an explicit association in this passage between perception and the existence of appearances, just as in the Analogies of Experience. Moreover, the existence of appearances is again spelled out in terms of the temporal relations among our perceptions. They are three in number: persistence through time, succession, and simultaneity. Elsewhere in the Duisburg Nachlaß, these three temporal relations are associated with the concepts of substance, ground and consequence, and community.

A passage in R4682 makes this very clear:

> The analogies of appearances mean as much as this: if I did not determine every relation among them through a universal condition of the relation in time, I would assign no appearance its place.
>
> Thus the concepts substance, ground and whole[^13] serve only to assign every reality in appearance its place, because every relation represents a function or [stricken: power of] dimension of time in which the object that is perceived is to be determined, and appearance is converted into experience. (17.669.3–10)

This passage is halting and unpolished, but it seems to foreshadow the Analogies of Experience in all its details—including the use of the word 'analogy' in what seems to be the relevant sense, except that, instead of 'experience' in the genitive, we have 'appearances'. We do not have to look far in the Duisburg Nachlaß to find other points of contact.

In R4681, we find a distinction between three 'conditions of perception' and three 'rules of perception' that seems to correspond to the distinction in the Analogies of the Critique between the subjective order of time and its objective counterpart. This distinction is most familiar to us from Kant's presentation of the Second Analogy. Our representations succeed one another in inner sense. But that succession is merely subjective, i.e., it fails to give us knowledge of temporal succession in objects, unless it is governed by a rule that makes it necessary. When I regard a landmark of interest to me, like the Brandenburg Gate, I take it in successively: perhaps I first look all the way up at the four-horse chariot driven by a woman brandishing a cross in iron with an eagle perched on top; then perhaps I cast my gaze on the relief below the horses' hooves; then perhaps I take in the four columns beneath. But that order of succession is contingent: I could perfectly well begin with the columns and make my way up to the horses, chariot, and driver. By contrast, the order of succession in my perceptions is necessary when I perceive the logs in our hearth catching fire after having perceived the lighting of a match. A physical law is in play here that can be taken to express the principle of causality Kant defends as the Second Analogy. In the latter case, we have objective succession in time; in the former, we have subjective succession. Rules and necessity are the key to distinguishing between subjective and objective time determination in all three Analogies.[^14] They are also the key to the distinction in our extended passage from R4681 between 'conditions of perception', which seem to underlie subjective temporal determinations, and 'rules of perception', which seem to underlie objective ones. I will now quote in turn two successive passages from R4681 that bear on this. The reader will note the occurrence in the first passage of the expression 'conditions of perception'. The expression 'rules of perception' does not yet occur in this passage, but I am sure that the notion behind the expression is on display in this passage, and it does occur in the second passage. The first passage reads as follows:

> Before any eventuality, something must always precede (condition of perception).
> Before any eventuality any manner of preceding can take place, but, among this diversity, there is one manner of preceding that this event must always follow.
> A reality always cleaves [added: to a point in time and to that which determines it] to that which accompanies it, [stricken: which] whereby the point in time is determined (condition of perception).
> There is all manner of accompanying, but, among this diversity, there is something that is always there.
> [stricken: An aggregate is many in reciprocal relation, but among this many there must]
> [added: In [stricken: that] respect of that which is simultaneous, there is always taking together [Zusammennehmung] (condition of perception).]
> [added: But] there can be a taking together [added: with] all manner of things. But [stricken: where the many are reciprocally determined, this connection is objective] where something is to be regarded as objectively connected together, there is a reciprocal determining of the manifold amongst one another.
> If there were not something always, i.e., something [stricken: constant] permanent, stabiles, there would be no fixed point or determining of the point in time, hence no perception, i.e. determination of something in time.
> If something constant did not precede [added: prior to an eventuality], there would be nothing among the many that precede, to which that which happens belongs in a sequence, it would have no determinate place in the sequence. (17.665.22–666.14)

This passage is fragmented and exploratory. But it is clear enough that Kant is trying to express the difference between subjective temporal determination and its objective counterpart: in all three 'modes' or 'dimensions' in time, namely permanence, succession, and simultaneous coexistence. The presence or absence of some here unspecified rule—it will be called a 'rule of perception' in the passage that immediately follows—makes the difference in each case. Its presence imparts a certain necessity to the sequence or order of our perceptions that it would otherwise lack. In the case of succession, for example, the relevant rule of perception requires not merely that something or other precede any given eventuality, which is all that the corresponding 'condition of perception' requires, but that some special kind of thing precede in the sequence: presumably that kind of thing that always produces that kind of eventuality as its effect, under some empirical law of nature. The stronger necessity imparted to the temporal sequence of our perceptions by the relevant rule of perception is then precisely what makes the sequence objective: we will be justified in saying that the logs catching fire in our hearth came after the striking of a match.

Kant sums up the matter in the next paragraph (this is the second of my two passages from 4681): 'Through the rules of perception, [stricken: things] objects of the senses can be determined in time; in intuition, they are merely given as appearances. Following these rules, a quite different sequence is found from that in which the object was given' (R4681–17.666.15–18). It is natural to infer, given the other passages we considered, that the 'rules of perception' at issue here are associated with the three categories: substance, causality, community—to the extent that they apply to appearances a priori.

It would seem, then, that an exposition of appearances is the application of those three categories to appearances. It determines a priori the temporal relations among them. As such, it has more in common with construction than it does with observation. Like observation, it targets appearances or empirical intuitions. But observation relates our sensible concepts to objects a posteriori. Exposition, like construction, relates sensible concepts to objects a priori. It does so by making it possible for us to assign to the objects of our empirical thought their place in time relative to other appearances. The interesting idea in play here is that using an empirical concept like that of Squirrel or Red requires an operation of the understanding, as the faculty of thought, that not only directs our thinking to those appearances that we take to be squirrels and/or red, but does so in a way that indexes our thinking to time—and to place at either different times or the same time. We may well have occasion to reflect on Squirrel in the abstract. But we do sometimes report squirrel sightings. This requires an ability to provide when's and where's. So too general reports of squirrel behaviour: are they active mostly at certain times of the year, certain times of day, or chiefly after sunset? Has their behaviour changed over the course of time? Does it differ from one place to another? Perhaps even reflections on Squirrel in the abstract will include references to time and place—at least to the extent that such reflections include any consideration of life cycle, habitat, and patterns of behaviour. In the Duisburg Nachlaß, Kant is trying out the idea that exposition of appearances relates our empirical thought of things like squirrels to the objects we take to be squirrels, just because it lays out for us a priori the temporal relations in which we can think of squirrels as relating to one another, to their physical environment and to us as both observers and as features of that environment.

This idea will be developed in much fuller detail, and with twists foreign to the Duisburg Nachlaß, in the Analogies of Experience in the *Critique of Pure Reason*.[^15] But the Duisburg Nachlaß anticipates that later development—at least on the details to which I have called attention in this subsection.

There is more to say about the expression 'exposition of appearances'.[^16] That will be some of what is to come in the next two subsections of this chapter. Let me just observe for now that the expression seems to drop out of Kant's critical writings on these issues. For the most part. It does occur in the section of the Critique on Phenomena and Noumena.[^17] We may well wonder what this occurrence tells us (if anything) about the points of contact we have found between the Analogies and the Duisburg Nachlaß. Kant says at A247/B303: 'Its principles [sc. those of the understanding—AL] are merely principles of the exposition of appearances,[^18] and the proud name of ontology, which presumes to give synthetic a priori knowledge of things as such in a systematic doctrine (e.g. the principle of causality) must make way for the humble name of a mere analytic of pure understanding.' One point of interest in this passage is that Kant explicitly uses the expression 'exposition of appearances' in referring to a central lesson of the Critique: traditional metaphysics—anyway, the part of it concerned with ontology—cannot withstand the test of critical scrutiny and must be replaced by the Transcendental Analytic. This is what we would expect from the Robespierre of metaphysics. It would be easy to assume that Kant is already preparing his coup in the Duisburg Nachlaß, at least to the extent that he is laying the groundwork for an exposition of appearances. I will argue in the subsections to come that that assumption is too easy.

### §1b An important claim: we can represent a sensibly given object only according to its relations—more on the exposition of appearances

Let us return to our focus passage from R4674. I quote it a second time to refresh our memory:

> X is the object. This can be given a priori through construction, but through exposition (which is something quite other than observation, which has nothing connected a priori with A) we can recognize in the subject conditions a priori under which A in general relates to an object, namely to something real. This object can be represented only according to its relations and is nothing other than the subjective representation (of the subject) itself, but made universal, for I am the original of all objects. (17.646.5–14)

In this subsection of Chapter One, I would like to make and develop another simple observation—simpler, once again, in the stating than in the developing. It is that Kant makes an important claim here: a sensible concept A can represent an object X 'only according to the object's relations'.

We might expect, from the previous subsection, that temporal relations are at issue: persistence through time, succession and simultaneous coexistence. For they are the relations of special concern to the exposition of appearance. No doubt they are at issue. But I think Kant must mean any kind of external relations, including spatial ones with no overt temporal index, as in geometry. We may take him to say that all the marks of the concept A are two- or more-place predicates and that A has no monadic one-place predicates expressing the inner nature of X itself. One simple, but inconclusive reason for thinking that he means any kind of external relation is that he does not explicitly restrict his claim to temporal relations in our focus passage from R4674. Another, more compelling reason is that spatial relations are at the focus of a complementary account of exposition he gave sometime in the late 1770s. That account is illuminating. I will use it later in this subsection to tease out more of Kant's thinking in the Duisburg Nachlaß. But some stage-setting is required first.

The claim under discussion in our passage from R4674 is controversial. It says, in effect, that we can represent an object only according to its relations, and not according to its inner nature, independent of its relations with other things in space and time. One might well wonder how Kant can live with this claim. The later evidence suggests he would continue to think it prima facie counter-intuitive and the earlier evidence suggests he one time thought it altogether false. In the *Critique of Pure Reason*, he would say, 'to hear tell that a thing should supposedly consist entirely of relations certainly raises eyebrows [macht stutzig]' (A285/B341). Kant assumes that the reader of the Critique takes the following idea to be entirely obvious and natural: 'the internal is the substratum of all relations or external determinations' (A283/B339); in other words, one thing can externally relate to another just in case they both have some kind of being—'internal', as Kant calls it—independent of their external relations. He is about to argue that this idea is wrong, but it is plain from his own words and laborious efforts to find the right formulation for his argument in this section of the Critique that he expects resistance from his reader and that he himself feels (or once felt) the pull of what he takes to be his reader's intuition.

Kant's ambivalence is not surprising: he himself had argued vigorously for the intuition in the Physical Monadology of 1756. His concern was to argue against Leibniz and his followers who might be understood to say that all the relational properties of a substance (its 'external determinations') are reducible to its monadic properties (its 'internal determinations') and that our thought of the former, however confused and obscure, can be shown ideally to reduce to thought of the latter. The early Kant held, by contrast, that the two kinds of properties in a substance are really distinct and that our knowledge or thought of the one kind is not parasitic on knowledge or thought of the other. For unlike Leibniz and his followers, Kant was convinced early on that created substances act on, and produce change in, one another. An implication of his commitment was that some properties in a substance are the handiwork of other substances and are therefore intrinsically relational: we could not discursively characterize such properties without making essential reference to the other substance whose effect they are and to the relation in which the two substances stand to each other. Nevertheless, the early Kant was happy to grant Leibniz that some properties of a substance are genuinely monadic and not to be explained by way of its external relations. Indeed, he said quite explicitly in the Physical Monadology that it is impossible to have (or to conceive of) relational properties in a thing, unless some subject or true substance underlies them. This subject cannot be just a set of relations or relational properties: that would run counter to the principle of sufficient reason.[^19] Hence, it must be a true substance with special inner determinations or monadic properties that make it the substance it is—in addition to the relational properties it has due to the way that it interacts with other substances (1.481.27–29).[^20]

The contrast between the Physical Monadology and the Duisburg Nachlaß is striking. Kant says in R4674 that we can represent an object according to its relations only and not according to its inner determinations or monadic properties.[^21] In 1756, he insisted that, unless we can in principle represent a substance according to its monadic properties, we cannot represent it according to its relations. The reason for this change of position must have something to do with the view he had developed in the early 1770s about the sensible conditions of our knowledge.

The early Kant could agree with Leibniz so far as he did, because he—like Leibniz—believed that our representation of a true substance with monadic properties depends on the intellect without the senses. He had not yet drawn a radical distinction between the principles of sensibility and those of the intellect. But he certainly believed—again like Leibniz—that the two cognitive faculties have certain non-negligible differences overlying their deeper, fundamental unity. Thus the intellect brings to light that which the senses leave clouded and concealed, namely the sufficient reasons underlying the sensibly manifest, relational aspect of material things.[^22] To counteract the senses, the intellect must somehow work its way behind the appearances. Once Kant had radically sharpened the distinction between the intellect and sensibility in the inaugural dissertation of 1770, he had to give up the Leibnizian idea that the effect of the latter faculty is to cloud and conceal the object of the former (2.394.30–34, §7). This meant in turn giving up on the idea of using the intellect to get behind or around what is sensibly given to us. As Kant says in our passage from R4674, the intellect can apprehend nothing in appearances except for the way that they relate to one another in space and time. As he would later say in the Critique, 'Matter is substantia phaenomenon. I seek what inwardly belongs to it in all the parts of space that it occupies and in all the effects that it produces, and that—to be sure—can only be appearances of outer sense' (A277/B333). The implication here is that I never find anything other than relations or external determinations when I set out to discover the internal nature of the material things that appear to us. He concedes that I will learn something about what he calls the 'relatively internal' determinations of such things, namely that they too are relational. But he also says that 'the absolutely internal [sc. non-relational determination—AL] of matter is indeed a mere lark [Grille]' (A277/B333).

There is another angle from which to consider the impact of the inaugural dissertation for the Duisburg Nachlaß. It will bring us back to the question what is meant by 'exposition of appearances'—a question that we have not yet fully answered.

As I just indicated, the early Kant agreed with Leibniz, in effect, that the inner is the necessary ground of the outer, i.e., of all external relations. But he also agreed with Leibniz in characterizing the inner as simple and the outer as infinitely divisible. A witness to this can be found in a passage in the Critique: 'Substances as such must have something internal that is free from all outer relations and therefore also free from composition. The simple is thus the foundation of the internal of things in themselves' (A274/B330). Kant is not writing here in his own voice, but rather in what he takes to be the voice of Leibniz. Still, we recognize this as a view he himself held in the Physical Monadology. He argued there that the true substances out of which bodies are composed are simple: they fill space and thus externally relate to one another by exercising forces of attraction and repulsion on one another, but that is just to say that they fill space by the 'sphere of their activity' (Proposition Six). The sphere of their activity is indeed infinitely divisible, because it is just the space throughout which the effects of their forces can be felt by other monads. But the monads themselves are not divisible, because they have inner determinations, which—being inner—have no direct impact on other monads and hence are not present in space (Proposition Seven). Thus the inner, which is the necessary ground of all external relations, is itself simple.

We may wonder (and will consider at length in subsection §1d) whether the doctrine of sensibility in the inaugural dissertation can accommodate anything like the principle I just enunciated on behalf of the early Kant; and even if it can, why it should be required to do so. For now, the only thing that matters is that it implies that appearances are given to us under conditions that exclude simple substances. Kant himself clearly understood this to be an implication of the new doctrine. He makes comments to this effect in his efforts to clarify what he meant by an 'exposition of appearances'—not in the Duisburg Nachlaß, but in the alternative source I alluded to at the outset of this subsection, namely his lectures on metaphysics, as recorded in the student transcript referred to as L1. This transcript is believed to have been made between the years 1777 and 1780.[^23] It is close enough in time that it can be used on some points to supplement the Duisburg Nachlaß—or, anyway, give us a range of interpretative possibilities for making sense of it. Unlike the Duisburg Nachlaß, it is not fragmentary (though the part on ontology has unfortunately been lost), and it is written up in complete sentences. That makes it a valuable resource.

The relevant passage from L1 is interesting in part because of the new light it sheds on what Kant meant by 'exposition of appearances'. We need to think this through carefully. It will delay for a few paragraphs our discussion of the 1770 doctrine of sensibility and its exclusion of the simple. But the delay is unavoidable because L1 explicitly connects this issue with exposition. Indeed, that is the source of its interest to me. The part of the passage from L1 on exposition as such reads as follows (we will consider its continuation immediately hereafter):

> Every appearance is, as representation in the mind, under the form of inner sense, which is time. Every representation is so constituted that the mind goes through it [durchgehet] in time. That is: the mind exposes appearance [exponirt die Erscheinung]. Thus every representation is exposible [exponibel]. For example, if the mind has a representation of a line, it goes through all parts of the line and exposes the appearance [exponirt die Erscheinung].
>
> We do not represent body in any other way than by going through all its parts, and that is the exposition of appearance. Thus we cannot be conscious of the object in any other way except by exposing it. (28.202.7–17)

The geometrical example in this passage will naturally recall the way the same example is enlisted several times in the Critique to make a similar point. There are at least three passages in the Critique where Kant says that we cannot think, conceive, imagine, or cognize a line without drawing it in the mind or the imagination. One of these passages is in §17 of the B-Deduction (B137–138). Another is in §24 of the B-Deduction (B154). A passage in the A-Deduction makes the same point, but uses the example of a triangle: 'we imagine a triangle as object when we are conscious of putting together three straight lines following a rule' (A105).[^24] These passages will come up for discussion in §3c of the next chapter and §2b of Chapter Four. The point for now is that they are all expressing a conception of understanding (in the non-technical sense of just 'getting it') borrowed from the regimented presentation of classical geometrical proofs and that this conception lies at the basis of what Kant means in our passage from L1 (and in the related passages of the Duisburg Nachlaß) by 'exposition of appearances'.

In Euclid and Apollonius, a proof almost always starts with a general statement or enunciation of whatever is to be proved.[^25] The proposition is then followed by the presentation of a special, though representative case. Consider, for example, the fifth proposition in Book One of Euclid's *Elements*. The enunciation of the proposition is general. This is as it should be: the hope is to secure a result that applies not to some particular isosceles triangle, but to all of them. The enunciation reads accordingly: 'in isosceles triangles, the angles at the base are equal to one another, and, if the equal straight lines be extended further, the angles under the base will be equal to one another.'[^26] After the enunciation, though, a special case is presented, together with a labelled diagram. For the Fifth Proposition in Book One of Euclid's *Elements*, that part of the proof reads as follows: 'Let ABC be an isosceles triangle having the side AB equal to the side AC, and let the sides BD, CE be produced further in a straight line with AB, AC.'[^27] An appropriately labelled diagram accompanies this text. It is followed by a declaration in the first person—the 'diorismos'[^28]—that restates the general enunciation of the proposition in terms of this special case: 'I say that the angle ABC is equal to the angle ACB, and the angle CBD to the angle BCE.'[^29] One special function of this part of the proof is that the diagram it is bundled with serves as the basis from which auxiliary constructions are carried out: from those constructions, previously proven propositions, axioms, postulates, and definitions, the proposition is proved for the special case; since the special case is representative, what holds for it is taken to hold for all such cases. But this part of the proof has a second function, which is—I think—the matter of interest for Kant.

It is often difficult to understand the enunciation without the statement in terms of the special case and its accompanying diagram. The enunciation of Proposition Five in Book One of Euclid's *Elements* is easy enough to understand on its own. That is why I did not bother to reproduce the diagram. But even the elementary propositions in Book One of Apollonius' treatise on conic sections are difficult to state discursively—and at least as hard to understand. Their enunciations are very long with relative clauses embedded in relative clauses. A reader's temptation is often to skip the enunciation and go straight to the diagram and the statement of the special case. Apollonius himself dispenses with the enunciation for some of the propositions in Book Three; he does so for about a third of them in Book Four. It is worth pointing out, not as a matter of historical curiosity, but as a way of underscoring how significant the difficulties of understanding the enunciation of these propositions are that, in his Arabic recension of Apollonius' treatise, the early twelfth century astronomer Abū 'l-Fath· al-Is·fahānī[^30] simply dispenses with them, apparently taking the reader's natural temptation to be a dependable guide.

There is another historical detail to add: the Greek word for the restatement of the proposition in terms of a special case and its accompanying labelled diagram is 'ekthesis'. We would translate that into English as the 'setting out'. In the case of Proposition Five in Book One of Euclid's *Elements*, the ekthesis sets out the isosceles triangle ABC as a representative example of all the triangles for which the proposition, if proved, will hold. The literal translation of the Greek word into Latin is 'expositio'. I think that Kant is using this sense of 'expositio' when he speaks of an 'exposition of appearances' in our passage from L1, but also in the Duisburg Nachlaß. This seems more plausible prima facie in our passage from L1, and the related passages in the Critique I mentioned earlier (where admittedly the expression is not used), just because the example is overtly geometrical and because it is used to make precisely the point I have been discussing: we cannot grasp the relevant concept (or proposition to be proved) unless we 'set out' the relevant figure. In the case of Apollonius, where the claim has the greatest plausibility because of the complexity of the enunciation, the reader does not merely cast a glance at the diagram: if you wish to understand the enunciation, you must work through all the details of the labelled diagram, redrawing it as it were, in your imagination, scrupulously following step by step the instructions given in the ekthesis. The claim may seem overblown for propositions whose enunciation is as simple as that of Proposition Five in Book One of Euclid's *Elements*. But I take Kant to be saying that the point holds even in the simplest cases: these cases are simple just because they are familiar; they are familiar either because we have already deliberately set out the figure in our mind and do not need to repeat the exercise or because we can do it so fast that we scarcely take notice of what we are doing.

I must not be taken to imply here that Kant knew any Greek—much less that he knew 'expositio' is Latin for 'ekthesis'. He must have taken up the Latin word as he found it used in Latin treatments of geometry available at the time. It would take me too far afield to document the details. I refer the curious reader to those currently known to me in footnote 31.[^31] We will return to the geometrical sense of 'exposition' in the subsection to come. For now, there are three quick points to make, the third of which will take us back to Kant's controversial claim in R4674 of the Duisburg Nachlaß that we can represent the object only according to its relations.

The first quick point is that Kant's remark in L1 (and the related passages in the Critique) represents the 'setting out' of figures in geometry as dynamic: it is a process that takes time.[^32] There is no suggestion of this in the writings of Euclid, Apollonius—or in the related discussion by Proclus in his commentary on Book One of Euclid's *Elements*. The ekthesis is some text and a labelled diagram. They are static. That is what you would expect: text and diagram on the page do not move. In any case, ancient Greek mathematicians avoid suggesting that mathematical objects can move or that proofs unfold in time. Let me add, though, that the point Kant is making does not just—or does not chiefly—concern the objects of mathematics; it is a point about understanding them. Kant is saying, in effect, that our understanding of what the mathematicians are trying to prove requires an active engagement that cannot take place in an instant. This is not because we might struggle to understand, though we often do. It is rather because understanding as such is a temporal process, according to Kant, that involves successively 'going through' the parts of the labelled diagram.[^33]

The second quick point is something that we will return to at greater length in the next subsection. That is why I feel free to characterize it here as 'quick'. It is this. Kant wants to extend the idea of ekthesis or exposition beyond the sphere of mathematics to our understanding of appearances more generally. That is what he is trying to do in the Duisburg Nachlaß. He is plainly gesturing at such an extension in our brief passage from L1 when he says, after discussing the example of the line: 'We do not represent body in any other way than by going through all of its parts, and that is the exposition of appearance. Thus we cannot be conscious of the object in any other way except by exposing it.' Just as we understand what is true of the line, if a successful proof is given, by 'going through all its parts', as required by the steps involved in 'setting it out' or giving an exposition of it, so we understand what is true of a body, or a system of bodies, if we go through its parts, as required by the steps involved in giving an exposition of appearances.

It is in this connection, I think, that we should understand remarks made in the Critique to the effect that a 'going through of the manifold' is required for carrying out the synthesis of apprehension and indeed for perception (A99, B162). It is worth quoting the passage in §26 of the B-Deduction where this idea is elaborated. The word 'exposition' does not appear here, but the meaning of the word—in Kant's extended sense—is clearly on display: 'When, for example, I convert the empirical intuition of a house into perception by apprehension of its manifold, . . . I sketch [zeichnen] its shape, as it were' (B162). When I perceive a house, I do not simply take in sense impressions; I 'set it out' by successively sketching its parts in space—just as Euclid sets out the parts of the isosceles triangle ABC in the ekthesis of Proposition Five in Book One of the *Elements*.

Somebody might now object that Kant is speaking in B162 about something other than exposition, as I have been characterizing it from ancient geometry: perception is at issue, not understanding, as before; and, understanding is something different from perception. I do not presume to settle here the myriad issues raised by this objection: it naturally presses the relevance of our current debates about whether Kant was a non-conceptualist. All I wish—all I need—to say right here is that the passage at B162 does not neatly distinguish perception and understanding as we might like. For Kant goes on immediately after the passage I just quoted to say that the same 'synthetic unity' he thinks is involved in sketching the house and that is supposed to convert the relevant empirical intuition, or its manifold, into a perception 'has its seat in the understanding' (B162).[^34] To be more specific, he says that it necessarily involves the category of quantity/magnitude (Größe): the act of sketching the house in space must be in accordance with this category—which makes perfect sense, if we think of perception as a kind of exposition—because what we are doing is determining relative magnitudes in length, area and volume: the ekthesis of Proposition Five in Book One of the *Elements* sets out triangle ABC such that side AB is the same length as the side AC; the ekthesis of the house might set out the front of the house, if I am facing it directly, as a parallelogram whose exterior walls are the same in height. That cannot be accomplished, not even crudely, without understanding and being able to apply the category of quantity/magnitude. Let me just add, before moving on to the third quick remark I will make before ending this subsection, that the exposition of appearances, as intended in the Duisburg Nachlaß, involves more than single acts of perception like that of a house. It also involves connecting perceptions with one another: it is a setting out of their relations (as we learn from our focus passage in R4674). How that might work more concretely we will take up in the next subsection.

This now takes us to the third and last quick remark. It concerns the continuation of our passage from L1 and its relevance for the claim in R4674 of the Duisburg Nachlaß that we can represent an object only according to its relations.

As I indicated earlier, this claim seems to express Kant's awareness that the doctrine of sensibility in the inaugural dissertation had implications for his earlier metaphysical commitments. It implied that the understanding (now in Kant's technical sense) cannot, even in principle, represent appearances according to their 'inner determinations', as he once believed in the Physical Monadology of 1756—nor can it represent them as grounded in 'the simple'—at least not if we mean by that simple, immaterial substances like the physical monads the early Kant took to be the underlying ground of all material bodies. The continuation of our passage from L1 resonates sympathetically with this thought. It says explicitly that 'the simple' is excluded from any 'exposition of appearances':

> Thus we cannot be conscious of the object in any other way except by exposing it. The reason is because all our given representations happen in time. All objects of the senses are exposible in our power of representation. This means that we can successively determine our mind in time; we also call it going through appearance [das Durchgehen der Erscheinung] where one successively goes from one part to the other. From this it follows that there is no appearance and no part of a given appearance that could not be divided to infinity. Thus there is nothing simple in appearance,[^35] neither in the following appearance, nor where the manifold of the appearance is. For the present can be set forward only insofar as the mind goes through it and exposes appearance. (28.202.15–28)

Why must 'becoming conscious of the object' involve an exposition of appearances? All our representations are in time. That does not just mean that our sensible representations are given to us in time, one after another. It also means that we develop them in time, namely by 'going through' each part of them one after the other. That process cannot take place in fits and starts: it has continuity, just as time itself does. That which has continuity has no simple parts; it is infinitely divisible. The temporality of exposition excludes the simple—from the process itself. But the outcome of the process is a kind of understanding, namely that which comes from setting forth something like a diagram. Moreover, it is understanding of a certain object. The constraints on the way we understand the object are reflected in what we will be permitted to report about the object. Thus we may take continuity of the time taken to sketch out the object to reflect the continuity of the object's extension in space and the continuity of its persistence and change through time. As I said, exposition does not take place in fits and starts. The objects we expose do not undergo instantaneous alterations; nor are they constituted of simple, indivisible parts. These implications are nicely stated in the continuation of the passage just quoted:

> Now it should be shown that, for these reasons [sc. because of the previous remarks about the exposition of appearances—AL], no appearance consists in simple parts. All appearance stands, as representation, in time and is exposed. A part of the whole appearance is exposed in a part of the whole time. Every part of the appearance thus lies in a part of time. But no part of time is an instant; rather a part of time is itself a time. An instant is only the limit of time. Hence, to every part of the appearance there belongs a part of time. Consequently, there is no part of the appearance that is not in time. Now since the time is infinitely divisible, so too there is no part of the appearance that would not be infinitely divisible. For every part of the appearance is between two limits of time between which it is gone through. The going through of a part of the appearance cannot take place in an instant. But that part which is enclosed between two limits has itself parts. For between two instants is always a time. Thus every part of appearance can in turn be exposed. There is no simple appearance.[^36] (28.202.29–203.6)

Thus we find in L1 an explicit connection between the exposition of appearances and the exclusion of the simple from things sensibly given to us. Note that this connection implies in turn that we represent appearances as having a determinate, continuous magnitude. To say that neither the process of exposition nor the alterations that appearances undergo can take place in an instant is to say that they have some determinate duration and that this duration can be measured. To say that it takes time to set out or expose a line in space and that the line so set out is not itself a point or composed of points but lies between two points as its limits is to say that it has a determinate length. As in the passage from B162 in §26 of the B-Deduction I quoted earlier, the possibility of assigning continuous magnitude is built into the exposition or sketching out of appearances. L1 states this implication explicitly: 'Hence, every appearance has a magnitude [Größe]; consequently, there is nothing simple. No part of appearance, neither of inner nor of outer sense, neither in sequence nor in aggregate, is simple. Thus all appearances are exposible [exponibel] in time' (28.204.14–18).

I take Kant to be saying the same thing, though much more elliptically, in our focus passage from R4674 in the Duisburg Nachlaß. Through the exposition of appearances, he says there, we can represent the object only according to its relations. But that means we can represent it only as something infinitely divisible, i.e., as something with continuous magnitude. We never grasp it in an instant, as by an intellectual intuition of its 'inner determinations'; we must always elaborate our understanding of it by taking the time to 'set it out'—in the course of which we represent one object according to its relations with another, e.g., by comparing continuous magnitudes of the same kind with respect to their size and by determining the ratios in which pairs of such magnitudes stand to one another. Our passages from L1 develop these ideas with respect to the spatial (geometrical) relations of appearances. But the same thing will hold of their temporal relations—and indeed their relations in time and space together. If I cannot represent, imagine, or conceive of a body (at a given time) except by running through all its parts as they are extended in space, then I cannot represent, imagine, or conceive of a natural process taking place somewhere through a determinate length of time except by running through in order all its stages in time.

In the next two subsections of this chapter, we will examine the role Kant assigns to the thinking subject in the exposition of appearances. There are two angles from which to do this. One concerns the significance of the relation between the ekthesis in a proof of classical geometry and its associated enunciation. That will be the angle of the next subsection: §1c. The other concerns the relation between external relations and continuous magnitude, on the one hand, and the simple or internal, on the other hand. Kant seems not to have abandoned his commitment in the Physical Monadology that the latter is the necessary ground of the former—despite his radical claim in our focus passage from R4674 that we can represent the object only according to its relations. He has dispensed with physical monads, but the thinking subject will be the simple, immaterial substance that grounds these relations insofar as it carries out, and serves as the foundation of, the exposition of appearances. That will be the angle of §1d.

### §1c Another important claim: I am the original of all objects—and still more on the exposition of appearances

We return once more to our focus passage from R4674 of the Duisburg Nachlaß:

> X is the object. This can be given a priori through construction, but through exposition (which is something quite other than observation, which has nothing connected a priori with A) we can recognize in the subject conditions a priori under which A in general relates to an object, namely to something real. This object can be represented only according to its relations and is nothing other than the subjective representation (of the subject) itself, but made universal, for I am the original of all objects. (17.646.5–14)

We make a third simple observation about this passage. It makes another controversial claim: I am the original of all objects. What does this mean? Our discussion of exposition in the previous subsection can steer us towards part of the answer.

Notice that this new claim seems to be a gloss on another claim about the object in our focus passage: 'it [the object] is nothing other than the subjective representation'. As Kant himself clarifies in parentheses, it is a representation of the subject itself. By 'subject', Kant surely means the thinking subject—as opposed to the subject of a judgement or the subject underlying a bunch of accidents—because the word 'subject' comes hard on the heels of the word 'subjective', which is never used to modify anything having to do with subjects in these other two senses. But 'representation of the subject itself' as a characterization of the object is verbally ambiguous. It could refer to a representation that the thinking subject has, forms, or receives, in which case Kant would be saying that the representation, so formed or had by the thinking subject, is itself the object.[^37] But the expression could also mean that the thinking subject is the very thing represented by this representation.[^38] In that case, the object is a representation of the thinking subject, and the thinking subject is itself the object represented by this representation. This sounds topsy-turvy. But it is indeed suggested by the gloss: 'I am the original of all objects.' In other words, I am the object of that representation which is itself constitutive of the object.

The claim is admittedly Delphic. But we may find a clue to Kant's thinking in a qualification he makes in passing. He does not merely say that the object is the representation of the subject; he says that it is this representation, 'but made universal'. Our focus passage from R4674 is not the only place in the Duisburg Nachlaß where the universal and the object are explicitly associated. In R4675, Kant writes: 'For the setting of objects for my representations is something that always requires that the representation be determined in accordance with a universal law, for indeed the object consists in the universal point'[^39] (17.648.15–17). It seems that the thinking subject is the source of this universality. That is suggested by our focus passage from R4674. But it is plainer still elsewhere. Thus Kant writes in R4676: 'The I constitutes the substratum for a rule as such, and apprehension relates every appearance to it.' We can elaborate on this by invoking what we learned about exposition in the previous subsection.

Universality is fundamental to the ekthesis in a proof of a proposition in classical geometry. Setting out the relevant figure gives us a special case of the enunciation or general statement of the proposition. If the general statement could not even be enunciated as such, there would be no special case of it to consider. The enunciation of Proposition Five of Book One in Euclid's Elements applies universally to all isosceles triangles. The setting out of the special case of the isosceles triangle ABC fulfils its function as an ekthesis, because it gives us a specification of the relevant rule: the isosceles triangle ABC has two equal sides and will be found, once the proof has been completed, to have two equal angles at the base and two equal angles under the base—as do all such triangles. The reader of Apollonius may well be tempted to skip the enunciation and go straight to the ekthesis. But it is really the enunciation that tells us what is to be proved.[^40] The ekthesis is relevant just because it gives us a representative example. Its usefulness comes from its relation to the generally stated enunciation. If the exposition of appearances is, for Kant, an ekthesis in anything like the sense of classical geometry, it must be related to something universal. Otherwise, it will be an instance of nothing in particular and therefore unrelated to any object of knowledge. I take Kant's concern to associate 'the object' with 'the universal' as a recognition of this important point. He is denying that any given appearance is an object of knowledge all by itself; it can become an object for us only if it is related to a universally applicable rule, law or principle. That gives Kant a special task: he must figure out what plays the role of enunciation in the exposition of appearances. It seems clear from R4676 that the thinking subject is a part of the story. That is what I take Kant to indicate when he says in our focus passage from R4674 that 'I am the original of all objects.'

The way to test this idea—and bring it to life—is to see whether there is anything more in the Duisburg Nachlaß about the relation between the thinking subject and the exposition of appearances.

We can start the test by asking ourselves what the exposition of appearances is supposed to set out. To be sure, it sets out appearances. But to be more precise, it sets out relations among appearances. After all, the object can be represented only according to its relations, as we learned in §1b from our focus passage in R4674. By implication, the relations among appearances set out by our exposition should be understood as special cases of universal relations. These, we presume, are supplied by the thinking subject. Plenty of textual evidence in the Duisburg Nachlaß supports that hunch.

First of all, Kant says explicitly in a couple of passages that the thinking subject has three special relations and that it is aware of them. For example, Kant writes in R4676: 'I am, I think, thoughts are in me. These are all relations that, although they do not give rules to appearance, nevertheless make it the case that all appearance is represented as contained under rules' (17.656.3–6). It is not clear from this passage all by itself which relations these are. But it is as clear as one could wish that they are indeed relations and that they are relations of the thinking self. We learn a bit more from a parallel passage about apperception in R4674, two paragraphs after our focus passage. Kant begins by characterizing apperception as 'the perception of one's self as of a thinking subject as such' (17.647.14–15). He immediately offers a complementary characterization: 'Apperception is the consciousness of thinking, i.e., of representations, as they are put in the mind' (17.647.16–17). That leads him to enumerate three relations. It is hard not to suppose that these relations are disclosed to us in apperception: 'Herewith are three exponents: 1.of the relation to the subject; 2.of the relation of following one another, 3.of taking together' (17.647.17–19). There is no way of being certain, but it seems plausible that the three relations enumerated here are the same as the three enumerated in the passage from R4676. If that is so, then 'I am' probably corresponds to 'the relation to the subject'. We will wonder: relation of what to the subject? Perhaps the answer is the relation of the subject to itself, or perhaps the relation of its representations to itself. Again, we may wonder what sort of relation is involved in 'I think'. Does it correspond to the second relation enumerated in our passage from R4674, i.e., the relation of 'following one another'? If so, what kind of 'following' is at issue: logical implication, succession in time, something else? Finally, how should we understand the third relation listed in both passages? All of this is unclear. But what is as clear as we could hope is that relations are in play that involve the thinking subject as such and of which the thinking subject is aware. This is both significant and helpful.

The second matter of significance is that Kant associates these three relations in our passage from R4676 with universality of some kind. This is clear from his talk of rules. He denies that the relations themselves are rules, but insists that they 'make it the case that all appearance is represented as contained under rules' (17.656.5–6). That is what we would expect, if indeed Kant's thought here is guided by the ekthesis in a proof of a proposition in classical geometry: it is precisely because the ekthesis sets out a special case of the enunciation that we may think of it as exhibiting or displaying a rule—under the condition, of course, that the proof of the proposition is successful. By analogy, we represent relations among appearances as special cases that exhibit a rule of some kind. They exhibit this rule, or count as a specification of it, by their relation to the relations of the thinking subject. We may think of the relations of the thinking subject as 'the original' of the relations we represent appearances as having with one another.

It is a short step from here to the cluster of claims in our focus passage from R4674. I cannot represent an object except according to its relations. But representing it according to its relations requires that I set out these relations as special cases of a rule or principle associated with the relations I know through apperception that I have, either with myself or my representations. The representation of the object is thus a representation of the thinking subject, 'but made universal', because it puts on display the relation of the thinking subject treated as a norm or standard. As Kant says in R4676, 'the I constitutes the substratum of a rule as such'. It is the source of all those relations that seem to regulate the way we represent relations among appearances. Thus it seems almost natural to say, as Kant does in our focus passage: I am the original of all objects.

The result of our test raises the plausibility of my reading of this peculiar claim. But it also raises a difficulty: what to say about an obvious disanalogy between the enunciation of a proposition in classical geometry and the relations of the thinking subject in the Duisburg Nachlaß. Perhaps we will find our way to conceding that the relations of the thinking subject are universal in some sense: at least to the extent that they are somehow normative for all appearances. But an enunciation is not universal in some vague, hard-to-figure sense: it is a universal statement that can be formulated discursively and counts as universal because it is true, if successfully proved, of all the members of a certain class of things. Relations, as relations, are not discursively formulated statements.

There is at least one thing we can say in reply to this difficulty. We can find discursively formulated universal statements in the Duisburg Nachlaß that are fundamental to the exposition of appearances. It is tempting to think that they are closely related to the three relations of the thinking subject and that they, in fact, are the enunciations we are looking for.

Examples of such statements are the 'rules of perception' stated in the passage from R4681 that I quoted towards the end of §1a. There is another example in R4675. I have already quoted part of this passage. The whole passage reads as follows:

> If my representation upon which follows [wenn meine Vorstellung worauf folgt], the object would not follow thereupon if its representation were not thereby determined as a consequence [als eine Folge], which would never happen except according to a universal law. Or it must be a universal law that every consequence be determined by something that precedes, otherwise I would set for the consequence of the representations no consequence of the objects. For the setting of objects for my representations is something that always requires that the representation be determined in accordance with a universal law, for indeed the object consists in the universal point. (17.648.9–17)

The universal law discursively formulated in this passage is, in fact, equivalent to the subjective 'condition of perception' stated at the beginning of R4681 (and quoted by me towards the end §1a). It is less strict than the corresponding objective 'rule of perception' stated in R4681. The 'condition of perception' in R4681, like the universal law stated here in R4675, requires only that any given consequent in a temporal series must be preceded by something: the kind of thing it is does not matter. The stricter 'rule of perception' in R4681 requires that any given consequent in an objective temporal series be preceded by a certain kind of thing. The kind of thing it is does matter, which suggests that Kant is thinking here of empirical laws: e.g., smoke is just the kind of thing that always follows a certain other kind of thing, namely fire, in an objective temporal series. In R4681, the 'rule of perception' is objective. In R4675, the corresponding 'condition of perception' is treated as objective. All this goes to show, for now, is that Kant's position was unstable. The thing that matters for us is that, in R4675 (as in R4681), we find a discursively formulated universal statement. We find in the continuation of the passage from R4675 that such universal laws are explicitly associated with the exposition of appearances and the three relations of the thinking subject.

The continuation of our passage from R4675 reads as follows:

> Likewise, I would not represent something as outside me [stricken: if] and thus make appearance into experience (objective), if the representations were not related to something that is parallel to my Self [Ich] whereby I refer them from me to another subject. So too if manifold representations did not determine one another according to a universal law. Thus the three relations in the mind require three analogies of appearance to transform the [stricken: functions in the mind] subjective functions of the mind into objective and to thus make them into concepts of the understanding that give reality to appearances. (17.648.18–27)

The word 'exposition' does not appear in this passage. But the talk of converting appearance into experience is an alternative expression used frequently in the Duisburg Nachlaß. The central issue of the passage is what must happen so that, out of a bunch of appearances, I get a representation of an object. Part of the solution to that problem lies in the three relations of the mind that we have encountered in R4674 and R4676. But we learn from this passage in R4675 that they are not the whole solution. In addition, we need three 'analogies of appearance'. Now we considered a passage from R4682 in which the expression 'analogy of appearance' occurs (17.669.3). Can that passage help us understand what is meant here? As we saw in §1a, it explicitly connects the 'analogies of appearance' with the three concepts, Substance, Ground, and Whole. It explicitly assigns these concepts a central role in the exposition of appearances. Finally, the first paragraph of R4682, which I have not quoted, elaborates the concept Ground as the very universal law we found under discussion in R4675. These observations taken together suggest that the disanalogy between the relations of the thinking subject and the enunciation of a proposition in classical geometry may be dealt with in just the way I indicated earlier: the role of enunciation in the exposition of appearances is not directly played by the relations of the thinking subject, but rather by the concepts of the understanding associated with them—insofar as these concepts have been elaborated into discursively formulated universal laws, like the different versions of the causal principle we find stated in R4675, R4681 and R4682.

The moves Kant has just made in R4675 and the related passages of R4682 are to be expected, if indeed he has modelled the exposition of appearance on the ekthesis in the proof of a proposition from classical geometry. In Euclid or Apollonius, the enunciation is not itself an isosceles triangle or an ellipse. Nor is it the whole class of isosceles triangles or the whole class of ellipses. It is, as I have indicated, a discursively formulated general statement about such figures. Even if the relations of the thinking subject are universal in some vague sense of applying to all relations among appearances, they are not universal in the more precise sense of the enunciation of a proposition in classical geometry. Perhaps that is why Kant denies in R4676 that the relations of the thinking subject themselves give rules to appearance: they make it the case that we can represent appearances as 'contained under rules', without themselves stating or giving the rules by which we take appearances to be governed (17.656.2–7). Suppose, however, that we have some way—call it an 'analogy of appearance' adopting Kant's language in R4682—of forming a general concept of each of the thinking subject's relations. That would be like forming the general concept of isosceles triangle or that of ellipse. If we could then formulate discursively the way that each of the thinking subject's relations applies to all the relevant relations among appearances, that would give us three universal statements analogous to the enunciation of a proposition about isosceles triangles or ellipses. When Kant formulates his principle of causality in R4675, R4681, and R4682, he discursively spells out what we encounter in apperception when we reflect on a certain relation of the thinking subject and form the concept Ground, and he thereby enunciates a 'universal law'—his words—that applies to all appearances that relate to one another in a certain way, namely when they objectively follow one another in the temporal order of succession. Thus when I represent an appearance A as the cause of appearance B, I am setting out their relation in time as that of the one following the other; I set out this relation among appearances as a special case of a certain relation of my thinking self disclosed to me in apperception: judging from R4674, that relation is the relation of 'following among one another' (17.647.18–19) in the relevant sense of 'following'—whatever it might be.

Given all the evidence to suggest that the ekthesis in the proof of a proposition in classical geometry is guiding Kant's thought in the Duisburg Nachlaß about the 'exposition of appearances', we may conclude that he has assigned the role played by the enunciation in such a proof to 'universal laws' formed by the understanding from the concepts Substance, Ground, and Whole that express the way the three relations of the thinking subject are supposed to regulate relations among the appearances. We have focused in this subsection on the ancestor of the 'second analogy', the principle of objective succession in the Duisburg Nachlaß. In §2b of this chapter, we will switch our focus to the ancestor of the 'third analogy', the principle of simultaneous coexistence. The switch in focus will help us detect the cosmological overtones of the Duisburg Nachlaß. The exposition of appearances is supposed to 'set out' relations among appearances in such a way as to convert them into the 'sensible world'. In truth, that conversion requires the application of all three universal laws in the Duisburg Nachlaß. But the principle of simultaneity can be shown to have a reasonably direct lineage from the 'Principle of Coexistence' in the Nova dilucidatio of 1755—a metaphysical principle that the early Kant explicitly elaborates in cosmological terms. Tracing that lineage will facilitate our investigation. That is why the focus will shift, as I have indicated, in §2b to come.

There is one last thing to say about our result so far. Kant's strategy in the Duisburg Nachlaß of deriving the concepts of Substance, Ground, and Whole from the mind's reflection on itself is not new. For example, Leibniz puts it to work in the Monadology where he says that the rational soul accedes to knowledge of necessary truths by 'reflective acts', that is, by those acts of intellect that 'make us think of what is called Self [Moy] and make us consider that this or that is in Us' (G6.612).[^41] The rational soul turns away from sense experience, reflects on itself and thereby comes to think of 'Being, substance, the simple or the composite, the immaterial or God himself' (G6.612). Leibniz says that these acts of reflection are 'the principal objects of our reasonings' (G6.612). This is apparently because they allow us to reason not only about ourselves and the things most closely related to us, but about all other things as well: insofar as we think of them as having being, as being substance or being simple or composite, or as relating to God somehow, we transfer our thought of ourselves to our thought of them.

Locke too can be understood to use the strategy, though to less florid effect. He would deny, to be sure, that it can yield the concept or idea of substance. The origin of that idea is supposed to lie not in the clear and distinct apprehension of myself as a thinking substance, but in the:

> Complication, or Collection of those several simple Ideas of sensible Qualities, which we use to find united in the thing called Horse or Stone, yet because we cannot conceive, how they should subsist alone, nor one in another, we suppose them existing in, and supported by some common subject; which Support we denote by the name Substance, though it be certain, we have no clear, or distinct Idea of that thing we suppose a Support.[^42]

Nevertheless, Locke claims that the mind's apprehension of itself is the origin of our idea of an active power. In Chapter XXI of Book Two of the Essay, he says 'but yet, if we will consider it attentively, Bodies, by our Senses, do not afford us so clear and distinct an Idea of active Power, as we have from reflection on the Operations of our Minds.'[^43] When we reflect on the operations of the mind, we recognize that we are, or have, an active power that we can exercise in different ways: in the voluntary actions we undertake and in the voluntary uniting of one idea in the mind with another. We also ascribe active powers to bodies whenever we conceive of one body as initiating motion in another. But Locke thinks that our idea of such powers in bodies comes from our reflection on ourselves. The ascription of active powers to bodies is thus the application of an idea to bodies that properly applies to me or my mind.

Thus Locke and Leibniz both seem to offer precedents for the strategy we have found emerging in the Duisburg Nachlaß. Indeed, we can imagine them both accepting the peculiar claim Kant makes there: I am the original of my objects—with the appropriate qualifications, of course. Kant's innovation in the Duisburg Nachlaß is twofold. First, against the backdrop of his engagement with classical geometry, he uses the three concepts derived from self-apprehension to formulate universal laws; second, against the backdrop of the theory of sensibility elaborated in the inaugural dissertation, he applies these laws not to things as they are in themselves, but to things as they appear to us in space and time taken to be pure intuitions. But the success of Kant's venture in the Duisburg Nachlaß is doubtful for the same reasons that we may doubt the success of Locke's and Leibniz'. Why should we expect concepts or ideas that express the mind's engagement with itself to apply to anything other than the mind? Once that question has been floated, we may take the next step and ask whether these concepts or ideas really do express the mind's engagement with itself and hence whether they apply to anything at all. That is, in effect, the point Hume makes against Locke on our idea of power in Section VII of the Enquiry concerning Human Understanding. The point might well be extended against the more florid claims of Leibniz in the Monadology. Henry Allison argues in his recent book on the Transcendental Deduction that, on my reading of the Duisburg Nachlaß, precisely the same sort of objection can be raised against Kant in the mid-1770s.[^44] He is right about that. He is wrong, however, to think that it calls my reading into question. The right inference to draw is that Kant's position in the Duisburg Nachlaß is an example of a well-attested pattern of thinking from the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries. It was attractive to the people who espoused it because it rests on a plausible assumption about human thought: we humans proceed in our thinking from the things most familiar to us (namely ourselves, as they believe) to things less and less familiar, namely all other things. The problem was just what Allison and Hume say it is: the assumption has as a consequence that our thought about the world is coloured by what we take to be insights into ourselves. Even if we should be right about what to say or think about ourselves, we should not inject our self-understanding into what we take to be understanding about things other than ourselves.

I indicated in the previous subsection that several passages in the Critique reprise from L1 the idea that representing, imagining, or conceiving appearances requires that they be 'set out', as by ekthesis. I don't think Kant ever gave up this idea. But he did seriously rethink it—probably in part because of the problem with its elaboration in the Duisburg Nachlaß, as I just spelled it out. I believe that §17 of the B-Deduction shows some of the fruit of this rethinking, as I will suggest at the end of §3d in Chapter Two, and that §26 shows the rest of the rethinking, as I will suggest in §3 of Chapter Four.

### §1d Spot the difference

We must take up one last question in this section of Chapter One. It concerns a point developed in §1b: the doctrine of sensibility in the inaugural dissertation of 1770 implies that the understanding cannot find among appearances anything simple to represent as the ground of their relations. The question is whether Kant takes this implication in the Duisburg Nachlaß in its most radical form, i.e., whether he takes it to mean that the understanding can find no simple ground anywhere. Is it relations all the way down? Must the understanding's natural expectation of somewhere finding a simple ground for these relations inevitably be disappointed?

One thing is certain: Kant thinks that the understanding cannot expect to uncover a simple ground during, or following, the exposition of appearances. For we saw that exposition presupposes continuity: of the process in time and of appearances themselves in time and space alike. Continuity excludes simple parts, things, or substances. But it does not follow that he dismissed the understanding's natural expectation altogether. I believe he thought it just had to be reinterpreted. One very simple reason for believing this is that Kant never says in any document I know of from the mid to late 1770s that the understanding is thoroughly deceived in this expectation. How might the reinterpretation go? I think that the Duisburg Nachlaß gives us a sketch and that it (the reinterpretation) is presupposed by the exposition of appearances.

The way to see this is to consider what Kant says, and does not say, in the Duisburg Nachlaß about the thinking subject. He says, of course, that it has three relations. We still do not know what these relations relate it to. They may be relations of the thinking subject to itself or relations of the thinking subject to its representations. It is hard to tell from the little Kant says. But that they are relations at all raises the question whether they themselves need to be 'set out', i.e., whether they need to be the focus of an exposition, just like the relations among appearances. Suppose they did. That would imply that the thinking subject is given to itself in time and that it must 'go through' every part of itself, one after the other. What evidence might there be that this is the case? One passage in the Duisburg Nachlaß does seem to mention the thinking subject's presence in time. In R4676, Kant seems to characterize apperception as 'the consciousness of the subject who apperceives, as of that which is given in the whole time' (17.656.20–22). The final clause of this passage is suggestive. It raises the question: what is given in the whole time? Proximity suggests that it is 'the subject who apperceives'. To boot, Kant has said a few lines earlier that 'everything must be given in time; it therefore encompasses everything within it' (17.656.19). If everything is encompassed in time, then why not the thinking subject? Is it not empirically true that our lives as thinking subjects play out in time? Kant himself should happily acknowledge that this is so, since, as we saw, he takes understanding—at least the understanding of appearances—to be a temporal process.

Be that as it may, the passage from R4676 I just quoted is ambiguous. It need not be taken to say that the thinking subject 'is given in the whole time'. It may be taken just as plausibly to say that the thinking subject is conscious of 'that which is given [to it] in the whole time', whatever this thing may be and however long or short 'the whole time' in which it is given. Perhaps what is given in the whole time is an hour-long display of fireworks. In that case, the passage tells us that the thinking subject who apperceives is conscious of fireworks presented to it for a whole hour. But to say that a display of fireworks is presented to me over the course of an hour and that I am aware of it for that time does not all by itself imply that I am given to myself in time. I may nevertheless be a thinking substance that knows itself as such through an immediate intellectual apprehension. Nowhere in the Duisburg Nachlaß can we find an 'ah-ha!' passage where Kant says, as deliberately as in the second half of §24 in the B-Deduction, that I can ultimately know myself only as something that appears to myself.[^45]

There is, moreover, good reason to think that the relations of the thinking subject cannot be 'set out' in the way that relations among appearances are set out, namely by exposition. The reason turns on their special role in the exposition of appearances. They are the source or basis of the universal laws that are to the exposition of appearances what the enunciation is to the ekthesis in the proof of a proposition in classical geometry. As we saw in §1c, the ekthesis is a special case of the enunciation. Without the enunciation, it would not be a special case of anything at all. If the exposition of appearances is analogous to the ekthesis in a geometrical proof, it is a special case of these universal laws. If the relations of the thinking subject were themselves in need of an exposition, we might worry about an infinite regress. We would have to ask of which universal laws the relations of the thinking subject are special cases. The answer could not be the universal laws of which relations among appearances are special cases. For the relations of the thinking subject are supposed to be the basis of these laws, not special cases of them. If the relations of the thinking subject are special cases of some other universal laws, we would then have to wonder where these laws come from. Are they somehow derived from still other, more basic relations? Would these relations in turn need to be set out by an exposition? If so, as special cases of still other universal laws? These considerations make it unlikely that the relations of the thinking subject can be set out by exposition, as relations among appearances are.

In that case, we may wonder how the relations of the thinking subject are known to the thinking subject, if not through exposition. We already know from R4674 (17.647.14–19) that they are disclosed to the thinking subject in apperception. The considerations we just rehearsed in the previous paragraph indicate that this disclosure does not take place in time: it happens in an instant or outside of time altogether. If it happened in time, it would require an exposition, just as appearances do. By implication, the thinking subject as such does not appear to itself. That means it knows itself through apperception by a direct, immediate intellectual grasp. Why not call it 'intellectual intuition'? There is a passage in R4677 where Kant explicitly denies that we have intellectual intuition (17.658.19–24). But context indicates clearly that he means that we have no intellectual intuition of appearances—what they are in themselves or what lies behind them—whose relations must be set out by exposition. The passage in R4677 is perfectly consistent with the idea that the thinking subject has an intellectual intuition of itself. If the thinking subject has a direct intellectual intuition of itself, then it knows itself as it is in itself and not as it appears to itself. Textual confirmation of this inference can be found in a passage of R4679 where Kant tries to formulate his idea that experience is possible only if appearances are subject to 'titles of the understanding', as he here calls the three concepts of the understanding: Substance, Ground, and Whole. He says what this means in each case: 'in all appearance there is substance and accidens; in the alteration of the same, cause and effect; in the whole of the same, reciprocal influence' (17.664.24–26). The three assertions made in this passage say nothing much more than that the concepts Substance, Ground, and Whole apply to appearances. For our purposes, the interest of the passage lies in what comes next: 'Thus these propositions [sc. the three just stated—AL] hold for all objects of experience. The very same propositions also hold for the mind with respect to the producing of its own representations and are moments of genesis' (17.664.26–28). Kant is apparently assuming some kind of distinction between the mind and the objects of experience. He does not say that the three concepts of the understanding apply to the mind, because it is an object of experience; rather, he says that they apply to all objects of experience and—in addition—to the mind as well, insofar as it 'produces its own representations'. This implies that, at least for the purposes of the quoted passage, the mind is not itself an object of experience, which implies in turn that it can apply the concepts of the understanding to itself not as a sensible thing, but rather as a thing in itself, i.e., as the kind of thing that belongs to the 'intelligible world' of the inaugural dissertation.[^46] In other words, it knows itself by a direct, intellectual intuition to be a thinking substance (whose accidents are representations?), to be the ground of certain consequences (its representations?) and to be a whole in its own right or the ground underlying a composite whole (made up of its representations?). If we ask, how the same concepts—that of Substance, Ground, and Whole—can apply both to things as they appear to us and to our own thinking subject as it is in itself, then the answer can only be: by exposition in the first case, and by apperception or direct, intellectual insight, in the second.

For the purposes of our discussion of exposition, the most significant passage along these lines can be found in R4684 at the end of the Duisburg Nachlaß. Kant explicitly claims here that the thinking subject knows by some kind of intellectual insight that it is a simple substance in a Latin point-form sketch of a rational psychology: 'Rational psychology with respect to the general predicates of the human soul: with respect to subject—substance; numerically one in man [unica in homine]; simple; immaterial' (17.672.9–10). We recognize the claim stated here as a bundle of rational psychological theses that would be rejected in the section on the Paralogisms in the Critique of Pure Reason (B402). But here it makes sense, at least given what we have seen, that the thinking subject should be simple (and hence immaterial): if it does not as such appear to itself and if it does not have to 'go through' itself in time by exposition, it does not have continuous magnitude of any kind. By implication, Kant denies in the Duisburg Nachlaß that it is relations all the way down. Relations come to a halt at the thinking subject, which seems to be their sufficient reason. It is certainly the sufficient reason of its own relations. By extension, it is the sufficient reason of relations among appearances, if only because it is the one who carries out their exposition.

The upshot of these considerations is that Kant seems not to have jettisoned the understanding's natural expectation that relations have a sufficient reason and that this sufficient reason is something simple. He has simply reinterpreted it as a fundamental piece of the exposition of appearances. He takes it to be perfectly true if we reinterpret it to mean that the exposition of appearances is an exercise carried out by a thinking subject of a certain description. It is a simple finite thing that has no direct intellectual insight into things that appear to it (either what they are in themselves or what lies behind them); the things that appear to it appear to it under its formal conditions of sensibility, which are space and time; it must 'set out' the relations among the things that appear to it in space and time on the basis of what it knows by direct intellectual insight into the relations it has either with itself or with its representations. This reinterpretation is interesting, because it shows that, for all the points of contact between the Duisburg Nachlaß and the Critique of Pure Reason that we noted in §1a–c, we can now spot the difference. For Kant will deny in the Critique that apperception is any kind of direct intellectual insight into the nature of the thinking subject and that we can know the thinking subject, by its means or any other, to be something simple (B407–408). Moreover, he will come much closer to conceding that 'it is relations all the way down'.

It is thus a mistake to read Kant's critical conception of the thinking subject back into the Duisburg Nachlaß. As far as I am aware, Wolfgang Carl was the first person to point this out.[^47] He based his observation on Kant's characterization of apperception in R4674 as the 'consciousness of representations as they are set in the mind' (17.647.16–17) and the talk about relations of the thinking subject in R4676 (17.656.2–7). Carl argued that the most natural way to read these passages, as they stand, is to understand them as making the mind out to be a thinking substance. I accept his conclusion. I take myself to have argued something more than he did. For one thing, I have found more textual evidence that he is right. But I have also argued that Kant remained committed in the Duisburg Nachlaß to two pre-critical positions at least: one regarding the nature of the thinking subject; the other regarding the sufficient reason of relations. More important, I have also argued that these pre-critical positions are meant to support Kant's efforts in the Duisburg Nachlaß to get his new account of the exposition of appearances off the ground. The interest here is not that Kant may still have been wedded in the mid-1770s to ideas he would later reject or revise. It is rather the work that he apparently thought they could do for him in his attempt to try out something new. This is important to see, because otherwise there may be some temptation to think that they are philosophically idle—an inadvertent expression of Kant's not yet having got all the way to the Critique, but of no philosophical significance as such.[^48] We will return to this set of issues in §2d of this chapter where we will see that they play into the cosmological aspect of the Duisburg Nachlaß.

In conclusion, I would like to point out that the Duisburg Nachlaß is trying to articulate some kind of idealism, but it is not transcendental idealism, even though it embraces the doctrine of sensibility from the inaugural dissertation. The idealism of the Duisburg Nachlaß is akin to that ascribed to Leibniz in L1. According to L1, idealism is the doctrine based on an idea, embraced by Kant in the Duisburg Nachlaß and not as such rejected in L1, that 'I intuit myself [sc. by direct, intellectual insight—AL], but bodies only as they affect me' (28.207.21–22). It says, moreover, that 'bodies are pure appearances that must be grounded by something' (28.207.26–27). The Leibnizian variant of idealism seeks this ground in something simple and substantial. Substantial things are taken to be active and thus to have an active force. That leads the Leibnizian idealist (on Kant's characterization) to reason as follows: 'I can represent to myself in substances no other force as essential than thinking force . . . . Representation is the only thing that I grant as absolute accident to substances. Hence, Leibniz says: all substances are monads or simple parts that have vim repraesentativam . . . ' (28.207.36–208.2). The Leibnizian will say that all material things that appear to us are thus the external manifestation of many monads harmoniously pre-established in the way they exercise their power of representation. The difference between this variant of idealism and the idealism of the Duisburg Nachlaß is this. As far as Kant is concerned in the Duisburg Nachlaß, the only monad that matters is the human thinking subject. That monad is indeed the sufficient reason of relations among appearances, but only by 'setting them out' by 'going through' them in time, not by the pre-established harmony of its representations with those of other monads. This makes the material world the exposition of a single monad's understanding of the manifold that appears to it.[^49]

## §2 The Exposition of Appearances as a Cosmology of Appearances

The Duisburg Nachlaß turns on the notion of an epistemological whole. Kant says repeatedly that, unless we form a connected whole out of our perceptions, there will be no converting appearances to experience and hence no a priori knowledge of sensibly given objects (17.643.7–22; 17.660.6–16). A priori knowledge of sensibly given objects is the result of the exposition of appearances. The exposition of appearances is thus the procedure whereby appearances are converted into experience. Hence, it will turn out that exposition must not merely 'set out' relations: say a relation of temporal succession between appearance A and appearance B over here, and a relation of simultaneous coexistence between appearance C and appearance D over there. It must 'set out' these relations in such a way that appearances are all related—or relatable—to one another in a global network of relations, in an overarching whole of appearances universally connected with one another. In other words, it must yield what I called in the introduction to this book a 'cosmology of experience'.

In much of the Duisburg Nachlaß, Kant tries to articulate the notion of such a whole as clearly and precisely as possible. But he had philosophical resources for doing so that went back to his earliest writings on metaphysics from the mid-1750s. The task of his early general cosmology was to formulate the right notion of a world in general. If one reads the Duisburg Nachlaß attentively, one cannot fail to notice that he frequently calls on his early cosmology, precisely at those moments when he tries to sort out how a whole of appearances is possible. My strategy here in the second section of this chapter will be to show precisely how Kant availed himself of his early cosmology in the Duisburg Nachlaß. But before doing anything else, I must recall those aspects of it most relevant for our purposes.

### §2a Some historical background—the early Kant's commitment to physical influx

The first systematic statement of Kant's early cosmology can be found in Section Three of the Nova dilucidatio, an academic dissertation of 1755. The purpose of Section Three is in part to refute Leibniz' system of pre-established harmony, not just as an hypothesis of the mind–body union but as a general cosmology as well. Leibniz denied that created substances really act on one another. Any change a substance undergoes is its own doing: the effect of an active force that the substance exerts on itself by the inner law of its own nature. The appearance of real interaction in the world is the effect of God's infinite wisdom. God orchestrated creation so that change of state would arise in one substance in concert with that in all others. Kant believed that Leibniz' system was false and that the true system of causes is that of 'physical influx' or real interaction, as we would say today. Thus Kant opens Section Three of the Nova dilucidatio with the so-called 'principle of succession', according to which 'no change can occur to substances except insofar as they are connected with one another; reciprocal dependence determines mutual change of state' (1.410.18–20).

The principle of succession clearly expresses the early Kant's commitment to physical influx. It says that no change will take place in the world, unless substances really act on one another; it is supposed to be true for all substances. If we accept the principle of succession, we shall be inclined to say that real interaction takes place between body and soul: if the soul's inner state changes at all, if one representation comes after another, the soul must have suffered the effects of an influence, presumably that of a body, and indeed that of the body with which it is most intimately associated (1.411.32–412.10).[^50] Just to that extent, the principle of succession will serve as a principle of rational psychology. But it will also apply to the simple, immaterial substances that Kant—like the Wolffians—believed were the sufficient reason of all matter. These substances or monads must really act on one another, if they undergo any change at all. Since monads were supposed to be the constitutive elements of the whole material world, the principle of succession will serve as a principle not only of rational psychology, but of general cosmology as well.[^51]

Kant's system of physical influx in the Nova dilucidatio rests on a second principle, that of coexistence, according to which, 'Finite substances do not relate to one another at all by their existence alone, nor are they involved in any interaction [commercium], except insofar as they are sustained, in accordance with their mutual relations, by the common principle of their existence, namely the divine intellect' (1.412.36–413.2). Just because substances exist, it does not follow that they will really act on one another. They will really act on one another, just in case God planned and willed they should do so. While the principle of succession says that real interaction is the sufficient reason of all change in the world, the principle of coexistence concerns the sufficient reason of real interaction itself: created substances must be governed by an eternal law proceeding from the very source of their existence, namely God's intellect (1.413.38–414.8). I have discussed the principle of coexistence at length elsewhere.[^52] For our purposes, it is enough just to note a couple of its consequences that seem to be relevant for understanding Kant's remarks about the whole of connected appearances in the Duisburg Nachlaß.

First, the principle of coexistence tells us under what condition a given substance can be part of the world. It is not enough that the substance should have been created by God; it must stand in some external relation with all the other substances in the world. On Kant's view in the mid-1750s, all external relations, including spatio-temporal ones, are, in the first instance, the effect of real interaction among substances. Hence to be part of the world, a substance must be subject to divine laws that cause it to stand in thoroughgoing relations of mutual interaction with all the other substances in this same world. God might have elected to create a certain substance without subjecting it to any such laws. In that case, the substance would stand outside of the world. If God elected to create a bunch of substances and to subject the one lone substance, together with the newcomers, to laws of interaction different from those that now prevail among substances in this world, the alien substances would form a whole world apart, and there would be no connection between things in the alien world and things in our own (1.414.10–26).

The second thing to note is that the principle of coexistence is intended in part as a criticism of Leibniz' general cosmology. On the one hand, it is taken from Leibniz' own critical engagement, in the correspondence with Clarke, with Newton's natural philosophy.[^53] But, together with the principle of succession, it is turned against Leibniz. For Leibniz, created monads collectively form a world just to the extent that God harmoniously pre-established their self-changing activity. Every monad is subject, not to universal laws of real interaction, but rather to the special internal law of its own, individual nature: since the nature of any given monad differs from that of all the others, God must legislate a tailor-made law for each. Thus every monad effects change in itself, as its own law dictates, without taking any notice, as it were, of the other monads. By an act of supreme cleverness, God adjusted the internal law of one monad to that of every other so that harmony would prevail among them. As a result, the world outwardly appears to hang together: substances seem to be really acting on one another and thus seem to relate to one another in space and time. That such relations are apparent rather than real follows, on Leibniz' view, from the fact that every monad is entirely self-sufficient. Indeed, Leibniz sometimes says that every monad is, as it were, a world apart. Thus a given monad in the world would continue to play out its course under the law of its nature, even if God annihilated all the other monads. The presence or absence of its companion monads is completely indifferent to it. What this apparently indicates to Kant is that the world does not really cohere, on Leibniz' view: it is not a single world held together by real relations of interdependence of all its parts; it is on the contrary an aggregate of as many different worlds as there are monads, loosely federated by God's fitting the law of one monad to coincide happily with that of another.

In the Nova dilucidatio Kant expresses the line of criticism I just indicated as follows: Leibniz' system of causes cannot give us universal harmony in what Kant takes to be the strict sense of the term. Harmony in this sense is possible just in case the totality of creatures stand to one another in real relations of mutual dependence. The presence or absence of companion creatures cannot be a matter of indifference: any given creature is necessarily affected by all the others, according to a few simple laws of universal interaction—like, for example, Newton's law of universal gravitation (1.415.1–15). Thus only the system of physical influx can give us universal harmony. Leibniz' system gives us not harmonia, but rather consensus—mere agreement, rather than universal interdependence (1.415.20–26). In Section Four of the inaugural dissertation of 1770, where implications of the principle of coexistence come up once again, this talk about universal harmony versus mere agreement is plainly cashed out in terms of world-wholes. Thus Kant says that a world-whole can be accounted real only if true, universal harmony prevails throughout; a world-whole must be accounted ideal or even imaginary if, as in the case of Leibniz' system, mere agreement without universal harmony obtains.[^54] Thus Kant writes as follows:

> And so, if through the sustaining of all substances from one, there be a necessary conjunction of all, whereby they constitute a unity, the universal interaction of substances will be through physical influx, and the world-whole will be real; but if not, the interaction will be sympathetic (that is a harmony without true interaction) and the world-whole will not be other than ideal. (2.410.20–25)

In short, physical influx alone has the makings of a real world.

One last observation about Kant's early metaphysics is in order. At the basis of Kant's distinction between real world-wholes with universal harmony and ideal world-wholes presenting mere agreement is the idea that necessity of the appropriate kind prevails in the former but not in the latter. This is already plain in the passage from the inaugural dissertation that I just quoted. For Kant says explicitly that the conjunction of substances in a real world-whole is necessary. By contrast, the conjunction of substances in an ideal world-whole is contingent. Kant's idea can be spelled out from what we have just seen. On Leibniz' view, necessity characterizes the relation between the present state of any given monad and its future. For the future is just the effect of the monad acting on itself in the present. Likewise, the monad's present state is the necessary consequence of its self-changing activity in the past. But since the monad can act only on itself, the presence or absence of companion monads is completely indifferent to it, as I indicated earlier. The future of a given monad would be the same, whether or not God decided to annihilate all other monads. On the other hand, if we have a real world-whole in which the conjunction of substances is necessary, the presence or absence of companion substances is not a matter of indifference. Quite the contrary. For, in that case, every substance makes a causal contribution to the future of every other substance. Instead of separate series of necessary causal connections, reaching into different (though divinely coordinated) futures independently of one another, we have a complex of interwoven, necessary causal connections uniting created substances in such a way that they determine the future of the whole together. To that extent, the conjunction of substances in a real world-whole is necessary, and the world, taken as a whole, presents true, universal harmony.

### §2b Echoes of the earlier metaphysics in the Duisburg Nachlaß

I shall return to Section Four of the inaugural dissertation in §2c of this chapter. For now, let me note that ideas related to the principle of coexistence and its cosmological implications come up frequently in the Duisburg Nachlaß. It was perfectly natural for Kant to think through this material in terms of his earlier metaphysics. One of the concerns of the Duisburg Nachlaß is to explain the possibility of empirical knowledge. Kant early on associated knowledge with forming wholes of some kind.[^55] Such an association had already led him in the inaugural dissertation to present the difference between the two sources of human knowledge, sensibility and intellect, as a difference of worlds: the one sensible, the other intelligible. The Duisburg Nachlaß may be understood as a continuation of Kant's reflections from the inaugural dissertation on the sensible world, i.e., on what he now takes to be a unified whole of appearances converted into experience by exposition along the lines I elaborated in §§1b–c of this chapter.

To support my claim, I must first document the points of contact in the Duisburg Nachlaß with Kant's early metaphysics. R4675 is a good place to start. Kant says, for example, that 'all that which is at once in reciprocal relations belongs to a whole: contra vacuum [stricken: separans] interrumpens (vacuum [stricken: ter] terminans) . . . ' (17.648.28–30). This remark is now familiar to us from Section Three of the Nova dilucidatio as a statement of the principle of coexistence. Just a few lines later, in the context of some very obscure reflections about certain unspecified activities of the thinking subject, Kant repeats the remark, which reads as follows: 'All that which belongs to an aggregate [added: objectively] is in reciprocal determination to one another, for otherwise it is only a subjective [added: ideal] whole' (17.649.12–14). Kant here seems to be drawing the distinction now familiar to us from the inaugural dissertation between real wholes and their ideal counterparts. For he characterizes a real or objective whole as one whose constituent parts are in 'reciprocal determination'; an ideal whole is presumably one in which reciprocal determination does not, or cannot, come into play.[^56]

These remarks are not made in passing, for Kant proceeds to explore the matter, apparently drawing on aspects of his early metaphysics in different ways. The reflections that immediately follow the passage I just quoted once again address the familiar distinction between the real and the ideal, though now much more generally than before. They read as follows:

> The ideal or real subject
> – – – – series
> – – – – aggregate
> The first are only [stricken: actus] activities of the mind, the second is something to do with the objects in themselves in relation to thought without distinction of the subject. Both are distinguished through the necessity of the relation, which flows from universality: the subject, whatever serves as an underlying ground; the member of the series, from whence ever something determinate follows; the manifold, whereof each is determined by, and determines in turn, every one of the remaining ones. (17.649.21–31)

The first thing to note about these remarks is that the focus has widened beyond wholes or aggregates to include series and subjects as well. Thus a subject is anything that underlies something else; a series is some kind of sequence such that one member somehow follows another. Finally, an aggregate is a whole or manifold whose constituent parts universally determine one another. Why has Kant widened the focus of his considerations? An answer to the question is suggested by the fact that he comes round, by the end of this page of reflections, to mention three relations, 'unities' or 'agreements' (17.650.9–10;16,20–21) that seem to correspond to the concepts of subject, ground and consequence in a series, and aggregate or composite whole (R4682–17.669.6–10). It is presumably with the thought of subsequently clarifying his ideas about these fundamental concepts and the 'universal laws' associated with them, as discussed in §1c of this chapter, that Kant at first introduces the quite general remarks about subjects, series, and so forth, which I just quoted. And that is already the second thing to note about these remarks, namely just how general they are. Kant does not indicate what sort of subject, series or aggregate he specifically has in mind. His remarks could apply to any: to immaterial substances as well as to appearances. Our passage has thus the character of something like a general metaphysical interlude. The task of general metaphysics or the 'science of being in general', as Wolff characterized it in the Ontology, is to show what pertains to all beings, either absolutely—as in the case of immaterial substances in and of themselves—or under some condition—as in the case of appearances which are given to us under the conditions of human sensibility.[^57] Kant seems to be using points from general metaphysics in the passage quoted above to collect and clarify his thoughts. He is asking himself what he can reasonably say in the most general terms about anything insofar as it can be conceived as subject, as a member of a series of ground and consequences, or as an aggregate. Perhaps he believed these general considerations were more settled and easier to grasp, having at least been through the business about wholes or aggregates as often as he had in his metaphysical writings up to the 1770s. If that is the case, he must have thought that he could use the general metaphysical interlude to orient himself so as ultimately to get a fix on the things that he plainly found more difficult and elusive, namely the exposition of appearances and the problem of converting appearances into experience. Thus it is interesting to note that the general metaphysical point he highlights in this passage is just that sometimes we will call a subject, series or aggregate real; sometimes we will call it ideal. His problem for now is to determine—in the most general terms—what makes the difference.

Kant's first solution to the problem in the passage we have been considering is to say that something is ideal if its character as a subject, series or aggregate is an 'activity of the mind' (17.649.24). If its character as a subject or what-have-you is 'something to do with the objects themselves' (17.649.24–25), it will count as real. How exactly is Kant thinking of the matter? That is hard to say, since he says so little. But he goes on almost immediately to appeal to another consideration: that of necessity. What apparently distinguishes the one kind of subject, series, or aggregate from the other is whether a given subject necessarily underlies something else, whether one member of a given series necessarily follows another or whether the constituent parts of a whole necessarily determine one another. If so, the subject, series, or aggregate is real; otherwise, not. In the reflections that immediately follow this passage, and until the end of the page (17.650.22), this element of necessity is the consideration that Kant focuses on. Thus, a little further along, he says that 'necessity prevails in all three unities' (17.650.16). Context suggests that the 'three unities' in question are that of subject, series, and aggregate respectively.

Now our general metaphysical interlude has something in common with the passages on wholes from the early metaphysical writings we were considering in §2a. It treats necessity and dependence on an activity of the mind as mutually exclusive properties. Some sort of necessity will always be found in a real world-whole, but never in an ideal one. Every ideal world-whole will somehow depend on an activity of the mind, but no real one ever will. In the early metaphysical writings, the reason for dividing things up this way was the attack mounted against Leibniz' pre-established harmony. According to the early Kant, pre-established harmony can only give us ideal world-wholes. Such a world-whole is a collection of substances we mistake for a real world-whole when the substances are so harmoniously pre-established that they appear to us to engage in real interaction and to have universal, necessary connections with one another. Thus an 'activity of the mind' is indeed the ground of every harmoniously pre-established ideal world-whole, namely a certain inference based on error. When a world-whole is real, on the other hand, its character as such does not depend on error, even if our beliefs about it happen to be mistaken. For universal real interaction and the necessity that accompanies it are its sufficient reason.

Whether or not the general metaphysical interlude in the Duisburg Nachlaß is drawing on precisely these anti-Leibnizian considerations, it seems to treat the distinction between the real and the ideal in the same way as the early metaphysical writings, namely as mutually exclusive properties. But the central preoccupation of the whole Duisburg Nachlaß will require that this distinction, as laid out in the general metaphysical interlude, be suitably modified when later applied to appearances. For, unlike their counterparts in the early metaphysical writings, the real wholes in the Duisburg Nachlaß (and their counterparts among series and subjects as well) depend on an activity of the mind, just as much as the ideal ones. This can be seen quite plainly in a passage not from R4675, which has been our focus so far, but from R4674, where Kant characterizes the special 'linking' [die Verkettung] needed for the real wholes in the Duisburg Nachlaß as 'a representation of the inner operation of the mind that connects representations . . . so as to make a whole in accordance with the matter' (17.643.12–17). Something like the distinction in the general metaphysical interlude will continue to play throughout the Duisburg Nachlaß. Kant plainly recognizes that he must spell out more clearly what sorts of 'activities of the mind' he thinks are involved in the formation of wholes and distinguish the ones that produce real wholes from those that produce merely ideal ones. The criterion of necessity—already so important in its own right for generating our distinction in the early metaphysical writings—will stand out as a crucial factor. This will mean that necessity and being, or depending on, an activity of the mind can no longer be mutually exclusive properties. Some activities of the mind will be accompanied by necessity; and, this is precisely what will qualify them to represent appearances as real subjects, series, and aggregates and thus to convert appearances into a whole constitutive of experience.

The new alliance of necessity and some not yet specified operation of the mind sets the tone for much of the Duisburg Nachlaß. It is indeed symptomatic of a turn in Kant's thinking: he takes important principles from his early metaphysics—those that typically had their home in his cosmology—but adapts them for the purpose of investigating the conditions of possibility for empirical knowledge. The move requires not only that he interpret these principles in epistemological terms, but also that he make important adjustments as he goes along, without which the principles could not do the work assigned them. To see this epistemological turn play itself out in the Duisburg Nachlaß, we must examine more closely how Kant reappropriates his earlier distinction between the real and the ideal: grounding it on necessity, just as he had in Section Four of the inaugural dissertation, but departing from his early metaphysics by coupling necessity with some activity of the mind or intellect. Kant's reflections on the concept of aggregate offer the most telling case for our purpose, just because they are more obviously and straightforwardly related to his early metaphysical treatment of worlds than the other concepts he is interested in, namely that of subjects and series.

Two remarks are in order before considering the textual evidence more closely. They both concern Kant's use of the word 'aggregate' (Aggregat). First, the choice of word itself is puzzling, because Kant reserves it elsewhere for collections of substances that do not form a real world-whole. L1 reports Kant as making a familiar point to his students in the following terms: 'Accordingly, all substances in the world stand in commercium and thereby constitute a whole [ein Ganzes]. An aggregate [ein Aggregat] is not yet a whole; there we have only plura that stand in no reciprocal connection' (28.196.9–12). A few lines further along, he is reported as saying: 'The aggregation of substances [die Aggregation der Substanzen], in which there is no community, does not yet constitute a world. The reciprocal determination, the form of the world as a composite, rests on commercium' (28.196.26–29). Given passages like these, it is surprising to see real world-wholes designated by 'aggregate' in the Duisburg Nachlaß. But there can be no mistaking Kant's words or meaning here. He seems to use 'whole' [Ganzes] and 'aggregate' [Aggregat] interchangeably. That is attested by the two statements of the principle of coexistence from R4675 I quoted earlier: 1. 'All that which is at once in reciprocal relations belongs to a whole[^58] [zu einem Ganzen] . . . ' (17.648.28–29); 2. 'All that which belongs to an aggregate[^59] [zu einem aggregat] [added: objectively] is in reciprocal determination to one another, for otherwise it is only a subjective [added: ideal] whole [Ganzes]' (17.649.12–14). These passages say pretty much the same thing, and yet the one word is freely substituted for the other. Kant may well have been stricter in his language with his students than he was with himself. We should not therefore be reluctant to read his talk about 'aggregates' in the Duisburg Nachlaß as having to do with real world-wholes based on commercium.[^60]

The second remark concerns a possible ambiguity of both words in the Duisburg Nachlaß. Sometimes they specifically designate one of the three relations of the thinking subject we discussed in §1c of this chapter or the concept of the understanding corresponding to it. This is plain whenever Kant deliberately speaks of wholes or aggregates, by contrast with subjects and series (or grounds and consequences), or when he attempts to state the 'universal law' specifically governing aggregates for the exposition of appearances. We find him doing this when he states the 'principle of coexistence' in R4675 (17.648.28–29; 17.649.12–14). For he apparently intends this principle as a statement of the 'universal law' for 'setting out' the relation of simultaneous coexistence: if the principle of coexistence gives us a way to 'set out' states of appearances as coexisting simultaneously at a given moment, then it will have given us a way to represent all those appearances as jointly forming a dynamic whole or aggregate at that moment. More could be said about this. But all that matters for now is that the principle of simultaneous coexistence we find in R4675 is supposed to be distinct from, but complementary to, the principle of causality that, as we saw in §1c, is stated as the 'universal law' for 'setting out' appearances as succeeding one another in time.

On the other hand, and this is where the ambiguity comes into view, the joint task of all three concepts, including the concepts of subject and series, and thus the joint task of all three of their associated 'universal laws', is precisely to form a whole out of otherwise disparate, unconnected appearances. Thus Kant says in general terms that 'without concepts such as these [sc. all three concepts of the understanding—AL], appearances would be altogether separate and would not belong to one another' (17.664.12–14), as they would presumably in a whole of some kind. In a passage from R4674 I quoted earlier, Kant says in general terms that a certain 'representation of the inner operation of the mind', which might well be any one of the three concepts of the understanding, is just that 'linking' [Verkettung] that 'connects representations, not merely setting them alongside one another in intuition, but making a whole according to the matter'[^61] (17.643.15–17). 'Here is thus unity' he goes on to say next ' . . . whereby the manifold is brought into one' (17.643.17–18). This sense of 'whole' and its equivalents seems more general than that which we considered in the previous two paragraphs. It seems to refer to experience as such,[^62] i.e., to the outcome of applying all three concepts of the understanding and their associated 'universal laws' in the appropriate way. This is perfectly intelligible in cosmological terms: understanding the world, as it appears to us, will not only involve mapping out the positions of things that simultaneously coexist in different places at any given moment; it will also involve relating any given time-slice of the sensible world to any other in the order of succession. It will also involve determining how long any given state of affair may last. Consequently, a whole or aggregate taken as the concept of the understanding associated with the principle of coexistence is just one of three necessary conditions for a whole of appearances converted into experience. It is not always possible to say for sure which sense of 'whole' or 'aggregate' Kant has in mind: that of the specific category or universal law or that of experience in general. In R4675, they usually seem to refer to the relevant concept of the understanding.

If we now turn back to R4675 to examine the textual evidence for the epistemological turn more closely, the first thing to note is that Kant begins to change his vocabulary along the way. In the passages I quoted earlier, the relevant distinction was at least verbally the one familiar to us from the inaugural dissertation, namely that between the 'real' and the 'ideal'. But in the second paragraph beyond the general metaphysical interlude, Kant brings the term 'objective' into play—presumably by contrast with 'subjective'. It is clear enough, however, that this new distinction corresponds to the old one: narrowing the focus once more on aggregates, Kant seems to distinguish between objective and subjective aggregates just as he had distinguished between real and ideal aggregates in the previous two paragraphs. Thus he writes:

> Aggregates objectively considered must have a common [gemeinschaftlichen] ground of unity, whereby the manifold depends on one another. [added: The consequence from this is that the many, which agree together among one another, have a common ground]. (17.650.1–4)

An objective aggregate is one in which the constituent parts depend on one another. The result is some kind of interdependence or harmony, i.e., thoroughgoing reciprocal determination, as we saw earlier in R4675 (17.648.28–29; 17.649.12–14; 17.649.30–31). By implication, an aggregate whose constituent parts do not depend on one another is subjective. This is the word Kant uses explicitly for such aggregates in one of the statements of the principle of coexistence we saw earlier: 'All that which belongs to an aggregate [added: objectively] is in reciprocal determination to one another, for otherwise it is only a subjective [added: ideal] whole' (17.649.12–14). That Kant substitutes the one set of terms for the other is already a mark of the epistemological turn in his thinking. Since the concepts of the understanding are supposed to be necessary conditions of empirical knowledge, the pair of terms needed to capture the relevant distinction must have the appropriate epistemological connotations. 'Subjective' and 'objective' thus seem more natural—at least to that extent, though we shall have to return to their significance for Kant in due course.

The second thing to note is that, as one would expect, Kant goes on immediately to cite necessity as that which distinguishes the objective from the subjective—apparently following the example of his distinction between the real and the ideal in the inaugural dissertation. Now we already had occasion in §1c to discuss the question how Kant conceives the objective in the Duisburg Nachlaß. We saw that he associates it with universality: the exposition of appearances is supposed to yield a representation of the object, though only according to its relations, because it sets out these relations as a special case governed by a universal law. Here in R4675 we find that necessity is also a key factor. Kant clearly thinks that the objective requires both: a universal law applies to all its special cases; its special cases thereby relate to one another necessarily (by reciprocally determining one another, if we have applied the principle of simultaneous coexistence to them). This squares with an explicit remark in the general metaphysical interlude of R4675: '[The ideal and the real] are distinguished through the necessity of the relation, which flows from universality' (17.649.21–31). Our current passage from R4675 plays up necessity in the following terms (note the mention of a universal rule of appearances at the end of the first sentence):

> The inner necessity of appearance, since namely the same is considered freed up from everything subjective and is considered as [stricken: among one] determinable by a universal rule (of appearances), is the objective. The objective is the ground of the agreement of appearances among one another. (17.650.11–15)

In the inaugural dissertation, the real went together with necessity, because a real world-whole is supposed to present a certain necessary conjunction of substances that manifests itself worldwide (because of universal laws) as true harmony. The point in R4675 seems to involve a parallel, namely that the objective goes together with the inner necessity of appearances, because the objective consists in 'agreement of appearances among one another' inasmuch as they are governed by a 'universal rule'. It is not obvious what Kant means by 'agreement' here. But given the way R4675 resonates sympathetically with ideas from the inaugural dissertation, it is tempting to think that something like true harmony, except among the appearances, is what Kant has in mind. Before jumping to that conclusion, I must make a few more observations about Kant's vocabulary.

'Agreement', as in 'agreement of appearances among one another', is my translation of the German 'Einstimmung'. Kant consistently uses this word in R4675, rather than 'Harmonie'. As we saw earlier, he had taken care in the Nova dilucidatio to distinguish (in Latin) true harmony—harmonia—from mere agreement—consensus: the latter he reserved in the inaugural dissertation for harmoniously pre-established ideal world-wholes; the former he reserved for real world-wholes based on universal real interaction. It is important to remember that Kant was bilingual, at least as far as philosophy was concerned: philosophical ideas were as likely to occur to him in Latin as in German. 'Einstimmung' is just the nearest German equivalent for the Latin 'consensus'—i.e., agreement, common accord, or consensus, as we would say even in contemporary English. It may very well be that precisely these Latin resonances were in Kant's mind when he used the word 'Einstimmung' in the Duisburg Nachlaß. Hence the choice of this word in R4675, rather than 'Harmonie' (which originally comes not from the Latin, of course, but from the Greek, 'harmonia', meaning a fitting together), might possibly indicate that the wholes and aggregates of the Duisburg Nachlaß have more to do with Leibniz than the Kant of the Nova dilucidatio or the inaugural dissertation.

But while we must always be sensitive to the Latin resonances of Kant's German, we have already seen enough even in R4675 to infer that agreement of appearances underwritten by the appropriate rule is after all something rather like the true, universal harmony in Kant's earlier metaphysical writings. For the key association is already in place, and we cannot be mistaken about this. On the one hand, Kant has said in no uncertain terms that a real or objective whole is formed by the reciprocal determination of all its parts; on the other hand, he has said that such reciprocal determination must be accompanied by necessity. The two claims together suggest the picture of a genuine world-whole, which we represent as being formed not by independent, though coordinated strands of causal necessity, as in Leibniz' picture of things, but rather by some kind of network of interwoven strands of causal necessity implicating every part of the whole in the determination of every other part of the whole. The only thing that is not entirely clear from what Kant says in R4675 is the scope of the agreement or harmony of the parts in such a whole. Is it absolutely immediate and universal, as in the world-wholes Kant envisaged in his earlier metaphysical writings, or is it more restricted than that? Is it simply that any given part in a real or objective whole must stand in a relation of necessary, reciprocal determination with some one other part? Or is it rather that every single part of the whole must stand in such a relation with every other part? Though Kant's express words often leave the question open, the latter seems to be what he intends in one of the passages we have seen from R4675. Thus, while still writing at a level of high metaphysical generality, Kant characterizes a real aggregate as one in which necessity 'issues from universality' and prevails among a manifold of parts 'whereof each is determined by, and determines in turn, every one of the remaining ones' (17.649.27–31; cf. R4674–17.646.21–23).

Assuming that, by 'agreement of appearances among one another', Kant does have in mind true, universal harmony, as characterized in his earlier metaphysical writings (except applied now to a whole of appearances rather than a world-whole of created substances), it is all the more interesting to note that he insists several times in R4675 on what he calls a 'common ground of unity'. Thus a common ground of unity must be present when appearances agree with one another in such a way as to form an objective whole. We have already seen Kant make this point in a passage I quoted earlier. 'Aggregates objectively considered must have a common [gemeinschaftlichen] ground of unity', he says, 'whereby the manifold depends on one another' (17.650.1–3). 'The consequence from this', he adds, 'is that the many, which agree together among one another, have a common ground' (17.650.3–4). At the end of this page of notes, Kant once again widens the scope of his considerations and calls for a common ground of agreement in every case where appearances may be regarded as objective. Hence, he associates such a ground with all three relations or concepts of the understanding: 'The objective is the ground of agreement of appearances, whence threefold agreement: 1. in the common subject, 2. in the [stricken: a] common beginning, 3. in the common whole' (17.650.20–22). What exactly does Kant mean by a 'common ground of unity or agreement' in any of these cases? It is hard to know given the cursory and fragmentary nature of the passages we have been considering. But the constellation of ideas now in play in R4675 is once again suggestive of themes from Kant's early metaphysics. For just as Kant insists in R4675 that the agreement of appearances in objective wholes or what-have-you requires some common ground of unity, so he claims in the inaugural dissertation that a common ground of existence is the necessary condition of all real world-wholes characterized by a true, universal harmony of all their parts. I would like to explore this a little further for two related reasons. First, it will add detail to Kant's continued commitment to what I called in §1d the expectation of the understanding that external relations must be grounded in something simple. Second, it can ultimately shed light on what Kant says in the Duisburg Nachlaß about apperception and the role the mind has in producing objective aggregates in which appearances agree among one another.

### §2c That a world must have a common ground of existence

Kant makes a big deal of the common ground underlying the unity of a world in Section Four of the inaugural dissertation—in the very same context in which he distinguishes between real and ideal world-wholes, as we saw earlier. Thus he writes, for example:

> Therefore, UNITY in the conjunction of the substances in the universe is the consequence of the dependence of all on one. Hence the form of the universe attests to the cause of its matter, and no other but the unique cause of all things [causa universorum unica] is cause of the whole of them [causa universitatis], nor is there an architect of the world, who is not also its creator. (2.408.16–19)

Again, he writes:

> If, just as the inference from some given world to the unique cause of all its parts is valid, so too the converse argument proceeded from a given common cause of all things to their connection with one another and thus to the form of the world . . . , the primitive bond of substances would not be contingent, but necessary through the sustaining of all by a common principle, and thus the harmony issuing from their very subsistence, being founded on their common cause, would proceed according to common rules. (2.409.2–10)[^63]

These passages are important and deserve comment.

The unity of a world apparently depends on the fact that all its parts owe their existence to a single, common cause. But it would be a mistake to infer that one and the same divine being will have created a full-fledged world simply by creating a bunch of different substances. For that would violate the principle of coexistence, to which Kant is as committed in the inaugural dissertation as he was in the Nova dilucidatio:

> Several substances being given, the principle of the possible interaction among them does not consist in their existence alone, but something else is also required, from which their mutual relations might be known. For by their very subsistence, they do not necessarily relate to anything else, unless perhaps it be their cause. And yet the relation of caused to cause is not interaction, but dependence. Therefore, if some interaction should come about between them and other things, there must be a peculiar ground determining this interaction precisely. (2.407.16–22)

This is an explicit restatement of the principle of coexistence: substances will not act on, or relate to, one another just because they depend on a cause (or indeed on the same cause) for their existence. Hence one and the same God must not only create a bunch of substances, he must subject them to a law of universal interaction. Otherwise they will not form a world.

The principle of coexistence naturally raises a question: how should we understand Kant's remarks in the inaugural dissertation about the unity of the world presupposing the dependence of all creatures on a common cause? Here is a suggestion.

For now, Kant is after the most fundamental, minimal condition without which a bunch of created substances cannot qualify as a world at all. Kant's concern is to state a necessary, but even by his own lights insufficient condition of worldhood. A bunch of substances will count as a world, if it presents the right kind of unity, i.e., it must form a unified whole of mutually and universally interdependent parts. But sometimes Kant also stresses the fact that an appropriately unified world-whole has numerical oneness. Numerical oneness and unity go together, but the first depends on the second: unity holds a world together and prevents it from dissolving into either a plurality of lesser worlds or a plurality of scattered, unrelated parts. If Kant can state that condition without which unity and hence numerical oneness are impossible, he will have pinpointed the ultimate necessary, though insufficient condition of worldhood. The condition Kant picks out is the fact that all the parts of a candidate world must owe their existence to a single, common first cause. If there were more than one first cause, there would be more than one distinct world or—anyway—collection of things; and, these distinct collections of things would be completely cut off from one another. As Kant himself puts it:

> If there were a plurality of first and necessary causes along with the things they had caused, their artefacts would be not a [single] world, but worlds, because they would in no way be connected to the same whole; and, conversely, if there were a plurality of worlds besides the actual one, then there would be given a plurality of first and necessary causes, but such that the one world would not be in any interaction with another, nor would the cause of the one world be in any interaction with the cause of another. (2.408.21–26)

If it could be shown that a bunch of substances were the handiwork of different creators, we could infer that these substances do not collectively form one and the same world and hence that the unity requisite for a real world-whole cannot prevail among them.

Behind the very strange claims in the passage I just quoted are several related theses about the cause of a world that will be directly relevant for understanding the Duisburg Nachlaß. Kant is taking for granted in the inaugural dissertation that the cause of a world is necessary. He is using the word 'necessary' in a sense we have not yet had occasion to discuss. He means to say that the first cause is necessary both in the sense that the world would not exist without it and in the sense that this cause cannot itself fail to exist. It belongs to Kant's conception of a being necessary in the latter sense that such a being must be absolutely self-sufficient: it depends on nothing else, either for its existence, or for any of its properties. This self-sufficiency is supposed to disqualify it from interacting with anything—including other necessary beings, were there more than one of them. For interaction involves mutual dependence of one thing on another for change of state and hence the acquisition of new properties. Since there can be no interaction among necessary beings, such beings cannot relate to one another in any way. For again Kant's view is that the possibility of real relations among things depends on interaction. It supposedly follows from this that there can be no world formed of necessary beings (2.407.32–408.3). But it also follows that there can be no interaction or real relations among contingent beings, if these contingent beings were created by different necessary beings. Given that any two necessary beings would be cut off from each other, their respective creatures must likewise be cut off from one another. Hence, the creatures of a given necessary being would form a world amongst themselves. But in the absence of a relation between them and any necessary being other than their own creator, there could be no link with the creatures of any other world. Worlds can be formed only out of those creatures that proceed from one and the same common cause.

As I said earlier, Kant is apparently trying to state the most fundamental, minimal condition for obtaining that unity among creatures requisite for any real, numerically one world-whole. It is a minimal condition to be sure, because, as Kant himself indicates, one and the same necessary being must do more than create a bunch of substances in order to produce a world: the substances must be subjected to a universal law of real interaction. But Kant's point in the passages from the inaugural dissertation we have just been considering seems to be that numerical oneness and unity of the necessary being itself must indeed be given to start out with: for no numerically one, truly unified world can proceed from the uncoordinated creative activities of a multiplicity of unrelated necessary beings. Singleness and unity of the cause of the parts of the world is a necessary, though insufficient, condition for that unity of creatures needed to hold the world together and make it one. Here is a cosmological expression of the understanding's expectation that it is not relations all the way down: the world as such is a universal network of external relations made possible by laws of real interaction, but these laws of real interaction must come from a single, simple being. The same pattern of thinking will be applied to experience and the thinking subject. Some of that pattern was already discernable in §§1c–d of this chapter. The cosmological overtones will become discernable in §2d, the subsection that follows this one.

### §2d That an objective aggregate must have a common ground of unity

Let us return now to the Duisburg Nachlaß. After distinguishing real series, subjects, and aggregates in R4675 from their ideal counterparts, Kant narrows his focus back to aggregates; he says that any aggregate objectively considered must have a 'common ground of unity'. For otherwise the constitutive parts of the manifold would not mutually depend on one another. To these remarks, he adds the following thought: 'The consequence from this is that many things, which agree amongst one another, have a common ground' (17.650.1–4). Now all this talk about a common ground of unity is highly suggestive of the considerations from the inaugural dissertation that I finished spelling out. Just as Kant was concerned in the inaugural dissertation to state a minimal, but fundamental condition for obtaining that unity among creatures essential to any real, numerically one world-whole, so he is concerned in R4675 to state a minimal but fundamental condition for obtaining that agreement among appearances essential to any objective aggregate. Just as singleness and unity of the cause of the world is a necessary condition in the inaugural dissertation for that unity of creatures needed to hold the world together and make it one, so singleness and no doubt unity of the common ground is a necessary condition in R4675 for that agreement of appearances needed to hold an objective aggregate together and make it one.

An important parallel is emerging here. In the inaugural dissertation, the common ground of existence for all the finite substances in a world was God. The common ground of an objective aggregate of appearances in R4675 will be the thinking subject—and, indeed, the unity of the same. But how deep does the parallel run? To answer our question, we must examine more closely Kant's remarks about the relation between the thinking subject and wholes or aggregates.

The following passage from R4675 is important for our purposes, because it says that unity of the thinking subject is a necessary condition for converting a manifold into some kind of whole. The expression 'objective aggregate' does not appear in this passage. Kant uses 'whole' (ein Ganzes) instead, without any qualification. This suggests that the term should be taken in the broader sense I identified in §2b, namely as experience in general or the result of applying any or all three concepts of the understanding (including that of a dynamic whole or aggregate) to otherwise disparate appearances. The passage reads as follows:

> The condition of all apperception is the unity of the thinking subject. From thence flows the connection [added: of the manifold] according to a rule and in a whole [in einem Ganzen], because [stricken: as much] the unity of the function must suffice as much for subordination as for coordination. (17.651.13–16)

This is a difficult passage to unpack, because it is highly compressed. To make sense of it, we must take it apart one bit at a time. Let us start with the first sentence.

As we saw in §1c, Kant characterizes apperception in R4674 as 'the consciousness of thought, i.e., of representations, just as they are put [gesetzt] in the mind' (17.647.16–17). Assuming uniformity of the technical vocabulary, the opening claim in the passage from R4675 I quoted in the previous paragraph is that the thinking subject has some kind of unity and that this unity is in turn the condition of apperception, i.e., the consciousness the thinking subject has of its own thought. It is not clear what kind of condition is in play here. On the face of it, there seem to be two possible readings, both of which we must consider before trying to understand the passage as a whole.

The first, and more natural, reading is this. A thinking subject cannot be conscious of representations as they are set in its mind, unless it has inner unity, i.e., unless it is something single and simple. If it were internally fragmented, it could be conscious of the different parts of its mind no more than a properly unified one can be immediately conscious of representations in the minds of other thinking subjects. The idea here would align with what Kant would later characterize in the Critique of Pure Reason as the 'nervus probandi' of the Second Paralogism (A351–354): the metaphysics of thinking subjects would then give us the condition of all apperception. The second reading of the claim need not have metaphysical import. It would treat unity of the thinking subject as a condition of apperception in the following sense: as something that informs or accompanies it—the state in which it always finds itself. The claim might then be taken to mean that one cannot be conscious of thought without also being conscious of having unity, i.e., as being one and the same thinker of the different thoughts one has. The claim, so construed, might have metaphysical import, if being conscious of having unity depended in turn on being a single, simple, thinking thing. But it need not have such import, if one argued that consciousness of having unity depends on being conscious of producing unity in the manifold. It would then fall in step with the claim in the Critique that 'the mind could not possibly conceive the identity of itself in the manifold of its representations, and indeed a priori, if it were not aware of the identity of its operation [Handlung] . . . . ' (A108). The claim, so construed, would have no metaphysical import if it were followed by further argument like that in the section of the Critique on the Paralogisms of Pure Reason (B421–422). But given the options, how should we understand Kant's claim in our passage from R4675?

As we saw in §1d, there is evidence in the Duisburg Nachlaß that Kant still conceived of the thinking subject in the mid-1770s as a single, simple, immaterial substance. That evidence is important for construing our passage from R4675. It indicates that Kant had not yet reached the conclusions that would force a non-metaphysical reading of the claim about the condition of all apperception we started with. Moreover, they show that Kant was positively committed to just the sort of metaphysics most compatible with the most natural reading of it. On this reading, the thinking subject can be conscious of its thoughts just because it is something single and simple. This it is, we have seen, by being a single, simple substance that thinks. There are no good reasons for accepting the second, non-natural reading, and excellent ones for rejecting it: either it is not as natural as the first one, or it is positively anachronistic. Hence, the first reading is the better construal of the opening claim in our passage.

After having stated the condition 'of all apperception', Kant goes on to make a remark, important for our purposes, but as ambiguous as the claim we started out with. He says: 'From thence flows the connection of the manifold according to a rule and in a whole . . . . ' As Wolfgang Carl points out, 'from thence' can be read in two ways. Either it refers to the whole claim that precedes it, in which case 'connection of the manifold etc.' flows from apperception, as made possible by the unity of the thinking subject. Or it refers directly to the unity of the thinking subject, in which case that unity will be the source of 'connection of the manifold etc.'.[^64] Carl calls the first reading 'epistemological' and the second one 'ontological'. He argues for the latter from an analysis of Kant's remarks about the so-called 'functions of apperception' here in our current passage from R4675 and elsewhere in the Duisburg Nachlaß. I am not convinced that these remarks can solve the problem at hand. In fact, I doubt that we can determine which way this sentence should be understood. But it does not matter for our purposes. Given the evidence we reviewed in §1d, we know already that Kant treats the thinking subject as a single, simple immaterial substance. As such it has unity. The 'epistemological' reading will be as metaphysical as the 'ontological' one. The difference would be a matter of emphasis. The 'epistemological' reading would make apperception an intermediary: the unity of the thinking subject makes apperception possible; apperception makes possible the connection of the manifold according to a rule and in a whole. The 'ontological' reading would make the connection of the manifold depend directly on the unity of the thinking subject. Either way, the unity of the thinking subject—as a single, simple, immaterial substance—is the ultimate ground of the connection of the manifold.[^65] This is all that we need to bring more sharply into focus the parallel between the thinking subject in R4675 and the divine being in the inaugural dissertation.

Whatever else there is to say about converting the manifold 'according to a rule and in a whole', the larger context of R4675 indicates that the relevant rule will be such us to make the relevant whole a unified one. But if we now bring into play the considerations I just finished spelling out, we must infer that no rule-governed, unified whole could be produced out of a manifold, if the thinking subject were not a rational substance and therefore possessed of unity as well as the attendant numerical oneness. This fleshes out the parallel we saw emerging earlier. For the unity of the thinking subject is playing the role of necessary condition for connection of the manifold in a unified whole—and therefore presumably for objective aggregates as a species of unified whole—just like that played by the numerical unity of the divine first cause in the inaugural dissertation for any real world-whole with true, universal harmony.[^66] That this unity is just a necessary condition can be seen from the following passage in R4679: 'A whole is possible in the unity of the mind only insofar as the mind, from the one part-representation, determines the other, and the other way around, and all of them are grasped in one operation that holds for all' (17.663.8–11). To produce a connected whole out of the manifold, the mind must not only have the unity of a thinking substance; it must also legislate something like a law of mutual determination of 'part representations' by one another. This idea closely parallels the idea in the inaugural dissertation we discussed in §2c: the unity of the divine first cause of the world is just a necessary condition of the world's worldhood; the divine first cause must also subject all the parts of the world to a law of universal mutual interaction. Thus we can see that the parallels between the inaugural dissertation and the Duisburg Nachlaß are not superficial.

The only thing missing from our passage in R4675 is any explicit talk of 'common grounds'. When we encountered such talk in R4675 before, there was no explicit mention of the thinking subject or the importance of its unity as a necessary condition for converting a manifold into a properly unified whole. Now one wonders whether there might be a passage where the relevant considerations about the thinking subject come into play together with the familiar talk about common grounds. Such a passage would explicitly identify the thinking subject as the common ground of the relevant wholes—and indeed precisely by virtue of its own internal unity. Such a passage would thereby bring into even sharper focus the parallel emerging between the thinking subject in the Duisburg Nachlaß and the divine first causes of the inaugural dissertation. As it turns out, there does seem to be a passage that fits the bill, not in R4675, but elsewhere in the Duisburg Nachlaß. Thus Kant writes in R4678:

> That there lies in the soul a principium of disposition as well as of affection. That the appearances cannot otherwise have any order and cannot otherwise belong to the unity of the power of representation except that they are in accordance with the common principium of disposition [gemeinschaftlichen principio der disposition]. For all appearance, with its thoroughgoing determination, must indeed have unity in the mind, and must therefore be subject to such conditions, whereby the unity of representations is possible. Only that which is required for the unity of representations belongs to the objective conditions. (17.660.6–14)

There are at least two points of interest in this passage. First of all, Kant seems to be calling on the unity of the thinking subject here, just as he did in the passage from R4675 we were considering in the previous paragraph (17.651.13–16). At any rate, he uses language that recalls his preoccupation with such unity there. Thus he speaks of the 'unity of the power of representation'.[^67] As before, Kant seems to be saying here that unity of the mind or the thinking subject is a necessary condition of order among appearances. I take it, from the larger surrounding context, that such order among appearances is precisely what we will find in any manifold that has been converted into a rule-governed, unified whole.

The second point of interest is that here, as in some of the other passages from R4675 we have examined (17.650.1–4), we do indeed find explicit talk of a common ground, and, indeed, a common ground of order among appearances. Kant's expression is different from that in the relevant passages of R4675 only insofar as he chooses the Latin 'principium' over the closely related German 'Grund' or 'ground'. But that difference is negligible, and the word Kant uses to qualify the former term in the passage under consideration is the same as the one he used in R4675 to qualify the latter, namely 'gemeinschaftlich', which I have been rendering as 'common'. Thus it seems that the thought expressed in the passage under consideration is no different from the one we found expressed in the relevant passages of R4675: unless appearances have, or belong to, a common ground, namely the 'common principium of disposition in the soul', they will not present the requisite order or unity. The one genuine novelty here is that this common principle has to do with some activity, faculty, or state in the soul which Kant calls 'disposition'. We shall have to consider what Kant means by this term, but the more important point for now is this.

Pulling the previous two points of interest together, our passage from R4678 seems to give us the evidence we were looking for that Kant is explicitly drawing a close connection between the thinking subject, insofar as it has unity, and the common ground of order in those appearances converted into a rule-governed, unified whole. Indeed, the second (incomplete) sentence of the passage, as I have translated it, is pretty explicit on this point. For it seems to make a direct association between subordinating appearances to the common ground and having them belong to the 'unity of the power of representation', i.e., unity of the mind or the thinking subject, as I am inclined to suppose. Appearances will belong to the 'unity of the power of representation' and thus be capable of showing the relevant order, he says, just to the extent that they have a common ground. This direct association seems to bear out the idea that Kant wants to say in the Duisburg Nachlaß, as he had in the inaugural dissertation, that unity and hence numerical oneness of the common ground—in this case the mind or the power of representation—is at least a minimal, but fundamental condition of unity and hence presumably numerical oneness in something like a world-whole made of appearances, or an objective aggregate.

One important question left over from this is what Kant means in R4678 by 'disposition' as in 'common principle of disposition in the soul'? That he should use the term at all here makes the passage at least superficially different enough from passages in R4675 involving a common ground of order among appearances to make us wonder whether something else is going on in R4678. One way to quell this suspicion perhaps is to consider the Latin origin of the German 'Disposition', which I have rendered by its English cognate.

'Disposition', in either modern language, comes directly from the Latin, 'dispono', which means: to distribute, to set in order, to appoint, to arrange, to draw up. Supposing, as it is usually safe to, that Kant is aware of the original meaning of Latin borrowings into (philosophical) German, the common principle of disposition in the soul is presumably just that principle whereby the mind arranges appearances, or sets them in order, or assigns their place in some kind of rule-governed, unified whole. This is, moreover, pretty much what Kant seems to be saying in R4678: 'That the appearances cannot otherwise have any order and cannot otherwise belong to the unity of the power of representation except that they are in accordance with the common principium of disposition [gemeinschaftlichen principio der disposition].' In other words, there can be no order among appearances unless appearances are subject to the appropriate ordering principle.

If this is the right way to construe Kant's words, it seems that the sudden use of 'disposition' does not signal any change of issue or shift in meaning from talk about common grounds in R4675 to talk about common principles in R4678. Rather, it apparently serves to qualify Kant's thought about the common ground of order in appearances. Granted such a ground or principle must itself present the appropriate unity and presumably the attendant numerical oneness—as we have already seen in R4675. But, as we learn from R4678, it must also find itself in the state of, or be engaged in the activity of, disposition, whatever that turns out to be more concretely. To the extent that it is in this state, or engaged in this activity, it will be possible to establish order and unity among appearances.

The one disadvantage of reading the passage in the way I have been suggesting is that it ultimately makes the passage uninformative. Kant seems to be saying that appearances cannot be ordered, if there is no way to dispose them, i.e., to order them. This is the result of tying the meaning of 'Disposition' directly to order and arrangement. Perhaps Kant intended the word to be so construed. But before drawing that conclusion, it is important to realize that there may be another way to construe 'Disposition' that weakens the ordering connotations. This will be the result, if we take the word to be a substantival form of über etwas disponieren—another Germanization of the same Latin verb, which in this case means: to have, to have at one's command or disposal—in short, a synonym of über etwas verfügen. If we construe Kant's words in this light, he can be understood to say something a little bit more nuanced, namely that appearances cannot be ordered, unless they can be put at the mind's disposal or unless they can somehow make themselves available to it. I prefer this reading, in spite of the fact that the construal of Disposition as a substantival form of über etwas disponieren might seem a little artificial. For there is a parallel passage in R4677 (17.658.19–24), which I will discuss shortly, in which Kant specifically uses über etwas disponieren to make the very point I just indicated, and, indeed, in such a way as to make the philosophical significance of the point quite plain. The sympathetic resonances in the language and ideas of the two passages favour the second construal of Disposition in the passage at hand.[^68]

However we decide to read the passage at hand, the use of 'Disposition' is not going to alter the fundamental association Kant apparently wants to make between the unity of the mind or thinking subject and the common ground of order or unity in appearances. It serves merely to qualify Kant's ideas about this association. The second way of construing 'Disposition' qualifies things by laying a condition on the common ground: given that the mind has the requisite unity to be our common ground, it must have some kind of jurisdiction over appearances, for otherwise it will not be able to establish order among them.

The passages we have been examining show that the parallel between the thinking subject in the Duisburg Nachlaß and God in the earlier cosmological writings runs deep. This can be seen from Kant's remarks in R4675 and elsewhere about the necessity of a common ground for any objective aggregate and the unity of the thinking subject as fulfilling that condition. The question now is what to make of this parallel. The best way to approach this difficult question is to begin by considering some of the important differences between the thinking subject in the Duisburg Nachlaß and the divine first cause of the earlier cosmological writings.

### §2e Peculiarities of the Duisburg Nachlaß

It would be a mistake to assimilate Kant's reflections in the Duisburg Nachlaß so closely to those of the inaugural dissertation as to overlook the significant differences between thinking subjects and divine first causes, especially those related to their role as common ground. One obvious difference is that a divine first cause is supposed to be a necessary being, while thinking subjects are contingent. But there is at least one other difference—a more significant one for our purposes. A divine first cause creates the manifold substances out of which it forms a whole world. The thinking subject cannot: the manifold is just the effect of objects acting on its senses. I propose now to think through the implications of this important difference.

Precisely because a first cause creates its own manifold, it establishes a certain relation with its creatures, namely that of radical dependence. Because of this relation, the manifold of created substances is entirely at the creator's disposal: the divine first cause can do to them, what it cannot do to the creatures of any other divine first cause, namely whatever it pleases. But since the thinking subject does not create its manifold, the manifold does not depend on it at all, and this raises a question that does not arise in the case of a divine first cause. How does the manifold come to be at its disposal in the first place?

The first part of Kant's answer to this question in the Duisburg Nachlaß seems to turn on the thinking subject's capacity to take notice of appearances and its own representations of them in inner sense. This can be seen most clearly to start out with in Kant's remarks about perception. In R4679, he says that 'empirical intuition is appearance' (17.663.32) and that perception is 'appearance whereof one is conscious' (17.664.1). In R4681, he says that 'we only perceive something if we are conscious of our apprehension and hence of existence in our inner sense . . . ' (17.667.27–28).[^69] Every empirical intuition or appearance is given to us in time and therefore inner sense (17.656.19), but an implication of Kant's remarks is that not every one of them is conscious. Only those are conscious whose 'existence in inner sense' we have noted or 'apprehended'; and these, but only these, will count as perceptions. Now having perceptions is a necessary condition for having a manifold at one's disposal. Hence, the act of consciously apprehending appearances, as they are given to us in inner sense, is at least the first part of Kant's answer to our question.

More of the story emerges from other passages in the Duisburg Nachlaß. In fact, we have already examined one of these passages in R4678 (17.660.6–16): the passage in which I argued that 'Disposition' might be taken as a substantival form of über etwas disponieren. Rather than revisit that passage, let us examine the clearest of the passages relevant to our present purposes:

> If we intuited intellectually, there would be no need of any title of apprehension to represent an object. For then the said object would not appear at all. Now appearance [stricken: is, has] must be subjected [stricken: to a ground] to a function, whereby it is at the mind's disposal. (R4677—17.658.19–24)

The interest here to start out with is that Kant says explicitly in the third sentence of the passage, as I have translated it, that appearances must be at the mind's disposal. Moreover, he addresses the question how this is possible: appearances must be subject to something he calls a 'function'. What a function is, or might be, is obscure for now. But we learn something important from Kant's remarks in the opening sentence of the passage I just quoted. These remarks turn on the contrast between a mind capable of intellectual intuition and our own.

I argued in §1d that, for Kant in the Duisburg Nachlaß, our mind has an intellectual intuition of itself, but not of things other than itself. A mind capable of intellectual intuition of things other than itself would never have to deal with appearances to represent an object. For it would have direct insight into things as they are in themselves, presumably by generating its own manifold by the very power of its intellect. Such a mind would have this in common with the divine first causes of the inaugural dissertation: there would be no question how the relevant manifold can be at its disposal or how it could have knowledge of this manifold, since such a manifold would be radically dependent on it. The inference is that such a mind would need no 'title of apprehension'. The German word 'Titel', like its English cognate 'title', has a legal connotation: I have a right to something or jurisdiction over it, if I have title to it. Thus 'Titel' is a complement to 'Disposition' in its different forms. Anything to which I have legal title is legally at my command or disposal. By extension, a 'title of apprehension' is just whatever puts at my legal disposal those appearances of which I am conscious. The word 'function' comes into play when we ask for more about these titles of apprehension: 'Appearance must be subjected to a function whereby it is at the mind's disposal.'

As odd as it may sound, the word 'function' has no technical meaning at all for Kant: neither in the Duisburg Nachlaß, nor in the Critique of Pure Reason. It means nothing more than the actual doing of whatever it is I need or want to do. If I need or want to wash the dirty dishes in my sink, my function is not my resolution to wash the dishes. Nor is it perhaps the promise or agreement with you to carry out the task. Nor is it even the task as such: as it might be described, say, in a job description. It is the actual carrying out of the task: right here, right now—with dish soap and running water. Kant's use of the German 'Funktion' is thus very close to the Latin 'functio'. That has an important consequence for getting straight what Kant is trying to say. Though the word has no technical meaning and can thus appear with no change of meaning in writings of very different perspective, like the Duisburg Nachlaß and the first Critique, it is extremely abstract. It covers any kind of doing or carrying out: from the actual business of doing my dishes to the actual business of forming a judgement about something. I will not defend my claim here. I will do so in §2d of Chapter Three where I discuss at length the meaning of 'Funktion' in the Transcendental Deduction and elsewhere in the Transcendental Analytic. For now, I would just like to point out first that, in R4676, Kant uses the expression 'actus' (at 17.656.20), which is Latin for the doing or performing of a thing, after first having written 'function'. That is circumstantial evidence, especially since 'function' is crossed out. But it is suggestive. I would also like to point out that Kant has a good reason to use this very word in the Duisburg Nachlaß. The thinking subject does not get title to the manifold without exercising it. It (the thinking subject) is not unlike a legal person who, under certain conditions, will lose title to a piece of property if that person fails to develop it in a certain time. The word 'function' would refer, in the case of such a person, to the doing of whatever must be done to retain title to the property. In the case of the thinking subject, it refers to the doing of whatever must be done to retain title to the manifold. This is left rather vague: either because Kant is not yet sure what to say about it, or because he does not think that the details matter much. But he does not leave it completely indeterminate.

In fact, our discussion in §1c about the exposition of appearances gives us the one detail that does seem important. We saw that exposition in Kant's sense is the 'setting out' of relations among appearances in just the same way that the ekthesis in the proof of a proposition in classical geometry 'sets out' a special case of whatever is to be proved. The ekthesis in any such proof is always associated with a general statement or enunciation. So too the exposition of appearances. The problem is just that the relations among appearances 'set out' by exposition are ultimately associated with relations of the thinking subject. Those relations are not as such universal in the same, precise way as the enunciation of a proposition in classical geometry: they are not discursively formed, general statements that are true for a whole class of things. Hence, something must be done: a concept of each of these relations must be formed, and this concept must be elaborated as a universal law applicable to all relations among appearances that follow the pattern of the relevant relation of the thinking subject. In the case of the thinking subject's relation of ground (to its own representations as consequence?), the universal law will be a principle of causality that governs the relation of succession among appearances. In the case of the thinking subject's relation of whole (in its relation to its representations as forming an aggregate?), the universal law will be a principle of reciprocal determination, like the peculiar restatements of the principle of coexistence we found in R4675 (17.648.28–30; 17.649.12–14) that presumably govern the relation of simultaneous coexistence among appearances. Kant characterizes the 'principle of the exposition of appearances' at the beginning of R4674 as a 'representation of the inner operation of the mind' that yields 'universality'. This leads him to say: 'Thus it is not forms, but rather functions upon which rest the relations of appearances. The exposition of appearances is thus the determining of the ground upon which rests the hanging together [Zusammenhang] of sensations in the same [sc. relations?—AL]' (17.643.19–23). The functions at issue are the actual doing of just the thing that must be done, namely the expressing of the relations of the thinking subject as the universal laws of which relations among appearances are special cases. By implication, the relations of the thinking subject are not themselves functions, as I once said in print[^70]—and as Wolfgang Carl has claimed.[^71]

I should also point out here that Kant sometimes uses the word 'exponent'. I take this expression to be a synonym for 'function': it is an active participle of 'expono', the verb that corresponds to 'expositio'. An 'exponent' is whatever carries out the exposition of appearances. That describes what a function is, because a function expresses a relation of the thinking subject as a universal law governing relations among appearances. Without that universal law, there can be no exposition, because there can be no 'setting out' of relations among appearances as a special case of anything.[^72] This matters for understanding a passage we have already discussed, but deserves a second look in this context. It is the passage from R4674 that characterizes apperception as 'the perception of one's self as of a thinking subject as such' (17.647.14–15). The word 'function' does not appear in this passage. But there is mention of 'acts of thinking', 'operations of thinking', and 'exponents' in ways that suggest the meaning of 'function', as I just laid it out. The passage reads as follows:

> Apperception is the consciousness of thought, i.e., of representations as they are put in the mind. Herewith are three exponents: 1.of the relation to the subject, 2.of the relation of following among one another, 3.of taking together. The determining of [the concept—AL] A in these moments of apperception is the subsumption under one of these acts of thinking. One cognizes [added: it as in itself determinable and hence objective, namely] the concept A, when one brings it under one of these universal operations of thinking, by means of which it comes under a rule. Such a statement is a principle of the rule, hence of the knowledge of appearance through the understanding whereby appearance is considered as something objective . . . . (17.647.16–26)

Note first that the exponents mentioned in this passage are not themselves relations of the thinking subject. Kant says, in effect, that, to each relation, there corresponds an exponent. He apparently refers to these exponents when he speaks of 'these acts of thinking' and 'these universal operations of thinking'. This confirms that the exponents are the doing of something by the mind engaged in thinking. What is that mind doing? It brings the concept A under a rule and thus makes possible knowledge of appearances through the understanding such that appearance may be 'considered as something objective'. The word 'objective' suggests that the understanding acquires knowledge of appearances by representing an object. We know from an earlier passage in R4674, which was the focus of our discussion in §1a–c, that it can represent an object 'only according to its relations'. Thus the exponents or acts of thinking at issue here are just functions or operations of the mind that actually carry out the exposition by appearances by stating the general rule of which their relations are special cases.

If we step back from the details of these passages, we can see now that the three functions, exponents, or acts of thinking that correspond to the relations of the thinking subject enumerated in the passage just quoted from R4674 are precisely the way that the thinking subject gains title to, or takes command, of the manifold. They are what enable the understanding to convert appearances into experience, i.e., to produce a world-like whole out of appearances. Without them, the thinking subject would possess the unity characteristic of a single, simple thinking substance. But that unity, all by itself, is just a necessary condition for the worldhood of experience. The worldhood of experience requires, in addition to the unity of the thinking subject, that the thinking subject take command of appearances. It does so through its functions. The appeal to functions in the Duisburg Nachlaß completes the special adaption of Kant's early cosmology to the new account of appearance. It supplies the thinking subject with a human or finite analogue of God's power to unite his creatures into a real world-whole that exhibits true, universal harmony.

### §2f Looking ahead

Our plan now is to begin in earnest our examination of the B-Deduction. We will discover at least two important changes or innovations. One of these will be a new account of pure apperception that sets aside assumptions about the metaphysical nature of the thinking subject. That innovation will bring with it the need to carry out an exposition of myself as an appearance. As we will see in Chapter Five, such an exposition is the theme of the second half of §24 and the whole of §25 in the B-Deduction. It will turn out that I cannot know or represent myself, except according to my external relations, which means that I must 'set myself out' in time, just as I set out bodies or things other than myself. Another innovation will be a recognition of the need to think carefully about the relation between the understanding and imagination. The Duisburg Nachlaß has nothing to say about this, which is surprising given the role of ekthesis in Kant's thinking about experience in the mid-1770s. It is obvious, on reflection, that imagination must have a role to play in the ekthesis of a proof in classical geometry, just because the 'setting out' of the enunciation includes a labelled diagram. If the conversion of appearances into experience is anything at all like an ekthesis in the original sense, imagination must contribute something like the labelled diagram in a geometrical proof. But how is that supposed to work? We will consider this question in §2 of Chapter Four.

We will also find that, however important the innovations I just mentioned, the B-Deduction is very conservative. It hangs on to two things at least from the Duisburg Nachlaß. One of these has just been indicated, namely the idea that experience is something like the ekthesis of a proof in classical geometry: it sets out appearances as specifications of the universal laws of nature. But the second thing that is taken over from the Duisburg Nachlaß by the B-Deduction is the idea that the effect of converting appearances into experience by exposition is to produce a world. The sensible world in the B-Deduction—like that in the Duisburg Nachlaß—is based on principles taken from the cosmology of Kant's early metaphysics and carefully adapted for the purposes at hand. This is not so obvious in the first part of the B-Deduction: the part that runs from §15 to §21. But as I will argue in §2 and §3 of Chapter Four, the cosmological overtones can be heard clearly in §26 of the B-Deduction. I will argue in the conclusion of this book that the cosmology of experience whose foundation is laid in §26 gets completed in the System of Principles. This will mean revisiting the Analogies of Experience. The conclusion of this book and §1a of this chapter may thus be thought to serve as book ends, so to speak, for all the intervening chapters.

[^66]: One question left over from all this is what Kant means at the end of this passage by 'subordination' and 'coordination'. He says that 'from thence [sc. from either apperception or its condition, the unity of the thinking subject] flows the connection [added: of the manifold] according to a rule and in a whole, because [stricken: as much] the unity of the function must suffice as much for subordination as for coordination.' 'Subordination' and 'coordination' are terms that appear in the inaugural dissertation, and indeed in a passage in Section One on the notion of a world in general. I believe that we can take this passage of the dissertation as a clue to the meaning of the terms in the passage we have been considering from the Duisburg Nachlaß. The passage in the inaugural dissertation reads as follows: 'The FORM [of a world], which consists in the coordination of substances, not in their subordination. For things coordinated reciprocally relate to one another as complements to the whole, whereas things subordinated are related to one another as caused and cause, or generally, as principle and principled. The former relation is reciprocal and homonymous so that any correlate is related to the other as both determining and determined. But the latter relation is heteronymous, being certainly from the one side nothing but a relation of dependence, while from the other a relation of causality. This coordination [which constitutes the form of a world] is conceived as real and objective, not as ideal and depending on the mere will of a subject, through which you may form a whole by summing any manifold you wish. For by embracing many things together, you effect with no difficulty a whole of representations, but not thereby the representation of a whole' (2.390.5–15). It is quite clear from this passage that things are coordinated with one another if they mutually and reciprocally relate to one another in such a way as to form a real, objective (world-) whole, whereas they are subordinated to one another if they stand in the relation of ground and consequence to one another. If this passage from the inaugural dissertation is any guide to understanding our passage in the Duisburg Nachlaß, and I believe that it is, Kant may be understood to say here just what we expect him to say in light of everything else we have seen him say in R4675, namely that the unity of the thinking subject is the ultimate necessary condition for converting a manifold into a unified whole where we represent all of the parts as coordinated with one another, i.e., related reciprocally to one another in the way just indicated, and where we also represent some of them at least as subordinated to one another as caused to cause. Hence the occurrence of the seemingly mysterious terms 'coordination' and 'subordination' does not indicate a shift away from the cognitive-cosmological considerations in the spirit of the inaugural dissertation, which have so far typified R4675. Quite the contrary, it confirms our finding that such considerations are at the heart of what is going on—at least in R4675, and indicates, moreover, that the unity of the thinking subject has a central role to play in such considerations.
[^67]: A word of caution is in order here. There is a lot of talk of unity in this passage. The word 'unity' occurs no less than six times. But it seems the word can be used in at least two different ways. First of all, it may be used with respect to the mind or the thinking subject itself. I surmise that this is the case when Kant speaks of 'the unity of the power of representation': I take it that 'power of representation' and 'mind' are synonymous. But the word 'unity' can also be used with respect to appearances and representations, as distinct from the mind in/to which they are present. Thus Kant says in the third sentence that appearances themselves must have unity, i.e., they must have unity in the mind and must therefore be subject to the conditions whereby the unity of representations is possible. (It seems that the unity appearances have in the mind and the unity of representations are, if not exactly the same, then very closely related things.) I take it that this twofold use of 'unity' corresponds precisely to Kant's concern here and in parallel passages in R4675 to specify the necessary condition for any unified whole of appearances (i.e., the unity of appearances in the mind or unity of representations, as Kant puts it here). In the passage under consideration, as in our previous R4675 passage, the sought after necessary condition seems to be the unity of the mind, i.e., the unity of the power of representation, as Kant puts it here. We can see this by taking the third sentence together with the second (incomplete) sentence. In the third sentence, Kant stipulates that there must be some condition through which the unity of appearances in the mind is possible. Kant seems actually to specify the needed condition in the second sentence. (This is suggested by the 'for' which opens the third sentence and by the way that the third sentence seems to refer back to the second; indeed, it would not have much content if it did not refer back to the previous sentence.) Thus we learn from the second sentence that appearances will have order and presumably unity in the mind to the extent that they belong to the unity of the power of representations and are thus in accordance with the so-called common principle of disposition.
