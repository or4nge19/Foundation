## 4 A Cosmology of Experience

### §26 of the B-Deduction

### §1 Henrich’s Challenge: What Does the Second Part
of the B-Deduction Contribute to the Argument
as a Whole?
A natural question is raised by the ‘proof-structure’ of the Transcendental Deduction
in the second edition of the first Critique: do we have one argument with two distinct
steps or two distinct arguments for the same conclusion? This is a question, as Dieter
Henrich pointed out in 1969, because by the time Kant reaches the final conclusion in
§26, he seems to give us nothing more than a restatement of the conclusion already
reached in § 20.[^1] Thus we learn from §26that the categories are ‘conditions of the
possibility of experience and so hold a priori even for all objects of experience ’
(B161), but from § 20we are supposed to have concluded already that the categories
are possible as ‘a priori cognitions of an intuition as such ’ (B159). One could think
that these two statements differ only verbally, which would mean, if true, that Kant
has given us two arguments for the same conclusion —a thought apparently con-
firmed by the fact that he speaks in the first lines of §26as though the Transcendental
Deduction had already been carried out in §20 (or rather in §20 and §21 taken
together). But it is hard to see what would have motivated such a proof strategy. It
looks bad when a philosopher offers you more than one argument for the same
conclusion—as if to invite you to pick the one you happen to like best. For this
reason alone, we would prefer to think that the Transcendental Deduction in the
second edition of the first Critique is, in fact, a single argument in two distinct
steps. But then, as Henrich pointed out, the challenge is to say what the second step
contributes to the whole argument, over and beyond the first step. I believe that an
important clue, for responding to this challenge, is found at the end of the second
step of the argument where Kant characterizes what he ultimately expects to
achieve. At the beginning of § 26, the section where all the pieces are supposed to
fall together, he says this:

> Now the thing to be explained is the possibility, by means of the categories, of knowing a priori the objects that might appear only to our senses, and indeed not in accordance with the form of their intuition, but rather in accordance with the laws of their combination [Verbindung], and thus of laying down, as it were, the law for nature and even of making nature possible. (B159–160)[^2]

[^1]: Henrich,‘The Proof-Structure of Kant ’s Transcendental Deduction ’, in the Review of Metaphysics ,
volume 22, number 4 (1969).

This passage is significant because it announces that an attempt to answer the
question how nature is possible will be the final step of the deduction.
Kant characterizes this question in § 36of the Prolegomena as ‘the highest point
that transcendental philosophy can ever conceivably touch upon and to which it
must also be led, as its limit and completion ’ (4.318.3–5). He thereby confers such
weight upon the question as to make more plausible still the idea suggested by the
passage I quoted from B159–160, namely that answering this very question is the task
of the second step of the B-Deduction, and thus the B-Deduction as a whole.
Moreover, Prolegomena §36helps clarify what is going on in § 26of the B-Deduction
by specifying two different ways in which we can state the question, depending on the
two-fold sense of the word ‘nature’. Thus it can help us see which sense of the word is
at issue in the final step of the B-Deduction and hence which statement of the
question the B-Deduction is ultimately trying to answer.
We learn from Prolegomena §36first of all that ‘nature’ in the material sense is just
the totality of all appearances. If we ask how nature is possible in this sense, the
answer will turn on the claim from the Transcendental Aesthetic that space and time
are not things in themselves but the pure forms of our sensibility: appearances are
given to us in space and time, so construed, if they are given to us at all (4.318.7–15).
But we learn second of all that ‘nature’ in the formal sense has to do with appearances
as governed by laws so as to form some kind of unified whole. It is characterized in
the Prolegomena as ‘the totality of rules under which all appearances must stand if
they are to be thought of as joined in one experience’(4.318.16–18). Kant conspicu-
ously uses ‘experience’ in the singular here. He should be understood to concur with
his remark in the A-version of the Deduction: ‘There is only one experience in which
all perceptions are represented as in thoroughgoing and law-bound connection
[Zusammenhang]. . . . If we speak of experience in the plural, then what we really
mean is just so many perceptions, insofar as such perceptions belong to one and the
same universal experience ’ (A110). ‘One and the same universal experience ’ is the
target of our question how nature is possible in the formal sense. But it does not
figure in either the asking or the answering of the question how nature is possible
in the material sense. For the answer to this question in the Transcendental
Aesthetic will only explain how things can appear to us. It is at least possible for
things to appear to us without our being able to situate them relative to one
another in ‘one and the same universal experience ’, i.e., as parts of one and the
same law-governed phenomenal world. A natural implication is that the conditions
of possibility of nature in the ‘formal sense ’ do not lie in our sensibility, which
means that they must lie in our understanding. But this is a problem, because the

that appearances do conform to the ‘conditions of unity ’ of our understanding and
hence, as we learn from the Prolegomena, that these conditions make nature
possible in the formal sense. It must show, in other words, that they make possible
the totality of rules that unify appearances and thereby convert them into nature as
a composite of matter and form, i.e., ‘one and the same universal experience ’.
In Prolegomena §36, Kant says that the answer to the question of the possibility of
‘nature’ in the formal sense can be found in the ‘Transcendental Logic ’ of the first
Critique. Since the Transcendental Deduction is only a small part of the Transcen-
dental Logic as a whole, the Deduction as such can contribute only a part of the
answer. The natural thing to think is that it contributes the ‘in principle part of
the story’: it will show that, at least in principle, the understanding has what it takes to
make nature possible in the formal sense; the details will be more fully specified in the
‘System of Principles ’—especially in the ‘Analogies of Experience ’ (cf. B262–263).[^5]
These suggestions are confirmed by the remarks we started with from B159–160 at
the beginning of § 26of the B-Deduction where Kant says, as we recall, that the thing
to be explained is how, by means of the categories, we can know appearances a priori,
‘not in accordance with the form of their intuition ’, which we now see would only
explain how nature is possible in the material sense, ‘but rather in accordance with
the laws of their combination [ Verbindung]’, which —as Kant himself says in the
continuation of this passage —is to explain how the understanding makes nature
possible, but more specifically in the formal sense, as we now see from Prolegomena
§36. Our passage at B159–160 does not make explicit use of the distinction between
‘nature’ in the material sense and ‘nature’ in the formal sense. But the final stretch of
§26, from B163 to B165, does. That passage opens with the claim that ‘appearances
and hence nature as the totality of all appearances ( natura materialiter spectata )’
(B163) receive laws a priori from the categories. It ends with the following pro-
nouncement: ‘hence, all possible perceptions and thus everything that can ever reach
empirical consciousness, i.e., all appearances of nature, according to its [sc. nature ’s—
AL] combination, stand under the categories, upon which depends nature (considered
merely as nature), as upon the original ground of its necessary lawfulness (as natura
formaliter spectata)’ (B164–165). ‘Natura materialiter spectata’ and ‘natura formaliter
spectata’ are just the Latin equivalents for ‘nature considered in a material way ’ and
‘nature considered in a formal way ’. I take ‘nature considered merely as nature ’ to
mean the composite of matter and form, i.e., ‘one and the same universal experience ’
unified by the totality of rules made possible by the understanding.
The preoccupation with nature and the problem of explaining how it is possible
in the formal sense is something new and peculiar to the second step of the

First of all, he speaks of it as consisting in perceptions —a special class of ‘given

accompanied by empirical consciousness. Second of all, he says that these percep-

such perceptions can indeed constitute one, single, universal experience. But noth-

includes different items of empirical knowledge that can each take the form of a
judgement, and hence that each item of empirical knowledge will present a certain
kind of unity at a minimum, namely the objective unity he associates with the form
of a judgement as such (B 473–474). But the first step of the B-Deduction did not
give us any reason to think that the judgements expressing our different items of
empirical knowledge collectively form some kind of unity amongst themselves —
much less a unity that we would be willing to characterize as ‘nature’. That is at least
part of what apparently remains to be shown in the second step of the argument.
One way to capture what is distinctive about the second step of the B-Deduction
is to note that Kant ’s reflections in § 26are quite straightforwardly cosmological.
The talk, of course, is about universal experience and the laws that govern it. We
saw that this talk can be translated in terms of ‘nature’ in the formal sense. But
Kant’s talk in these terms is an adaptation of his earlier general cosmology for the
purposes of the critical philosophy.
The early Kant believed that general cosmology should be one of the special
branches of metaphysics and that its task should be to develop an account, from
first principles, of the nature of a world in general. But he argued, in effect, that such
an account would be inadequate if it characterized a world only as a whole that is not
itself a part of another whole. For that would not distinguish the totality of all there is
from a world as such. A world is not merely an ultimate aggregate or heap of all
existing things; it is a whole that exhibits unity by virtue of the fact that its parts are
subject to laws of universal interaction such that each and every one is related to
every other. This insight should be understood as the ancestor of the distinction in
meaning of the word ‘nature’ in §36of the Prolegomena. We might say on behalf of
the early Kant, but using the language of his later self, that the totality of all the finite
things God created is a world, but only in the material sense, while the laws he laid
down to allow for the external relations among these things, as in a single, unified
whole, is a world in the formal sense. The characterization of a world as a whole that

We can see that this must be the answer to our question when we recall the way
Kant tries to convince the reader that a transcendental deduction of the categories is
necessary. He makes the simple observation that the categories ‘do not present us
with the conditions under which objects are given in intuition ’ (B455). Appearances
cannot fail to conform to these conditions, because they are precisely the conditions
under which things appear to us, if they appear to us at all. They are the conditions of
our sensibility. But the conditions of our sensibility are not categories, which implies
that objects can appear to us without as such having to conform to the requirements
of the understanding: ‘Whence a difficulty emerges here that we did not encounter in
the field of sensibility, namely how subjective conditions of thinking are supposed to
have objective validity , i.e., yield conditions of the possibility of all knowledge of
objects’ (B455). This difficulty can be reformulated as the punchline I quoted earlier
(in §4), which I now quote once more with the continuation of the passage:
appearances might perhaps be so constituted that the understanding could find in them
nothing conformable to the conditions of its unity and everything would lie in such confusion
that, for example, nothing would offer itself up in the succession of appearances that would
serve as a rule of synthesis and thereby correspond to the concept of cause and effect so that
this concept would be thus quite empty, null and without meaning. (A 123/B456)
The worry concerns not only the concept of cause and effect. How can we be sure that
any of the categories have command over appearances? One could perhaps imagine
finding evidence, say, that some appearances conform to some of the categories, but
not to others, or to all of them, but only under peculiar conditions. That would get us
isolated pockets of unity among appearances, but we would still worry that rogue
appearances can evade the rule of the categories and so persist in a state of lawless
confusion. We could not ease such worries until we had established a thoroughgoing
unity from which no appearance would be exempt. But that would require giving the
categories (and hence the understanding as such) complete command over all
possible appearances. That in turn would bring into play a complete totality of
appearances under a special title of the understanding. [^8] The disposition of the new
totality would not yet be clear to us, for it will depend on what exactly the relevant
title permits and what the understanding and its lieutenants may (and do) legitim-
ately command. But if indeed we put to rest our worry about the categories, the title
itself will be secured and hence the scope of the totality will be universal and all-

But the thought sketched in the previous paragraph must be spelled out more fully.
This may be done, at least programmatically, by elaborating first of all the compari-
son between God and our understanding that is naturally suggested by Kant ’s
appropriation of general cosmology for epistemic purposes, and already touched
upon in earlier chapters.
In the Critique of Pure Reason , Kant no longer takes for granted that general
cosmology is possible. Indeed, he will argue at length in the Transcendental Dialectic
that it is vulnerable to different kinds of antinomies. The problem of the Transcen-
dental Deduction is not what God can do to make a world out of the things he has
created; it is rather what human understanding can do to construe anything given to
our sensibility as belonging to ‘one and the same universal experience ’. It will thus be
natural to stress the important and familiar differences. We will say that God ’s
intellect not only designs the world, it also brings it into existence; we will also say
that our own understanding is not creative, but rather discursive. It forms judge-
ments about the objects of its knowledge, but only if it has received a manifold of
intuition under the conditions of our sensibility. Thus we may understand the
Transcendental Deduction as a reflection on human cognitive finitude. Our finitude
is marked by the fact that we have two, radically different faculties of knowledge:
understanding and sensibility —the one a faculty for thinking; the other a faculty for
sensing. That we can have knowledge of objects is possible only if these two faculties
cooperate. Kant himself explicitly draws a related contrast early in the B-Deduction,
namely that between a finite discursive understanding like our own and an intuitive
understanding capable of generating its own manifold through itself or through the
mere thought or consciousness of itself (B 468,B 164–165). There is nothing wrong
with saying these things. They happen to be true. But they are not everything there is
to say about the matter. Even for Kant in 1781, it is also true, with the proper
qualifications, that we were made in God ’s image.
In the first place, we may compare the target of our human understanding and that
of its divine counterpart. By ‘target’, I mean that towards which they are directed or
that which they would ultimately take as their object, were it possible for them to do
so. I claim that they have the same target —with certain qualifications. The target of
God’s intellect is all of creation. But so too for ours.
To be sure, we cannot even aim at things in themselves, because they are as good as
nothing for us; by the same token, things as they appear to us are as good as nothing
for God. For since he presumably generates his own manifold out of himself, he is
never confronted by appearances. That means he could never know what it is like for
us to know the things that we know —not without the mystery of the incarnation.
Moreover, God grasps the whole of creation all at once; we cannot. We cannot even
address the most fundamental questions about it without contradiction: whether it is
finite in space and time; whether it has ultimate parts or not, etc.? Those are the

qualifications, and they are significant enough that one may well doubt that anything
interesting is left of my claim. Nevertheless, Kant denies that we can take interest in
even just one or two appearances unless we can situate them in as much of the ‘one,
universal experience ’ as we can construct in a finite time interval. This could seem
overblown: do I have any care at all for the ‘one, universal experience’ if my interest is
arrested by a couple of mice in the pantry? The answer, I think, must be, Yes. I can tell
what they are, where they are, when they are active, and the circumstances under
which I might lure them into a trap. That presupposes a backdrop of knowledge and
assumptions otherwise unavailable to me were I not able to conceive of my rodent
guests and myself as nearly neighbouring parts of one and the same phenomenal
world. Moreover, were I not able to find my way in this world, the planning and
timing required to capture them would be impossible. Success in my venture will not
require that I work out the position of the mice relative to that in the night-time sky
of Sirius or Orion ’s Belt or the Milky Way. But that is just to say that the compass of
my interest may be wide or narrow-ranging. Still, I must be able to widen it to suit my
purposes, because the focus of my interest can vary. That means that my under-
standing is directed—at least potentially—at everything that could possibly appear to
us in one and the same phenomenal world: mouse or meteor, if it can appear to me,
my understanding must have title to it. Granted, we cannot ultimately complete in
thought our construction of the whole phenomenal world. But there can be no
empirical thinking or knowledge if our understanding is not at least directed towards
it—the whole of it, at least in principle. What we strive for and what God immedi-
ately grasps are thus the same in scope, namely the whole of what is or can come
before our senses.
We and he have a second thing in common: we are both directed at the whole of
what is or can appear as a legislator. God converts the totality of things he created
into a world by subjecting them to universal laws of community. The Critique
imposes upon us a cognitive duality. We are an epistemic pairing of sensibility and
understanding; hence we are not legislators through and through. The Critique
withholds the capacity to legislate from the former, which yields the conditions of
all possible appearances, i.e., the principle of nature in the material sense, as we learn
from § 36of the Prolegomena . But since they can do nothing to give us nature in
the formal sense, the capacity to legislate falls to the latter. It falls to the under-
standing to provide the ground for the un iversal laws under which all possible
appearances must stand if they are to be regarded as belonging to one and the
same universal experience. Just as God had to legislate universal laws of commu-
nity to form a world out of his creatures, so the understanding has to legislate
universal laws to appearances in order to give us nature in the formal sense and
thus nature as a composi te of matter and form. Kant has to be aware of this
parallel. It seems to underlie his characterization of the understanding at the very
end of the A-Deduction:
We characterized the understanding above in many different ways: as a spontaneity of
knowledge (by contrast with the receptivity of sensibility), as a faculty for thinking, and also
as a faculty of concepts or also one of judgements, which characterizations —when examined
more closely—come to the same thing. Now we can even characterize it as the faculty of rules .
This characterization is more fruitful and comes closer to the truth. Sensibility gives us forms

        
(of intuition); understanding, rules. The latter is always busy surveying appearances in order to
seek out rules among them. Rules, insofar as they are objective (and thus necessarily adhere to
knowledge of the object), are called laws. Although we learn many laws through experience,
these laws are still only particular determinations of higher laws still, among which the highest
(under which all others stand) proceed a priori from the understanding and are not borrowed
from experience, but rather provide appearances with their lawfulness and, precisely in this
way, make experience possible. Thus the understanding is not merely a faculty that makes rules
for itself by comparing appearances; it is itself the source of legislation for nature .⁹(A459)
Kant’s claim, in both versions of the Deduction, that the understanding gives laws to
nature is astonishing, on the face of it. But not in light of the general cosmology from
which it proceeded and his apparent willingness to treat God ’s relation to the world
as a paradigm for the relation of our understanding to nature. It is perhaps a sign of
how significant Kant took this paradigm to be for evaluating our power of judgement
that it is in evidence not only in both versions of the Transcendental Deduction, but
also in the Groundwork for the Metaphysics of Morals where the test of our maxims
for contradiction in the will depends on seeing whether, in acting on the relevant
maxim, we can imagine ourselves as legislating laws to a world that is practically
viable as a world.
Still another angle suggests itself from which to elaborate programmatically my
claim that  is just the thing required to complete the
Transcendental Deduction. It has to do with Kant ’s conception of knowledge and
its relation to its object. This conception is important, because if the Deduction is
successful, it will have explained how the relevant relation, conceived in the relevant
way, is possible: we will conclude that it is made possible by the categories. As we
learned in Chapter Two, by the end of § 6as a whole, Kant ’s conception of this
relation is at work in § 16of the B-Deduction. But I now want to suggest that it is
incomplete or inoperative in § 16unless paired with Kant ’s conception of our
understanding as a legislator.
I argued in Chapter Two, by the end of § 6as a whole, that Kant ’s conception of
knowledge and its relation to its object in § 16of the B-Deduction is motivated by a
polemical animus against a rival conception. The rival conception claims that the
object is completely independent of knowledge. This claim is supposed to ensure
that knowledge is not wishful thinking, but how knowers and learners can engage
with the object is mysterious. Proponents of the claim may well assume that we
satisfy ourselves that we know our object when we compare what we think we know
about it with the object itself. But it is hard to see how we can make such a
comparison, if the object is independent of everything we know: it is just a some-
thing = X, we know not what. Kant may be understood to correct this problem, in
both versions of the Deduction, by denying that the object of knowledge as such is
independent of knowledge. This does not make knowledge wishful thinking, for it
turns out that knowers and learners are constrained in what they say about the
object by the evidence, i.e., by whatever they already know about it. This makes the
object—as an object of knowledge —an inevitable byproduct of knowledge. How will

I establish that I know something about the dodecahedron: for example, that it can
be inscribed in a sphere? It won ’t be by bouncing one off your forehead. It will be by
mustering Euclid ’s elements —the relevant items of prior knowledge —and success-

The brief review in the previous paragraph of the lesson learned in Chapter Two,
by the end of § 6as a whole, will now help us confront Kant ’s conception, in both
versions of the Transcendental Deduction, of knowledge and its relation to its object
with a remark, relevant for current purposes, in the Duisburg Nachlaß. As we saw in
§1c of Chapter One, Kant writes in R4674: ‘The setting of objects for my represen-
tations is something that always requires that the representation be determined in
accordance with a universal law, for indeed the object consists in the universal point ’
(410.9711.48–410). We also saw that this is not a throwaway remark: Kant associates the
object, as an object of knowledge or representation, with laws and universality
elsewhere in the Duisburg Nachlaß. Now I argued in § 3d of Chapter Two that
Kant’s conception, in the Duisburg Nachlaß, of knowledge and its relation to its
object is aporetic —in precisely the same way as the rival conception that Kant
explicitly excoriates in the A-Deduction at A 437ff. If that is right, the Transcendental
Deduction is offering a corrective to one of Kant ’s own views. Nevertheless, the
association of the object —as an object of knowledge —and universality is not some-
thing Kant ever wished to correct. The aporia in the Duisburg Nachlaß does not lie in
this association, but rather in the assumption —or unallayed worry—that the object is
something that stands ‘over and against ’ our claims to know it. Having made that
observation, I wish to point out how striking it is that talk about laws and universality
is absent from § 16of the B-Deduction. This in turn makes plausible the suggestion
that, in the B-Deduction, Kant needs to say more about the relation between
knowledge and its object, namely that it depends on the universal laws prescribed
to nature by the understanding. But, of course, that detail is not presented until § 26.
There it falls into place only with the presentation of the cosmology of experience.
That the cosmology of experience presented in § 26of the B-Deduction is supposed
to complete the story initiated in § 16about knowledge and its relation to its object
can be confirmed by the programme and details of § 26. But a very helpful parallel
proof-passage can be found in the A-Deduction at A 444. It raises the spectre of
failure, the cost of which will be to leave us with the principles of nature in the
material sense, but without a principle of nature in the formal sense. In that case,
Kant says,
it would be possible for our soul to be filled with a swarm of appearances without experience
being able to arise therefrom. As a result, all relation of knowledge to objects would fall away,
because the connection [Verknüpfung] according to universal and necessary laws would be
missing for it ;¹⁰hence, to be sure, they would be thoughtless intuition, but never knowledge,
and thus they would be as good as nothing for us.

‘Thoughtless intuition’ all by itself will not give us any relation to an object necessary
for knowledge. But nor will any empirical concept either —at least not all by itself.
The concept Mouse and the concept Pantry do not yield knowledge of the rodent
appearances I have to contend with. Like all concepts, they are ‘universal with respect
to their form ’ (A439). But ‘universal’ can be said in many ways, and when Kant
invokes the universal, in the passage quoted above from R4674of the Duisburg
Nachlaß, as required for knowledge or representation of an object, he explicitly refers
to universal laws —as in universal laws of nature ( 410.9711.48–410). Universal laws of
nature are precisely what is at issue in the passage I just quoted from A 444, i.e., those
‘universal and necessary laws ’ that produce a necessary connection among all
appearances and thereby convert them into one and the same, universal experience.
They are what ‘set an object for our representations ’, to speak in the language of the
Duisburg Nachlaß, and thereby make it possible for concepts like Mouse and Pantry
to contribute to my knowledge. They —or rather the higher principle seated in the
understanding that makes them possible —do so by introducing necessary connec-
tions among the relevant appearances so that I can think of the mice and myself as
parts of one and the same phenomenal world. Must I, as a consequence, entertain the
thought of a direct, necessary connection between me and the mice? Perhaps, indeed,
I must. For Kant thinks that I must be able to conceive of myself as standing in some
kind of cosmological community with all possible appearances. Necessary connec-
tions of some kind, governed by some kind of universal law, will be needed to hold
this community together. Otherwise, the mice and all other possible appearances
will be as good as nothing for me. Our passage from A 444offers no further details.
But it helpfully and explicitly indicates that Kant ’s conception of knowledge and its
relation to its object, as we found elaborated in both the A-Deduction and in § 16of
the B-Deduction, needs to be supplemented by . The
supplement in the B-Deduction will be provided in § 26.
The preceding considerations help make it intelligible why Kant would think that a
cosmology of experience is needed to bring the Transcendental Deduction to a
successful conclusion. But it will do so only if Kant is really committed to thinking
of our understanding as a legislator. If that conception is just a suggestive metaphor,
the cosmology of experience will itself be little more than decorative rhetoric. That
means it cannot be an afterthought, simply grafted on to the other conception Kant
has of the understanding, namely as a faculty of thought or as a faculty for using
concepts (in judgements). In other words, the one conception must be naturally
convertible into the other; there must be a natural fit between Kant ’s account of
concept use and the cosmology of experience. But this is in fact the case, because the
use of empirical concepts, for Kant, depends ultimately on the principle of nature in
the formal sense.
Concepts are always the outcome of a synthesis, as we learn from § 49of the
B-Deduction—especially the footnote to B 467. But we should expect the principle of
nature in the formal sense to make any such synthesis possible. Two reasons for this
expectation recommend themselves. First of all, the principle at issue makes possible
a priori the necessary connection of appearances that converts them into the unified,
systematic whole that we call ‘universal experience ’. Second, all our general
concepts—if they have any Sinn und Bedeutung —make some kind of reference to

this whole. The concept of Grizzly Bear will include all those marks that we put
together in the relevant act of synthesis: ‘Brown’, ‘Big’, ‘Omnivorous’, ‘Inordinately
Fond of Salmon and Blue-Berries’, and whatever else we wish to include. But however
rich or however thin we make this concept, our synthesis will have taken place
against the backdrop of what we take to be this animal ’s habits and therefore its
habitat. There can be no coherent thought about the grizzly bear ’s habitat without
some reference to where, and under which conditions, we think it lives. This point
comes into even sharper focus when we consider, as Kant himself routinely does in —
say—the lectures on logic, that general concepts are often used for the purposes of
comparison. I might want, for example, to compare grizzlies and black bears or
grizzlies and grey wolves or grizzlies and pink flamingoes. Though the animals
I compare might well occupy very different ecological niches in very different
environments, my comparison will tacitly assume that their respective environments
are features of one and the same planet, and that I myself am somehow in the midst
of it all to discover what there is to compare or contrast. Once we start thinking along
these lines, we will soon conjure up a totality that is more than just the sum-total of
what can appear to us. We will be on the path towards the idea of a single, unified,
universal experience that embraces all of what is or can appear in a structured, law-
governed system of some kind. The only way Kant knows to account for such a
totality is to invoke an analogue of divine legislation, as elaborated in his early
cosmology. Thus he will be led to think of the understanding as a law-giver for
nature, just to the extent that he thinks of it as a faculty for thinking or as a faculty for
using concepts and framing judgements. The notion of ‘nature’ in the formal sense is
just the thing Kant will invoke as he tries to account for the conditions of possibility
of empirical knowledge—at least, once he has seen his way to recognizing that the use
of general concepts has an important role to play in this account.
At least three objections may be raised against the argument sketched in the
previous paragraph. The first is that it holds —if at all —only for empirical concepts,
but not for mathematical concepts. Why should we think that these concepts depend
on the principle of nature in the formal sense? We can surely form and make use of
the concept Parabola without making any reference to ‘one and the same universal
experience’. Now that claim may well be true, as a matter of fact, but not —I think—as
an account of Kant ’s position. Kant himself says explicitly that:
Although all the principles and the representation of the object with which [sc. mathematics —
AL] is concerned are produced in the mind completely a priori, they would nevertheless mean
[bedeuten] nothing at all if we could not always set out [ darlegen] their meaning [ Bedeutung]
upon appearances (empirical objects). Hence, one requires that even an abstract concept be
made sensible, i.e., that the object corresponding to it be set out, because, without it, the concept
would remain (as one says) without sense [Sinn], i.e., without meaning [Bedeutung]. (B 51212)¹¹
Kant goes on to say that the requirement at issue here can be met in mathematics by
the construction of figures that correspond to our concepts —at least in geometry.

Equivalent procedures can be carried out, he says, in arithmetic. We will happily
concede that such procedures, in either branch of mathematics, will make the relevant
concepts sensible, but we may still be in doubt whether making these concepts sensible
makes any kind of cosmologically loaded reference to the sensible world. I cannot
fully address this important objection here; I will present more of the story in the
conclusion to this book. For now I will just say this.
Kant will certainly remind us that, without the principle of nature in the formal
sense, appearances will not ‘conform to the understanding ’s requirement of unity ’
and everything will lie in such confusion that, though a swarm of appearances may
crowd upon our soul, they will be as good as nothing for us —‘less even than a
dream’. In such a state, how could we collect our thoughts enough to carry out any
procedure that suits our mathematical purposes? How could we even clarify to
ourselves what our mathematical purposes are? But suppose now that we have
some mathematical concepts and that they themselves have ‘sense and meaning ’.
They will then apply to ‘appearances as empirical objects ’. But these appearances
cannot have been parts of a swarm crowding upon our soul. For then there would
be no making any sense of them at all. It follows that the application of mathem-
atical concepts to appearances is a sign that appearances have been converted by
the relevant laws of nature into a world. But precisely because our mathematical
concepts apply to appearances, it also follows that the ‘sense and meaning ’ they
convey to us may be taken to give insight into the mathematical arrangement of
different parts of the world relative to one another. If, then, we agree that the
concept Grizzly Bear has cosmological overtones, then, given the assumptions in
play, we must agree that our mathematical concepts do too. This implies that the
synthesis necessary for our mathematical concepts ultimately depends on the
principle of nature in the formal sense.
The thought just expressed will surely provide an answer in outline to the second
of the three objections I am anticipating against my claim that the principle of nature
in the formal sense is the necessary condition of all thinking. This objection will
depend on observing that the empirical concepts I invoked earlier, to make my claim
plausible, were all zoological. It will hardly be a matter of controversy, so the
objection goes, that our concepts of different animal species will ultimately make
reference to their habitat and may thus be construed as referring to the sensible world
as I represent Kant as characterizing it: without making such a reference how can
I think of grizzly bears as indigenous to Alaska and the Canadian North-West
Territories? The objection will then ask whether all empirical concepts must make
such a reference. Why, for example, should we think that colour concepts do? Surely
we can speak in general terms of Pink and Green without being understood to go on
about worlds as such? What cosmological overtones are to be heard in the judgement
that pink is not a category? But here the reply will be as before: the principle of nature
in the formal sense ensures that appearances do not swarm upon my soul in such
confusion that they are less even than a dream, for otherwise I could not even begin
to collect my thoughts. All our empirical concepts, and all the judgements we might
ever form out of them, testify to the principle of nature in the formal sense: it is
indeed the condition of their possibility. Whatever ‘sense and meaning ’ they have is
thus a window —however narrow it may be —on the sensible world. As it goes for

mathematical concepts, so it goes for empirical ones —even those that have nothing
to do with zoology.
Perhaps it might now be objected, in the third place, that even if the understanding
as a faculty of concepts presupposes the existence of an understanding that legislates
laws, that understanding need not be our own: it could, of course, be God ’s. The
divine understanding may very well be the one whose legislation unites creatures,
their habitats, and myself into one, great-big, universal cosmological whole. My use
of concepts, it might be added, plays out against the world-whole that God legislated
into being. The point as such must simply be conceded. But it does not matter and
thereby loses its punch as an objection. For Kant himself unequivocally associates the
understanding as legislator and the understanding as user of concepts; and, he
identifies both with human understanding. His reason for doing so is this.
As we saw earlier, Kant believes that the fundamental principle for all use of a finite
understanding like our own rests on self-activity. I cannot use a concept unless
I myself have first put it together, namely by synthesizing a manifold of intuition.
I might, of course, ‘pick up ideas ’ from other people. Kant cannot be taken to deny
this. But his view seems to be that, however significantly I may be in fluenced in my
thinking by my teachers, parents, and friends, they cannot do my thinking for me.
I take up what they have to offer me intellectually, and I have to work out for myself
what I take it to mean. This is an act of some kind of synthesis or self-activity. But
I also take Kant to say that, underlying it all, is an act of synthesis or multiple,
ongoing acts of synthesis that are our best efforts to construe the larger environment
in which we and the objects of our interest find ourselves. Those acts of synthesis will
surely differ in outcome depending on whether our immediate interest is directed
towards the habitat of grizzly bears or that of pink flamingoes or the relation of the
respective habitats of those creatures to one another. But formally they will all have
something in common: they will be, or presuppose, in turn, acts of legislation by
virtue of which we conceive the objects of our interest as standing under laws that
unite them into a single, cosmological whole. This idea should not imply that there is
no God or that, if there is a God, he is neither a creator, nor a legislator, nor a maker
of worlds. There is almost certainly no good philosophical argument that could
establish any of those things. Anyway, Kant would presumably say that such an
argument, if formulated, would be prey to one or another dialectical fallacies or
illusions. But he would also presumably say that, even if there is a God, and he is
indeed a creator and a legislator and a maker of worlds, that will not help us in our
thinking or use of concepts. For the purposes of our understanding, we have to do the
legislating ourselves. Otherwise, the world and the things that inhabit it will be as
good as nothing for us.
The upshot of all this is to complete the programmatic reflections we began in § 4a
of Chapter Two where we asked the question: what is the scopos of the Transcen-
dental Deduction? We there announced the idea, developed at length in the rest
of Chapter Two and in Chapter Three, that the scopos of the first part of the
B-Deduction is thinking —as it discloses itself to itself. In § 4of this chapter, we saw
that the cosmology of experience is the scopos of the second part. We have now just
seen programmatically how the scopos of the one part and the scopos of the other fit
together: the cosmology of experience, in the second part of the B-Deduction, is

        
supposed to make good on the treatment of thinking in the first part. It does so first
by making good, in the way just indicated, on the conception of knowledge and its
relation to its object, at work in § 16, and the principle underlying all thought and use
of concepts announced in § 49. But it even promises to make good on the account
of judgement in § 15and hence the argument that completes the first step of the
B-Deduction in § 20and §21. For the special contribution of § 15is to show that the
objective unity of apperception can convert concepts into judgements by bestowing a
truth value upon them. The effect of this is to make them universally valid for all
judges: if we all had all the evidence for any given judgement, we would all have to
assent to it, if true, or reject it, if false. That does not sound like a cosmological claim.
But it is, without a doubt, because it presupposes a community of judges: all capable,
at least in principle, of evaluating evidence and weighing it. But no such community
would be possible except in a world where, at least in principle, evidence can become
available to judges and judges can become available to one another to jointly assess it.
Again, if appearances should crowd upon my soul so that I might as well be
worldless, I will not be able to collect my thoughts —much less consider whether
I should align my judgement on some matter with the verdict of my fellow judges: the
evidence and my fellow judges will be as good as nothing to me unless we are all
somehow part of a universal world order, stable enough to allow us, at least in
principle, to confer with one another, if we wish, to consider the evidence at hand.
I take Kant to be saying in § 26of the B-Deduction that the doctrine of judgement —
however its details should be understood —does not have a hope of yielding fruit
unless it is supplemented by a cosmology tailor-made for the judges in question. We
human judges exercise the power of our understanding on appearances. For that
reason, a cosmology of appearances is the only kind that can supply a foundation for
us as judges.
I can bring home these reflections about the relation between thinking and
cosmology by observing a note of sympathetic resonance between the B-Deduction
and the Prolegomena.
As we noted in § 4of this chapter, Kant says in § 36of the Prolegomena that the
question how nature is possible is ‘the highest point that transcendental philosophy
can ever conceivably touch upon and to which it must also be led, as its limit and
completion’ (4.318.3–5). But, in § 49of the B-Deduction, he characterizes the syn-
thetic unity of apperception in precisely the same terms. For he says in the footnote to
B467: ‘The synthetic unity of apperception is the highest point to which one must
attach all use of the understanding, even logic and thereafter transcendental
philosophy—indeed this faculty is the understanding itself. ’ The highest point that
transcendental philosophy can reach in the one passage is surely the same as the
highest point it can reach in the other. It is explicitly identi fied with the synthetic
unity of apperception in the passage from § 49of the B-Deduction. In the continu-
ation of the passage from § 36of the Prolegomena, it is characterized in very broad
strokes as ‘the constitution of our understanding according to which all those
representations of sensibility are necessarily related to one consciousness ’
(7.6411.412–54). It is striking that the passage just quoted avoids the expression
‘synthetic unity of apperception ’. But, as I indicated in § 5d of Chapter Two, this is
typical of the Prolegomena, and I will assume that the synthetic unity of apperception

is intended by both passages. If that is right, the passage from the Prolegomena—
taken in context —indicates that the synthetic unity of apperception is cosmological
in outlook. We would never have guessed that from § 49or any subsequent part of the
first step in the B-Deduction. For the first step is just trying to establish that it is the
ground of all thinking. It will take the second step of the B-Deduction to show that
the synthetic unity of apperception is the ground of all thinking just because it is the
ultimate ground of nature in the formal sense. How will it do that?
The answer, of course, will depend in part on appealing to the synthesis of the
productive imagination and its relation to that of the understanding. But instead of
getting into that just now, I would like to point out another note of sympathetic
resonance—this time between the Duisburg Nachlaß and the forward-looking sig-
nificance of § 49in the B-Deduction. As in R4674and elsewhere in the Duisburg
Nachlaß, Kant is setting out in § 49of the B-Deduction a minimal, but fundamental
condition for his cosmology of experience. He is preparing to argue in § 26that there
can be no single, universal experience —nature as a composite of matter and form —
unless it is overseen by a single, self-same thinker. In § 5e of Chapter Two, I stressed
differences: the Duisburg Nachlaß ensures the numerical identity of this thinker by
taking for granted the metaphysics of the rational soul, while § 49of the B-Deduction
does not. § 49of the B-Deduction sets aside the metaphysics by making the novel
claim that the thinker must not only exercise command over the manifold; she or he
must also take command over him or herself as a thinker. Unity of the thinker is the
effect of producing unity in the manifold. This idea will —Kant hopes —be shown
later to furnish the principle of nature in the formal sense. The principle in § 49of the
B-Deduction that every manifold of intuition must stand under the synthetic unity of
pure apperception will thus prove to be the ground of all those laws under which all
appearances must stand if they are to be thought as universally connected in one
experience. But however forward-reaching this idea may be, it reaches back, for its
basic insight, to the Duisburg Nachlaß and beyond to the cosmology of the inaug-
ural dissertation where the numerical identity of the creator was the ultimate ground
of the world-hood embracing the totality of all its creatures (as argued in § 5c and
§2d of Chapter One). This underscores one last time the peculiar parallel between
Kant’s conception of the human understanding, even in the B-Deduction, and the
divine first cause of the early cosmology. The point I wish to stress now is that the
parallel does not signal a vestige of older ways of thinking, but rather a deliberate
and imaginative adaptation of an idea to whose core kernel of truth Kant was still
committed. The cosmological language is not idle talk; it expresses the central and
ultimate idea of the B-Deduction —as adapted to suit the special limitations of our
human understanding. Again, the first step of the B-Deduction cannot secure this
idea all by itself. That is why the B-Deduction is an argument in two steps rather
than one.¹²

§1b A problem on the horizon?
A problem appears to loom on the horizon. I acknowledged in § 4a and elsewhere in
this book that Kant no longer believes, in the Critique of Pure Reason , that general
cosmology is possible as he and others once conceived it, namely as a special branch
of metaphysics based on world concepts. These concepts inevitably lead us to think
and speak of absolute totalities (B 767–768): you cannot conceive of a world without
thinking of it as the whole of all that is or can be. That is the source of the trouble,
because such talk is supposed to lead to antinomies. So Kant argues in the Tran-
scendental Dialectic. I have found it impossible to discuss the cosmology of experi-
ence I claim to be at work in the Transcendental Deduction without speaking of such
totalities. We may thus wonder how the cosmology of experience could be immune
to the antinomies that, as Kant now claims, undermine the cosmology of his early
metaphysics. If it too must succumb to antinomies, it can play no part in the
Transcendental Deduction. My interpretative claims about the Deduction will have
to be rejected.
I would argue, however, that this problem is only an apparent one. The first
element of my argument is the simple observation that Kant himself speaks of
absolute totalities in § 36of the Prolegomena. He uses the word ‘Inbegriff ’ when
presenting both the principle of nature in the material sense and that of nature in the
formal sense. I recall that nature in the material sense is supposed to be the ‘totality
[Inbegriff ] of all appearances’ (7.6411.11), and nature in the formal sense is supposed to
be the ‘totality [ Inbegriff ] of those rules under which all appearances must stand if
they are to be thought as connected in one experience ’ (4.318.16–18). Prolegomena
§36is supposed to set us on the path to the conclusion of the Transcendental
Deduction; it winds down with the following familiar claim:
the understanding is the source of the universal order of nature insofar as it grasps all
appearances under its own laws and thereby, from the very first, brings about experience
(according to its form) a priori through which [sc. order —AL] all that which is to be known
only through experience is necessarily subjected to its [sc. the understanding ’s—AL] laws.
(7.655.10–44)
Kant cannot take himself to be saying anything dialectical in § 36of the Prolegomena.
Nor is he self-deceived, because not all talk of cosmological totalities is susceptible to
antinomies.
The world concepts that give rise to the antinomies are ideas of reason. They begin,
in fact, as concepts of the understanding, but become ideas when reason tries to
extend them beyond the limits of the empirical, while nevertheless trying to keep
them connected to it. This comes about, as Kant explains, when ‘reason demands
absolute totality, for any given conditioned thing, on the side of the conditions
(under which the understanding subjects all appearances to synthetic unity ’ (B769).
The understanding will have carried out, by means of the relevant category, an
empirical synthesis of the manifold in a perfectly legitimate way. It will be in the
nature of this synthesis to have imposed some kind of condition on the manifold. But
reason will come along and demand that the category continue its synthesis by
imposing a condition on the condition and a condition on that condition, never

resting until it reaches the absolutely unconditioned. It (reason) makes this demand
in accordance with its own special principle: ‘when the conditioned is given, then the
whole sum of conditions and therewith the absolutely unconditioned is also given ’
(B769). When the relevant category submits to this demand, it becomes an idea. But
not every category is suited for this: only those ‘in which the synthesis makes up a
sequence and indeed a sequence of conditions, for some conditioned thing, that are
subordinated to one another [sc. as thing conditioned to its condition —AL]’ (B769).
The cosmological ideas that generate antinomies are just extensions of those cat-
egories that fit this description. From among the categories of relation, this includes
the category of cause: for reason can keep asking after the cause of a given cause, and
this will generate a sequence that may extend the category beyond the empirical if
reason is allowed to keep asking unconditionally, according to its special principle.
On the other hand, the category of substance is not included, because, though reason
may well try to apply it to something supersensible, it will never generate a regressive
sequence of conditions like that generated by the category of cause.
Now the respective principles of nature in the material sense and nature in the
formal sense, as presented in § 36of the Prolegomena, govern absolute totalities.
Moreover, they do so as condition governs thing conditioned. That is what makes
them principles. But neither of these two facts alone, nor both together, make these
principles cosmological ideas of the kind under discussion. For one thing, they are
not themselves concepts of the understanding: in the one case, we are dealing with
the pure forms of our sensibility, which do not have their home in the understanding
at all; in the other case, we are dealing with the synthetic unity of pure apperception,
which—as Kant says in §48of the B-Deduction—is more fundamental even than any
of the categories (B464). Might reason come along and demand the conditions that in
turn make these principles possible? Of course, it might. But that, says Kant in § 36of
the Prolegomena, would be a self-defeating enterprise:
How this peculiar property of our sensibility [sc. that of having space and time as its formal
conditions of possibility —AL] is itself possible or how that of our understanding and of the
necessary apperception that grounds it is possible cannot be analysed further and answered,
because we always once again require them for all answering and for all thinking of objects.
(7.6411.511–65)
Reason cannot ask for the principles of the principles of all human thought and
knowledge just because the principles at issue are the very ones needed to satisfy its
request; it seems that they cannot be expected to report on their own conditions of
possibility. If that is right, reason could make its demands, but no sequence of
conditions could be generated by it.
The cosmology of experience presented in § 36of the Prolegomena and at the end
of the Transcendental Deduction is not in danger of generating antinomies, because
it is not built on the cosmological ideas we have just now been discussing. To be sure,
absolute totalities come into play once we invoke the principles of nature in the
material sense and that of nature in the formal sense. But we should always expect
this of principles. If something counts as a principle at all, then it cannot fail to
govern or ground absolutely all of the things that depend on it. Otherwise it would
not count as a principle of the relevant things. It thus makes perfect sense to say, as

        
Kant does in § 36of the Prolegomena, that space and time, as principles of nature in
the material sense, make possible ‘the totality of appearances ’: it would be crazy to
imagine that any possible appearance could fail to conform to them. That is precisely
what justifies the talk about a totality —indeed, an absolute totality —here. The same
can be said about the synthetic unity of apperception. As the principle of nature in
the formal sense, it makes possible ‘the totality of rules ’ the understanding lays
down for appearances. These are the rules that provide for the universal lawfulness
of experience. All of them without exception depend on the synthetic unity of
apperception.
One might still wonder, though, about nature as a composite of matter and form —
that to which Kant refers in § 36of the Prolegomena as experience in the singular and
at A110as ‘one and the same universal experience ’. Nature in this sense is the result
of appearances having been subjected to laws of connection prescribed by the
understanding. It is some kind of whole. For if the Transcendental Deduction
succeeds, no possible appearance can be exempt from these laws and hence all
possible appearances can be fitted into it somehow. We need no longer worry
about a swarm of appearances crowding upon our soul that will be for us even less
than a dream. But a new worry may well take the place of the old one, precisely
because it has been so natural to characterize the universal whole into which all
possible appearances can be fitted in cosmological terms. It seems to leave us a
totality ripe for antinomies.
So it does —under the right conditions, namely if a suitable category is comman-
deered by reason.
Nature as a composite of matter and form is produced by a synthesis carried out by
the understanding with the help of its categories. We just saw that some of these
categories can be enlisted by reason to generate a regressive sequence of conditions
that aims at reaching the unconditioned. This will happen when reason requires
these categories to continue illegitimately a synthesis that they have carried out in an
otherwise legitimate way. The joint effect of their legitimate synthesis —together with
the legitimate synthesis of all the other categories —will be nature as a composite of
matter and form. The effect of their illegitimately extended synthesis will be anti-
nomies of different flavours. Here is an example.
Under the categories of quantity, the understanding subjects appearances to the
Axioms of Intuition and thereby conceives them as having an ‘extensive magnitude’.
That will involve a synthesis of some kind. It is both legitimate and useful: it allows us
to measure and count objects of experience, in both time and space. We can thus
conceive of a given natural or historical process as having had a determinate duration
and a given region as having a determinate area. But when reason steps in, it will
consider the duration of the given process as something conditioned, and it will
regard as its condition the whole time that came before it. Following its principle, it
will thus demand that the understanding keep up its synthesis of appearances,
seeking the time before the beginning of the process, the time before that time,
never stopping until it has accounted for the whole of past time. But if the under-
standing accedes to this demand, we will face the first part of the First Antinomy: we
will just as reasonably conclude that the world began in time as we will that the world
did not begin in time and is therefore eternal. We will face the second part of the First

Antinomy and discover how helpless we are in deciding whether the world is in finite
in space, when reason demands that the understanding account for the extent of the
region that bounds a given region, in a given direction, and then demands that it
account for the extent of the region that lies beyond the neighbouring region, and so
on, never resting until it has accounted for the absolute whole of neighbouring space.
For then we will just as reasonably conclude that the whole of neighbouring space has
an outer limit as that it does not.
The antinomies arise not because of anything special about nature as a composite
of matter and form, but rather because of our capitulation to reason ’s demand for the
unconditioned in those cases where the continued synthesis of the understanding will
generate a regressive sequence of conditions. That much, I think, is secure. It does,
however, raise a question.
Nature as a composite of matter and form is not itself a principle like that of nature
in the material sense or that of nature in the formal sense; it is rather the result of
these principles having been applied to appearances in concert. Consequently, the
totality-talk associated with it cannot be taken in the straightforward, unproblematic
sense associated with the principles as such. We may ask, therefore, what it means to
speak of the ‘one universal experience ’. What sort of totality is this? Here I think the
answer must be that it is a never fully completed one. By that I mean we must expect
the understanding to keep synthesizing appearances, but we may not expect it to
reach the unconditioned. Certain questions must therefore be left open, notably
whether nature had a beginning in time and whether it has limits in space. But
other questions may be regarded as settled, at least in principle. One such question
will come up for discussion in what follows. It concerns different fragments of nature:
a natural process here (like the freezing of some water); a region in space there (like a
house built upon a certain lot someplace). Can we expect —without fear of
antinomies—to situate such fragments relative to one another in one and the same
universal experience? The answer has to be, Yes: at least if the Transcendental
Deduction is successful. The understanding may not be empowered to satisfy
reason’s demand for the unconditioned, but it is empowered to help us determine
when the house was built, how long it stood on that plot, and whether it coexisted in
time with the freezing of the water and to determine where, in space, the water froze,
relative to the plot of land on which the house once stood, now stands, or is expected
to stand in the future. If we cannot be con fident that the understanding can help us
determine these things, then a swarm of houses and frozen puddles may well swarm
upon our soul and be for us even less than a dream.
One last question is raised by the thought just considered. Is it reasonable to call a
never fully completed totality a totality? Here we must concede that it usually is not.
In this case, however, I see no harm or paradox, precisely because the Transcendental
Deduction—if successful —guarantees a priori that no possible appearance will be
exempt from the legislation of the understanding. This means that the order of
nature as such is complete. The actual assigning of a place in this order to appear-
ances is not. At least, not yet: it will continue as long as appearances keep being given
to us. But the guarantee that any possible appearance can be assigned its place in the
order of nature is as good as it gets. Perhaps the thing to say, then, is that nature as a
composite of matter and form is a world in the making. We cannot anticipate where

        
our world-making will ultimately take us. If that is right, then, an important
correction to our concept of a world as such is required.
Before the Critique of Pure Reason , Kant and the other metaphysicians would
sometimes characterize a world as a whole that is not a part of another whole. That
characterization can no longer be embraced by the cosmology of experience set up by
the Transcendental Deduction: at least, not without an important quali fication. The
expressions ‘nature as a composite of matter and form ’ and ‘universal experience’ can
both mean two different things. First, they can be taken to refer retrospectively to all
the synthesizing carried out by the understanding up to now. Taken in that sense,
they may well refer to a whole. But as long as the future is open to continued,
legitimate synthesizing, this whole will most likely prove to be a part of another
greater whole, namely the result of amalgamating it with additional world fragments
we later synthesize. If that is how these expressions are taken, the familiar charac-
terization of a world can no longer apply, and we shall have to think of our world as
always under construction. On the other hand, it might well continue to apply, if our
two expressions are taken to designate the order of nature laid down a priori in the
Transcendental Deduction and then elaborated in detail in the System of Principles.
For that order, as an idealized system of principles, is indeed complete.

Our problem now is to understand the argument of § 26of the B-Deduction. This

it is directed at empirical intuitions. This is, of course, a controversial claim on Kant ’s
part. It has the effect of denying that our thought of mathematical objects through the
categories can be knowledge, because it is not directed in the first instance at
empirical intuition, but rather at pure intuition. I will not try to defend this claim.
Let me just say that I believe it is at least partly justified in Kant ’s eyes by the
dependence of mathematics on the cosmology of experience —along the lines
sketched above, towards the end of § 4a—and that it is supposed to help set the
stage for § 26by focusing our attention on perception: a perception is an empirical

introduces the figurative synthesis carried out a priori by the productive imagin-

intuition carried out by the understanding. I recognize that this distinction is

fundamental to the proceedings. But if I may be permitted to quote the Gospel: by
their fruits shall ye judge them. The fruits of this distinction cannot be harvested
until §26. Let us, then, move on. I will have something to say about the imagination
later, in § 5c of this chapter.
The argument of § 26of the B-Deduction is highly abbreviated. I will try, in what
follows, to unpack it and shed light on it from different angles. That will take the
whole of § 5of this chapter. As a preliminary, it will be useful to have the argument,
such as it is, before us. Here is the thrust of it.
It begins with the claim that perception is possible through the ‘synthesis of
apprehension’, i.e., the ‘putting together ’ (Zusammensetzung) of the manifold in an
empirical intuition. Because this manifold is given to us in an empirical intuition, it
must agree with the pure forms of human sensibility, which are space and time. This
is an expected consequence of the Transcendental Aesthetic. But now Kant claims
that space and time are not just forms of intuition, but intuitions in their own right.
As a result, they each have a manifold. This manifold is given a priori. It can be
synthesized a priori. If so, space and time are themselves represented a priori
according to the determination ( Bestimmung) of unity that accompanies all synthesis
and makes it possible. This is an expected consequence of § 49and § 16of the
B-Deduction. Kant now claims that the synthesis of apprehension must agree with
the conditions of the unity of this synthesis and that these conditions are due to
the categories. It follows that the categories are conditions of the possibility of the
synthesis of apprehension and hence that all perception depends on them. This leads
Kant to make a statement with a certain air of finality: ‘Therefore all synthesis
through which even perception is possible stands under the categories; and since
experience is knowledge through connected perceptions, the categories are thus
conditions of possibility of experience and so also hold a priori for all objects of
experience’ (B161). This looks like what Kant set out to prove, and so it is not
unreasonable to think that the argument ends here.
The argument, as I just presented it, will be the object of this section, i.e., § 5of the
current chapter. Its purpose, as I will argue, is to provide a foundation for what I will
call the ‘cartography of the sensible world ’. Its chief concern is the role of map-
making in our thinking and knowledge of the things we perceive. That we can make
maps—in space and time together—allows us to keep track of the empirical objects of
interest to us and situate them relative to one another and to us who are following
their progress. It is the sign that we have converted appearances into one, single,
universal experience, i.e., the world in which we live and seek to satisfy our curiosity.
You cannot make a map of things relative to one another if these things are parts of
different worlds or parts of no world at all or parts of a world of which you yourself
are not a part. If the categories can be shown to make map-making possible, with
the aid of the figurative synthesis of productive imagination, the Transcendental
Deduction will have ful filled its mission. My strategy throughout § 5of this chapter
will be to confront the argument in § 26of the B-Deduction from B 481to B 496
with parallel passages in the A-Deduction and L 4, the transcript of Kant ’s lectures
on metaphysics from the late 1770s. We will find that our reflections on ekthesis or
exposition from Chapter One are relevant for these passages, the issues that arise in
them and their significance for the B-Deduction. (One interesting consequence will

     
be that, though all human thinking is discursive, it must also be graphic in nature —
insofar as it depends on the cosmology of experience, as I earlier suggested it must
at the end of § 4a.)
We will discover, however, that one piece of the puzzle is missing in the argument
and considerations Kant raises from B 481to B 496, namely universal laws. Consid-

§2b What are pure images of space and time good for?
One way to test my proposal is to examine the two examples Kant gives from B 495to
B163in §26in the B-Deduction. I take them to illustrate the role played by the pure
images of space and time. The first example is that of my perception of a house. Kant
spells it out as follows: ‘For example, if I convert the empirical intuition of a house

unity of space and of outer sensible intuition as such; and I draw its shape [sc. that of
the house] according to this synthetic unity of the manifold in space ’ (B162). The
passage says explicitly and unequivocally that I do not have a perception of the house,
even if I have an empirical intuition of it, until I draw its shape, as it were. This surely
means that I must take time to produce an image of it for myself. The image might
involve something as simple as registering whether the house is one-storey or two,
whether it has a flat roof or gables, or the arrangement of its windows and doors
relative to one another, i.e., whatever I take away from my encounter with the house
by taking time to run through and put together the manifold of my empirical
intuition of it. This thought is a special application of the more generally stated
claim made in § 58of the B-Deduction:
We cannot imagine [uns denken] a line without drawing it [ ziehen] in thought, a circle without
describing it, the three dimensions of space without setting three lines perpendicular to each
other from the same point, or even time itself without attending to the mere carrying out of the
synthesis of the manifold by successively determining inner sense through the drawing of a
straight line to serve as the outer figurative representation of time (B 487).
We clearly recognize this claim as an echo of the one stated in our passages from
L4: I cannot represent something —be it a line or a body —unless I take time to ‘set it
out’. This is the image formation associated with ekthesis or exposition. The claim at
B162in §26of the B-Deduction, as in the St. Peter ’s example in L 4, is that exposition
is required even for perception. But the passage from B 495also says explicitly and
unequivocally that an intuition of space in its own right is necessary for producing
the image constitutive of my perception of the house. In this respect, it differs from

the treatment of the ‘synthesis of apprehension ’ in the A-Deduction, which can be
understood to claim at A 1212only that, in addition to the synthesis of apprehension
carried out on the manifold of an empirical intuition, we can also carry out such a
synthesis a priori —which synthesis would yield an a priori representation of space.
I can find no explicit suggestion in the relevant parts of the A-Deduction (though it
may well be implied) that any such representation of space is a necessary condition
for the image-making involved in the empirical synthesis of apprehension. But that
idea is clearly central to § 26of the B-Deduction and the passage I just quoted from it.
There is more to say about this idea in the B-Deduction and the way it applies to the
house example. But it will be useful first to review quickly the second of the two
concrete examples Kant offers us at B 496.
The second example concerns the temporal relation in my perception of a certain
physical process, namely the freezing of water at the right temperature. I apprehend
two states: water in a liquid state; the same substance in a solid state. The two states
have a certain temporal relation, but my apprehension of them as such will leave this
relation indeterminate, unless I do something. We expect Kant to say that what
I must do is take the time to run through the manifold and put it together. He never
explicitly says this, but it is hard to imagine —from context —that he could have
anything else in mind. Running through the manifold may mean nothing more
complicated than registering —as I just did —the short inventory of the two different
states of the substance I have apprehended. But it is harder to see what putting it
together might involve. Here Kant speaks only in the most general terms. He can be
understood to say that it involves producing ‘synthetic unity of the manifold ’ a priori
as given by the pure form of inner sense. In other words, it involves an intuition of
time in its own right whose manifold has been synthesized a priori. We have no
indication what this is supposed to mean concretely, but that in itself suggests that
Kant expects us to fill in the picture ourselves with some kind of parallel with the
previous example of perceiving the house. This could seem like too great an expect-
ation, given how stingy he was with the details there. But the idea may, in fact, be as
simple as this. Putting the manifold together to perceive the freezing of water involves
putting the two different states we have apprehended on some kind of time-line such
that we represent the solid state as coming after the liquid state. But the use of a time-
line in this way presupposes we can represent time itself as a one dimensional line
directed towards the future, i.e., it presupposes a pure image of time.
If this very simple idea is on the mark (and its very simplicity suggests that it is), we
can perhaps operate in the reverse of Kant ’s expectations and use it to shed a little
more light on the house example. Thus we might say, by analogy, that perceiving the
house presupposes a pure image of space, because ‘drawing its shape ’ by putting
together the manifold of the relevant empirical intuition is nothing more than
externally arranging the parts of the house relative to one another. But doing so
involves—at least implicitly —the ability to represent these parts as laid out on some
kind of a grid: two dimensional, if we focus on a given surface; three dimensional, if
we try to relate surfaces to one another. The use of either kind of grid is itself some
kind of representation of space, just as the time-line is some kind of representation of
time. But if we agree that the latter is a pure image, then we must agree that the
former is as well.

     
If we step back and take stock of the two examples given by Kant at B162and B163,

associated with the a priori representations of space and time in their own right
and what purpose these representations are supposed to have: a priori image-making
is a necessary condition for empirical image-making. All of this turns on the
assumption that perception is not a passive taking-in of what is going on around
us. It is a self-active attempt to construe the manifold given to us in our intuitions: it
is image production, not image reception. As such, it essentially involves situating the
elements of an empirical manifold relative to one another in space and time. But it
cannot carry out this activity unless resources are available to us for orienting our
imagination in space and time. It seems reasonable to suppose that these resources
just consist in a capacity to situate parts of space relative to one another and parts of
time relative to one another. We put that capacity to work when we produce time-
lines and spatial grids, which is a form of image-making a priori. It is hard to see how
we could engage in any empirical image-making —even perception —without it. If
that is right, then, the argument from B 493to B163in §26of the B-Deduction is just
that perception presupposes as the condition of its possibility not only the pure forms
of space and time, but whatever makes possible this capacity to form images of spatial
and temporal relations a priori. If the necessary condition of this capacity can be
shown to be the categories, then the categories are the necessary conditions of all
perception.

At the very end of this passage, Kant offers us a way to produce an image of the whole

discussing earlier. We can produce an image of the phenomenal world if we start with
the image of time as a straight line produced inde finitely in the direction of the
future. Then all we have to do is drop perpendiculars to this line. Each perpendicular
will be the line along which we imagine all the states that obtain at a given moment.
Then it will be a very simple matter to establish, for two non-successive processes,
that a given stage of the first took place at the same moment as a given stage of the
second. For it will simply be a matter of tracking the two processes from one
perpendicular to the next. There will be no reason to wonder if the two processes
took place in different temporal streams, because all the perpendiculars of interest to
us fall on the same time-line. Kant observes in the passage I just quoted that it is a
mistake to think that succession is the only temporal relation and that the absence of
succession secures all by itself a purely spatial relation among non-successive states —
the relation we call ‘simultaneity’. For simultaneity is itself a temporal relation: a
connection of things at the same moment. If we overlook that fact, we may doubt
whether non-successive states have this connection or not. If they do not, they are as
good as banished from the sensible world as we know it. But if they have it, it should
be possible to produce an image that situates them in time relative to one another at
the same moment and relative to all the states and events that have either preceded
them or that will come after them: and so it is, namely by constructing the time-line

a synthesized intuition of time as such, nor a synthesized intuition of space as such,
but rather a synthesized intuition of space, as the order of simultaneous coexistence,
mixed together with time, as the order of succession. That very mixture yields a
surface that can serve as a map of the phenomenal world. Just as we used a straight
line extended in the direction of the future to situate the solid state of the freezing
water as coming after the liquid state in the order of succession, and just as we used a
two-dimensional grid to map the lay-out of —say—the arrangement of doors and
windows on the front of the house, so we can use the surface described in the
inaugural dissertation to situate the freezing water and the house relative to one

the freezing of the water, then we will give it a position on each of the perpendiculars

house were built and destroyed before our particular puddle of water froze, then it
will have to occupy a position on the perpendiculars that precede those on which the
stages of freezing are mapped out; it will have to occupy a position on subsequent
perpendiculars, if it were built thereafter. But precisely because this image re flects the
insight—unfamiliar in Kant ’s time —that simultaneous coexistence is as much a
temporal relation as succession, it gives us a way to represent all the things that are
sensibly given to us relative to one another in one and the same universal experience:
it is just a matter of finding the right perpendicular. For whatever turns up on any
perpendicular can be understood to fall on the same time-line. As long as we can
represent all our perpendiculars as falling on the same time-line, we do not have to
worry that anything sensibly given to us has a place in any other experience besides
the one and only experience that we refer to as nature as a composite of matter and

form. Were it hypothetically possible for something to have a place in a world other
than, or parallel to, this one, it simply would not appear on any of the perpendiculars
in our image.

presented to us in the inaugural dissertation, it becomes more plausible to think —as
Kant does in § 26of the B-Deduction —that pure synthesized intuitions are an
essential part of the answer to the question how nature as such is possible. These
intuitions are, as we have seen, images of space and time, and images of space and
time mixed together in the way just indicated. The wrong thing to say, of course, is
that the relevant images make nature possible in the formal sense, because they give
us a likeness of it. For one thing, the image described in the inaugural dissertation
does not resemble the sensible world: at least not the way a current photo resembles
me as I am right now. The image we have been discussing looks like a toothed comb,
if anything, but the sensible world, at least as it has appeared to me in the time I have
been aware of it, does not. For another thing, if it did resemble the sensible world the
way a current photo resembles me as I am right now, it would not be useful as an
answer to Kant ’s question. The purpose of a passport photo is to help identify the
holder of the passport. The question how nature is possible is not —on the face of it—
a problem of identi fication. Pure synthesized intuitions, as Kant understands them,
help make nature possible by giving us a way, or by representing our ability, to orient
ourselves and the things of interest to us relative to each other, and to us, in the larger
empirical context in which we either encounter them or expect to encounter them.
Hence, they serve the same function as a map of a city —like the map Kant describes
in the passage from 511.569in L 4that we discussed in § 5a of this chapter. (That
passage is a witness to his long-standing interest in the significance of maps.)
A map is an image that no more resembles the relevant city than the toothed comb
in the inaugural dissertation resembles the sensible world. Consider a topographical
map of a city with lots of ups and downs. The ups and downs will give the city a
certain ‘look’ to the people on the ground visiting it, but that ‘look’ as such will be
missing from the map. The map itself will have a quite different ‘look’, because it will
be covered with more or less irregular contours that will resemble concentric
wrinkles on a surface that may have once been crumpled up and has not yet been
properly ironed out. But if we learn how to interpret the ‘wrinkles’, we will under-
stand how the surface on which the city has been laid out changes in elevation from
place to place; and, we will understand this to the same degree of accuracy with which
the map has succeeded in representing these changes. In just the same way, the
toothed comb gives us the spatio-temporal lay-out of the sensible world or, rather,
those things in that part of the sensible world in which we happen to take an interest
for whatever reason. Now we never usually worry that the map of a city will fail to
give us some kind of orientation of that one city. But the worry that pure synthesized
intuitions of space and time might fail to capture the sensible world —or the part of it
we are interested in —as one experience hangs over us because the question Kant
raised is how nature itself is possible. This worry is eased, however, once we realize
that the toothed comb recognizes the temporal aspect of space and then situates
space to time, as the order of succession, in such a way that we can track the histories
and expected futures of different things relative to one another. Hence, we may say

     
with equanimity of the things we can hypothetically imagine as having no position at
all on the toothed comb that they are as good as nothing for us. The pure synthesized
intuitions of space, time, and space and time mixed together make nature possible in
just the way that a map makes it possible for us to grasp the lay-out, orientation, or
topography of a city. That is why I conclude that the function of the pure synthesized
intuitions of space, time, and space and time mixed together, introduced in § 26of the
B-Deduction, is to lay the foundation of a cartography of the sensible world.
Experience as such would be impossible without it.
The last thing to say about these intuitions and their role in the argument of § 26in
the B-Deduction is this. In § 16, we saw that Kant had a peculiar way of understand-
ing the relation between knowledge and its object. He could be understood to say that
it is a mistake to consider the one without the other, as though they were two separate
orders of things and as though we could determine the truth only by setting them side
by side and comparing them for some kind of match. As an alternative to this way of
thinking, Kant could be understood to say that knowledge and its object are not
separate from one another. On the contrary, knowledge exhibits its truth by con-
structing its object, just as the ancient geometers exhibited the truth of their know-
ledge of —say—the dodecahedron by constructing one: if the construction of the
thing yields a dodecahedron that can be seen, by the very nature of its construction,
to conform to our claims about it, then we are within our rights to say that we know
the truth about it. Now if it is true that pure synthesized intuitions advance the
argument of §26in the B-Deduction, it must be the case that, at a minimum, they are
compatible with Kant’s special understanding of the relation between knowledge and
its object and, moreover, that they help somehow to make this understanding more
concrete and thereby more plausible. In fact, I think they do so admirably well. For if
it is fair to say that they serve the same function as the map of a city, it might be said
just as plausibly that they serve the same function as a blueprint.
A blueprint is an image that can be used as the basis of a construction —as, for
example, in the construction of a building: if nothing else, it tells the builders where
everything is supposed to go. But so too for the time-line for the freezing water
which tells us where the liquid and the ice are supposed to go in the order of
succession. So too inevitably for the toothed comb which tells us where anything of
interest is supposed to go in the sensible world as a whole —or, rather, in that part
of the sensible world of concern to us. We can use this blueprint as the basis for
constructing further details about the sensible world, whether by measurement,
prediction, or some combination of the two. For that matter, even the map of a city
might be understood as a blueprint. The city already exists: it is not as if the map is
needed to inform construction workers where to build each of its different neigh-
bourhoods. But we can and do use maps as blueprints from which to build models
of a city in our imagination. We use such models when, for example, we plan
future visits to the city or when we give directions to people who have lost their
way. These models exhibit our knowledge of a city in something like the way a
geometric construction exhibits our knowledge of the relevant figure: if we ultim-
ately find our way, or if we help somebody else find his or her way, our model of
the city has survived a test that establishes its practical viability. If, then, we prefer
for some reason to describe Kant ’s pure synthesized intuitions as maps of the

sensible world, the conclusion will remain the same: they advance the argument of
the B-Deduction by finding a crucial element in the answer to the question how
‘nature’ is possible that takes note of, and does justice to, the important reflections
in § 16about the relation between knowledge and object for a finite intellect.
That said, the argument has so far said nothing about universal laws. We saw in
§1a of this chapter that an account of universal laws must be worked in to the
argument before it can be brought to a conclusion. The cartography of the sensible
world is not yet the full story. We will try to complete it in § 6.
§2c Some unfinished business: productive imagination
We must attend to some unfinished business before we move on to § 6, the first piece
of which concerns the productive imagination.
Imagination as such is supposed to be the faculty that produces images. The
considerations we reviewed in § 5b indicate that it must include a capacity to produce
images a priori. This it does by carrying out a priori its characteristic synthesis on a
manifold of sensible intuition. In § 57of the B-Deduction, Kant calls this synthesis
‘figurative’ to distinguish it from the ‘intellectual’ synthesis carried out by the
understanding through the categories ‘with respect to the manifold of an intuition
as such’ (B484). The distinction is important, because, as a matter of principle, image
formation—even when it is carried out a priori —is not the same as understanding.
This is obvious from the fact that no image has logical structure and no judgement
has an orientation in space. At the same time, it is important to recognize that the
distinction is purely conceptual, because imagination and understanding do not
operate independently of one another. We never receive ‘the manifold of an intuition
as such’; nor then does the understanding effect any kind of synthesis of one. Such a
manifold is an abstraction useful only for conceptually isolating the activity of the
understanding to help us see what makes it distinctive. The manifold we actually
receive is always of a specific kind, namely the one given to us under the forms of
human sensibility. It undergoes synthesis by the understanding, but not without the
help of the imagination. By the same token, the imagination never synthesizes a
manifold of human intuition without direction by the understanding. The result if it
did would be random, blind, and unsuited for being taken up into knowledge. To be
sure, Kant characterizes the imagination as a ‘blind . . . function of the soul . . . of
which we are seldom conscious ’ (B436). But that is what it is, considered in itself
as distinct from the understanding. It is useful to consider it in this way, precisely to
help us learn what distinguishes the two faculties from one another. Considered with
respect to the project of acquiring knowledge, however, they are joint partners; their
venture can never advance —or even get started —if they go their separate ways.
Hence the importance of clarifying how they relate to each other. I think that Kant
is admirably clear on this point: imagination serves at the behest of understanding.

The interest of this passage in my mind is the way it develops the central assumption
upon which I believe it is based, namely that understanding (in the non-technical
sense of just ‘getting it’) is a form of ekthesis. Imagination is required, because ekthesis
always requires a tailor-made image. Consider the statement of any proposition in
Apollonius’ treatise on conic sections. Kant will deny that you can ‘get it’ unless you
set out a labelled diagram as a representative, special case. That diagram has to be
tailor-made to meet the conditions of the proposition. Imagination alone can prod-
uce it. As Kant himself puts it at the beginning of the passage quoted above, ‘it alone
can give the concepts of the understanding a corresponding intuition. ’ How can it do
this? The answer is that it can spontaneously determine our sensibility a priori, i.e., it
can produce a synthesis of the manifold tailor-made for the requirements of our
concepts and ultimately for those of the categories. The peculiarity of Kant ’s position,
as staked out in this passage and elsewhere in the Critique, is that it generalizes the
underlying assumption beyond the domain of classical geometry. If all understanding
(in the non-technical sense) is a form of ekthesis, then tailor-made images will be
required in our efforts to understand not only Apollonius ’ treatise on conic sections,
but physical processes, and anything else of interest as well. Hence, the productive
imagination must always intervene between understanding and human sensibility,
because otherwise we would not ‘get’ anything.
From the defence of the inaugural dissertation in 1770, Kant insisted on the radical
distinction between understanding and sensibility. But we can see from the Duisburg
Nachlaß and echoes of it in the Critique and other writings that that distinction went
hand in hand with the idea that human beings do not understand the things they
wish to understand without the aid of images and diagrams. Images and diagrams are
not themselves understanding, but understanding is impossible without them. The
problem for Kant was not just to work out how sensibility and understanding relate
to each other given the different principles that govern them, but also to work out
how the understanding takes up and manages the images necessary for it to carry out
its principal function. That would have been a problem for him even if he had never
distinguished sensibility from understanding, just because understanding is not itself
imagination.²[^5] The Duisburg Nachlaß did not address the problem; no mention of
the imagination can be found in it. One of the distinctive features of the Transcen-
dental Deduction (both versions of it) is not just that the problem is finally addressed,

Kant has deliberately emphasized the word ‘heterogeneous’ and thereby indicated
that the problem is the difference in kind and principle of the categories and
intuition: the difference between them is so great that he says we cannot conceive
how the former can apply to the latter. A ‘transcendental doctrine of judgement ’ is
needed to explain how this difference may be overcome. It will call on a ‘transcen-
dental schema’ to serve as ‘a third thing that must stand in sameness [ Gleichheit] on
the one hand with the category and on the other hand with appearance and that
makes possible the application of the former to the latter ’ (B41010). The transcendental
schema is well suited to be the ‘third thing’, he says, just because it is intellectual, like
the category, and sensible, like intuition.
Kant must, of course, be taken at his word in this passage. But again I deny that
such words are helpful. With or without Kant, we can all agree that numbers and
apples are ‘completely heterogeneous’: apples can be eaten; numbers cannot. Yet who
can really think that their heterogeneity stands in the way of using numbers to count
the apples left in the pantry? It seems to me that something more enlightening can
and should be said to motivate the Schematism.
I take the Schematism to address a question not yet raised in the Transcendental
Deduction—not even by the very end of § 26of the B-Deduction. The question
concerns the intuitions of space and time that we were discussing in § 5b of this
chapter. They are pure images tailored to the requirements of experience, as laid
down by the categories; we will naturally wonder how they are produced. The
Transcendental Deduction does not address this question. It tells us only that they
are, or involve, a synthesis carried out by the imagination and that this synthesis must
conform to the synthetic unity of pure apperception. But how does the imagination
do this? We surmise that it takes instructions from the categories. But, again, how?
This is a tough question, because the answer —to start with —is that the imagin-
ation takes its instructions from the categories by interpreting them. It has great
latitude in this, precisely because its interpretation takes the form of an image, while
its instructions do not. Here indeed is heterogeneity. But heterogeneity as such is not
the problem; it is rather just what is needed. For without it, no graphic interpretation
of the categories would be possible. The latitude it bestows on the imagination allows

it to offer multiple interpretations of the same thing from different perspectives:
witness the different ways we manipulated the image of the phenomenal world in
§2b, taking it sometimes as a toothed comb, sometimes extracting from it a single
perpendicular and rearranging it as a surface or as a three-dimensional spatial grid.
We need these different perspectives, in our search for understanding, precisely
because we are finite beings and no single image can capture every aspect of the
object our interest may be fixed upon.²⁶The source of the problem here is something
else, namely the fact that not every interpretation goes.
In—say—the ekthesis of a proposition about ellipses, it would be absolutely
intolerable for the imagination to dish up the image of a triangle. For we can all
agree that the image of a triangle is not the image of an ellipse. But what about the
image of two circle arcs that meet? Strictly speaking, that is not the image of an ellipse
either. Yet it is less obvious that such an image would be intolerable for the purposes
at hand. Indeed, we often find the image of two circle arcs that meet standing in for
ellipses in medieval Arabic manuscripts of Apollonius. The reader of these manu-
scripts is never fussed by the device: it is apparently good enough; it is probably better
than the image of a circle. But this indicates that a principle of some kind must lay
down constraints to guide our judgement of what is good enough and what is not.
Here we have a problem, because it is not obvious how this principle should be
spelled out. Still, we can be certain of at least one thing —indeed, the most important
thing. The principle at issue cannot itself be an image —not without the threat of an
infinite regress. Kant is explicit on this point. To signal the difference, he calls the
principle a schema, and he characterizes it as ‘the representation of a universal
procedure of the imagination for providing a concept with its image ’ (A473/B4113).
The imagination has a schema for each concept. We may infer that a schema is
always needed to guide the ekthesis of a proposition in geometry: if the proposition is
about ellipses, for example, the schema will give the concept of ellipse a representative
image: one, moreover, that is acceptable as such: never a triangle, but perhaps two
circle arcs that meet. Suppose now that experience as such involves an ekthesis,
namely that of relations among appearances specifying in different ways the univer-
sal laws of nature. In that case, it will require special schemata to interpret the
categories as pure formal images that lay the foundation of our cosmological cartog-
raphy. The task of the section in the Critique on the Schematism, in my view, is to
sketch an answer to these very natural questions about the interpretative role played
by the imagination in all ekthesis and especially in that which makes experience
possible. I would need to say more about this, were I to take my reflections beyond
the Transcendental Deduction. Since that is not my aim here, I hope that this will do
as a programmatic statement of the idea.

The interest of this passage is that it helpfully supplies details about ‘exposition’ that
are obviously relevant for issues in the Transcendental Deduction that we have been
discussing throughout § 5of this chapter. Ekthesis or exposition is the temporal
operation of ‘going through ’ a manifold. It begins at a certain moment and ends at
another. The time lying between those moments can be divided into durations of
shorter and shorter length; we can carry out these divisions as long as we like. But we
will never find that the time we originally set out is composed of points or moments.
Ekthesis is thus the operation of setting out continuous magnitudes: in the first
instance, that of the time it takes to complete; in the second instance, that of the
object of interest to us, which could be a length, an area, a volume, the duration of
some other process (like that of the freezing of water), or anything else that may
count as a continuous magnitude in its own right. But, as such, we can see that it
must be subject to space and time —as forms of intuition with the special unity that
bestows continuity on any magnitude set out by ekthesis. This important detail, so
helpfully presented in the passage I just quoted from L 4, is plainly presupposed in
passages of the Transcendental Deduction we have been discussing: for example, the
presentation of the synthesis of apprehension in the A-Deduction from A 1211to A433
as the temporal operation of ‘going through ’ the manifold and ‘putting it together ’
and the presentation of the argument of § 26in the B-Deduction from B 493to B163.

I argued in § 5b that the a priori image-making Kant takes to be necessary for all

sensible world is too. Map-making under conditions that make a world as such

indeed the object consists in the universal point’ (410.9711.48–410). This is indeed one of
the philosophical lessons one may derive from engaging with the proof strategy of
classical geometry. If it is true that we will not understand —in the non-technical
sense of just ‘getting it’—the proposition to be proved without working through the
labelled diagram of the representative case presented in the ekthesis, it is also true that
the ekthesis alone is insufficient: the enunciation of the general statement is just as
fundamental. I do not just wish to prove that, in a given triangle ABC whose sides AB
and AC are equal, the angles ABC and ACB are equal too. I wish to prove that this is
the case for all isosceles triangles. So it could be claimed that, without the enunciation

of the general statement, we do not have an object of knowledge or investigation.
Kant may well have had an opportunity to read parts of Isfah ānī’s recension of

survived only in Arabic translation: Books Five, Six, and Seven.[^6] Had he, he would
have been struck that this recension gives only the ekthesis and leaves out the
enunciation. I believe that he would have argued —not unreasonably —that this
stands in the way of understanding. He would have said that the ekthesis gives the
enunciation an image and thus concrete meaning. But without the enunciation, all we
really have is an image. Then what? An image all by itself is not knowledge of
anything. So our cosmological cartography must relate to something equivalent to
the general enunciation of a geometrical proposition. It will only thus be the ekthesis
of the universal laws of nature. That means we now have a real shot at understanding
the world, because we will know how to give images and concrete meaning to these
laws, and the laws themselves make it possible for our maps to have genuine
cosmological significance. These ideas are finally worked out in detail in the Analo-
gies of Experience, which Kant could have called an ‘exposition of appearances ’. But
perhaps because he associated that expression with the Duisburg Nachlaß and its
failure, he mostly suppresses it in the Critique.
I believe that this last point helps answer a question I have long had about the
meaning of the expression ‘relates to an object ’—as when Kant asks whether the
categories relate a priori to an object. What does he mean? I was never satisfied
I knew. Is he just waving his hands? But I now think that he means something
reasonably precise. Once the categories have been schematized and stated as the
universal laws of nature —as in the Analogies of Experience —they can relate to
objects of knowledge in just the way that the enunciation of a proposition in classical
geometry, together with its ekthesis, will relate to an object of knowledge, provided, of
course, that we succeed in proving it.
[^6]: Apollonii Pergaei conicorum lib. V. VI. VII. Paraphraste Abalphato Asphahanensi, Florentiae ex typographia Cocchini, 1661.
