## Conclusion

The Transcendental Deduction is supposed to show that the categories prescribe laws
to nature and thereby make it possible as 'one and the same universal experience '.
Thinking, judging and knowing are acts of world-building by our understanding:
appearances are its raw materials, but its technique for making a world out of them
is legislation through the categories. This result is stated by Kant in §26 of the
B-Deduction at an astonishing level of generality. There we learn from him only
that the laws issued by the categories must be cosmologically significant and must
therefore apply universally to anything that can be sensibly given to us in space and
time. In other words, they cannot be empirical laws; they must hold a priori. But it is
natural to wonder what else might be said about them. The Duisburg Nachlaß tries
(repeatedly) to give statements of the universal laws required for the exposition of
appearances. By contrast, the Transcendental Deduction offers not even so much as a
hint. It leaves us wondering. How many laws of nature are there? How might they be
stated? §26 of the B-Deduction does not even give the reader a promissory note with
a forward-looking reference to the System of Principles.

When we get to the System of Principles, however, it becomes perfectly clear that
the Analogies of Experience are the laws at issue at the end of the Transcendental
Deduction. For Kant says explicitly that the Analogies are the laws by which the
understanding makes nature possible:

> By nature (in an empirical sense [im empirischen Verstande]), we mean the connection
> [Zusammenhang] of appearances, as far as their existence is concerned, according to universal
> rules, i.e., laws. Thus it is certain laws, indeed a priori ones, that first make a nature possible.
> Empirical laws can obtain [stattfinden] and can be found only by means of experience, and
> indeed consequent upon those original laws according to which experience is first possible.
> Thus our Analogies actually set out [darstellen] the unity of nature in the connection of all
> appearances under certain exponents [Exponenten] that express nothing other than the
> relation of time (insofar as it encompasses [begreift] all existence in itself) to the unity of
> apperception, which can only obtain in synthesis according to rules. Together they then say: all
> appearances lie in a nature, and must lie therein, because, without this unity a priori, no unity
> of experience, nor then any determination of objects in the same, would be possible. (B263)[^1]

The language and rhetoric of this passage clearly recall the language and rhetoric at
the end of §26 of the B-Deduction. Hence, we may infer that the Analogies of
Experience are supposed to complete the cosmology of experience in the *Critique
of Pure Reason*.

To a reader of the Duisburg Nachlaß, this conclusion does not come as a great
surprise, because the three universal laws it repeatedly tries to state —the object of our
discussion in §1c of Chapter One —are the immediate ancestors of the Analogies of
Experience: they include a law of persistence through time, a law of causality or
succession, and a law of community or simultaneous coexistence. The continuity of
Kant's thinking from the mid-1770s to 1781 is striking. But just as striking for present
purposes is the following point of difference. In the Duisburg Nachlaß, Kant only
seems to be aware of the three relational categories; he has a table of twelve in the
*Critique of Pure Reason*. The conclusion of the Transcendental Deduction is sup-
posed to hold for all twelve categories —not just for the three that yield the Analogies
of Experience in the System of Principles. But if the Analogies of Experience are
the universal laws that make nature possible, as claimed at the end of §26 in the
B-Deduction, what shall we say about the other nine categories? Do they not also
prescribe laws to nature? Does nature not depend as much on them as on the three
relational ones? If not, the Transcendental Deduction —even if successful, so far as it
goes—will give us a much more restricted result than Kant initially led us to expect:
one that gives title over appearances to the relational categories, but to no others. In
that case, we could still worry that the other nine categories are 'empty, null and
without meaning'. But Kant could not have intended that to be the result. Could he?[^2]

If we are to solve the conundrum, we must first concede that the Analogies of
Experience are indeed the laws that make nature possible. This must be conceded,
because that is precisely what Kant says in the passage from B263 I just quoted from
the System of Principles.[^3] It is careful and deliberate too. It follows naturally from his
characterization of an Analogy of Experience as 'just a rule according to which unity
of experience is supposed to arise out of perceptions ' (A178/B218). A perception is a
sensation or empirical intuition of which we are conscious (A225/B272). Perceptions
as such need not be connected with one another.[^4] We can imagine being conscious

(B219). One and only one feature of the sensible qualities we perceive can be
'anticipated'. That is their degree or intensive magnitude: 'all sensations as such are

We can flesh out this point if we reconsider an observation from §1a of

Corresponding to the difference of target between the Analogies, on the one hand,
and the Axioms of Intuition and Anticipations of Perception, on the other, is a
difference of modality and a claim about what can and cannot be constructed. Kant
distinguishes between the existence of appearances and their possibility; he says that
the latter can be constructed, while the former cannot. The principles that allow us
to construct the possibility of appearances are the Axioms of Intuition and Antici-
pations of Perception. Kant puts it as follows:

> [The Axioms of Intuition and Anticipations of Perception], which I called mathematical
> because they make it legitimate to apply mathematics to appearances, had to do with [gingen
> auf] appearances as far as their mere possibility goes, and they showed how appearances, with
> respect to both their intuition and the real in their perception, can be produced according to
> rules of a mathematical synthesis and hence, with respect to both the former and the latter,
> how numerical magnitudes and therewith the determination of appearance as magnitude can
> be used. Thus will I be able, for example, to compose [zusammensetzen] the degree of the
> sensations of sunlight out of, say, 200,000 illuminations by the moon and render it determinate
> a priori; that is, I will be able to construct it. Hence, we can call these principles constitutive.

By contrast, the Analogies of Experience supply rules a priori for the existence of
appearances. But these rules do not license any kind of construction. The continu-
ation of the passage I just quoted from B221 reads as follows: 'Things are quite
different with the principles [sc. the Analogies —AL] that are supposed to bring the
existence of appearances under rules a priori. For since existence cannot be con-
structed, they will only be concerned with the relation of existence and can yield no
other save merely regulative principles' (B221–222). The Analogies cannot supply
rules that would allow us, in any way, to determine a priori the existence of
appearances, because existence as such cannot be constructed. Instead, they supply
the rules that allow us to determine a priori the relations of existence among
appearances. We never determine a single existence in isolation from others, but
always existence in relation to more existence. Such a relation will be temporal. Kant
continues: 'When a perception is given to us in a temporal relation with other (albeit
undetermined ones), it cannot be said a priori which other perception and how great
a perception, but rather how it [sc. the other perception —AL] is necessarily con-
nected with the given perception, with respect to existence, in this mode of time '
(A178/B222). I find this passage obscure in certain respects.[^6] But the main point is

clear enough: the Analogies supply the rules by which we can know a priori how the
existence of one appearance (as empirical intuition or perception) relates in time to
that of another, i.e., with respect to duration, succession, and simultaneity. This is
supposed to imply that the Analogies can indeed give unity to experience and thus
make nature possible, but the Axioms of Intuition or the Anticipations of Perception
cannot. The inference may be justified as follows.

Although the Axioms of Intuition and Anticipations of Perception are constitutive
rather than regulative principles, and although they target the possibility of appear-
ances rather than their existence, they are just as much concerned with relations as
the Analogies. For the construction of extensive and intensive magnitudes will always
involve comparisons, as Kant's own example at B221 indicates. He claims, in effect,
that I construct the degree of illumination of the sun by comparison with that of the
moon: one degree of the former is equal to 200,000 degrees of the latter. Other
representative examples will work in the same way: when I compute the sum of the
sizes of the interior angles of a triangle, I express the result as a certain sum of right
angles; when I state the Pythagorean theorem, I compare the area of a certain square
with the sum of the areas of certain other squares. Relations of magnitudes are
involved in each case. This suggests that the Axioms of Intuition and Anticipations of
Perception may be expected to lay the foundations of a general theory of mathem-
atical ratios.[^7] For the purpose of such a theory is to give us principles and techniques
for comparing magnitudes with each other and their relations with respect to size.
Such a theory can be useful for engaging rigorously with cosmological questions —
like those we try to answer by mathematically predicting or retrodicting astronomical
events. Ptolemy's Almagest is a witness to that. In the service of mathematical
astronomy, such a theory will even help us compare temporal magnitudes and ratios
of such magnitudes. For example, it may help us determine whether the time between
the most recent solar eclipse visible in Montreal, Quebec and the previous one was
longer or shorter than the time between the previous solar eclipse visible in Montreal,
Quebec and the one before that. But a general theory of magnitudes and their
relations as such is not cosmological in Kant's sense; nor indeed are the mathematical
principles of the understanding that he takes to be its ultimate foundation. For these
principles cannot as such make it possible to determine a priori the temporal
relations of appearances, as far as their existence is concerned. For that, the under-
standing must call upon principles other than the Axioms of Intuition and Antici-
pations of Perception. So Kant claims, having in mind the Analogies of Experience.

But why are temporal relations so important? They are precisely the ones that
define the structure of our world as a world. They include not only duration, as when
we say that the unrefrigerated ice-cream took only ten minutes to melt; nor only
succession, as when we say that the burning of Moscow in 1812 happened after the
arrival of Napoleon's troops in the city. They also include simultaneity, the relation of
appearances that coexist at different places at the same time. The Analogies give us
rules a priori for bringing the existence of appearances (as empirical intuitions) under
all three temporal relations. Just to that extent, they can make a world out of
appearances.

It is perhaps the relation of simultaneity and its corresponding rule whose cosmo-
logical significance is most readily apparent. Kant himself calls attention to it in a
footnote to his concluding summation of the Analogies. There he says that

> the unity of the world-whole in which all appearances are supposed to be connected is
> obviously just an inference from the [sc. until now —AL] tacitly accepted principle of the
> community of all substances that are simultaneous. For if they were isolated, they would not, as
> parts, constitute a whole; and, if their connection (reciprocal influence of the manifold) were
> not already necessary for the sake of simultaneity, one could not infer from the latter, as a
> merely ideal relation, to the former as a real one. (B268n)

The thought expressed here is closely related to that expressed in a footnote to
Section Four of the inaugural dissertation and that I discussed in §2b of
Chapter Four. Simultaneous coexistence is not what we get if the order of succession
has been suspended. For it is conceivable that succession could be suspended and that
things would no longer relate to one another at all, in which case they would cease to
be parts of any world whatsoever: 'For, by withdrawing succession, there is somehow
removed a certain conjunction that prevailed through the series of time. But there
does not arise another true relation, such as the conjunction of all things at the same
moment. For simultaneous things are at the same moment of time, just as successive
things are joined at different moments of time ' (2.407.48–64). Simultaneity is just as
much a temporal relation as succession. If, then, the order of succession should be
suspended, the special temporal relation of coexisting at the same moment would
have to come into play for anything to be simultaneous with anything else. If that did
not happen, things would be completely cut off from one another. They would cease
to be parts of one and the same world. Indeed, the world would cease to exist as a
world. If, then, we have means a priori for determining the relation among appear-
ances of simultaneous coexistence, we may infer that appearances that coexist at the
same moment are parts of one and the same sensible world and indeed that this
world is not an ideal cosmological whole, as Kant observes in the footnote to B268 of
the Critique quoted above, but a real one.

The remarks in the preceding paragraph should not be taken to imply that
succession is any less cosmological than simultaneity. After all, we can run the
thought experiment in the passage from the inaugural dissertation quoted above
the other way around. Suppose the order of simultaneous coexistence were sus-
pended. It would not then follow automatically that the temporal relation of succes-
sion would come into play. If it did not, then the world as it existed before the
suspending of simultaneity could not advance into its own future. Indeed, no future
would be left for it. A world as such is not just whatever may coexist at a given
moment; it is all those things as they collectively advance into a shared future, having
collectively emerged out of a shared past. It must be possible to situate them all on a
common time-line. That would not be possible without the order of succession.
Hence, the order of succession is just as cosmological as the order of simultaneous
coexistence. Kant stresses simultaneity in both of the footnotes quoted above just
because his insight about it was not obvious to his contemporaries: they did not
recognize, as he did, that the order of simultaneous coexistence is not just space, but a
temporal relation in its own right —just like succession. But once we have come
round to Kant's insight, we must acknowledge that both succession and simultaneity
are not just temporal relations; they are the relations constitutive of the sensible
world as such: nature as a composite of matter and form. Duration will also come
along for the ride. For processes and states of affairs cannot have a duration unless
they can be situated on the common time-line, with respect to some enduring
temporal point of reference, and thereby recognized as parts of the sensible world.

Though the Axioms of Intuition and Anticipations of Perception give us the
resources to compare and relate appearances as magnitudes, they do not themselves
give us the resources to relate them as existences in either the order of succession or
the order of coexistence. They make it possible for us to compare durations as
magnitudes and compare the ratios of such magnitudes. But they are not themselves
the kind of principle that makes duration as such possible (see below). Nor do they as
such make possible succession or simultaneity —even though some of the temporal
magnitudes they help us compare may succeed one another and others may coexist at
the same moment. Because the Axioms of Intuition and Anticipations of Perception
have no special jurisdiction over any temporal relation as such, they do not count as
laws of nature. They have no immediate cosmological significance of their own, even
if they are useful for mathematical astronomy. That is why they cannot be said to
make nature possible. Does it follow, then, that the Transcendental Deduction has
neglected to give them any title over appearances and thereby left it in doubt whether
the categories of quality and quantity might be 'empty, null and without meaning '?
The answer must be, No. But it will also depend on spelling out how these categories
relate to the categories of relation.

I take the Transcendental Deduction to be a class action legal brief that is supposed
to vindicate any pure concept of the understanding that can be legitimately derived
from the Table of the Logical Functions of Judgement. But it does not seem to apply
to all the categories in the same way. It will vindicate the relational categories directly,
for they are the categories that can be shown to prescribe laws to nature. It will
vindicate the other categories indirectly, if it can be shown either that they somehow
depend on, or that they are somehow in the service of, their relational counterparts
and the legislation carried out through them by the understanding (here I continue to
focus on the categories of quantity and quality). Preliminary confirmation of my
suggestion may perhaps be found in remarks Kant makes in §22 of the B-Deduction
about mathematics and the question whether, in itself, mathematics may count as
knowledge.

The passage I have in mind opens with the claim that thinking an object and
knowing an object are not the same thing. Knowing an object requires a concept
through which the object may be thought and an intuition through which it is given.
For without an intuition, the concept will count as thought, so far as its form goes
(der Form nach), but it will be 'without any object and through it no knowledge of
any thing will be possible ' (B146). But, the argument continues, all intuition possible
for us is sensible (sinnlich), and 'sensible intuition is either pure intuition (space and
time) or empirical intuition of that which is immediately represented through
sensation as real [wirklich] in space and time ' (B147). Kant now claims that 'through
the determination of the former [sc. pure intuition —AL], we can have knowledge a
priori of objects (in mathematics) as appearances, but only as far as their form goes;
whether there could be things that must be intuited in this form still remains however
an open question ' (B147). This claim is now supposed to lead to the following
preliminary conclusion: 'As a result, no mathematical concept —so far as it goes
[für sich]—is knowledge, except insofar as one assumes that there are things that may
be set out only in accordance with the form of that pure intuition ' (B147). This
conclusion is tantamount in context to the claim that mathematics is not knowledge
unless it has some kind of empirical application. I regard this claim as indefensible.[^8]

It matters for my purposes just because Kant uses it to draw the following general
inference about the categories:

> As a result, the pure concepts of the understanding yield knowledge, even if they are applied to
> intuitions a priori (as in mathematics), only insofar as they [sc. the pure intuitions —AL],
> therewith too the concepts of the understanding by means of them, can be applied to empirical
> intuitions. As a result, the categories, by means of intuition, give us no knowledge of things
> except through their possible application to empirical intuition, i.e., they serve only for the
> possibility of empirical knowledge. (B147)

The result is thus that the categories cannot yield any knowledge unless they apply to
empirical intuition and hence perception. It (this result) has important implications
for the categories of quantity and quality.

For we saw that their associated principles, the Axioms of Intuition and Antici-
pations of Perception, do not target empirical intuition, as the Analogies of Experi-
ence do, but rather pure intuition. This is because their task is to make it possible
for us to construct the possibility of appearances, i.e., to construct their extensive
and intensive magnitudes. But the passage we have been reviewing from §22 of the
B-Deduction indicates prospectively that the Axioms of Intuition and Anticipations
of Perception do not as such give us any knowledge of anything. Indeed, neither they
nor the constructions they make possible will count as anything more than an
'engagement with a mere phantom of the brain (Hirngespinnste)' (B149). They will
yield knowledge only if they relate somehow to the possibility of experience, i.e., can
be shown to have some kind of application or relevance to empirical intuition and
perception. Such an application will not consist in connecting perceptions or

Empirical laws can only be discovered through experience. That is what makes
them empirical. Kant insists on this point at the end of §26 in the B-Deduction:

> Even the pure faculty of understanding suffices on its own to prescribe laws a priori to
> appearances through mere categories only for those laws upon which rests a nature as
> such . . . , and for no other laws save these. Because particular laws concern empirically
> determinate appearances, they cannot be completely derived therefrom [sc. from the laws
> upon which rests a nature as such —AL], though they [sc. the particular laws —AL] all stand
> under them. Experience is needed, in addition, to discover them. (B165)

Though empirical laws of nature can be discovered only through experience, they
nevertheless stand under the 'laws of a nature as such ', i.e., the Analogies of
Experience, as the conditions of their possibility. That is what will make them laws.
This suggests that empirical laws of nature are specifications of the Analogies of
Experience. Kant himself uses the language of 'specification' in a parallel passage of
the A-Deduction. Thus he writes at A127–128: 'To be sure, empirical laws can as
such in no way derive their origin from pure understanding . . . . But all empirical laws
are just particular determinations [besondere Bestimmungen] of the pure laws of the
understanding under which, and according to whose norm, they are first possible and
appearances take on a lawful form.' I take the expression 'particular determination' to
mean the same thing as 'specification', which leads me to the following observation.

In many cases, the statement of an empirical law will make explicit mention of
specific magnitudes and their ratios. We see this, for example, in the statement of
Newton's law of universal gravitation which relates the magnitudes of gravitational
forces to each other directly as the magnitudes of the masses of attracting bodies and
indirectly as the squares of the magnitudes of the distances. This law will count as an
empirical specification of the Third Analogy (because it involves a relation of
reciprocal influence), and yet it would be unthinkable without the mathematical
principles of the understanding. For they are the principles that make it possible
for us to understand the theory of magnitudes and apply it to appearances (as
empirical intuition or perception). This observation suggests that the Transcen-
dental Deduction will give title over appearances to the categories of quantity and
quality not because they prescribe laws to nature, as the categories of relation do,
but rather because they help make it possible for the laws prescribed by the categories of
relation to be specified empirically—just to the extent that the empirical specification
of these laws will so often appeal to specific kinds of magnitudes and their ratios.

Now perhaps it might be thought as well that the mathematical principles help
make possible the statement of the Analogies themselves —or, anyway, that of the
first Analogy. For, at least in the second edition of the Critique, the principle of
the first Analogy seems to appeal directly, in its statement, to the concept of
magnitude. It reads as follows: 'substance persists in all alteration of appearances,
and the quantum of the same is neither increased nor diminished in nature ' (B224).
'Quantum' is just the Latin word for magnitude. The first Analogy is stated as a
conservation principle. A certain magnitude is conserved, namely the total quantity
of substance in the world. We must surely take this quantity to be an extensive
magnitude. Surely? Well, what would it even mean to speak of the degree of substance?
Can substance be more or less intense, as heat or illumination can? But, in that case,
we must apparently infer that the Axioms of Intuition play a role in stating the
principle of the first Analogy. If so, their assistance to legislation by the under-
standing is even more significant than I suggested in the previous paragraph. But so
significant that the first Analogy would be as little conceivable without them as
Newton's law of universal gravitation?

Let me say frankly that I do not know how to deal with this question to my
complete satisfaction. Then let me just add two further observations that may help
sketch part of an answer.

The first observation is that the mention of magnitude in the statement of the first
Analogy is surely different from that of magnitudes in the statement of any of the
empirical laws of nature we may think of. Perhaps that is why Kant uses the Latin
'quantum' for magnitude in its statement rather than the more usual German
'Größe'.[^9] In the statement of the first Analogy, the mention of 'quantum' seems to
be nothing more than a place-holder. We really do not know what sort of magnitude
is conserved. I am not even sure that we know, from the statement alone, that it is an
extensive magnitude: the only reason for thinking it might be is how silly it seems to
suppose that it could be an intensive magnitude. By contrast, the statement of an
empirical law of nature, like Newton's law of universal gravitation, mentions specific
kinds of magnitude: for example, forces, accelerations, distances, and masses.
Moreover, it will tell us precisely how these magnitudes relate to one another. For
example, it will tell us that magnitudes of one kind relate to each other directly as the
magnitudes of some other kind and inversely as the squares of some other kind still.
The statement of the first Analogy tells us only that some magnitude —otherwise
completely unspecified except that it is the magnitude of substance —never increases
or diminishes. Specification of the kind of magnitude will, I suppose, depend on
the different ways the first Analogy itself can be specified empirically. The first
Analogy as such —precisely because it is a universal law that the category of
substance prescribes to nature a priori —cannot specify itself. Thus it must hold
open a place in its statement for mention of the relevant kind of magnitude
whenever it is applied to appearances in the statement and cognition of the relevant
special empirical laws. It would be wrong, then, to inflate the importance of the
mathematical principles of the understanding for the statement of the first Analogy:
they do not make the first Analogy possible; rather the first Analogy makes room for
them to kick in as the need arises.

The second observation elaborates on the first. In the proof of the first Analogy, we
learn that the permanent —that whose 'quantum' never increases or diminishes —
makes it possible to assign a very specific kind of extensive magnitude to appearances
(as empirical intuition), namely duration. Kant is explicit about this, and he now
uses the more usual German 'Größe' instead of the Latin 'quantum'. For he says that

'through the permanent alone can existence in different successive parts of the
sequence of time come to have a magnitude [Größe] that we call duration' (B226).
I take this claim to indicate that the first Analogy sets the stage for a special
application of the Axioms of Intuition.

Precisely because the first Analogy makes it possible to assign duration to appear-
ances in the sequence of time, it makes possible our use of the Axioms of Intuition
for the construction and comparison of durations, the manipulation of their ratios,
and the comparison of these ratios with the ratios of other magnitudes —for example,
in the statement of Galileo's kinematic law of falling bodies. That law is stated in
Day Three of the Discorsi as a proportion: the distances through which bodies fall,
starting from rest, are to each other as the squares of the times. Kant would say, as
I understand him, that the Axioms of Intuition make it possible for us to apply the
relevant body of mathematics to appearances to help support this law, i.e., by
constructing the proportion expressed by Galileo's law as the result of mathematic-
ally manipulating the proportion he states earlier in the Discorsi as the so-called
Mean Speed Theorem and the proportion he states earlier still as the definition of
naturally accelerated motion (the final speeds of bodies falling from rest are as the
times, not the distances). The reader may consult Day Three of the Discorsi for the
details of Galileo's argument. All that matters, for my purposes, is that this argument
depends in part on a mathematical theory of magnitudes —taken (with some reser-
vations by Galileo) from Book Five of Euclid's Elements. For Kant will surely say that
this theory ultimately rests on the mathematical principles of the understanding. He
will also say just as surely that Galileo would never have had the opportunity to apply
this mathematical theory to the theory of uniformly accelerated motion were it not
possible, as a matter of principle, to assign durations (as magnitudes) to appearances
in the sequence of time. The first Analogy guarantees that this is possible, and thus it
gives Galileo (and not just him) scope and opportunity to use the Axioms of Intuition
in the science of kinematics.

I would like to make one final observation, this time about the relation between
all the mathematical principles of the understanding and all three Analogies of
Experience. It will generalize the observation I just made about the relation between
the first Analogy and the Axioms of Intuition. Our review of §22 of the B-Deduction
suggests that, in general, the mathematical principles of the understanding gain title
over appearances because of the title over appearances secured by all three Analogies
as universal laws of nature. For suppose we had only the Axioms of Intuition and
Anticipations of Perception, but not the Analogies of Experience. We would then
have no experience, no nature, no sensible world, because we would then have no
categories that could prescribe laws to nature. We could not use the Axioms of
Intuition and Anticipations of Perception for anything, much less to construct
magnitudes a priori in pure intuition, because a swarm of appearances would
crowd upon our soul and be even less than a dream to us. It may well be that,
confronted with such a swarm, we could never sufficiently collect our thoughts to
carry out constructions in pure intuition in a coherent way. But even if we could,
neither the mathematical principles of the understanding nor the constructions in
pure intuitions we carried out by their means would yield any knowledge or have any
objective reality. For §22 of the B-Deduction teaches us, in effect, that their claim to
yield knowledge or to have objective reality will always depend on whether they can
be applied to empirical intuitions. But I take it that, unless the Analogies of Experi-
ence organize appearances (as empirical intuitions) into a world, no such application
would be possible. For how can a swarm of anything —much less one of appearances
as empirical intuitions —be mathematized? On the assumption that swarms cannot
be mathematized, the Axioms of Intuition and Anticipations of Perception gain title
to appearances from the argument of the Transcendental Deduction just because it
follows from this argument that the Analogies of Experience are the laws of the
understanding that make nature possible.

But that raises still more questions. What about the categories of modality? How
does Kant think that they get title to appearances? Do they legislate laws to nature
like the categories of relation? The principles associated with the categories of
modality are the so-called Postulates of Empirical Thought. Are they themselves
laws of nature like the Analogies of Experience? No, because the categories of
modality do not even purport to relate to objects as such. Kant says that they 'express
only the relation [sc. of a concept —AL] to the faculty of knowledge ' (B266). He
clarifies as follows:

> If the concept of a thing is already fully complete, I can still ask of this object whether it is
> merely possible or also actual or, if actual, whether it is also necessary. No further determin-
> ation is thereby thought in the object itself; rather, the question is only if it (together with all its
> determinations) relates to the understanding and its empirical use, to empirical judgement and
> to reason (in its application to experience). (B266)

Kant says, moreover, that:

> The principles of modality are not objectively synthetic, because the predicates of possibility,
> actuality and necessity do not in the slightest extend the concept of which they are predicated
> by adding anything to the representation of the [sc. concept's—AL] object. But since they are
> nevertheless synthetic, they are so only subjectively, i.e., they add to the concept of a thing
> (real), of which they otherwise say nothing, the faculty of knowledge in which it originates and
> has its seat. Thus if it is in the understanding, in connection with the formal conditions of
> experience, its object is called possible. If it is connected with perception (sensation as the
> matter of the senses) and is determined through the same by means of the understanding, then
> the object is actual. If it is determined through the connection of perceptions according to
> conceptions, then the object is called necessary. Thus the principles of modality say, of a
> concept, nothing other than the operation [Handlung] of the faculty of knowledge through
> which it is produced. (B266–267)

We have already touched on modality. We saw that Kant associates possibility with
any synthesis carried out by the understanding (aided by the productive imagination)
on a manifold of pure intuition. The Postulates do nothing more than say explicitly
that all concepts produced under these conditions express possibilities. We also saw
that Kant associates existence with any synthesis carried out by the understanding
(aided by the productive imagination) on a manifold of empirical intuition. The
Postulates say explicitly that all concepts produced under these conditions express
actualities. The Postulates also say explicitly that all concepts produced under these
conditions, and subject to the Analogies of Experience, express necessities. This
means that the Postulates are not themselves laws, but rather the principles by
means of which we can distinguish between laws of nature and their consequences
and statements of other truths that cannot count either as laws of nature or their
consequences. By implication from all of the above, the only laws of nature pre-
scribed by the understanding are the Analogies of Experience.

I do not wish to dwell on the Postulates of Empirical Thought. I would just like to
point out a further implication of Kant's treatment of them and his treatment of the
mathematical principles of the understanding.

Because the Postulates, Axioms, and Anticipations assist or complement the
Analogies, and even depend on their title to get title of their own over appearances,
Kant may be understood to represent human understanding as governor of the
sensible world —through and through. He should not be taken to say that our
understanding produces a world out of appearances when it subjects them to the
Analogies of Experience —and that it also engages in extra-cosmological activities in
the off hours. Governing the sensible world is not a side-line for it. It can make no
legitimate use of its principles besides the one that assists its cosmological function.
The use of the Axioms and Anticipations for constructing magnitudes will be in vain
unless undertaken in the service of empirically specifying the laws of nature. These
principles may not be used to look for an independent mathematical reality that we
could profitably study while turning our backs on the sensible world. To think
otherwise, for Kant, is Platonic Schwärmerei. By the same token, the principles of
modality may not be used to carve out a field of possibles, independent of what is
actual and even necessary in experience, upon which we might unleash our specu-
lative curiosity. It is another kind of Schwärmerei to wonder what if . . .? What if we
can communicate with spirits?! Human understanding is governor of the sensible
world. That is a full-time job; holidays from it are not provided for. The activities of
our understanding are legitimate, according to the Transcendental Deduction, only
insofar as they get that job done.[^10]

It remains just to consider briefly the programme suggested by the general
argument for the Analogies of Experience —and its historical significance.

This argument programmatically calls for completing the cosmology of experience
whose foundations were laid in the Transcendental Deduction. The issue will no
longer be how title over all possible appearances may be given to the relational
categories, but rather whether the schematization of these categories may be counted
on to give us the rules a priori for the temporal relations of appearances and thus for
the conversion of appearances into a single, universal sensible world.

The general argument of the Analogies —like that for each of the three in turn —
depends first of all on the premise that time cannot be an object of perception.
This premise is stated without any argument of its own, as though its truth should
be obvious to the reader. I suppose that it is. I can perceive the motion of the
second hand around the face of a ticking clock, but that motion is not itself time.
It is a conventional, though very useful indicator of its passing. Since we cannot

perceive time, we cannot perceive any of its three modes: duration, succession, or

when we ask how this is possible, Kant's answer will be that, as objects of

The final clause of that last sentence is the general principle of the Analogies —at least
as stated in the B-edition. If it does indeed follow from the considerations that
precede it in the passage just quoted, Kant will have proved the thing he set out to
prove. For our purposes, the immediate point of interest is its talk of necessary
connections, because such talk takes us back to §1 of Chapter One.

In §1a of that chapter, I discussed the principle of coexistence, as stated in
Section Three of the *Nova dilucidatio* of 1755, and its cosmological implications.
This principle says that 'Finite substances do not relate to one another at all by their
existence alone, nor are they involved in any interaction, except insofar as they are
sustained, in accordance with their mutual relations, by the common principle of
their existence, namely the divine intellect ' (2.414.69–74.5). It (the principle in
question) imposes a certain condition on membership in a world. Substances do
not belong to the same world —or to any world at all —just because God brought
them into existence. They jointly form a world just in case every one of them
externally relates to every other. This they do just in case God subjected them to
universal laws of community.

We saw that Kant's elaboration of this principle —not just in the *Nova dilucidatio*,
but also in Section Four of the inaugural dissertation —turns on a distinction between
a real world-whole in which true universal harmony prevails and an ideal one in
which we find mere agreement of all the parts. This distinction itself turns on
necessary connections. In an ideal world-whole, as —say—one whose laws of com-
munity conform to the Leibnizian system of pre-established harmony, the external
relations of one creature to another are contingent. They are the artefact of God's
having coordinated the special law that governs the inner nature of one creature with
that of another. Everything that happens to any given creature is the effect of its inner
nature expressing itself under the constraints of this law. Its fate would be unaffected
by the presence or absence of any other creature. To us it will always appear as if the
fate of any given creature is inextricably tied up with that of all the others. But, in the
system of pre-established harmony (at least as Kant understands it), we are deceived
by God's wisdom, because he adapted the parts of the world so perfectly to one
another. This is not to deny all necessary connections here: they will prevail between
any state of any given creature and its earlier states. But the succession of this
creature's inner states does not directly affect the succession of inner states in any
other creature. We may thus regard each creature as a world unto itself. That is why
the early Kant characterizes a world harmoniously pre-established by God as an ideal
world-whole. In a real world-whole, by contrast, the necessary connections will
directly relate the inner state of a given creature to that of its fellows. This is not to
deny that necessary connections will also prevail between present and past states of
things (between their future and present states too). But it does deny that separate
temporal streams will run in tandem —as would seemingly be the case in an ideal
world-whole. In a real world-whole, the past is a collective one, as too the future,
because the order of succession is shared by all. This in turn is because, at any given
moment, the present is also shared by all. For necessary connections will externally
relate all creatures at any given moment in the order of coexistence under the laws of
community governing the world. The condition, of course, is that these laws be laws
of universal real interaction. Any creature subject to these laws will belong to the
world in which they apply, because they will secure its place in the common history
and/or future by connecting it to every other such creature —not contingently, as in
an ideal world-whole, but necessarily —as the effect of the real influence the creatures
of this world exercise on one another. The nature of these laws of real interaction will
depend on what the author of this world has specifically elected for it. But, even at
this level of generality, we can say this much about them (on the early Kant's behalf):
they will not be special laws each governing the nature of a different creature; they
will be universal laws that govern all creatures at once —and thereby help ensure that
the necessary connections they produce apply across the whole world.

The necessary connections handed down by the Analogies of Experience in the
*Critique of Pure Reason* are also meant to produce a real world-whole. This, as we
know, is one whose constitutive parts are not God's creatures, but things as they
sensibly appear to us—subject to the important qualifications we considered in §2b of
Chapter Four to avert cosmological antinomies. It is the world-whole Kant calls
experience. Experience is more than the sum of all possible appearances, just as a
world in Kant's early cosmology is more than the sum of all God's creatures.
Necessary connections are required to convert appearances into experience just as
much as they were required in the early cosmology to convert God's creatures into a
world. In the one case as in the other, these connections are the effect of legislating
the relevant laws of community. But God does not have jurisdiction over the
manifold of appearances given to us under the conditions of our sensibility. Only
we do. The Transcendental Deduction gives us —or rather our understanding —the
right to serve as legislator. In this capacity, it (our understanding) imposes necessary
connections on appearances by 'combination', i.e., by acts of self-activity in the sense
we considered in §1b of Chapter Two. God cannot do the legislating for us; we must
do so, on our own behalf. Though appearances are given to us under the conditions
of our sensibility, their connections are not. The understanding must therefore
actively exercise its legislative rights over them, by taking them up and handing
down to them the relevant necessary connections. The necessary connections estab-
lished under the principle of coexistence, as stated by the Third Analogy, will be
subject to its universal law of mutual influence and thereby cause the relevant
appearances to coexist at the same moment: 'All appearances must stand, in our
mind, in community (communio) of apperception as contained in a possible experi-
ence; insofar as objects are to be represented as connected as existing simultaneously,
they must reciprocally determine their position in time and thereby constitute a
whole' (B261). The necessary connections established under the Second Analogy as
our understanding's universal law of cause and effect will regiment appearances in
the order of succession. The Second and Third Analogies will jointly ensure that all
appearances represented as simultaneously coexisting right now have proceeded out
of a shared history and are advancing together into a shared history. (As we have
seen, the First Analogy lays down the universal law of the understanding that makes
possible as such the determination of how long something lasts.)

We know, of course, that the Analogies in the *Critique of Pure Reason* were not
Kant's first attempt to elaborate in detail a cosmology of experience —in spirit akin to
the general cosmology of the *Nova dilucidatio*, but tailored to our human faculties of
knowledge. In §1b of Chapter One, we saw how Kant tried to do this in the Duisburg
Nachlaß. The problem was how to produce a real or 'objective whole ' out of
appearances, i.e., to convert them into experience. An important part of the solution
was to invoke necessary connections to distinguish between real and ideal or object-
ive and subjective relations among appearances. In R4678, we found that 'The inner
necessity of appearance, since namely the same [sc. this necessity —AL] is considered
freed up from everything subjective and is considered . . . as determinable by a
universal rule (of appearances), is the objective ' (17.648.31–38). Appearances can
be united into the objective whole Kant calls experience in the Duisburg Nachlaß if
necessary connections are applied to them —in accordance with three universal laws
governing their relations of existence with respect to duration, succession, and
simultaneity. These laws are the ancestors of the Analogies in the *Critique of Pure
Reason*. As in the Critique, they have their source, in the Duisburg Nachlaß, in special
functions of the understanding that ultimately harken back to pure apperception. In
the Duisburg Nachlaß, Kant calls the conversion of appearances into experience by
means of these functions the 'exposition of appearances '. I see no reason not to use
this expression for the programme of the Analogies of Experience. But there are at
least two important differences between the earlier and the later treatment of the
programme.

First, an exposition of appearances needs to call on the imagination and —more
specifically—its capacity to produce images a priori. We saw that, in classical
geometry, the ekthesis, expositio, or setting out of a general proposition we wish to
prove involves presenting a representative special case in a labelled diagram. The
Kantian exposition of appearances will set out a special case of the universal laws of
nature—with the aid of pure images of space, time, and space and time mixed
together. It involves a cartography of the sensible world, as I argued in §2 of
Chapter Four. The Analogies of Experience are the schematization of the categories
of relation. They provide appearances with necessary connections —and thus provide
the understanding with means drawn from the productive imagination to register
graphically the way appearances externally relate to one another in the sensible
world. One of the striking deficits of the Duisburg Nachlaß is that it has nothing to
say about image-making in experience. The exposition of appearances laid out in the
Analogies of the Critique is thus more complete.

Second of all, it has jettisoned the metaphysical commitment of the Duisburg
Nachlaß to the idea —so important to Kant's earlier self —that external relations
among things and knowledge of such relations are always grounded on something
simple and on the knowledge of that simple thing as such. In §1b of Chapter One, we
explored this commitment and the role it plays in the Duisburg Nachlaß. We saw
that it leads Kant in the Duisburg Nachlaß to treat apperception as a direct intellec-
tual insight into the thinking subject as a simple substance. We also saw that this
move was motivated at least in part by the concern to adapt the early general
cosmology to the special human case of producing a world out of appearances. The
early Kant had argued that, though creatures do not form a world just because their
creator has brought them into existence, they will not form a world unless they are all
creatures of one and the same creator —a creator who exhibits internal unity and
numerical identity. Simplicity in short. If the cosmology of experience is going to
reinterpret general cosmology in human terms, it must take God out of the picture
and make the human mind or intellect the governor of the sensible world.[^11] But, as
governor of any kind of world, it must exhibit the same internal unity and numerical
identity. As we saw in §1d of Chapter Two, the account of pure apperception in §16
of the B-Deduction tries to satisfy this condition without importing the metaphysics
of the thinking subject that had animated the Duisburg Nachlaß.

If we take the two points of difference that I just mentioned to be corrections to the
Duisburg Nachlaß by the *Critique of Pure Reason*, then the conclusion must be that
Critique has tried to salvage as much as it could of this first attempt at a cosmology of
experience. That observation suggests in turn that the Critique—like the Duisburg
Nachlaß—has made a curious judgement about Kant's early general cosmology: that
cosmology is perfectly true, but not as a dogmatic account of what it takes for a bunch
of things in themselves to constitute a world; it gives us instead the right way of
understanding the relation of our own understanding to the manifold of appearances
given to it under the conditions of our human sensibility.
