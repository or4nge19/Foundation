## 5 Empirical Self-Knowledge (§§24–25)

### Cartography and Autobiography

In the middle of §24 of the B-Deduction, Kant interrupts the argument to resolve a
paradox. He announces, ‘Here is the place to make intelligible the paradox that must
have struck everyone in the exposition of the form of inner sense ’ (B155). Kant is
wrong about at least one thing. The second half of §24(and what follows in §25) is
not the place for this. However precisely the relevant paradox should be stated, its
solution in full depends on the details of §26, because it concerns empirical knowledge of myself and such knowledge is possible under the same conditions as
empirical knowledge of anything else. We learned from §26 of the B-Deduction
what these conditions are: the cartography of the sensible world and the universal
laws of nature at its foundation. I am going to argue that empirical knowledge of
myself depends on the former (and hence on the latter), because it involves situating
my personal history (including that of my inner life) in the history of the world in
which I find myself.[^1] That is why it would have been better if Kant had treated both
the paradox and its solution after §26. But I acknowledge that that order of presentation would not have been ideal either. Having reached §26, readers would have felt
as if they had gone the whole day with their shoe laces undone, but would also have

Having reached §26, readers would have felt no need to tie them up now: their natural inclination, after having slogged
through the difficult argument of §26, would have been to take their shoes off and
put their feet up. The problem raised and allegedly solved in §24 and §25 is very
interesting indeed, but I take it to be a secondary problem. For that reason, we can do
no better than to follow Kant’s cue and discuss the problem itself at some length—but
here in this final chapter, even at the risk of disrupting Kant ’s own order of
presentation. This chapter has two sections: one devoted to the paradox and how
to understand it; the other devoted to its solution.
### §1 The Paradox
### §1a As considered from the perspective of inner sense
We must consider first of all how the paradox should be understood. The remark just
quoted from §24 at B155 contains an explicit back reference to §8 of the Transcen-
dental Aesthetic. But it is more naturally taken as referring to an addition of the
B-edition to the ‘general remarks’ (Allgemeine Anmerkungen) in §8, at the very end of
the Transcendental Aesthetic. This addition runs from B67 to B69. It is prefixed by
the Roman numeral II, which suggests that we may take it as the second ‘general
remark’ (of which there are four in the second edition). Its concern is to offer a
justification for Kant’s ‘theory of the ideality of outer as well as inner sense, and hence
of all objects of the senses as mere appearances ’ (B67). In the second half of this
second remark, Kant spells out the problem associated with inner sense that he will
later address in §24 of the B-Deduction. The passage from the second general remark
of the Transcendental Aesthetic reads (at least to start with) as follows:

> Everything that is represented by a sense is, to that extent, always an appearance. Hence an
inner sense would either have to be rejected or the subject that is its object would have to be
able to be represented by it [sc. inner sense—AL] only as an appearance and not as [the subject]
would judge himself in himself, [as he would] if his intuition were mere self activity, i.e.,
intellectual. In this matter, the whole difficulty rests on [the question] whether a subject can
inwardly intuit himself. (B68)[^2]
It is plain from what follows immediately hereafter that the solution to the difficulty
indicated at the end of the quoted passage rests in turn on whether and how the
subject can ‘affect’ himself (i.e., his mind). For Kant concludes by saying: ‘The subject
intuits himself . . . according to the way in which he affects himself from within, and
hence as he appears to himself, but not as he is in himself ’ (B69). Yet Kant gives no
account here how self-affection is possible. He mentions in passing notions we are
now familiar with from the Transcendental Deduction: apperception, spontaneity,
self-activity, the sensibly given manifold of intuition. But a first-time reader would be
very perplexed, because these notions have not yet been explained. How they in turn
might explain self-affection is a fortiori a mystery.
When Kant states the paradox of inner sense in §24 of the B-Deduction, he
explicitly raises the problem of self-affection. Indeed, he represents it as the source

of the paradox. The passage running from B155 to B156 reads as follows: ‘How does
[inner sense] present even ourselves to consciousness, only as we appear to
ourselves and not as we are in ourselves, because we namely intuit ourselves as
we are inwardly affected, which seems to be contradictory, since we would have to
relate to ourselves as suffering an effect [ leidend]’. In other words, we would have
to relate to ourselves as suffering an effect of our own agency. Hence, there seems
to be a contradiction, because it looks as if we are both the agent and the patient of
our own inner states. That is a contradiction on the assumption that to say I am a
patient in this respect is equivalent to denying that I am an agent in this same
respect: it is seemingly both to affirm P and not-P of myself, namely that I am and
am not the agent of my own inner states.
Having said that, the ‘paradox’, such as it is, seems sophistical. We will find a
contradiction only if we agree that being the patient of our inner states is formally
inconsistent with being the cause of them. But why should we agree to this? If I take
my left hand into my right hand, I will have a sensation of warmth and pressure in
both of them. It seems obvious, and totally unproblematic, that my right hand is
both the agent and the patient of such a sensation and that the same is true of my
left hand. Equally obvious and unproblematic things can be said about my self and
my inner states.
Suppose that, as I am going about my business, I am suddenly so startled or
surprised by something —it could be a noise from the street or a cyclist who rides by
me, too close and fast for comfort —that my very state of surprise grabs my attention
in such a way that I become for a time completely absorbed by it. To be sure, I can
blame the cyclist or the car horn for having surprised me in the first place. But
I cannot directly blame either of them for the specific effect that my surprise as such
has on me and perhaps continues to have on me for some time thereafter: I may, for
example, be surprised that I am so surprised. Though I might well have been self-
oblivious before, I am now fully aware of myself as being surprised. By virtue of the
intensity and suddenness of my surprise, and the second-order surprise that it may in
turn provoke, I will be the cause of my self-awareness and will be agent to that extent.
But to the extent that the surprise has forced this self-awareness upon me, I will also
be the patient of my inner state. Where is the contradiction in this?
The answer to my question surely turns on Kant ’s characterization of inner sense
in the Transcendental Aesthetic as part and parcel of human sensibility and sens-
ibility as a faculty of receptivity, i.e., as a capacity to be affected by objects. This means
that, thanks to inner sense, we can be affected by ourselves, i.e., we have a passive
receptivity for our own inner states. But this is also supposed to mean, in the nature
of the case, that our inner states draw attention to themselves. They force us to take
notice of them; they do so, it would seem, by affecting our inner sense. This implies in
turn that our inner states have a kind of active power and that we and our inner sense
can suffer its effects. But how can our inner states have any such power at all, given
that they are nothing but modifications of our sensibility, which is purely passive? If
they cannot act and thereby affect us, how can our sensibility indicate to us what
inner states we have? Will it not turn out that all my inner states are like the condition
of being hypertensive: they will collectively form my condition without my ever being
subjectively aware of what my condition is? Or perhaps the way to ask the question is

this. Since inner sense is passive, it cannot affect itself. But something must affect it,
precisely because we have inner states and inner sense apprises us of what they are.
What, then, affects inner sense, if not inner sense and our inner states? Kant ’s answer
to this question in §24 at B157(and likewise, in more veiled terms, at B69in the
second general remark of §8of the Transcendental Aesthetic) is: the understanding.
Indeed, that is precisely what makes it self-affection, for I am my understanding.
As Kant himself puts it: ‘That which determines inner sense is the understanding
and its original capacity for combining the manifold of intuition, i.e., for bringing
it under an apperception (as that upon which rests even its own [sc. the
understanding’s—AL] possibility ’. What follows is a multi-clause, run-on sentence
that stresses the difference between the understanding and sensibility —in terms now
familiar to us from §15 to §21of the B-Deduction. That sentence may be paraphrased
as follows. The understanding is not a faculty of intuition, nor is sensibility able to
synthesize the manifold given in intuition. Unless a manifold of intuition is given
to us in sensibility, the understanding will have nothing to synthesize; the manifold
of intuition will not be synthesized, unless sensibility passively submits to the
activity of the understanding. It is thus the understanding —in association with
the imagination —that affects inner sense, inasmuch as it synthesizes the relevant
manifold of the relevant intuition. As Kant himself puts it: ‘Thus [the understand-
ing] exerts on the passive subject, whose faculty it is, that operation [ Handlung]—
called transcendental synthesis of the imagination —of which we may rightly say that
inner sense is affected thereby ’ (B156). If, then, the paradox under discussion is
indeed closely associated with Kant ’s characterization of sensibility as a receptive
faculty, the solution will turn on Kant ’s characterization of the understanding as an
active faculty (and its special relation with the imagination).
We will return to the solution in due course. For now, it may be well to observe
that Kant ’s own diagnosis of the paradox seems to have changed somewhat —or,
anyway, to have undergone a shift in focus. In the second ‘general remark ’ in §8of
the Transcendental Aesthetic, Kant says that ‘the whole difficulty rests on this:
namely how a subject can inwardly intuit himself. But this difficulty is common to
every theory’ (B69).[^3] Every theory of inner sense —or self-apprehension, to put it in a
more philosophically neutral way —is faced with a certain difficulty (not specified
here). But it looks as if Kant himself takes the paradox addressed in §24 in the B-
Deduction to be of concern specifically—indeed, perhaps solely —to his own theory
in the Critique. As well he should. For not every theory of inner sense depends on the
characterization of sensibility as a purely receptive faculty. A sign that Kant himself
now sees things this way in §24is that, after having presented the paradox in the
passage from B157I quoted above, he says, ‘For this reason, the preference in the
systems of psychology has been to treat inner sense and the faculty of apperception,
which we ourselves carefully distinguish, as one and the same ’ (B156). I take Kant
here to mean this. As long as inner sense and the faculty of apperception are treated
as one and the same, our capacity to take notice of ourselves is not a purely passive,
purely receptive faculty. Rather, it is partly passive and partly active, and hence there

may be difficult under this assumption (indeed very difficult), but not paradoxical. It
apparently becomes outright paradoxical only once we accept Kant ’s characterization
of inner sense in particular and sensibility in general (and before we learn anything

The reasons for the shift in focus from the second general remark in §8in the
Transcendental Aesthetic (B67–63) to §24 of the B-Deduction are not immediately
obvious. Perhaps the explanation is just that Kant has more sharply articulated, in the
later part of the book, what he takes to be the difficulty associated with self-affection
that he himself now needs to address. But once the shift has been noted, it can help us
see that Kant is directly engaging in §24 of the B-Deduction with his own ideas in the
Critique. The nature of this engagement can be seen, first of all, in light of the contrast
with these ideas that emerges from passages in his anthropology lectures from the
early 1770s. (Of course, a contrast with these same ideas also emerges from the works
of Kant’s contemporaries and predecessors. But if we wanted to use any of them to
play up the contrast, we would ultimately have to work out which of these contem-
poraries and predecessors, and which of their works, Kant could have read and might
have been thinking about, whereas we can safely suppose that he was still aware in
earlier. This assumption will simplify things for us.)
### §1b Self-affection does not always lead to paradox —especially
if inner sense and apperception are conflated
In the Collins lectures on anthropology from the Wintersemester of 1772/1773, Kant
is reported as having said, without comment and without any recognition of having
ventured on to difficult ground, that ‘we find in our soul two sides, as it were: one by
virtue of which it [sc. the soul —AL] is passive [ leidend]; the other, by virtue of which
it is active [ thätig]’ (25.48.1–2). The passage continues as follows: ‘In the first case
[sc. where the soul is passive —AL], I am a play [ ein Spiel] of all the impressions that
happen upon me from nature [ die auch mich von der Natur geschehen ]; in the other
case, I am a free, self-active [ selbstthätiges] principium’ (25.48.2–44). This remark, so
far as it goes, could easily be developed in directions that would take us, Kant, and his
students away from the issues of self-awareness that we have been discussing. By
passivity of the soul, Kant could (and did) have in mind the effects on the soul of
objects acting on it from without. That sort of passivity would certainly involve
affection, but not self-affection. Still, self-affection seems to have been very much at
the centre of this early part of the anthropology lectures in the 1770s. This can be
seen in the light of Kant ’s explicit, though idiosyncratic, appeal to elements of Stoic
psychology and ethics. (I am not going to go out of my way to argue that Kant is
appealing to Stoicism: it ’s all over the place in the anthropology lectures from this
time. Were one in any doubt about this, one would only need to look at the different
transcripts: the word ‘Stoic’ appears with enough frequency to put the doubt to rest.)
sense faces the same difficulty of explaining how self-affection is possible.

The familiar Stoic thought at issue here is that human beings —unlike the non-
human animals —are capable of a distinctively human sort of misery ( Elend) that
depends on freely making false judgements about what is good and what is bad.
Suppose you come down with some disease that causes you to suffer all sorts of pain
and discomfort.5You will not fail to feel this pain and discomfort, and to that extent
you or your soul is indeed purely passive. But Kant and his Stoic sources will deny
that the pain and the discomfort you experience is the source of any distinctively
human misery you may also feel. Assuming that you are indeed miserable, this will be
due to the fact that you judge your pain and discomfort to be a bad thing, and —as a
result of this judgement —you freely produce in yourself a feeling of desolation at the
thought of yourself as having to endure something bad. If we are Stoics (and perhaps
even if we are not), we will recognize that your desolation is not the same as the pain
and discomfort you experience because of your illness. But, then, as Stoics, we will say
that though the pain and discomfort you experience are inevitable as long as you are
not cured, your desolation is entirely ‘up to you ’. This is just another way of saying
that you or your soul is ‘self-active’, acting—as it apparently does—on your own state
of psychic well- or ill-being. The proof of the soul ’s capacity for self-activity is that if
you are sufficiently philosophical, you can come to see by the light of your own
reason the force of arguments (that ultimately come from Plato ’s Socrates with
variants due to the different heads and members of the Stoic school) according to
which pain and discomfort are not bad in themselves —any more than death itself. If
you can really see the force of these arguments (and it does not matter what they
might be for present purposes), you will free yourself from your desolation, even if
that alone will not relieve your pain and discomfort. As Kant himself is reported as
having explained to his students in the Wintersemester of 1772/1773, very much in
the spirit of the Stoics, ‘all dejectedness [ Betrübniß] comes for the most part from
this: one makes a great deal of the importance of life. A sage regards everything in the
world, even his own life, as unimportant. That helps him outweigh and react [ reagirt]
against strong sensations ’ (25.42.48–42).[^6] The Stoic elements in the early anthropology lectures indicate that Kant took the
active capacity of the soul to be (at least in some situations) a capacity for self-
affection. The lecture transcripts edited in volume of the Academy edition indicate
that he would sometimes signal this (e.g., in the last sentence of the passage just
quoted) by characterizing the mind as ‘reacting’ to its own states, i.e., to those states
or sensations it passively endures as a result of our interaction with the external
environment. The reaction at issue always seems (as at the end of the passage just
quoted) to involve an evaluative judgement according to which we take the relevant
passive state to be good, bad, or indifferent. This judgement colours the perception
we have of our current condition: we now feel encouraged, relieved, indifferent, or
despondent, as the case may be. These feelings are the effect of our own self-affection.

role in that history. Kant is indeed faced with a paradox of his own making. He is now

enduring, episodes of hunger and the like and that of other people who know the
details of my life as bystanders to it. The answer, I think, is that the time-line and
maps of my life drawn up by others will differ from the ones I draw up for myself in
the way that biography differs from autobiography. A biographer can lay out the
time-line of important events in a person ’s life and indicate the places where those
events happened, perhaps even overtly including maps to help readers follow the
story. Those events will be laid out on the same time-line and set in the same places
on the same maps we might find in that person ’s autobiography, so long as the
biographer and the subject of the biography are in agreement. However, the auto-
biography will also assign positions on the time-line and on the relevant maps to
episodes of hunger, pain, and the like. This idea requires elaboration.
An autobiography is a formal record of a person ’s empirical self-knowledge. Such
knowledge does not just announce the having of hunger, pain, or pleasure; it also
indicates when I was beset by hunger and where I was at the time. Those places and
times matter just as much to the life of a person as the episodes themselves. A patient
who consults a physician about certain symptoms will be asked not only to describe
them, but also to give a narrative about them When and under what circumstances
did they first occur; when and under what circumstances do they occur now? Do they
coincide with the arrival of cold fronts? Are they more severe when atmospheric
pressure is low and the humidex is high? The narrative will make references to times
and places—not just with respect to the patient and his inner states as such, but also
the things around him at those times and places, insofar as they might have an
impact on his well-being. Thus empirical knowledge of myself situates my inner
states in the world. It expresses my ability to determine their external relations to one
another and to the states of things other than myself. It may sound strange to speak of
my inner states as having external relations to anything. But if I have empirical
knowledge of them at all, then I must indeed represent them as having such relations.
How else can I recognize that my hunger yesterday afternoon lasted only twenty
minutes, during which time the Montreal suburb of Brossard was hit by a sudden,
torrential downpour, or that my hunger came upon me as I was sitting in my of fice—
or that I was not just hungry, but hungry for a certain special I know is served at a
certain lunch counter, two blocks from my of fice, from 44:to 48:on weekdays?
Even my dreams externally relate to each other and to things outside me —at any rate,
those I can remember. I can relate to you where I was living when I had the most
unpleasant dream of my life, when I had that dream and under what circumstances.
That dream externally relates to the pleasant dream I had last night by occupying a
different position on the toothed-comb I use, in effect, to chart the course of my life.
That is why even my most unpleasant dream will never be less than a dream to me,
for neither it nor any of my other dreams and inner states will crowd upon my soul
like a swarm of appearances cut loose from the world.
How can these considerations help us understand the difference between biog-
raphy and autobiography? An autobiography records the ‘being-in-the-world’ of its
author’s inner states by determining their external relations to one another and to the
states of things other than the author as differences of position on the toothed-comb
we discussed in § 1b of Chapter Four. A biography can do that too, of course, but it
will have to rely entirely on the available documents and testimony. An

autobiography must also rely on such things, because no one remembers or knows
because its author and its subject are one and the same person. A biography and an
autobiography of the same person could, in principle, coincide in every respect: so
too their arrangement of the toothed-comb that graphically represents the course of
the relevant person’s life. Such perfect coincidence must be possible if indeed we take
seriously the idea that they both claim knowledge about one and the same thing. In
point of fact, however, they will always differ, and not only because they may stress
different aspects of that life. The autobiography will likely be richer in certain details
than the biography. Precisely because its author and subject are the same person,
it will have much more information about that person ’s inner states. The wealth of
its information on this score, rather than its direct access to it, is the important
difference here. As a result, it may, in effect, assign more positions on the toothed-
comb to episodes from the otherwise private inner life of its subject than the
biography can. I will return to the difference between biography and autobiography
in the next subsection: there is more to say about it. Our considerations about image-
making can take us only this far for now.
So much for the images involved in the exposition of myself —as in autobiography.
Now let us return to the concept for which these images are supplied, namely the
empirical concept of myself or the life I have been leading. Both the biographer and
the autobiographer will use this same concept, assuming that they are trying to tell
the life-story of the same person. Consider the German author Alfred Andersch. In

biographical study of him called Alfred Andersch Desertiert: Fahnen flucht und
Literatur ( 4300–438). The central question of this book, as indicated by the title,
is whether Andersch really did desert, as claimed in the autobiography. It is a
serious question, because, after Andersch died in 432, another autobiographical
report of his capture by the Allies surfaced in his papers: Amerikaner—Erster
Eindruck (Americans—a first impression). This document was written in 4300
during his internment. If it is to be believed, Andersch was lost, having been
separated from his unit accidently, when he stumbled on Italian partisans and
American soldiers, weapon in hand. He surrendered —happily so —but as an enemy
combatant, not as a deserter. Thus we may take the authors of the biography to be
testing the claim in Andersch ’s 438utobiography that the concept Deserter may
be predicated of the empirical concept of Alfred Andersch. The biographers refer
to their subject in the third person. But I believe Kant would say that all four
authors are using the same concept, namely the concept Alfred-Andersch whose
object is the man by that name. I do not believe that the conventions governing the
use of personal pronouns affect anything so far.
Even if that much be granted, however, it may still be objected that Kant would not
allow for the concept Alfred-Andersch, because only one thing falls under it. All
concepts are ‘universal with respect to form ’ (A4), he says, and this suggests that

more than one thing will always fall under them. Indeed, he sometimes characterizes
a concept as a representation that ‘holds for many things ’ (B37). But the objection is
not a good one at the end of the day. For Kant explicitly recognizes that we routinely
make judgements whose subject is a singular term, like the judgement that Socrates is
mortal. Moreover, we use such judgements as premises and conclusions of valid
syllogisms. The judgement I just gave as an example is, of course, the conclusion of
the familiar go-to syllogism everybody cites. Kant does not deny that the subject term
of this judgement or that of others like it is a concept. On the contrary, he grants that
the predicate of such judgements relates to the subject in precisely the same way that
the predicate of a universal judgement relates to its subject: in the one case, as in the
other, the predicate is a concept that applies to everything falling under the subject
concept.23 In this, Kant expresses his agreement with the‘logicians’, as he refers to
them. These are his words:
The logicians are right to say that one can treat singular judgements [sc. those whose subject is
a singular term like ‘Socrates’—AL] just like universal judgements when using judgements in
syllogisms [ Vernunftschlüsse]. For precisely because they have no extension [ Umfang], their
predicate cannot be inferred to apply only to some of that which is contained under the
concept of the subject, while being excluded from something else [sc. contained under this
concept –AL]. It [sc. the predicate —AL] holds for that concept [sc. the subject —AL] without
exception, exactly as if it [sc. the subject —AL] were a common concept [gemeingültiger Begriff]
with an extension for whose entire meaning [ Bedeutung] the predicate held true. (B 36)
To be sure, Kant is not in complete agreement with the unnamed logicians. For he
denies that ‘Socrates is mortal’ and ‘All human beings are mortal’ are, in fact, the same
kind of judgement. The latter is a universal judgement, he says, while the former is a
singular one. The difference depends on the concept expressed by the subject term.
More than one thing falls under the subject of a universal judgement, as we noted,
but only one thing falls under the subject of a singular judgement. This is supposed
to imply that the subject of the former has an extension ( Umfang), while that of the
latter does not. This in turn is supposed to justify reserving a special place on Kant ’s
Table for singular judgements —separate from the place reserved for universal ones.
Be that as it may, the point of interest to us is that if Kant is willing to allow for
singular judgements, then he has to allow for the concept Alfred-Andersch, without
which we can form neither the judgement that Alfred Andersch was a deserter from
the Wehrmacht nor its negation.
Now that we have discussed which images and which concepts are involved in the
exposition of myself, we may return to the claim Kant makes in the passage at B154
from §24 of the B-Deduction that I quoted at the beginning of this subsection.
I cannot conceive of a line, he says, without drawing it in thought. I must supply
my concept with an image, and the judgements I make about lines using this concept
will be analogous to the ekthesis in the proof of a geometrical proposition —or more
precisely to the part of the ekthesis that restates the general enunciation in terms of
the representative special case given with the labelled diagram. Ekthesis is involved in
empirical self-knowledge too. Or so Kant claims, against all expectations: Alfred

Assuming that we have a better sense than we did how an exposition of myself
would proceed, the question we must consider now is whether the idea of such an
exposition can help Kant explain how ‘self-affection’ is possible and so solve the
paradox announced in §24 of the B-Deduction.
§ The solution to the paradox
As we saw in § 4of this chapter, the paradox Kant must solve in §24 of the
B-Deduction arises from his distinction between pure apperception and inner
sense: the former makes possible all uses of the understanding, while the latter is
the pure form under which all my inner states are possible as modifications of my
mind. They are different sources of knowledge that are both supposed to contribute
to my empirical knowledge of myself. The question is how. One way to focus the
question is to ask more specifically what role pure apperception is supposed to play.
We know what to say about the productive imagination: its role is to supply the
when’s of my life. The role of pure apperception is to supply an intellectual synthesis.
But how is this supposed to work? We had occasion in §  of Chapter Two to discuss
the relation between synthesis and apperception in a general way. My strategy now is
to reflect on this relation in a more focused way, namely insofar as it is relevant for
empirical self-knowledge. The first step of this strategy is to notice that there may
well be a direct connection between Kant ’s treatment of pure apperception in the
B-Deduction and ‘exposition’ or ekthesis in classical geometry. This observation will
allow me to build on the considerations I presented in § nd thus to bring into focus
the significance of the intellectual synthesis of pure apperception for the exposition of
myself as appearance.

Consider that, in the B-Deduction at least, Kant frequently uses the following
well-known, but peculiar, tag for pure apperception, absent from both the Proleg-
omena and the A-Deduction: he refers to it as ‘the I think ’. He deliberately uses the
definite article, which presumes not only that there is only one ‘I think’, but also that
we—his readers—know what he is talking about. Kant speaks of ‘the I think’ the way
others speak of ‘the Middle Class’, ‘the Dollar’, or ‘the Church’. We may need to study
up on these things, but we have an idea what is at issue when we hear such talk
about them. Kant ’s talk about pure apperception, using the distinctive tag of the
B-Deduction, seems to follow the same pattern —even if, as is probable, it is always
short for ‘the I think that must be able to accompany all my representations ’. This is a
puzzle, because no one who reads the B-Deduction for the first time gets this talk of
‘the I think ’ as one might get talk about ‘the Middle Class ’. Even after first-time
readers reach the end of the re flections in § 46that are supposed to clarify what is at
issue, they may still have the impression of having joined an unfamiliar conversation
about something long after it started. But it may be possible now to supply the
context for that conversation.
In the B-Deduction, Kant uses the tag ‘the I think ’ and its unabbreviated coun-
terpart in the same way that we might speak of ‘the QED that must be able to follow
every successful mathematical proof. ’ Once you notice this, you will find it striking
that ‘the I think ’ or—more usually—‘the I say ’ is itself an essential part of a proof in
classical geometry. It is what signals the end of the ekthesis, namely the restatement of
the enunciation in terms of the representative special case given in the labelled
diagram—or the ‘diorismos’, i.e., ‘specification’. Euclid restates or speci fies the enun-
ciation of Proposition Five in Elements Book One —the proposition about the
equality of the two angles at the base of an isosceles triangle —speaking in the first
person: ‘I say that the angle ABC is equal to the angle ACB .... ’24In fact, the ‘I say’ is
just as much a fixture of his proofs as the ‘QED’ or the ‘that is what was to be proved ’.
If, by analogy with a proof in classical geometry, pure apperception is ‘the I think ’ or
‘I say’ of the ekthesis, then it is something like what we would today call the assertion
stroke. That is surely some of its signi ficance for Kant too, but clearly not all.
We have many passages in Kant ’s writings that re flect on the ekthesis in geomet-
rical proofs—none of which reveals to us directly what he might have said about the ‘I
say’ that must be able to accompany the restatement of every general enunciation in
terms of a representative special case set out in a labelled diagram. But I would
suggest, in light of the passages on the topic we have examined in this book, that he
would likely have said this: the ‘I say’ is an act of the understanding that gives ‘sense
and meaning’ to the general enunciation by associating it with a labelled diagram and
discursive form to the labelled diagram by converting it into a judgement. It is a
special intellectual synthesis in its own right that produces meaningful thought out of
image-making. The labelled diagram all by itself does not count as thinking; the
general enunciation all by itself conveys no understanding of anything. The two must
come together in the appropriate way. Otherwise we will not know or understand
what it is that we are supposed to prove, which means that the proof itself cannot

get started —much less take us to the anticipated QED. If Kant ’s thinking about
the ‘I think’ is in any way informed by his likely thinking about the ‘I say’, then the
implications for empirical self-knowledge will be this.
The ‘I think’ will convert a certain image into meaningful thought —about myself.
For example, we can imagine Alfred Andersch speaking in his own voice and
expressing the outcome of his own self-exposition in Kirschen der Freiheit with the
following words: I think—I tell you—that I am a deserter. The difference between this
statement and Euclid ’s statement about the equality of the angles at the base of the
isosceles triangle ABC is first of all that the relevant diagram is not the same:
Andersch builds his narrative on the cartography of his movements through the
Italian countryside in the spring of 4300. But that is not all, because anybody
interested in the events of Andersch ’s life at this time would have had to run through
this cartography and put it back together again. That is precisely what Döring,
Römer, and Seubert did in their iography, Alfred Andersch Desertiert . We
can take the biographers to have carried out an exposition of Andersch. But
Andersch carried out an exposition of himself. As odd as it sounds because of the
considerations we reviewed at the end of § 4c of this chapter to sharpen the paradox
announced in §24 of the B-Deduction, it is pure apperception itself or the intellectual
synthesis supplied by the ‘I think ’ that ultimately makes the difference between
biography and autobiography. For, in the case of the autobiography, Andersch is
both the one who reports what he thinks or says and the object of his report. This is
possible not only because the ‘I think’ has converted the relevant images into thought
about Andersch, but also because, in doing so, it has specified itself as the voice of
Andersch himself.
From §25of the B-Deduction, we learn that the ‘I think ’ is itself a thought. Kant
says: ‘This representation [sc. the “I think ”—AL] is a thinking [ein Denken ], not an
intuiting [ein Anschauen]’ (B481). But if we were at all tempted to say that the general
enunciations of Euclid and Apollonius have no ‘sense or meaning’ on their own, how
much more will we be tempted to say such a thing about this thinking which is the
‘I think’? For Kant also says in §25that the ‘I think’ all by itself registers nothing more
than the fact that I exist. In the sentence immediately before the one just quoted,
he says that: ‘In the transcendental synthesis of the manifold of representations as
such, and thus in the original synthetic unity of apperception, I am conscious of
myself not as I appear, nor as I am in myself, but rather only that I am ’ (B481).
Registering the fact that I am leaves my existence completely undetermined. Am
I deserter? Am I a hapless soldier who got separated from his unit and tried to rejoin
it? Those are questions left unanswered by Andersch ’s ‘I think ’—taken all by itself.
The answers he gives in Kirschen der Freiheit will ultimately depend on combining a
manifold of intuition given to him under the form of inner sense. The result of his
combination will be a determinate thought about himself, i.e., a specification of his
own ‘I think’. But let us be careful here. The same determinate thought that —say—
Andersch is a deserter would also specify the ‘I think’ of Döring, or that of Römer or
Seubert (provided that the modality be downgraded from actual to possible deserter).
If we ask any one of them what he thinks, he will say: I think it possible that Alfred
Andersch deserted from the Wehrmacht in June of 4300. But, in Kirschen der
Freiheit, Andersch does not simply answer the question what he thinks; he answers

the question what sort of life he himself had been living up to the time of his capture.
In other words, he speci fies the ‘I’ as much as the ‘I think’. Thus the ‘I think’ specifies
itself in two ways when it is used to carry out an exposition of myself: it not only
clarifies to myself and others what I think; it also ‘determines’ my existence —it
clarifies to myself and to others what I take to be the circumstances of my life. The
‘I say’ in proofs by Apollonius always help specify a general enunciation by convert-
ing a labelled diagram into meaningful mathematical thought. The only time it helps
specify enunciations about the speaker himself is in the preface to the treatise on
conic sections where Apollonius spells out the circumstances in which he decided to
compose the treatise. The implication, of course, is that all empirical self-knowledge
is knowledge of myself as I appear and not as I am in myself. For it depends on my
taking up a manifold sensibly given to me. But knowledge it is —at least if we credit
Kant’s account of experience more generally.
Do these considerations really solve the paradox announced in §24? They will if
they give Kant a way of explaining self-affection. For remember that the problem
arose because of the seeming disconnect between inner sense and pure apperception:
inner sense is a capacity to be affected, but not a capacity to affect itself; pure
apperception is a kind of intellectual spontaneity that can certainly complement
inner sense, but not in a way that I recognize myself in it, because pure apperception
as such is not the chief protagonist in my life. Granted, items of empirical self-
knowledge, if explicitly stated in full, will follow the pattern of judgements like this
one: ‘I think that I am hungry ’.25I who am hungry must turn out to be the same as
the I who reports ‘I think that I am hungry ’. If not, there will be a problem about
authority: who is making the report about my hunger —speaking in my voice, no
less—if not I who am hungry? But if the I who reports is signalled by the ‘I think’ and
the ‘I think ’ is an expression of pure apperception, then pure apperception and the
self whose inner state is empirically reported by such judgements must coincide.
Kant’s distinction between pure apperception and inner sense initially appears to get
in the way of this. But the problem is overcome if the ‘I think’, in helping me carry out
the exposition of myself is itself specified not only by the content of my empirical
knowledge but also by the circumstances of my life. It is not some free- floating
‘I think ’ that reports the most significant act in the life of Alfred Andersch —it
is Andersch himself. The two-fold specification of the ‘I think ’ will thus count as
self-affection.
### §1b A final thought about the intellectual synthesis of pure apperception
There is one last thing to say about the intellectual synthesis of pure apperception in
empirical self-knowledge. We saw in §2c of Chapter Four that it oversees the activity
of productive imagination. Without its oversight, imagination would be ‘a blind, but
indispensable function of the soul ’ (A78/B103). What does its oversight consist in?
It lays down the conditions that make possible all cartography of the sensible world.
That includes, of course, the cartography I carry out for my own life. But we also saw
in §2 of Chapter Four that no such cartography would be possible without the
legislation by the understanding that makes nature possible as a composite of matter
and form. When I carry out the cartography of my own life, I graphically represent
myself as a part of nature and my life as part of its history. That would be less obvious
if I spent most of my life quietly reading and musing in the narrow precincts of my
home study, which is why I used the example of Alfred Andersch ’s autobiographical
report about his desertion from the Wehrmacht. The importance of the where ’s and
the when’s in that report is front and centre. If we are sensitive to their importance,
as I argued we must be in §2 of this chapter, then we must take Kant to say that the
intellectual synthesis of pure apperception makes another contribution to our empir-
ical self-knowledge: it prescribes the universal laws that make it possible for us to
represent ourselves as living our lives in the world around us. I have argued in this
book that Kant takes us to be legislators. So he does. As beings endowed with
understanding, we legislate the laws of nature, but in doing so we empirically
recognize ourselves as parts of the same nature we govern intellectually. That
means we must think of ourselves not only as the source of these laws, but also as
subject to them. If we are legislators, we are also self-legislators —as much in the
natural realm as in the kingdom of ends.[^26]
