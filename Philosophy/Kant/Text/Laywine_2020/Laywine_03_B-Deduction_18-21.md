## 3 §18 and §19 of the B-Deduction

The first step of the B-Deduction is supposed to be completed by the brief argument of §20. This argument depends on the 'supreme principle of all use of the understanding' secured in §17 and the inference drawn in §19 that the supreme principle makes possible all judgement. Given an earlier characterization of the categories in §10 as 'logical functions of judgement',[^1] it is supposed to follow in §20 that no judgement of any object is possible without the categories and hence that the categories relate to objects a priori. The final clause of the previous sentence might suggest that §20 completes the Transcendental Deduction. But it does not, because it leaves open important questions. How is perception possible? How can our perceptions be connected? How can we convert perceptions into a cosmology of experience? These questions do not get addressed until §26. Before we can think through the complications on the horizon, we must step back and reflect on the considerations needed for the argument of §20 and introduced in §§18–19.

The purpose of §19 is to connect the 'supreme principle of all use of the understanding', elaborated in §17, with judgement. There is an easy way to do this, which is to stress the meaning of the word 'supreme': judgement is always an act of the understanding; all acts of the understanding are governed by the supreme principle (that's what makes it supreme); hence the supreme principle makes judgement possible.[^2] But ease comes at the cost of satisfaction: why things work out so is obscure. Greater effort will be required to shed light on the nature of judgement and thereby explain why judgement always depends on the supreme principle. The purpose of §19 is to generate that insight. The purpose of §18 is to set the stage for §19.[^3]

It does so by building on the reflections in §17 and by distinguishing between that which is objective and that which is subjective: as we learn from §17, the synthetic unity of pure apperception is objective because it secures the relation between our sensible representations and an object, and we learn in §18 that the unity of consciousness associated with the imagination is purely subjective. But, as we will see, §18 seems to characterize the subjective unity of the imagination in such a way as to raise useful puzzles about judgement. The solution to these puzzles is given in §19. It depends on the idea that the objective unity of consciousness is itself the form of all judgement. That in turn is one of the fundamental premises of §20. The purpose of this chapter of my book is thus to explore the way Kant uses §18 and §19 to complete the first step of the Transcendental Deduction in §20.

### §1 A Careful Look at §18: What Is Subjective and What Is Objective?

§18 is concerned with a distinction between the subjective and the objective unity of self-consciousness. The title of the section might suggest a focus on the latter, because it reads as follows: 'What is objective unity of self-consciousness'.[^4] But I think that this expectation is wrong, because we already know something about 'objective unity' from §17: it is that unity indicated by the supreme principle of all use of the understanding: the synthetic unity of pure apperception having taken up a manifold and thereby secured the relation between our sensible representations and an object. §18 adds nothing to §17. I infer that the focus of §18 is on the subjective unity of self-consciousness—and the way it differs from its objective counterpart. That is the new element introduced here.

The objective unity of self-consciousness obtains a priori, and it has its source in the understanding. So too the categories. But that is precisely why Kant characterizes them as subjective in §13. Indeed, that is why he formulates the problem of the Transcendental Deduction at B122 in §13 as being to understand how these purely 'subjective conditions of thought[^5] [sc. the categories—AL] are supposed to have objective validity, i.e., yield conditions of the possibility of all knowledge of objects.' Looking ahead to §20, we know prospectively that the categories are so many different expressions of the objective unity of self-consciousness. That makes the objective unity of self-consciousness just as subjective as the categories themselves.[^6] So if there is a distinction to be drawn at all between the objective unity and the subjective unity of self-consciousness, as claimed in §18, then there must be a subjective unity of self-consciousness that cannot be objective. Kant apparently takes us to assume at the outset that 'subjective' and 'objective' are mutually exclusive classes.[^7] While the Deduction will ultimately try to show us that our assumption is wrong in the case of the categories, §18 tries to specify the case in which it is right. As we will see, however, the case at issue is right in a funny way, because it too will challenge our assumptions. For it involves concepts and thus depends on the objective unity of consciousness, but it is thoroughly subjective in the way that it relates these concepts with one another. Thus the distinction drawn in §18 plays out as a distinction between subjective relations among concepts and objective relations among concepts. We will see later that Kant uses this distinction to motivate his ideas about judgement in §19.

§18 begins with a characterization of the objective unity of self-consciousness, reprised from §17: 'The transcendental unity of apperception is that unity whereby every manifold given in an intuition is united in a concept of an object. Hence, it is called objective' (B139). There immediately follows the claim that 'objective unity must be distinguished from the subjective unity of consciousness.' Kant characterizes subjective unity as 'a determination of inner sense through which the manifold for such a combination is given empirically.' 'Inner sense' is in Fettdruck for emphasis (cf. A98–99). But the emphasis does not help us see how subjective unity differs from its objective counterpart, for it too is a determination of inner sense—like all modifications of the mind.[^8] That the manifold be given empirically is a necessary condition for the subjective unity of consciousness. But it cannot be sufficient, because the objective unity of consciousness may also take up an empirically given manifold, as we learn from §17. The sufficient condition for any unity of consciousness taking up an empirically given manifold to count as subjective is that it depend on 'empirical circumstances', as Kant puts it a few lines later, and so prove to be 'completely contingent' (B140). 'Completely contingent' are Kant's words—ganz zufällig—not mine. What is he thinking? It is natural to assume that he is thinking of the personal idiosyncrasies of different people, because the example he gives is that of the association of ideas.

I will use the expression 'association of ideas', because that is what we say in ordinary, non-Kantian English. Kant himself speaks at B140 in §18 of 'the association of Vorstellungen'. One more usually translates 'Vorstellung' in Kantian writings as 'representation' (as I have done everywhere else in this book). Furthermore, one would resist translating it as 'idea', just because Kant reserves the German 'Idee' for the concepts of pure reason, which are not at issue here. But 'Vorstellung' seems to have been the eighteenth-century German equivalent of the eighteenth-century English 'idea', as a comparison of Locke and Tetens will confirm. That, together with the greater naturalness of our ordinary, non-Kantian English expression, tips the balance—in my mind at least—in favour of 'association of ideas', at least for discussing the relevant operation of the mind. No one says 'association of representations'. But, as a practical consequence of my decision to use the more natural expression, I will leave 'Vorstellung' untranslated in the passages from Kant that I will quote in the rest of §1 in this chapter—to avoid the misleading impression that he is already speaking of 'ideas' in his own technical sense in the Transcendental Deduction. I will use the untranslated 'Vorstellung' in my discussion of these passages. To revert to 'idea' here would baffle the reader.

So far as it goes, Kant's example seems to fit the bill, because no one disputes that association of ideas is subjective: it is different for different people. Moreover, it has an uncontroversial and intimate relationship with time, as befits 'a determination of inner sense'. For one thing, it takes time to associate ideas: maybe not a lot, but at least some. For another thing, it involves a conscious imaginative awareness of different things accompanying one another at the same time and following one another at different times. I sometimes associate Charlie Chaplin with kittens. That happens to be the first thing I think of when somebody mentions the 'Great Dictator': I think of the kittens and the Jewish barber together in the same scene when the weary war veteran has finally come home. This is a case in which association of ideas involves an imaginative awareness of things accompanying one another at the same time. Sometimes I associate with eagles the opening of the first movement of Mahler's fifth symphony in C-sharp minor. In that case, my thought of eagles will set off in my head the trumpet fanfare that famously opens the work. This is a case in which association involves an imaginative awareness of things—if musical notes are things—following one another in time. But these associations are peculiar to me. Other people will associate other things with eagles and Charlie Chaplin.

Still, however uncontroversial all of this might seem, Kant's example is odd, as too the way he develops it in the B-Deduction, because of the contrast between its treatment there and its treatment in the A-Deduction. I don't think these oddities have been pointed out before. The larger question will be what to make of them. Kant does not merely mention association of ideas in §18 of the B-Deduction; he even (uncharacteristically) gives a special case of the example (but one and only one). It turns on what people associate with a given word: 'One person associates the Vorstellung of a given word with one thing; another person associates it with something else' (B140). If the Vorstellung of a word is its meaning[^9] and understanding the meaning of words involves nothing more than associating these Vorstellungen with the things designated by the relevant words, then Kant's claim is in serious difficulty. How could we communicate with one another, if each of us subjectively conjures up something different for each word uttered by a speaker? The natural way to rescue Kant from this difficulty is not to read a theory of meaning—or anyway a theory of linguistic understanding—into the passage. Should we not suppose that Kant is just making the harmless observation that competent speakers of a language can perfectly well allow their imagination free reign to associate whatever comes to their mind when they hear or read words? I know perfectly well what the word 'eagle' can mean. But when I encounter it in speech or in a written text, I may also associate with it the trumpet fanfare at the beginning of Mahler's fifth, without for a minute entertaining the crazy idea that any part of Mahler's repertoire is the topic of discussion.

Having said that, the special example in §18 of the B-Deduction remains odd, because it is also cited in the A-Deduction where its significance is treated differently—so differently as to suggest a certain tension on this point between the two versions of the argument. The relevant passage can be found in the discussion of what Kant calls 'the synthesis of reproduction in imagination'.[^10] Unlike §18 of the B-Deduction, this passage of the A-Deduction introduces special cases of association other than that of words and their meanings. We will need to reflect on the other special cases, because we want to discover the pattern of treatment of all of them and especially the way the linguistic case conforms to it. This will help us appreciate the important differences between the A- and B-Deductions, which I will signal as we go along, but ultimately it will help us appreciate which fundamental insight animates them both on the question at hand: what do we mean by 'subjective'?

Association of ideas is the point of departure for this discussion in the A-Deduction, which opens with the following remark:

> To be sure, it is a merely empirical law[^11] that Vorstellungen that have often followed or accompanied one another ultimately become associated with one another [mit einander vergesellschaften] and thereby form a connection [Verknüpfung] such that, even without the presence of the object, one of these Vorstellungen produces a transition of the mind to another following a constant rule. (A100)

Kant says explicitly that the association of ideas is subject to a 'merely empirical law'. The words 'merely empirical' might naturally be construed as 'subjective'.[^12] Given what we have already seen of §18 of the B-Deduction, this might suggest in turn our distinction between the objective and subjective unity of self-consciousness. But the word 'law' complicates things. True enough: Kant will speak of 'laws of association' at the end of §19 of the B-Deduction (B143). Here we will find no complication, because Kant says that any Vorstellung that results from such laws has 'mere subjective validity', i.e., is valid only for the person who has it. The word 'law' is used here in a weak and flabby sense. By contrast, the word 'law' is used in a robust sense towards the end of the A-Deduction where it is reserved for those rules that may count as 'objective' (A114, A126). This does complicate things—at least for us readers of §18 in the B-Deduction. Yet the very thing that may strike us as a complication is what the Kant of 1781 wants to stress in the passage just quoted from A100 (and its continuation). I do not merely observe in myself an idiosyncratic tendency to associate Vorstellungen 'that have often followed or accompanied one another'; I can observe it in other people—so many other people, in fact, that it is hard to imagine that there might be exceptions. The 'law of association of ideas' is indeed an empirical law, perhaps like those laws that govern the weather: for example, when humidity is very high and atmospheric pressure is low, we can usually expect precipitation. This is the sort of 'empirical law' Kant has in mind at the very end of the A-Deduction: the ones that we can only discover empirically, he says, because 'they cannot as such in any way derive their origin from pure understanding' (A128). But that does not diminish their status as laws.[^13] It just means that we cannot demonstrate them deductively from the synthetic unity of pure apperception. There is no reason not to think that the 'law of association of ideas' is one of these empirical laws—and therefore 'objective' in the relevant sense (more will need to be said about this later). Once we start using the word 'objective', though, it will be difficult to speak of 'mere subjective validity'. Indeed, we will find that there are right and wrong associations, and hence associations that we may legitimately expect everybody else to make when the circumstances call for them.

It now seems an odd exaggeration to say, as we know Kant does at B140 in §18 of the B-Deduction, that the associations people make with words or anything else are 'completely contingent'. Indeed, the point that Kant plainly wants to stress in our current passage from the A-Deduction is at odds with the remark at B140: our associations are not contingent (or not completely so). This is precisely why he introduces the notion of an 'affinity' of appearances at A121–122 in the so-called argument 'from below'. The affinity of appearances is the 'objective ground of all association of appearances' (A122). Thanks to this objective ground, it is not 'completely contingent [etwas ganz Zufälliges] that appearances are suited for a connection of human knowledge' (A121). It does not 'remain in itself completely undetermined and contingent [zufällig] whether they can be associated' (A122). This seems not to be the rather weak claim that—necessarily—appearances must be associable somehow or other, perhaps in ways that might vary from person to person. It seems to be the more robust claim that—necessarily—appearances are associable in certain ways that are predictable for all of us: else how could they be 'suited for a connection of human knowledge'? It is to be expected, given the usual course of things, that people will associate smoke with fire and not with tadpoles or mud puddles. The association all by itself does not count as knowledge of smoke. But it can help us build up such knowledge and to that extent is 'suited for a connection' with it, if—say—we are moved to confirm the conjecture that the association naturally leads us to entertain, namely that the cause of smoke is fire. By the same token, however, it would be implausible to take the remark quoted from A121–122 as the maximally robust claim that all of us will inevitably associate precisely the same thing with any given appearance: for example, smoke with fire. Kant is talking about the association of ideas. Surely he accepts the basic empirical psychological facts: inevitability is just as out of place here as it is in forecasting rain when humidity is high and atmospheric pressure is low. Kant's claim at A121–122 is neither too weak, nor too robust. It is just right in a Goldilocks sort of way.

In the continuation of our passage from A100, Kant develops these ideas as follows:

> For without this [sc. a rule—AL], our empirical imagination would never have anything to do that is conformable with its capacity [Vermögen], but would thus remain inside the mind like a dead capacity, unknown even to ourselves. Were cinnabar sometimes red, sometimes black, sometimes light, sometimes heavy; were a human being sometimes in this form, but sometimes transformed into that of an animal; were the land on the longest day of the year sometimes covered in fruit, sometimes with ice and snow, my empirical imagination could not even get the opportunity, upon having the representation of red, to entertain heavy cinnabar in thought. (A100–101)

The point here is that I would never have had the opportunity to form the associations I do were there no rules or regularities that allow my imagination to settle into certain imaginative routines that reflect regularities among things as they appear to me. My imagination would thus remain inside my mind 'like a dead capacity', unknown even to myself, because it would never be called on to do anything. In the 'argument from below', Kant will say that such regularities point to the presence of an 'objective ground' underlying our associations (at which point he will introduce the 'affinity of appearances, as I indicated earlier: A121–122). But could this be true even for my highly idiosyncratic association of eagles and the trumpet fanfare at the beginning of Mahler's fifth? That association does seem to be 'completely contingent', which is precisely what made it suitable as an illustration of Kant's remarks at B140 in §18 of the B-Deduction. But if it is 'completely contingent', how could it be governed by empirical laws, and how could it point to anything 'objective'? Is it not a counter-example to Kant's remarks at A100–101? Did Kant not remember in 1787 what he had written in 1781?

To address this important question, at least provisionally, let me say a little bit more about my idiosyncratic association. It depends on my very first encounter with Mahler's music, which also happened to be my first encounter with the respective heraldic devices of the late nineteenth-century Central and Eastern European potentates: the eagle of the German emperors, the eagle of the Hapsburg emperors, and the double-headed eagle of the Russian Tsars. When and under what circumstances this joint encounter took place in my life does not matter. But what clearly does matter—and this is the second thing I should say—is that my idiosyncratic association cannot be explained by the law of association Kant mentions at A100. That law says that people will associate those Vorstellungen 'that have often accompanied or followed one another'. How often has Mahler's fanfare been accompanied by the relevant eagles in my life? Not very often at all. That could seem to be a serious problem for the Kant who speaks at A100–101, though perhaps it does indeed help the Kant who says at B140 in §18 of the B-Deduction that associations of ideas are 'completely contingent' and hence presumably not governed by any laws, empirical or otherwise. Let me suggest a possible solution to the problem: a solution that will seem to help the former Kant, but perhaps inconvenience the latter (I will argue at the end of this section that the tension between the two may well be apparent only).

In the first place, there may be laws of association to which our imagination is subject other than the one Kant explicitly invokes at A100: Kant does not claim that there is only one. Mahler and the eagles together made a very deep impression on me: that is the third thing I should say about my association. For perhaps there is also an empirical law such that a person will associate Vorstellungen, even if they have followed or accompanied one another only once, if those Vorstellungen together make a tremendous splash in that person's mind. In the second place, it seems reasonable to say that the laws of association—whatever they turn out to be—all depend on the stability among appearances that Kant invokes in our passage from A100–101. Perhaps that might seem counterintuitive in the case of splash-making Vorstellungen a person associates after only one exposure: would it matter if the relevant appearances underwent a lot of alteration thereafter? I think the answer must be: Yes, indeed it matters! Stability may not be required to produce the propensity to associate in this case: by hypothesis, that would be ensured by the splash. But it would be required to give the person subsequent encounters with appearances recognizable to him as the same as those that initially bowled him over. If, after my first encounter, the heraldic devices of the late nineteenth-century Central and Eastern European potentates subsequently underwent a constant metamorphosis—were sometimes mice, sometimes frogs, sometimes having one head, sometimes two, sometimes seven—it would never be possible for me to associate them with Mahler's trumpet fanfare, even if it (the fanfare) remained perfectly stable. For I would never have an opportunity to make the association at all. If that is right, or if it is at least a solution that Kant would have embraced, then the association of ideas—even in the cases that seem highly idiosyncratic and hence totally subjective—are governed by empirical laws that are themselves 'objective' or that point somehow to an 'objective ground' (more will need to be said about this later). But insofar as the Kant of A100–101 embraces this solution, the Kant of B140 in §18 of the B-Deduction must justify calling associations of ideas 'completely contingent'. He will also have to justify using this alleged contingency as the marker for the subjectivity of our associations. A discussion of this problem must wait a little bit longer, because we must first discuss the special case of words and their meaning. For that case is the point of contact between our passages in the two versions of the Deduction.

The special case of words and their meaning finally comes into play in the immediate continuation of the passage I quoted above from A100–101:

> Or were a certain word attached sometimes to this thing, sometimes to that, or even the very same thing called sometimes by this name, sometimes by that, without a certain rule prevailing here to which appearances were already subject of themselves, no empirical synthesis of reproduction could arise. (A101)

Kant treats this example exactly as he did the physical examples. Here, as in §18 of the B-Deduction, he says too little for us to discern how he understands the nature of language. That is a problem, because it makes it hard to understand what the focus of the example is supposed to be. He speaks of 'attaching' words to things. Are these things the meaning of words?[^14] It certainly seems as if the meaning of words and the possibility of our grasping these meanings correctly is playing a role in the passage. To be sure, there is no impossibility in making associations with words we do not understand. Perhaps one remembers the sound of a foreign word and associates with that sound the circumstances under which one heard the word for the first time. But it is not news that we can associate with sounds: I associate the Berlin S-Bahn with the first movement of Beethoven's Eroica symphony, because, as the train prepares to leave the platform it emits first a rising and then a descending major third that always completes itself in my head as the first theme announced at the beginning of that work after the opening two E-flat major chords. In the passage quoted from A101, however, Kant is inviting us to consider not sounds in general, but rather words. That must mean he is focusing on what is special about words. Spoken words certainly have distinctive sounds: that is what allows us to distinguish them from one another in speech. But what makes them distinctive as words, and distinguishes them from one another semantically, is their meaning. Surely that is what interests Kant in this passage.

Kant seems to think that what we can associate with a word is its meaning or—anyway—the thing we 'attach' it to.[^15] If that is what he thinks, then perhaps the passage at A101, if not the one at B140 in §18 of the B-Deduction, assumes that the association of ideas can be a mark of linguistic understanding: i.e., I can perhaps be taken to understand what words mean if I make the right association. It is hard to believe that Kant could think that linguistic understanding essentially consists in the association of ideas. That would not fit with what he says about understanding as such. Association of ideas is an act of the empirical imagination; understanding is an act of the faculty Kant calls the 'understanding'. The understanding and the empirical imagination are related to each other in ways we must still explore. But they are not the same faculty.[^16] I could not be said to understand what cinnabar is just by associating red with it. On the other hand, if I do understand what cinnabar is, you would expect me to associate red with it, and certainly not black. If I do associate red rather than black with it, you could take that as a sign that I understand what it is, even if you could not be certain I do on that basis alone.

If that is right and if it applies to linguistic understanding as well, then, as I say, Kant may perhaps think that a sign of linguistic understanding is that a person makes the right associations with a given word. It is a sign that I may well understand the meaning of the word 'cinnabar' if the word itself calls to mind for me the thing that linguistic convention attaches it to, i.e., a certain mineral of a certain description, rather than some plant or animal. But, of course, this could only be a sign, and not a decisive one at that. My understanding of the word as such presumably consists in something other than an association of ideas. What might that be? An ability to define the word? An ability to use it in a competent way? The ability to correct the use of it by people who do not know what it means? I cannot tell, because Kant says too little. But perhaps this does not matter for now. If I have faithfully captured the idea underlying our passage at A101, then I take the central point to be that the marking of linguistic understanding—namely by the association of the word with whatever it is normally 'attached to'—would be impossible if words constantly changed their meaning, i.e., if they were 'attached sometimes to this thing, sometimes to that'. Even if the things to which words were 'attached' remained stable, the marking of linguistic understanding would be impossible if the 'attachments' of words did not. Suppose that cinnabar were always red and never black, just as it is now, and that all its other properties remained as they are, but that the word 'cinnabar' were sometimes attached to the mineral, sometimes to a plant, sometimes to an animal. In that case, no one could ever associate anything with the word 'cinnabar'. That would be a sign that nobody could understand it at all. Indeed, it would be a decisive sign, assuming—of course—that it is reasonable to expect of a person who does understand what a word means that she can associate something with it. In the scenario Kant is envisaging, linguistic convention has collapsed—perhaps it never got started—and this is signalled by the complete absence of any association. The lesson here is supposed to be that our linguistic associations are made possible by empirical laws that point in turn to a certain 'objective ground'—whatever it is that makes it possible for us, competent speakers of the language, to use words competently and thereby understand what we and our interlocutors are saying (cf. A121–122).

Of course, one thing this business obviously means—a point I have already stressed—is that, for Kant at A101, what a person associates with a given word is not completely contingent, by contrast with his claim to the contrary at B140 in §18 of the B-Deduction. But if we step back from the thick of the two passages and think again, it seems that there must be a related difference. This difference may indicate a point of contact, which will ultimately take us to the moral of the story. It is surely reasonable to expect that a person who understands the meaning of a given word can associate with the word the thing it is 'attached to'. But surely it would be unreasonable to expect that that is all such a person can or would associate with the word. She might also associate with it one or more properties of the relevant thing. Instead of associating with the word 'cinnabar' a certain mineral of a certain description, I might just associate with it the colour red or a certain crystalline structure. I might also associate with it the uses made of the mineral, e.g., the production of pigment, or the places where we might expect to find deposits of it. But then it would surely be just as unreasonable to be surprised if a person who understands the meaning of 'cinnabar' did not also associate with it anything at all that might come into her mind, however seemingly remote from the natural history of cinnabar.[^17] We are, after all, talking about the association of ideas. Part of the interest of the passage at A101 is that it seems to be setting aside all these considerations. All that seems to matter there is that a person who understands what 'cinnabar' means can make the right association: that person will be able to associate with this word a certain mineral, rather than a plant or an animal. Is there any reason to think that Kant could be doubting at A101 that the more far-flung associations are possible? That hardly seems likely. On the other hand, the focus of his remarks at B140 in §18 of the B-Deduction seem to be just the far-flung associations left out of account at A101. Is there any reason to think that he is in doubt here about the not so far-flung associations? This is hardly likely either. Rather, the safer inference is that the focus of the two passages is different, because Kant is trying to emphasize different things.

One way to underscore this inference is to notice a point of contact between the two passages. The passage at B140 surely presupposes that if people associate anything at all with a given word, it is by having understood what the word means. It is precisely because you know what 'eagle' means that you can let your fantasy take flight. If you did not know what it meant, you might still let it take off—and associate something with the sound of the word when it is spoken or the shape of the word as it appears printed on the page. But, again, if Kant is going to mention words at all in this context, it is surely because he is interested in the fact that they are meaningful and competent speakers understand what they mean. As far as associating ideas is concerned, the imagination of a competent speaker of English can be just as readily fired up by the word 'eagle' as by an encounter with an eagle in the wild. But in view of all that, the Kant of 1787 would surely want to make an important qualification when he says at the very end of §18 that what one or another person associates with a word is 'not necessary and not universally valid' (B140). The qualification surely runs in the very same direction as things we have been discussing from A101: the relevant word cannot be 'attached sometimes to this thing, sometimes to that', otherwise we could not associate anything with the word at all. This implies that something universally valid is indeed in play, even at B140 in §18 of the B-Deduction: namely the understanding that all competent speakers of English have of the word 'eagle' (and all competent speakers of German have of the word 'Adler'). This understanding we all share will serve as a rule. The rule all by itself will not determine our associations such that we will all associate the same thing with this word. But at a minimum it will make them possible. Moreover, it will constrain them in such a way that whatever anybody associates with eagles will have something to do with eagles—at least in that person's mind. There may still be tensions between the Kant of 1787 and his earlier self from 1781: perhaps the latter would have pressed the former to accept a role for empirical laws in the psychology of association; perhaps the former would have pressed the latter to accept a wider scope for personal idiosyncrasies; perhaps each of them would have been frustrated by the obduracy of the other. But if, from considerations like the ones I just rehearsed, Kant could infer at A101 (and later at A121–122) that the association of ideas must point to, and depend on, something objective, then surely he could also do so at B140 in §18 of the B-Deduction. In fact, he does—in passing.

Just before observing at the very end of §18 that different people associate different things with the same word, Kant makes two remarks. The first concerns the difference between the 'original synthetic unity of apperception' and the 'empirical unity of apperception' involved in the association of ideas: he says that the former alone is 'objectively valid' (objektiv gültig, i.e., holds for all of us), whereas the latter 'has only "subjective validity"' (subjective Gültigkeit, i.e., holds for only some of us—indeed perhaps for only one of us). The second remark concerns the relation of the one kind of unity to the other: he says that the empirical unity of apperception 'is derived under given conditions in concreto' from the original synthetic unity of apperception. Kant does not specify which 'given conditions in concreto' he means. Moreover, he says explicitly that he does not want to go into details here about the 'empirical unity of apperception'. His reticence is no doubt explained by the fact that he would then be required to begin his discussion of the imagination and to distinguish the reproductive synthesis of the imagination at work in the association of ideas and the productive synthesis of the imagination carried out a priori. We know that he postpones this discussion until §24. But the important point for now is just that we may take these two remarks jointly as concurring with the claim we noted at A101 (and more explicitly at A121–122) in the A-Deduction: the association of ideas points to, and depends on, something objective. Despite the important differences between the passage at A101 and the passage at B140 in §18 of the B-Deduction, the two passages do seem to agree on this one important point at least. This in turn suggests that the differences we have noted are indeed due to a difference of emphasis. The A-Deduction is trying to stress the relation of dependence of the association of ideas on the original synthetic unity of apperception. On the other hand, the passage at B140 in §18 of the B-Deduction is trying to stress the difference between them. The difference of emphasis is itself due to a difference of argumentative strategy. More could be said about that, but to pursue it now would take us off course. The thing to do now is to address a natural objection that is sure to arise at this juncture.

It may, in fact, be objected that I have been equivocating on Kant's behalf, or if I have faithfully represented his position, that he himself is equivocating—and I along with him for good measure. The passages from the A-Deduction I have been quoting at A100–101 do not make use of the word 'objective'. But they are clearly setting the stage for parallel passages at A121–122, in the argument 'from below', that do make explicit use of the word. These passages, like our passages at A100–101, ask how the association of ideas is possible. The answer very roughly sketched in our passages at A100–101 turns on rules and regularities. The answer given by the parallel passages at A121–122 appeals to an 'objective ground' (objektiver Grund). Given that the two sets of passages are preoccupied by the same question, it is natural to take their respective answers—however sketchy—as complementing each other (and that has been my interpretative strategy so far). If that is right, then one might well think that some of what the 'objective' in 'objective ground' means is precisely the rules and regularities without which the association of ideas would not be possible. So far, so good. But the objection now comes plainly into view, if we read our passages at A100–101 in the most natural way possible, namely that there is indeed something 'out there' among the objects, independent of our knowledge and thought. Consider Kant's own examples in those passages: cinnabar does not change colour or weight; the land is covered with fruit in the summer—only in the winter is it covered with ice and snow. These things exhibit regularities. The linguistic examples are plainly intended to follow the same pattern as the physical examples: there is language out there, and speakers who speak it, and they exhibit regularities and behave in a law-like way, just as cinnabar does. The regularities in the world are not affected by what we think or know. Rather they impose themselves on the mind, and the internal regularities that directly govern the mind itself make it sensitive to them: it develops patterns of thinking that correspond to the patterns of behaviour in the objects themselves. The claim in the A-Deduction that the association of ideas, however subjective it may be, presupposes an 'objective ground', i.e., all the relevant rules and regularities, is apparently coupled with talk of objects out there, independent of the mind. But when Kant uses the word 'objective' in §18 in the B-Deduction, it is no longer attached to mind independent objects like cinnabar and the way they behave or don't behave. Instead, it attaches to the synthetic unity of apperception. If, then, we read §18 of the B-Deduction in the light of A100–101, surely we will end up equivocating on the word 'objective'. So goes the objection. It is perfectly valid, but it does not hold up, at the end of the day.

There is at least one simple thing to say in reply to it. The paragraph immediately following our passages at A100–101 corrects the impression produced by all the talk of cinnabar, fruit, ice, and snow. We are dealing here with things as they appear to us, and not things in themselves. 'Appearances are not things in themselves' says Kant, 'but rather the mere play of our Vorstellungen that, in the end, amount to determinations of inner sense' (A101). The rule or regularity invoked in the previous paragraph as the condition for the possibility of the association of ideas does indeed apply to cinnabar, fruit, ice, and snow, not as things in themselves, but rather only as appearances. Kant himself had already said as much at the end of that paragraph: 'if there did not prevail a certain rule to which the appearances[^18] were indeed of themselves subject, there could arise no empirical synthesis of reproduction.' (We may here take the latter expression to mean association of ideas.) By means of this corrective, Kant indicates unambiguously that our associations should not be taken to be habits of thinking imposed on the mind by patterns of behaviour among things independent of the mind. We might perhaps worry about treating fruit and cinnabar as a 'mere play of Vorstellungen'. That sort of talk much too naturally conjures up the spook of Berkeley. But if we put aside that concern for now, and address the concern immediately at hand, we can see how easy it is now to get rid of the impression of equivocation I mentioned earlier. For once the corrective is in place, it implies that the rules governing our associations are themselves somehow dependent on the mind's fundamental principles. Which one? By considering the options, we will find that our passages in the A-Deduction come into line with §18 of the B-Deduction.

The principle we are looking for is not already bundled with the formal condition of inner sense. Kant does not say explicitly why not. But it is easy enough to see that it must involve activity and hence some kind of spontaneity, which is absent from inner sense as a constituent element of the mind's sensibility—a purely receptive faculty for being affected by objects. This thought is surely what leads Kant to characterize the principle we are seeking as 'the ground a priori of a necessary synthetic unity' (A101). As we have already seen, all such unity is the effect of the mind's self-activity. Kant does not already here ascribe this 'necessary synthetic unity' to pure apperception. But that is no surprise. Our passages from A100–101 and their continuation at A102 appear in the section devoted to the 'synthesis of reproduction in imagination'; pure apperception is not introduced until the next section—the one devoted to the 'synthesis of recognition in concepts'. The absence of any mention of pure apperception around A100 and immediately thereafter is an artefact of Kant's presenting the three 'subjective sources of knowledge', at this stage in the A-Deduction, 'separate and apart from one another' (abgesondert und einzeln—A115). The interest of both the arguments 'from above' and 'from below' is that they present these same sources of knowledge 'united and in connection with one another' (vereinigt und im Zusammenhange—A115). Thus we find in the parallel passages around A121–122 in the argument 'from below' that the rule or principle invoked in our passages at A100–101, and the 'necessary synthetic unity' it makes possible (and thereby the association of ideas), is ultimately a principle of pure apperception. As Kant puts it at A122, 'We can encounter this [objective ground] nowhere else than in the principle of the unity of apperception with respect to all cognitions that are supposed to belong to me.' The principle at issue, as I argued in §3c of Chapter Two, is the same as the 'supreme principle of all use of the understanding' presented in §17 of the B-Deduction. Since that principle is the one that leads Kant to qualify the synthetic unity of pure apperception as 'objective' in §18 of the B-Deduction, the worry about equivocation undermining my reading of §18 is now put to rest.

Here is the upshot. The A-Deduction and the B-Deduction apparently agree that the association of ideas is made possible by the objective unity of pure apperception. But they disagree about the association of ideas itself. The disagreement may perhaps involve nothing more than a difference of focus, emphasis, or argumentative strategy. But it is such as to call attention to the special sense of the word 'subjective' at play in §18 of the B-Deduction. The A-Deduction assumes that the association of ideas is governed by empirical laws and that it therefore exhibits a kind of necessity (however weak). It calls on the objective unity of pure apperception to explain how this necessity is possible. By contrast, the B-Deduction assumes that the association of ideas is the expression of my personal idiosyncrasies. It uses the word 'law' in a flabby sense and shows no interest in the fact that the association of ideas can track regularities among appearances. Nor does it address the question how such tracking is possible. Instead, it addresses in passing the question how a person's idiosyncratic associations are possible as such. It appeals to the very same objective unity of apperception to answer this question. We may wonder how Kant can give one and the same answer to two such different questions. The solution to this puzzle must ultimately turn on Kant's insight in both versions of the argument that the association of ideas involves thinking and understanding (witness the assumption shared in both versions of the argument about the associations we make with words and their meanings). The special interest of §18 in the B-Deduction is the recognition that this insight holds even if we take the association of ideas to be the expression of highly personal idiosyncrasies. It implies that subjectivity, taken to be the expression of such idiosyncrasies, is not what we would get if the objective unity of apperception were turned off. If that ever happened, we would each be abandoned not to his or her subjectivity, but—to use Kant's lovely expression at A113—that which is 'nothing but a blind play of representations, i.e., less even than a dream'.

Now that expression, however lovely, raises a question. I said at the beginning of this section that the challenge of §18 is to specify a case of subjective unity of consciousness that can never be other than subjective. The unity of consciousness involved in the association of ideas is indeed subjective, but even this case depends on its objective counterpart. Can it, then, help Kant meet the challenge of §18? Is it subjective enough? Another way to ask the question is due to C. I. Lewis: did the Sage of Königsberg have no dreams? Lewis' formulation of the question might be taken as motivated by the worry that we cannot turn on objective unity without turning off subjective unity. If the worry is well founded, the Sage of Königsberg could have experience in his own peculiar sense, but neither dreams, nor associations of ideas. Lewis White Beck had an interesting solution to this problem,[^19] but it would take us far afield. For our purposes, a solution can be easily developed out of the considerations already in play. It depends on pointing out that the worry just mentioned is out of place: the on-switch for objective unity has two positions. In the one position, objective unity is on, but subjective unity is off; in the other, both kinds are on. This is the case where a person entertains a bunch of concepts, but relates them to one another in a personally idiosyncratic way, namely by association of ideas.

For David Hume, the association of ideas is nothing more than the association of images. All our ideas are copies of impressions. The only difference between impressions and their copies is degree of liveliness: an impression is lively as it enters the mind; the longer it lingers there, the more it fades. A faded impression resembles its livelier self, but it has lost—and keeps losing—the immediacy of the original. That is what makes it an image. Association can restore some of its liveliness: when I see one of her favourite porcelain teacups, I cannot help but conjure up in my mind a picture of my grandmother. That picture is livelier than it would be otherwise. It resembles my grandmother, or—anyway—the impressions I used to have of her when I could still enjoy her company: on the strength of this resemblance, I recognize that I am remembering my grandmother and not one of her sisters. But though the picture is livelier than it usually is, it remains a picture. Pictures are the currency of association for Hume. Kant rejects this. He does not deny that images contribute something to knowledge or that they play a role in human psychology or even that we can make associations with them. But he says in both versions of the Transcendental Deduction that the association of 'ideas' fundamentally involves concepts. I take this to mean that concepts are the currency of association.

The natural way for Kant to make good on this correction of Hume is to grant that the on-switch for objective unity has two positions one of which also turns on the subjective unity of consciousness. In this position, objective unity makes it possible for me to grasp a bunch of different concepts, but the way I relate these concepts is not objective, because it does not secure the relation between our sensible representations and an object that Kant takes to be definitive of the objective. All I get from associating ideas is a transition from one concept to another. That transition all by itself is not objective in the relevant sense, and Kant will play up this point in §19 of the B-Deduction. For the purposes of §18, the subjective transition from one concept to another is precisely what is needed: the relation of concepts brought about by association is a unity of consciousness that is thoroughly subjective, even though the objective unity of consciousness is at work in making possible each one of the concepts in play and our understanding of it.

I would argue that Kant's idea here is both reasonable and plausible. The way to show this is to consider a classic case of association. We find such a case in Freud's Psychopathologie des Alltags. In Chapter Two, Freud reports an encounter with a young, well-educated Jewish man. The two get into a conversation about the then bad carrier prospects for Jews. The young man heats up and misquotes a line from Virgil's Aeneid: he leaves out the word 'aliquis' and, after he has been duly corrected, wonders whether his slip can be explained. Freud does not disappoint: the misquote was the effect of the young man's fear that he may have impregnated a woman. The explanation is almost certainly false (which is not to deny the young man's fear). But it is built on what Freud's interlocutor freely associates with the word 'aliquis'. There is no reason to doubt that Freud reports the string of associations accurately. If he has, we may learn something.

After breaking down 'aliquis' into 'a' and 'liquis', the young man reports that he is led by association to think of 'Reliquien' (relics), 'Liquidation' (liquidation), 'Flüssigkeit' (fluid), 'Fluid' (fluid). Then he thinks of Saint Simon of Trent, a boy whose disappearance and murder in the fifteenth century were blamed on the Jews and whose relics the young man had seen in a church in that city. This in turn makes him think of a blood libel only just recently held to the account of Jews and the claim of a certain Kleinpaul that the alleged victims were so many incarnations of the Saviour.[^20] Then he thinks of a newspaper article he has read in Italian under the title: 'What Saint Augustine had to say about women'. This in turn makes him think of an eccentric person known to him as Benedict. Finally, he thinks of Saint Januarius and his blood miracle: a vial in a church in Naples contains some of his blood which is supposed to become liquid again on a certain day every year, and when it does not, the people of Naples get upset. That is when the young man falls silent, reddens, and then admits he is now thinking of a matter too intimate to divulge—concerning a woman. Freud is no slouch; he provides the punchline: 'You mean she didn't get her period?'[^21] Quite.

Now the first two associations after 'a' and 'liquis' are no doubt due to the presence of the word cluster 'liqui' in '-liquis', 'Reliquien' and 'Liquidation'. Its presence makes them sound enough alike that the transition from the one to the next to the next seems natural. There is no denying that we can associate words because of the way they sound. But little else in the whole sequence would be possible unless understanding were in play (even if images are in play as well). You cannot get from 'Liquidation' to 'Flüssigkeit' without understanding something about their connection in meaning, because the two words do not sound the same. As in English, the German 'Liquidation' usually means selling off something: the inventory of a store, real estate or other non-cash holdings. It has its roots in the Latin 'liquidus', which means fluid and liquid. Perhaps when we speak of 'liquidity' in English or 'liquide Mittel' in German, we are trading on the connection: it is as if our liquid wealth (such as it is) could flow freely into the economy, like water in a stream or a river. But even if you know nothing about Latin, you must still understand what the relevant words mean to get from 'Liquidation' to 'Flüssigkeit' or 'fluid'. Many of the young man's associations seem to involve understanding in the more obviously Kantian sense of applying concepts to different things of the same kind: blood is a kind of fluid; blood libel is a kind of injustice that Jews have historically suffered (like discrimination in professional life, the topic that got the ball rolling); Simon, Augustine, and Januarius were all saints and 'Benedict' is the name of a saint (and it means 'holy' or 'blessed' in Latin). It is possible that images of all or some of the relevant things accompanied the young man's thoughts as his mind made its way from 'aliquis' to his current predicament. But that string of images would never have occurred to him if he had not also related the relevant concepts to one another: Fluid, Saint, Injustice Historically Suffered by Jews. These are concepts that all of us grasp, if we are minimally educated. We all agree roughly on how they are to be understood. The proof is that we can all follow Freud's report about what the young man was thinking. The subjective element here is the peculiar relation among these concepts in the young man's mind. It is subjective in several ways. First, it holds (gültig) for the young man and nobody else. Even the young man might have related his three concepts in a different way under different circumstances. Second, his way of relating the three concepts does not itself relate to objects. In other words, the string of concepts is not a judgement whose truth value we could assess. This, as we will see, is a point developed in §19.

Two follow-up points are in order now. The first can be stated as a question: whether any general lessons can be learned from this one young man's reported associations. The reason for doubt is precisely that the ball gets rolling when Freud invites the young man to tell him what he associates with a word, namely 'aliquis'. Once words come into play, concepts and understanding will not be far behind, even if the sounds of the words do play a role in some of the associations to come. It may thus be objected that, in a case where words and their meaning are so important from the get-go, it is no surprise that concepts and understanding will be significant along the way. We may wonder, then, what would happen if something other than a word got the ball rolling—something so primitive in us as perhaps to keep concepts and understanding at bay. Would we not expect the associations to play themselves out in a currency we share with animals: if not images, then sense impressions? It is notorious that tastes and smells can have a significant impact on our memory and imagination. Here one might call on Proust and the madeleine. But precisely that case can be turned back in favour of an account of association more closely in line with the one I am ascribing to Kant. For no sooner has Proust tasted the madeleine, then he sets out à la recherche du temps perdu. He does something that no animal would do, as far as we can tell: he recounts a long, involved story. Perhaps it is special pleading on Kant's behalf, but I take storytelling to involve much more than a string of images and sense impressions (even those stories—like Lawrence Sterne's Tristram Shandy, Jean Paul's Siebenkäse, or Arthur Schnitzler's 'Leutenant Güstl' and 'Fräulein Else'—that deliberately take the form of an association of ideas). If that is right, and if Kant is willing to grant that animals associate ideas too, he will insist that the association of ideas in human beings is different from that in non-human animals, precisely because it involves relating concepts with one another.[^22]

The second point can be developed out of a criticism of Freud and the way I imagine he would respond to it. It suggests a strategy for resolving the tension noted earlier between the A- and the B-Deduction on the question whether association of ideas is completely contingent or exhibits a certain necessity. The criticism of Freud is due to the classical scholar, Sebastiano Timpanaro. Timpanaro rejects Freud's claim that the reason the young man left out the word 'aliquis' from the line of Virgil he tried to quote is precisely his fear that he was about to become a father and that this is shown by the associations I just reported from Chapter Two of Freud's Psychopathologie des Alltags. The first prong of Timpanaro's attack consists in pointing out, in effect, that the reported associations reveal only what the young man fears and not that the fear itself explains the omission of 'aliquis'. The second prong consists in pointing out that there is an empirical law that would explain the omission of 'aliquis' much better. It is the phenomenon of banalization, well known to professional text editors (Timpanaro was the acclaimed editor of Virgil).

Banalization is a concern for classical scholarship, because it sometimes explains the errors encountered in texts and manuscripts. It will come into play when the original text is unusual, notably when it poses uncommon grammatical or stylistic difficulties. In those cases, the copyist or anybody else just trying to remember the text will tend to reproduce it in ways that avoid grammatical, metrical, or other kinds of mistakes, but in ways that also tend to simplify and thus distort it. Timpanaro puts it this way:

> Anyone who has anything to do with written or oral transmission of texts (including quotations learnt by heart) knows that they are exposed to the constant danger of banalization. Forms which have a more archaic, more high flown, more unusual stylistic expression, and which are therefore more removed from the cultural-linguistic heritage of the person who is transcribing or rewriting, tend to be replaced by forms in more common use.[^23]

The omission by Freud's interlocutor of 'aliquis' from line 625, Book Four of the Aeneid is a classic case. Timpanaro points out that this line is highly unusual. Dido has just learned that Aeneas has left her. In her grief, she says this: 'Exoriare aliquis nostris ex ossibus ultor.' It is a prophecy that her betrayal will someday be avenged. The reader knows that the avenger is Hannibal; Dido does not. The line is grammatically ambiguous, perhaps to reflect the incomplete state of Dido's foreknowledge. On the one hand, 'exoriare' is a second-person jussive subjunctive. This means that Dido is directly addressing her future avenger: arise from our bones, Avenger! But, from the perspective of both English and German speakers, this sits very uncomfortably with 'aliquis', because it is indefinite no matter which of the two ways we can take it. Taken as subject, it will mean 'somebody'. On this construal, the natural translation of the whole line into English puts the verb in the third person singular: May somebody arise from our bones as avenger! If 'ultor' is taken as subject, with 'aliquis' as its attribute, we must still translate the verb in the third person, which gives us: May some avenger arise from our bones! 'Exoriare' and 'aliquis' sit so uncomfortably together, because we do not address an indeterminate person directly: Somebody, whoever you are (I know you are out there), arise from my bones and avenge me?! There is no adequate way to capture this tension in English, or in German for that matter. A person trying to quote from memory Book Four, line 625, but failing and recognizing that he is coming up short would try to reconstruct the line from its meaning and from the elements of it that he can remember. Text editors would expect that person to come up with something simpler and distorted. The most natural banalization of this line is to omit 'aliquis'. The omission of any other word would alter the line beyond recognition. Freud's travelling companion settles nervously on 'Exoriare ex nostris ossibus ultor!', which corresponds to the first of the three English translations I tried out in this paragraph. Freud corrects him on the spot.

Text editors like Timpanaro can produce many examples of this phenomenon. It is significant, because it challenges Freud's claim that what drives association is also what drives the slips, omissions, and misrememberings at the centre of the Psychologie des Alltags, namely the unique private drama of the person who slips up. But we could all fall prey to banalization, no matter what our personal preoccupations may be. I happen to think that Timpanaro can explain why Freud's travelling companion left out 'aliquis' better than Freud himself can. But it may be useful for our purposes to consider how Freud would have defended himself against Timpanaro.

First, the ambition of Freud's book is to state general truths about slips and omissions. Such truths would apply to all of us and thus count as universal psychological laws. One law stated in the book is this: 'What all these cases [that of the missing "aliquis" and others like it] have in common . . . is that that which is forgotten or distorted is brought into connection, on some path of association, with an unconscious thought content that has forgetting as an observable effect.'[^24] Moreover, Freud acknowledges that forgetting can be promoted by all sorts of other things that might also count as universal laws. Freud himself was a migraine sufferer and reports that, during mild episodes, he would forget all the proper names known to him. Indeed, it can be stated as a universal psycho-physiological law that one of the symptoms of migraine is cognitive deficiency. This is just to say that Freud's account is not so focused on a person's private drama as to exclude all consideration of universal laws. But Freud explicitly denies that any such universal laws are themselves the causes of our slips and omissions. He tries to make his case by means of an analogy. Going out late at night all by yourself will raise the likelihood of getting robbed, he points out, but if you should be robbed in such circumstances, you will not report to the police that you have been robbed by Darkness and Being-By-Yourself. You will say that you have been held up by an unknown thief. That thief will be charged with the robbery, should he ever be caught, not the factors that made it easier for him to do mischief. It is quite possible that Freud would have treated the tendency to banalization and other such psychological laws on the same footing as the universal psycho-physiological law involved in migraine and other indispositions: they are factors that promote forgetting in all of us, without being its true cause. If we ask after the true cause of forgetting, we will learn that it depends on the 'unconscious thought content' associated with the thing we have forgotten. The only way to learn the content of this 'thought content' is to subject the relevant person to analysis. The result of the analysis will always be that person's private drama: you are the thief who stole your recollection, not migraine, over-work, much less banalization. Timpanaro recognizes that Freud will defend himself in the way just indicated. The key thing for us is not the way Timpanaro tries to advance his case against Freud, but rather the following point. If we accept Freud's analogy, it will seem that slips, omissions, misrememberings, and the relevant associations are—to use the language we have learned from the Kant of 1787—subjective, completely contingent, valid only for the person who slips up—which in turn raises the question whether Kant would have been comfortable with my coopting of his vocabulary on Freud's behalf and whether he would have embraced Freud's clever analogy.

I believe that he and his earlier self from 1781 would have quite happily, not because either of them was a proto-Freudian, but because I think Freud's witticism about Darkness and Being-By-Yourself may have resonated with them both. It would have given them a way to say both that our associations exhibit a certain necessity and that they are contingent—without contradiction.

The thought underlying Freud's witticism depends on a distinction between two kinds of causes. On the one hand, it distinguishes the true cause of an event—like the thief who is the true cause of your misfortune—from its 'antecedent' causes. An antecedent cause is one that we would be reluctant to characterize as a true cause, but that we would happily characterize as a condition that raises the likelihood the event will take place, as being out and about by yourself on a dark night raises the likelihood that you will be robbed. The notion of an antecedent cause has its natural home in medicine. Epilepsy has lots of antecedent causes. Weather, hunger, thirst, alcohol consumption, sleep deprivation can raise the likelihood that an epileptic will have a seizure. But the antecedent causes are not themselves true causes: a seizure is caused by a neurological electrical storm. Freud, of course, had medical training. He also had a broad classical education—a better one than the young man's whose slip he analyses in Chapter Two of Psychopathologie des Alltags. So perhaps he knew that the notion of antecedent or 'prokatartic' cause goes back to ancient Greek medicine, or perhaps he had encountered classical texts that take up this notion from the ancient doctors. But the important thing here is not so much how or where Freud got the notion, but rather that he embraces it—and indeed in such a way as to treat all the psycho-physiological processes that might play a role in slips and omissions as antecedent causes. No doubt, he would have responded to Timpanaro's astute criticism by treating the phenomenon of banalization and all the other phenomena familiar to text editors that Timpanaro summons against him in precisely the same way. The true cause of a person's slips and omissions will be his personal psycho-drama.

Now a true cause is the sort of thing that the doctors would more usually formulate in universal terms, just as they would for antecedent causes. In the case of epilepsy, it is a universal truth or empirical law that seizures are caused by neurological electrical storms. By contrast, the true cause of slips and omissions—at least on Freud's view—is something different for each of us: the young man had his fear of parenthood; Freud himself admits to all sorts of hang-ups that he claims have caused his own slips. The problem for Freud, of course, is to uncover the psycho-drama of the people he analyses and then establish that they are indeed the true cause of their slip-ups. We may leave it to Freud and his critics to determine whether he succeeds. I, like Timpanaro, have my doubts. The important thing here is that, if Freud be granted his case for the sake of argument, the distinction between true and antecedent causes—tweaked for the purposes of psychoanalytic theory—would allow him to say without contradiction that slips and omissions are (antecedently) caused by empirical laws valid for everybody and nevertheless highly subjective—the (true) effect of something peculiar and different in each of us: the relevant empirical laws raise the likelihood for all of us that our private turmoil will express itself in its own unique way. The position I have sketched out on Freud's behalf may be indefensible on empirical grounds, but it is not intrinsically incoherent. This observation takes us back to Kant.

For perhaps Kant would be willing to say that the empirical law he mentions explicitly in the A-Deduction is an antecedent cause of our associations. He states that law as follows: 'Vorstellungen that have often followed or accompanied one another ultimately become associated with one another' (A100). It would be plausible to say that this law raises the likelihood that we will associate cinnabar with the colour red, because you will see a lot of red if you handle a lot of cinnabar. If it is true, as I suppose, that Kant is willing to countenance other empirical laws of association besides this (like the law about splash-making appearances I considered earlier), we would expect him to say that they too raise the likelihood that we will make certain kinds of associations. The antecedent causes of association can be stated in general terms, because they apply to all of us ceteris paribus. They are indeed empirical laws and therefore exhibit a certain necessity. But they must be distinguished from the true cause of our associations. It too can be stated in general terms: the true cause of association in all of us is the spontaneity of our imagination. But, by contrast with the antecedent causes, this cause will be contingent and subjective, because the way the spontaneity of imagination is exercised is up to each of us. Thus the empirical law Kant states at A100 raises the likelihood that I will associate red and cinnabar, but I might or I might not do so. Perhaps I will associate cinnabar with the quality of being heavy, or perhaps with some other quality altogether. I am the true cause of my associations; the way I exercise the spontaneity of my imagination is ultimately up to me.

If the distinction between true and antecedent causes is applied to the association of ideas, it turns out that our associations are subjective and contingent because of their true cause, and yet they exhibit a certain necessity because of their antecedent causes. The apparent tension between the claims of the A- and the B-Deduction disappears. There may be other problems on the horizon, notably that of justifying the claim that the association of ideas is an exercise of spontaneity. But that is Kant's view. It is not implausible. That may well have been enough for Kant. So I would suggest that the Kant of 1787 did not abandon his earlier claims about the association of ideas. I would suggest instead that he played up its true cause in 1787 after having played up its antecedent causes in 1781. That creates the appearance of tension. But it is one of appearance only.

Faithful readers of Kant may take issue with my suggestion, because it comes bundled with talk of 'causes' and because Kant says that causality is governed by the principle of the Second Analogy: whether and how that principle can be invoked in this context is unclear. But faithful readers should recognize that it is counterproductive to hang so much on the word 'cause' that we deny ourselves what seems to be a pretty good way of resolving an otherwise serious tension between the A- and the B-Deduction. It might also be urged against my suggestion that Kant had no medical training and that it is unclear whether he would have been aware of the distinction we have been exploring. I admit that my case would be stronger if I could document it from Kant's writings. But two things make me reluctant to give up my suggestion. First, it seems helpful. But second, Kant had as good a classical education as Freud. Even without medical training, he could have encountered the distinction in the Latin texts he studied in school that transmit Stoic ideas. One obvious text would be Cicero's De fato 41 which reports Chrysippus' use of the distinction to defend compatibilism. It would be fun to try to explore this, but not here.

### §2 A Careful Look at §19 of the B-Deduction: Why Should We Suppose that the Logical Form of all Judgements Consists in the Objective Unity of Apperception?

#### §2a The programme, a puzzle, and a proposal

The argument of §19 in the B-Deduction goes beyond §18 in developing the distinction between the subjective unity and the objective unity of apperception. It does so by introducing a new element for consideration, namely judgement. The fundamental claim of §19 is announced in the heading: 'The Logical Form of All Judgements Consists in the Objective Unity of Apperception of the Concepts Contained Therein'. If the claim is true, then the distinction between the objective and the subjective unity of apperception can be repackaged as the distinction between judgements (or their logical form), on the one hand, and the association of ideas, on the other.

Kant opens §19 with a critical remark about the logicians of his time. He says that he 'has never been able to find satisfaction with the explanation they give of "a judgement as such" [ein Urteil überhaupt]' (B141). He reports that they say judgement is 'the representation of a relation between two concepts' (B141). This will not do for several reasons. But the chief reason cited by Kant at the beginning of §19 is that it leaves completely undetermined 'what this relation [between two concepts] consists in' (B141). Therein lies the rub: if Kant is right, then the association of ideas is also a relation among concepts. Images may well be involved, but not without a transition in the mind from one concept to another. Freud's young travelling companion could not have merely associated images; he must have associated the concepts Fluid and Saint with the concept Injustice Historically Suffered by Jews. What would the image of a blood libel look like anyway? But if the association of ideas is a relation among concepts, we will wonder how it is to be distinguished from the relation among concepts peculiar to judgement. That is the challenge Kant lays down for the logicians in §19 of the B-Deduction. To see how serious this challenge might be, we must determine who the logicians are and what they say about judgement.

The characterization of judgement Kant attributes to them does seem to have been a commonplace in the textbooks. I have found it in Wolff, Baumgarten, and Meier.[^25] But a closer look might suggest that Kant's challenge is cheap. By this, I do not mean that his question about how to distinguish rigorously between judgements and the association of ideas is philosophically uninteresting, but rather that his complaint against the logicians may seem unfair and misdirected. For in the many passages where the logicians characterize judgement as 'the representation of the relation between two concepts', they make it very clear what this relation consists in. At §325 in the Vernunftlehre, for example, Georg Friedrich Meier characterizes it as follows:

> The logical relation of concepts consists in their agreement with, and their repugnance of, one another. . . . When we thus consider some concepts in relation with one another, we must represent to ourselves either that the one belongs [zukommt] to the other or that it does not belong to the other. When we do this, we are judging. Thus a judgement consists in a representation of the relation of several concepts to one another.[^26]

One concept 'agrees with' another if it 'belongs' to it (ihm zukommt), i.e., if the one 'can be represented as a mark' of the other. If the one does not belong to the other, i.e., cannot be represented as the other's mark, then they do not agree, i.e., they are repugnant to one another. In §293, Meier says that one concept 'belongs' to another if it is higher than the other in a logical hierarchy that results from forming concepts by abstraction. Experience confronts us with 'individual, actual things' (einzelne, würkliche Dinge). We have 'sensations' (Empfindungen) of these things, i.e., 'individual concepts' (einzelne Begriffe). These individual concepts are the lowest concepts in the hierarchy. We get higher concepts by extracting from them the part concepts that they include and that they have in common with other individual concepts. For example, perhaps I encounter a human being called Titus of whom I acquire by acquaintance an individual concept. Suppose that I observe in Titus all sorts of personal qualities, as revealed in his word and deed, that impress me in a certain way. His individual concept will include these qualities. Now perhaps I observe the same qualities in other people too. Then I will extract from the individual concept I have of Titus and the individual concepts of these other people a higher concept that expresses what they all have in common. I will ultimately arrive at the concept of Virtue. Meier puts it this way: 'So when we represent [uns vorstellt] an abstract concept [ein abgesondeter Begriff], we take an elevated position, as it were, from which we can take in many things together all at once by representing that which is common to all of them and in which they agree' (§293). From the higher position afforded to me by the abstract concept Virtue, I can capture in a single thought what is common to Titus and all his virtuous companions.

Now Titus and the concept Virtue are Meier's own examples. But his treatment of them is a little more complicated than I have indicated. He treats Virtue not as a concept abstracted directly from the individual concepts of the people I admire: as though only one rung up the ladder, but rather as being at least two rungs up. I will form on the first rung of the ladder what will turn out, upon further reflection, to be less abstract concepts of more specific preferred personal qualities: what I will perhaps later come to recognize respectively as Civic Virtue, Natural Virtue, and Christian Virtue. I will form the concept of Virtue as such only after I recognize what these preferred personal qualities all have in common. But to the extent that the concept of Virtue as such is higher on the ladder than Civic Virtue, I can 'represent it as a mark' of Civic Virtue. In other words, the higher rung concept 'belongs' to the lower rung concept or is included in it. The judgement 'Civic Virtue is a Species of Virtue' is just a representation of this relation between my two concepts. Of course, it often happens that one concept does not belong to another and hence cannot be represented as its mark. The concept Virtue does not belong to the concept Colour. I can represent the relation of not belonging in the judgement: 'Colour is not Virtue' (§293).

All this goes to show that Meier has a precise answer to the question: what does the relation of concepts essential to judgement consist in? Not only that, his account of this relation should be familiar to any reader of Kant's logic, because it corresponds almost exactly to what Kant had to say about the hierarchy of concepts in his logic lectures: as recorded both in his students' notes and in the published Jäsche logic. Even the technical vocabulary is the same. Concepts have an 'extension' (Umfang), namely all those 'lower' concepts that lie 'under them'; a concept's extension is all the greater, i.e., it has comparatively more concepts under it, the 'higher' it is up the ladder. On the other hand, the 'higher' concepts are contained in the lower concepts as marks (Merkmale) or 'part concepts' (Teilbegriffe). The lower a concept is the more marks it has.[^27] By the 1780s, the only big difference between Meier and Kant on all of this is that Meier apparently believes that there is a bottom rung on the ladder: that is where the lowest concepts of all are perched. These are the 'individual concepts' (einzelne Begriffe) or 'sensations' (Empfindungen) of 'individual things' that we get directly from experience and from which we extract the higher concepts. Kant explicitly denies in the note to §11 of the Jäsche logic that these lowest concepts are possible: 'because such a concept could not possibly be determined. For even if we did have a concept that we applied directly to individuals, there could nevertheless still be specific differences present that we either did not notice or that we left out of consideration' (9.97.23). If a concept is the lowest in the hierarchy, it is an infima species, i.e., a species that cannot be regarded as a genus with further, still lower species falling under it. But if it is conceivable, as Kant claims, that a concept we take to be lowest in the hierarchy has specific differences we either overlooked or left out of account, then there are concepts that fall under it and it is not the lowest in the hierarchy, against the assumption that it is. Of course, this objection will ultimately bottom out in the point taken from the Transcendental Aesthetic that sensations are not concepts (individual or otherwise) and hence cannot be made more and more abstract by the subtraction of marks or part concepts: there are no marks or part concepts to subtract from a sensation. But Kant's logic fundamentally agrees with that of Meier, at least on the matters we have been considering. So it seems, on the face of it, that his complaint about the unnamed logicians in §19 of the B-Deduction is cheap. Kant could be dissatisfied with Meier's logic for any number of reasons, but not because it leaves 'undetermined' the relation between concepts essential to judgement.

Kant's words at the end of the first paragraph of §19 of the B-Deduction may well be unfair. But they indicate that he is deliberately positioning himself against the textbook account of judgement. This suggests a way to get a clearer picture of how he is thinking about the distinction between judgements (objective unity) and the association of ideas (subjective unity), namely by probing the textbook account of judgement. That is what I will do in §2b. I will argue that the account has a defect. It struggles with certain obvious questions—indeed in a way that strongly suggests Kant was right: it does leave undetermined the relation between concepts that it claims is peculiar to judgement. I will argue that the insight from §18 of the B-Deduction, namely that our associations involve concepts, is used in §19 to press the textbook account of judgements at this, its weakest point. Then I will explore how Kant tries to remedy this defect and what we are supposed to learn about judgement and objective unity from this remedy. It will come as no surprise that the remedy involves reflecting on the logical form of judgement. The problem will be to see how Kant is thinking about this notion. I think I can offer a new perspective on this problem, in part by calling attention to the distinction Kant draws between form and function: I believe it has been badly misunderstood.[^28] I plan to continue focusing on Meier as spokesman for the textbook account, just because his was Kant's adopted textbook in the lecture hall and always an important point of reference for him. I will supplement Meier with Wolff as the need arises.

#### §2b Meier and some questions about judgement

For the purposes of this exercise, we will focus on the simplest case: that of affirmative categorical judgements. Meier's treatment of it raises certain questions. They ultimately turn on judgements and concepts and the difference between them. They come to a head in the case of false affirmative categorical judgements. It is not clear how Meier will deal with this one. Other cases would likely pose difficulties for him too. But this one indicates clearly how vulnerable he is to Kant's challenge, namely to account for the special relation among concepts that he (Meier) claims to be constitutive of a judgement. We may thus take it to be symptomatic.

I assume that Kant took Meier's theory of judgement to be roughly on the right track, just because he used it as the basis for teaching logic and as a point of reference in formulating his own account. If it did not stand a chance of getting things right, he would have used something else. But he also took it to be either incomplete or deficient in its elaboration. Johann Gottfried Kiesewetter is a witness. In a letter of 3 July 1791, he reminds Kant what he (Kant) said in answer to his (Kiesewetter's) question which textbook was best for teaching logic:[^29] 'You replied (all this remains vivid in my memory) that you lecture on logic out of Meier . . . , but that you are not satisfied with this textbook' (11.267.16–18). Sure enough, Kant does not merely parrot Meier, even in the logic lectures or in the published Jäsche handbook. I take all this to mean, on balance, that he sought not to jettison or discredit Meier, but rather to correct the deficiencies in his account of judgement. The idea in §19 of the B-Deduction was to set right an account that was only roughly on the right track.

My focus in this section on affirmative categorical judgements will naturally raise the question: what about the other forms of judgement? For now, it is enough to say just this. First, in focusing on these judgements, I am following Kant's lead in §19. But second, the focus can be justified by the simplicity of the case: it is just easier to figure out what to say about this one. It does not follow, however, that affirmative categorical judgements are more fundamental than other kinds of judgement, nor that the other kinds of judgement can somehow be converted into, or reduced to, these. Kant had better have something to say about what makes all the different kinds of judgement distinctive and distinguishable from one another. But he is not on the hook to do this in §19 of the B-Deduction. All he needs to do here is make a case for the dependence of the form of any judgement whatsoever on the 'supreme principle of all use of the understanding'—a case that does not depend so heavily on the meaning of the word 'supreme' as to be merely verbal.

As we saw, Meier characterizes judgement as 'a representation of the relation of several concepts' (§325). An affirmative categorical judgement is a representation of one concept as a mark or a part of another concept. When I judge that frogs are amphibians, I represent the concept Amphibian as a mark contained in the concept Frog. By the same token, I represent Frog as falling under Amphibian. An important question raised by this is how to distinguish between forming an affirmative categorical judgement about frogs and merely entertaining the concept Frog without making any judgement at all. In short, what is the difference between judgements and concepts? It seems impossible to entertain a given concept in a non-judging way without at the same time thinking of the other concepts that might be related to it. At least some of what it is to entertain the concept Frog is to conceive what frogs might be or might be like. If we entertain the concept Frog at all, we will also inevitably entertain some other concept: perhaps Amphibian, Speckled, Nocturnal, Cannibal, or Hoppy, or even some completely far-fetched one like Mammal or Avian. The entertaining of both concepts at once could perfectly well come about by association of ideas—but not if we are faithfully thinking through the issue on Meier's terms. From Meier's point of view, as far as I can tell, the only sort of relation among concepts is 'logical': the relation of being higher and lower on the ladder of abstraction. For example, the concept Speckled might be thought to be at least one rung higher than the concept Frog, and Frog might be thought to fall under it, along with a few other concepts as well. But, of course, this is precisely the relation said to be at play in the judgement: Frogs are speckled. For if the concept Speckled is higher on the ladder of abstraction than the concept Frog, it belongs to Frog as one of its marks. On the face of it, then, the textbook theory of judgement seems to obscure the difference between judgements and concepts, since it appeals to the same 'logical relation' to account for both. It makes life hard for itself too, because it does not help itself to an easy way of drawing the distinction that depends on the notion of assent: an affirmative judgement is a concept to which we have given our assent; a negative judgement is one from which we have withdrawn it—if we have neither given our assent, nor withdrawn it, the concept remains just a concept. So we may ask what the textbook theory could do if pressed to clarify itself on this point.

Perhaps it would say that the chief difference between concepts and judgements is just that a judgement is an explicit awareness of the relation among concepts that would obtain anyway, even if we were just thinking of frogs—say—by means of the relevant concepts without making any judgement about them. That may have been the idea behind a remark Kant is reported by Philippi to have made in his logic lecture in 1772. 'When I judge, I compare one concept with others and enquire whether they belong to it or are opposed to it, i.e., whether they are its marks or not. Only through this operation, through judging, do concepts become distinct [deutlich]. A distinct concept is nothing other than a judgement[^31]' (24.461.14–18). 'Distinct concept' connotes explicit awareness. Kant is reported to have characterized distinct concepts earlier in the lecture as follows: 'In . . . composite representations either we are conscious of the whole and at the same time of parts contained therein, and then the concept is distinct. Or we are conscious of the whole without being conscious of the parts, in which case our concept is indistinct or confused' (24.342.6–10). In a distinct concept of Frog, I am explicitly aware of Amphibian and Speckled, and I presumably understand how all three concepts relate to one another, namely as whole to parts. But that is just to say that I have indeed formed the judgement: Frogs are speckled amphibians. By implication, indistinct concepts are not judgements, because they do not involve the explicit awareness of the parts that constitute them. To the extent that any concept can be made distinct, if it is not already so, it seems to follow that every concept is at least potentially a judgement,[^32] (which is perhaps why Meier is willing in places to speak of true and false concepts (§329)—against the expectation that only judgements can have a truth value). Thus it might be argued that a judgement is an explicit awareness of the marks of a concept, or the operation by means of which we make the concept distinct. Until that operation has been carried out, we have an indistinct concept, but not a judgement.

Still, this solution should not satisfy Meier (or Kant in 1772, for that matter). One reason is that it is surely possible to be explicitly aware of Amphibian and Speckled as marks of Frog, while deliberately abstaining from forming the relevant judgement. In other words, not all distinct concepts are judgements, contrary to Kant's remark quoted above from the 1772 lecture on logic. Indeed, one and the same distinct concept might sometimes count as a judgement and sometimes not; sometimes it will count as a free-floating concept.[^33] This seems to be reflected in the surface grammar of our language. Treated linguistically, Frog is a noun, and Speckled is an adjective that can modify the noun. But it can do so in two ways. One way is to treat the adjective as a predicate, as when we form a judgement expressed linguistically by the complete sentence, 'Frogs are speckled.' In this sentence, 'speckled' is the predicate of the subject, which is 'frogs'. But we can also treat the adjective attributively, as when it is used in the expression 'speckled frogs'. This expression is not itself a complete sentence and so cannot linguistically express any judgement. The proof is that, if anyone should say 'speckled frogs', we would naturally be inclined to ask: and what about them? We would expect his answer, if stated fully and explicitly, to include the expression 'speckled frogs'. But we would also expect it to take the form of a complete sentence or judgement, which means that a predicate would have to be provided. Thus the person might use 'amphibian' as the predicate and answer our question with the judgement that 'Speckled frogs are amphibians.' The point is this: when the person we are imagining uses the adjective attributively, he can be understood to indicate that Speckled is a mark of Frog just as explicitly as when he forms a judgement about frogs and treats Speckled as the predicate. The incomplete expression 'speckled frogs' and the complete judgement 'frogs are speckled' both indicate unambiguously that the one concept is a mark of the other. If the so-called attributive use of adjectives tracks our thought and use of concepts, it takes the air out of the solution Kant might be taken to offer Meier in the logic lecture of 1772. It cannot be the case, as he apparently claimed, that 'a distinct concept is nothing other than a judgement.' For the same distinct concept, Speckled Frog, will be at work when we use Speckled attributively and when we use it as a predicate. But only when we use it as a predicate do we make an affirmative categorical judgement.

Meier himself surely understands this, because he writes: 'If, to boot, our rigorous [gelehrt] knowledge is to be interconnected [zusammenhängend], then it cannot consist in mere concepts, but rather these concepts must be combined with one another [mit einander] and thus set in a proper relation with one another, and this cannot ever happen without judgement[^34]' (§324). This passage is elliptic, but it also points forward to the chapter on syllogistic inference. The reason we cannot set our concepts 'in proper relation' with one another without judgement is that we need properly formed judgements to compare our concepts in such a way as to generate from them conclusions to demonstrative arguments. Suppose we take the following three concepts in isolation from one another:
(1) Luminous,
(2) Moon,
(3) That-Which-Renders-Bodies-Visible.
We could directly compare these concepts. If we did so, we would find that the second one is a singular concept, assuming it stands for the earth's moon and no other, while the other concepts are not. But that comparison all by itself would not get us very far. We would certainly not get an inference out of it. To get an inference, we must form judgements out of these concepts that can serve as true premises in a syllogism. This will involve treating the third of these concepts as the 'middle term'. The first concept will serve as the predicate of the major premise, and the second concept will be the subject of the minor premise. The middle term will be the predicate of the minor premise and the subject of the major premise. It will not appear in the conclusion, but it will allow us to relate the other two terms in such a way as to form a conclusion out of them whose subject is the second concept and whose predicate is the first. Thus the example syllogism, which I have taken from Wolff, proceeds as follows:
Major Premise: All things that make bodies visible (3) are luminous (1).
Minor Premise: The Moon (2) makes bodies visible (3).
Conclusion: The moon (2) is something luminous (1).
Inferences like this one relate truths to one another in such a way as to make our knowledge 'interconnected'. They bring this about by putting our concepts in 'proper relation' with one another. But this means forming premises out of our concepts, which means in turn that judgements are necessary. Judgements are more (or other) than free-floating concepts, as can be seen directly from the surface grammar. The middle term in a syllogism of the first figure, like the one above, is treated as a predicate in the minor premise. It may perhaps be expressed by a word that can function grammatically as an adjective, but then that word will not be treated attributively. No matter how distinct the concept expressed by the middle term may be, if it is not made the predicate of the concept taken to be the subject of the minor premise, no conclusion will be reached. Moreover, we must also be able to recognize, as here in the case of a syllogism of the first figure, when one and the same concept, namely the middle term, is a subject and when it is a predicate, because it is required to do double duty: as subject in the major premise and predicate in the minor. Whatever that capacity involves, it will be intimately tied up with whatever makes it possible to form judgements; and, without it, syllogistic inferences like the one I just borrowed from Wolff would be impossible. Hence, the implication of §324 of Meier's Vernunftlehre is precisely that judgements must be something more (or other) than free-floating concepts.

Now Meier has a way of solving the problem what makes the difference between concepts and judgements. The question will be whether it is adequate. It can be understood to play out as follows. The difference between a free-floating concept like the one expressed by 'speckled frogs' and a judgement like 'Frogs are speckled' is the presence or absence of an added concept. An affirmative categorical judgement is a special concept that arises when we add to two concepts a third concept of the relation between the other two as subject and predicate. This third concept is expressed linguistically by the copula. Let us hear the idea expressed in Meier's own words:

> Every [affirmative] judgement is always composed of at least three concepts: the subject concept, the predicate concept and . . . the connecting concept [Verbindungsbegriff]. . . . The subject of a judgement is that concept whereof we represent that the other concept belongs . . . to it. But that concept whereof we represent that it belongs . . . to the subject is called the predicate of a judgement. The representation of the agreement of the predicate with the subject is the connecting concept. (§326)

It is the connecting concept that explicitly represents the predicate as a mark that belongs to the subject. Meier would surely say that the connecting concept is missing from incomplete expressions like 'speckled frog' where one concept is used attributively with respect to another.

Now one might doubt whether this solution to the problem is sufficient. If you represent two concepts like Frog and Speckled together in an incomplete expression like 'speckled frogs', have you not already represented the relation between them? Does the concept of the relation between two concepts add anything at all to the two concepts themselves? This may seem doubtful, precisely because the relation in question is supposed to be constitutive of the concepts: how can you have them without also having the relevant relation between them while being aware that you have it? If you were not aware of this relation, in what sense would you be consciously entertaining the relevant concept, even in a non-judging way? Perhaps the best way to defend Meier's idea against an objection like this is to say that the connecting concept helps him draw an important distinction, so far not yet in view, between two different ways in which two concepts can relate to each other: it is one thing for two concepts to relate to each other as whole to part and another thing for the two of them to relate to each other as the subject and predicate of an affirmative categorical judgement. Meier believes that the latter relation is a relation of whole to part, just like the former. That has been the source of our problem all long, because it collapses the relation between two concepts in a judgement into the relation between two concepts on the ladder of abstraction. But for that very reason, it seems plausible to say, on Meier's behalf, that the relation of subject and predicate in an affirmative categorical judgement must be distinguished from the relation of whole to part as a special case, namely that very case we find exemplified in judgements. If Meier does not take this suggestion on board, he will be committed to thinking that a person who forms a given whole concept is at the same time making not just one judgement, but as many different judgements, in fact, as the whole concept has parts. That would be silly. For perhaps the whole concept Frog, as I have framed it, has the following parts: Amphibian, Hoppy, Speckled, and Nocturnal. But perhaps all I wish to say for now is that frogs are nocturnal. It is not implausible to suggest that, in making this judgement, I have done something to call special attention to the part–whole relation of Nocturnal to Frog, while setting aside all the rest. So it might be thought that Meier's motivation for introducing the 'connecting concept' is precisely to account for how this is possible: I call special attention to the part–whole relation of Nocturnal to Frog, while setting aside all the rest, by adding the connecting concept to two of them and not to any of the others. The effect is that I treat Frog as the subject of my judgement and Nocturnal as the predicate, and that is just to say that I form a judgement out of them.[^35]

Though the considerations I just spelled out may well motivate the introduction of the connecting concept into Meier's theory of judgement, they are no more than a promissory note until they can be fleshed out and properly defended. Meier says all too little about what he takes the connecting concept to be, include, or represent. He says nothing about how it is supposed to work. Hence, he leaves the field open to alternative ways of accounting for the relation between subject and predicate in a judgement, e.g. the Hobbesian or nominalist solution, which is just to make it a matter of convention.[^36] I said in the previous section that Kant's explicit criticism of Meier's theory of judgement in §19 of the B-Deduction may seem cheap. But until Meier can make good on the idea of a connecting concept, his theory is indeed open to criticism. It is true, as Kant says, that Meier leaves undetermined the relation between two concepts he (Meier) says is constitutive of a judgement. For it remains unclear, even after having factored the connecting concept into the mix, how precisely Meier will distinguish between judgements and free-floating concepts. This takes us back to a worry raised at the beginning of this section.

It turns out that concepts can relate to one another in several ways. One is the 'logical relation' of concepts on Meier's ladder of abstraction. Another is found in the association of ideas, assuming with Kant in §18 of the B-Deduction that our associations depend on, and trade in, concepts. In §19 of the B-Deduction, we are looking for a distinctive third way of relating concepts that will give us judgements, because a judgement is 'the representation of a relation between two concepts.' But so far, Meier has yet to deliver the goods. The reflections we undertook on his behalf apply to affirmative categorical judgements, notably when they are true. As we have seen in that case, the relation between concepts in a judgement risks collapsing into the 'logical relation of concepts' on the ladder of abstraction. But it is not at all clear what Meier will say about affirmative categorical judgements when they are false. Yes of course, he will say that, in such judgements, the concept expressed by the predicate does not belong to the concept expressed by the subject. We can see this in the judgement that frogs are fish. Fish does not belong to Frog. It may well be higher on the ladder of abstraction, but Frog does not fall under it. What are we going to say about such judgements?

They are odd. Because they are affirmative, they deploy the connecting concept, but because they are false, the connecting concept fails to connect the relevant concepts. To that extent, the relevant concepts do not relate to each other. As far as I can tell, Meier allows for only two possibilities: either one concept is the mark of another, or it is not. But if one concept is not a mark of another, why should we not characterize the two of them as 'free-floating' with respect to each other? And why should we think that two free-floating concepts constitute a judgement at all? Has a judgement been formed when Fish and Frog float freely? They could just be two concepts that a person might choose to entertain for whatever peculiar reason. If Meier insists that they do relate to each other, but not through the one being a mark of the other, then what else may we conclude, given the resources currently at hand, except that they have been freely associated by somebody by empirical laws of association? It is especially here that the insight of §18 in the B-Deduction packs a punch: Meier cannot tell us, in this case at least, what makes the difference between a string of associated concepts and a judgement.

Now it may be thought that a check is in place against these concerns, just because the connecting concept has been deployed in the case at hand. To be sure, it has failed to connect. But if it has been deployed at all, then Meier will no doubt push back and say that we have a judgement. It may be false, but falsehood does not undermine its status as a judgement. Why should it? Not all judgements are true. But what will Meier say if somebody replies that the failure of the connecting concept converts a false judgement into a fake judgement: i.e., it does not yield a judgement; it obscures from intellectual view the fact that two concepts, which are represented as—say—subject and predicate or as whole to part, freely float with respect to each other. A fake judgement is no more a judgement than a fake Picasso is a Picasso.

By taking a complete tour of all the things Meier can say about the simplest possible case—affirmative categorical judgements, both true and false—we can see, I think, that Kant's criticism in §19 is anything but cheap. In the best case, Meier reduces the relation between two concepts in a judgement to the 'logical relation' of concepts on the ladder of abstraction. In the worst case, he dissolves that relation altogether such that we must say either that no judgement is left or that the relevant concepts relate to each other as they do by association of ideas. If, then, we say with Meier that a judgement is the representation of the relation of two concepts, we must do more to specify what this relation consists in. Kant tries to do just that.

#### §2c Kant on the connecting concept and the logical form of categorical judgements

Immediately after having taken to task the unnamed logicians in §19 of the B-Deduction, Kant sets out in summary what he takes to be the right account of judgement:

> But when I investigate more closely the relating [Beziehung] of given cognitions in every judgement and distinguish it, as belonging to the understanding, from the relation [Verhältnis] determined by laws of the reproductive imagination, which only has subjective validity, I find that a judgement is nothing other than the way given cognitions are brought to the objective unity of apperception. This is indicated in judgements by the little relational wordling[^30] 'is' [das Verhältniswörtchen ist] to distinguish between the objective unity of given representations from the subjective. For 'is' designates the relating of these representations to original apperception and its necessary unity, even if the judgement is empirical and hence contingent. (B141–142)

The thought here is plausible, so far as it goes. There is a difference between judging that cinnabar is red and associating the mineral with its distinctive colour. The claim of the B-Deduction is that our associations are 'completely contingent' and hence 'subjectively' valid. If I associate red with cinnabar, that is valid for me. If you associate it with black, that is valid for you. But judgements are objectively valid. That must mean 'valid for everybody', because judgements, unlike associations, have a truth value. The truth value of a judgement is ultimately the same for everybody. We often disagree with one another about whether a given judgement is true or false, but that is only because some of us are in error. If we could correct all our errors, there would be no disagreement among us. Even now, in the face of error, we agree in principle that all the true judgements are true and all the false judgements are false. For Kant, the 'objective unity of apperception' is the key, because having a truth value will depend on relating our concepts to objects, and we know from §17 of the B-Deduction that relating our concepts to objects is impossible unless our given representations are taken up into the original transcendental unity of pure apperception. If the argument of §17 is in order, it follows that the objective unity of pure apperception is the basis of all judgement. Kant claims, in effect, that he can do what the unnamed logicians failed to do: he can specify the relation between concepts constitutive of a judgement, as they could not, just because he can show that this relation is somehow a consequence of the objective unity of pure apperception. Unless such unity comes into play, our concepts will float freely with respect to one another, or they will relate to one another under the empirical laws of association.

But the plausibility of this argument is only skin deep. As presented in §19 of the B-Deduction, the argument does too little to illuminate the nature of judgement as such and its relationship to the objective unity of pure apperception. Indeed, Kant's remark about the copula calls attention to this problem. It does so, not just because the 'little relational wordling "is"' is logically significant—if at all—in affirmative categorical judgements, while other 'wordlings' will surely have to go to work in other kinds of judgements, but more especially for this reason: if we are going to focus on affirmative categorical judgements, we should note how inconsequential 'is' is from a logical point of view. We use it in some languages, like German, Latin, and English. If a German speaker were to utter the words 'Zinnober' and then 'rot', other German speakers might naturally infer that he is making associations. Unless he says 'ist' after saying the one word and before saying the other, he cannot be taken to have made a judgement. But this is just a conventional peculiarity of German. Other languages can, or must, make do without the copula. If a speaker of ancient Greek were to utter the words 'τὸ κιννάβαρι' and 'ἐρυθρόν', it would not be clear, even to another Greek speaker, if he were making associations or making the judgement that cinnabar is red. If he then gave a disquisition on cinnabar, it would be clear that the latter was the case. This goes to show that, as far as linguistic expression is concerned, what counts as a judgement is indeed a matter of convention, which implies that, if logic—general or transcendental—has anything to say about what counts as a judgement, it had better make its case without leaning on the idiosyncrasies of a given language. Kant does not seem to have known Greek and so may not have been aware of this.[^37] But had somebody called it to his attention, I imagine that he would have then said that the linguistic conventions themselves ultimately rest on the objective unity of apperception and that each of them indicates this in its own peculiar way. But then we would have wanted to learn from him how he thought this should work.

In short, the thinking behind §19 in the B-Deduction needs to be brought to life. The way to do this is to compare Kant's theory of judgement with that of the logicians. We will continue to use Meier's theory as the point of reference, but we will pick up Wolff's theory along the way to fill out some important details. As before, we will focus on affirmative categorical judgements and the idea of a 'connecting concept' (Meier's appellation for it—Wolff calls it something else, as we will see). The comparison will allow us, by the end of this section, to shed some light on a notion that Kant assigns to general logic, namely that of the form of a judgement. Thereafter, we will pursue the comparison and explore what Kant takes to be the underpinnings of this notion in transcendental logic. That will allow us ultimately to consider in a fuller—and I hope more satisfying—way how §17 of the B-Deduction is developed in §19 and thus how the objective unity of apperception gets us judgements.

* * *

I argued in §2b that Meier's theory of affirmative categorical judgements is little more than a promissory note, because it leaves the idea of a connecting concept undeveloped and vulnerable to attack. I am betting, of course, that Kant would agree with me. If he did not, it would be harder to rescue his criticism of the logicians in §19 of the B-Deduction from the charge of being cheap. But it is striking that Kant never seems to have explicitly mentioned or directly engaged with the connecting concept in the logic lectures or the published Jäsche handbook. Nor have I found a 'Reflexion' on it in his hand in his personal copy of Meier. The natural explanation is not that Kant's practice was to pass over in silence the parts of Meier that he thought required further thinking. For he would occasionally stop presenting the material dogmatically and subject it to direct criticism.[^38] The natural explanation is rather that Kant believed his time in the lecture hall would be better spent discussing other things. It is easy to discern in broad brush what these things were, at least on the topic immediately relevant for my purposes. Judging from the student transcripts, the part of the lectures on judgement tended to follow a certain pattern of presentation. First, Kant would present one or more definitions of judgement: Meier's, his own (which could vary), some of each. Then he would present the different kinds of judgement, usually under the headings: quality, quantity, relation, and modality. Meier treats lots of issues related to these headings, but he does not do so systematically under the headings themselves. It is possible that this semi-independent programme in Kant's logic lectures simply swamped Meier's connecting concept. But Kant lectured on logic out of Meier for years. In that time, he had to have decided what he thought about it. My hunch is that he did not dismiss the connecting concept out of hand. I think that he may be understood to have modified it to fit his conception of logical form. Here the evidence is circumstantial, but not insignificant: once it occurs to you to confront the connecting concept with Kant's notion of logical form, the connecting concept is most naturally understood as transforming into the logical form of categorical judgements, as Kant conceives it. No violence is needed to induce this transformation, as I will argue in what follows.

The title of §19 of the B-Deduction announces that the 'logical form of judgement' will be of central importance: 'The Logical Form of All Judgements Consists in the Objective Unity of Apperception of the Concepts Contained Therein'. But this is odd, because there is no further mention of 'logical form' in the body of this section. Kant characterizes judgement in the passage from §19 I quoted above in the following terms: 'a judgement is nothing other than the way given cognitions are brought to the objective unity of apperception' (B141). This does not seem to be the expected characterization of the logical form of judgement, but rather a characterization of judgements as such. On a certain understanding of 'logical form', it could be both, namely on the assumption that 'logical form' is just what all possible judgements have in common: after all, that is what Kant's characterization of judgement is trying to capture. But this would not be helpful. We naturally expect talk about form to go hand in hand with talk about matter. Must there not be 'logical' matter to go with its formal counterpart? This is not an idle question: Meier, for example, says that every syllogism has both matter and form and that both are equally 'logical', because logic studies them both and the way they form a composite. It turns out that the form of a syllogism is the inference to its conclusion and the matter is its premises (§395).[^39] We remember, of course, that Kant explicitly denies in the Critique of Pure Reason that logic makes a study of anything other than form, as when he says this: 'General logic . . . considers only logical form in the relation of cognitions to one another, i.e., the form of thinking as such' (A55/B79). But this just complicates things. If the logical form of judgement is just what all possible judgements have in common, then the matter of judgement, which Kant will want to exclude from the study of logic, must be anything that distinguishes judgements from one another. And yet Kant himself thinks that judgements are sometimes distinguished from one another by their logical form: how else will he distinguish—say—categorical judgements from hypothetical ones on the table of judgements? So we need clarity from Kant about what he takes his characterization of judgement in this section to characterize and how it bears on logical form whose significance is announced in the title of §19. The way forward is to step back from the Deduction and think a little bit about Kant's efforts to clarify what he meant by the logical form of judgement elsewhere.

Kant seems to have made a point of distinguishing the matter and form of judgement in his lectures on logic. This can be seen in all the surviving student transcripts. In three of them, he characterizes the form of judgement in a way that holds only for categorical judgements.[^40] He must have intended that characterization as a representative example, and he must have hoped that his students would infer from the special case what holds true for all cases. That he wanted them to recognize other forms of judgement besides the categorical is plain from the fact that he always undertook to present these other forms under the familiar headings of quality, quantity, relation, and modality. It is also plain from the fact that he is reported in the Donau-Wundlacken transcript as explicitly criticizing Meier for characterizing all judgements in the parochial terms applicable only to categorical judgements. Thus Kant himself recognized that he faced a serious pedagogical and philosophical problem: how to convey intelligibly to his students what all the different logical forms of judgement have in common. The Wiener transcript records what may be the most successful attempt to solve this problem. If it succeeds better than the others, it is not because of the way Kant is reported as having distinguished between matter and form as such. On that score, he seems to have said only this: 'To every judgement there belongs matter and form. The matter comprises the cognitions in general, the concepts; the form must constitute the connection and unity of the representation' (24.928.36–929.1). This is unhelpful, because, as we have seen, there is no reason to think that just any connection or unity of representations will count as a judgement, and yet the claim here seems to be that a judgement is a judgement because of its form. Still, the quicky gloss on form comes on the heels of a characterization of judgement as such that adds useful detail and resonates verbally with the characterization of judgement that I quoted above from §19 of the B-Deduction. Thus the Wiener transcript reports Kant as characterizing judgement as follows:

> Judgement is in general the representation of the unity in a relation [Verhältniß] of many cognitions. A judgement is the representation of the way that the concepts belong to a consciousness universally objectively [allgemein objektiv]. If one thinks to oneself two representations, how they are connected together as cognitions and how they together constitute a single cognition [Eine Erkenntniß], then that is a judgement. Thus in every judgement there is a certain relation of different representations insofar as they belong to one cognition. (24.928.24–31)

It is presumably from this characterization of judgement that Kant hoped his students would understand the distinction between the matter and the form of judgement that he went on to present a moment (i.e., a couple of lines) later. Thus the matter of a judgement is just those concepts or cognitions out of which a judgement is made: the subject concept and the predicate concept, in the case of a categorical judgement.[^41] The form is the 'connection or unity' that supervenes on the matter when we make a judgement: the different concepts or cognitions that make up the matter of a judgement are connected by its form so as to constitute 'one cognition'. But not just any kind of connection or unity will do. It must be such that the concepts 'belong to a consciousness universally objectively'. That verbally sounds as if the concepts or cognitions, so connected and united, will be 'objectively valid' in the presumed sense of §19 of the B-Deduction, i.e., valid for everybody because of their jointly having a truth value. In sum, the form of a judgement seems to be the way that concepts or cognitions are connected so as to yield 'one cognition' that has a truth value.

The sympathetic resonance of this stretch in the Wiener transcript and our passage from §19 of the B-Deduction is unmistakable. This suggests that the pattern of thinking in both passages is the same. If that is right, then Kant's characterization of judgement in the latter passage is indeed a characterization of judgement as such, and we readers are expected to infer from it what is meant by 'logical form of judgement'. A safe bet is that it is to be understood along the lines just noted from the Wiener transcript. The only new detail in §19 of the B-Deduction is the appeal to the objective unity of pure apperception as that which makes the logical form of judgement possible. The Wiener transcript is silent on this.

I would like to hold off on pure apperception and how it is significant. The thing to see for now is that Kant should take Meier's connecting concept to be the logical form of a certain kind of judgement, namely categorical ones. One way to see this is to turn to Wolff, because he is more expansive than Meier about why the connecting concept is needed and because he can be understood to make precisely the same point about the connecting concept and its significance for affirmative categorical judgements that Kant makes for the logical form of judgement as such: according to Wolff, it (the connecting concept) unites two concepts into a single cognition that is objectively valid, i.e., purports to be about an object and thereby has a truth value that is the same for everybody.

Wolff expounds on judgement from §286 to §290 in Vernünftige Gedancken von Gott, der Welt, der Seele des Menschen, und allen Dingen überhaupt. The thought of interest to us developed in this stretch of text depends on the same conception of judgement taken up by Meier. Wolff says in §287 that judgement is an operation of the understanding that involves recognizing and distinguishing the species and kinds of things, and also their properties, alterations, effects, and relations with one another. When we explicitly recognize and distinguish kinds, species, properties, and so forth, we are forming judgements. But Wolff says in §288 that forming a judgement requires, in addition, that we distinguish between the thing itself and whatever we take it to have. Thus forming a judgement is not merely the representation of a thing and whatever we take it to have. It is a representation of the thing and whatever we take it to have as distinct from each other—and yet 'joined' (verknüpft). Wolff puts it this way: 'Thus judgement has to do with the representation of the joining [Verknüpfung] of two things with each other.' For example, we distinguish the colour red and the rose of this same colour, but we represent the rose and its colour as joined nevertheless when we judge that the rose is red. When we deny that this is so, if the rose is some other colour, we represent the colour red and the rose as separated from each other—as when we judge that the rose is not red. This is by and large the account of categorical judgements we have already seen in Meier, which is no coincidence, since Meier took it over from Wolff.

Wolff introduces what Meier will call the 'connecting concept' in §290 of Vernünftige Gedancken (note that Wolff does not use the same appellation as Meier to refer to it: instead of calling it the 'connecting concept', he refers to it more clumsily as 'the representation of the joining of two things'). However, he gives the rationale for it in §289. This is what he says:

> If I represent to myself a red-hot iron [ein glüendes Eisen], then I have indeed a concept of the iron as well as of red hot, and I represent to myself the two with each other. But when this happens, I do not yet make any judgement about the iron; rather I have a mere concept of the red-hot iron. So if somebody asks me what I am now thinking [denke], I will answer: I see [sehe] red-hot iron. By contrast, as soon as I represent to myself red hot as something distinct from, and distinctive of [als etwas besonderes von dem Eisen], the iron and consider that it is now joined with the iron, then I judge that the iron is red hot. Consequently, if somebody then asks me what I am thinking, I will answer: I think that the iron is red hot. Thus people indicate the difference quite plainly even in ordinary speech, namely when they are making a judgement and when they have a mere concept of something. We also see from the foregoing example that, by the judgement about the red-hot iron, we mean the representation of the joining of red hot with iron.

The first thing to say about this passage is that it clearly addresses the problem we discussed in §2b: how to distinguish between what I called 'free-floating' concepts and judgements. As I indicated, we can state the problem in grammatical terms as being concerned with the difference between the attributive use of 'red hot' and the predicative use of the expression. Only when we use 'red hot' predicatively, do we get a judgement. If we ask Wolff what lies at the basis of the predicative use of 'red hot', he will reply that it involves the representation of the joining of two things we take to be distinguishable from one another: the iron and its property of being red hot. That will mean deploying what Meier calls the 'connecting concept'. But when we use 'red hot' attributively, we get what Wolff calls a 'mere concept' that does not count as a judgement at all. For though we thereby represent the iron and red hot together, we do not represent them as distinct from each other and yet as joined. As Meier would put it, we have not deployed the connecting concept.

So far this indicates only that the passage I just quoted from §290 of Wolff's Vernünftige Gedancken confirms my hunch at the end of §2b that Meier introduces the connecting concept (following Wolff's example, as we now see) to explain the difference between judgements and free-floating concepts. But having seen this, we learn in addition that, for Wolff, judgements are distinctive in a certain way. We might express the idea on Wolff's behalf as follows. Judgements exhibit a kind of unity that we do not find in 'mere concepts', namely that which supervenes on two concepts when we represent them as distinct from one another and yet as 'joined'—presumably as subject and predicate, since our focus is on affirmative categorical judgements. Wolff might have said that this sort of unity is peculiar to complete thoughts. We do not have to coax from a speaker who has judged that the iron is red hot what she thinks of the iron. A speaker who does not make a judgement, but gives voice to a 'mere concept' like 'red-hot iron' leaves his interlocutors perplexed, because he has not shared with them a complete thought about the iron, even though he has qualified it as 'red hot'. But while I think that Wolff would embrace this development of his example, he himself tries to convey what is distinctive about the unity exhibited by a judgement from a different angle.

In the passage I quoted above from §289, he imagines a person who may perhaps be watching a blacksmith heat up some iron in his forge. The imagined person is entertaining the 'mere concept' of red-hot iron, prompted no doubt by the scene before him, and an interlocutor invites him to say what he is thinking. The person could have replied by simply saying 'red-hot iron'. But Wolff has him reply . . . in a complete sentence: 'I see red-hot iron.' This is a bit disconcerting, precisely because it is just as much a complete thought as the judgement Wolff contrasts with it by the end of the passage: the interlocutor is in doubt now about what the person is seeing no more than he will be in doubt a moment later about what the person thinks of the iron when he shares his judgement that it is red hot. But the key here is to track the use of 'red hot' in the two expressions. It is used attributively in the remark, 'I see red-hot iron'. It is used predicatively in the judgement, 'I think that the iron is red hot.' Wolff must be thinking that the predicative use of 'red hot' in the judgement produces a special kind of unity in the relevant concepts that has the effect of explicitly and deliberately deflecting the imagined person's thought away from himself and towards the iron in the blacksmith's forge. The person's thought is not just complete. It is so in such a way that it asserts something about something. This means it will be either true or false. If it is true, it is true for all of us. By contrast, the attributive use of 'red hot' in the first thought that the person shares with his interlocutor may well yield a complete thought (if it is introduced by an 'I see'). But this thought will not be deflected away from him and towards anything other than himself. Instead it will be a self-report: in this case, the person tells us about his visual experience and its content. We could characterize the self-report as a judgement. But it cannot count as a judgement in the meaty sense laid out for us at the end of the passage, because the content of the self-report, as experienced by the person who has it, will hold for this person and nobody else. It must be granted that, as a report, this person's self report has a truth value that will be the same for all of us: even if he is delirious, it is true that the person reporting on himself sees red-hot iron. But his seeing or conceiving red-hot iron is his own special subjective state. The subjective state of his interlocutor will be something else again. Thus it seems that Wolff has introduced in this passage something like Kant's difference between the subjective unity and the objective unity of consciousness (indeed in a way also suggestive of Kant's distinction in the Prolegomena between judgements of perception and judgements of experience).[^42] If Wolff had been willing to adopt Kant's language, he would have said that we get objective unity among our concepts when we form a judgement out of them by representing them as 'joined' such that the one concept is used predicatively with respect to the other. Meier would have put it this way: objective unity arises when we have deployed the connecting concept. But we get subjective unity when the connecting concept has not been deployed and the one concept has been used attributively with respect to the other.

It is a short step from here to the conclusion that Wolff's 'representation of the joining of two concepts' and Meier's 'connecting concept' must be purely formal in Kant's sense. From the Wiener transcript of the logic lectures we learn that Kant took the matter of a (categorical) judgement to be its constitutive concepts. There are faithful echoes of this idea in other student transcripts and in the Jäsche handbook.[^43] By adding concepts to concepts, we get new marks and hence new information. Consider the concepts Rose and Red. They could be the matter of a judgement: the rose is red. We could happily add the marks Prickly and Sweet-Smelling to the list, which would contribute to the matter of an expanded, conjunctive judgement: the rose is red, prickly and sweet-smelling.[^44] By contrast, the connecting concept, as understood by both Meier and Wolff, is not some further concept that can be added to a list like this, because it cannot ever be part of the matter of a judgement. If I add it to Iron and Red Hot, I do not get any marks or concepts that I did not have before. The result is just a reordering of the concepts I already had so as to make the one the subject and the other the predicate of a judgement. As we have seen, the connecting concept makes the difference between saying 'red-hot iron' and judging that the iron is red hot. But it cannot make this difference if it is just one more mark among many like Hoppy, Nocturnal, Speckled, and so forth. Since the connecting concept cannot conceivably figure in the matter of any judgement, it must constitute the form of a certain kind of judgement, namely affirmative categorical ones.

Thus it seems plausible to think that Kant must have been willing to take the connecting concept on board, on a certain condition, namely that it be treated as a form. If that is right, then he was showing both insight and originality. Insight, because, as I have argued, a case can be made that the connecting concept is needed, namely to distinguish free-floating concepts from judgements and because it is right, on reflection, to point out that the connecting concept is not one more mark among many. But the insight is original, because Meier and Wolff may well be confused about the connecting concept, and it seems that the best hope of rescuing it for them is to treat it in the way I have just suggested on Kant's behalf. Both Meier and Wolff can be taken to conflate two quite different claims about the connecting concept. Sometimes they seem to say that the connecting concept represents the relation of any predicate in any affirmative categorical judgement to the subject (whatever it may be). Sometimes they seem to say that it also represents the specific concepts that stand in this relation in some given affirmative categorical judgement. Again, suppose we judge that the iron is red hot. Given that we have formed a judgement out of Iron and Red Hot, we have deployed not only these two concepts, but also the connecting concept. Does the connecting concept represent Iron and Red Hot inasmuch as the latter is the predicate of the former in our example judgement? If so, there are as many different connecting concepts as there are judgements: one whereby we conceive of the relation between Red Hot and Iron when we judge that the iron is red hot; another whereby we conceive of the relation between Red and Rose when we judge that the rose is red, and so on. In fact, every judgement will turn out just to be a connecting concept whereby we represent a given concept like Red Hot as belonging predicatively to another concept like Iron. The reason for thinking that this must be Meier's view is just that Meier's characterization of judgement as such in §325 of the Vernunftlehre and his characterization of the connecting concept in §326 are almost precisely the same.[^45] The same sort of fudging may perhaps be found in §289 and §290 of Wolff's Vernünftige Gedancken. But Meier and Wolff would likely, and should certainly, reject this construal of the position, because it obliterates the distinction between a judgement and the connecting concept: unless this distinction remains sharply in view, it will make no sense to appeal to the connecting concept to explain how judgements are possible. But if Meier and Wolff resist the problematic construal of their view, they must say that what the connecting concept represents is just the relation of any subject and predicate in any affirmative categorical judgement. The question is how they can say this without taking on something like Kant's distinction between the form and the matter of a judgement?

Thus, without exercising any violence at all, we find that, when confronted by Kant's notion of logical form, the connecting concept easily transforms itself into Kant's logical form of categorical judgements. This suggests that Kant did not dismiss the connecting concept, but rather modified it to fall into line with his understanding of the logical form of judgement.

#### §2d Forms of judgement and functions of judgement

The result of the preceding section raises a question about the Kantian category of substance. It might be thought that a question on this topic should be postponed, because our immediate concern is to make sense of §19 of the B-Deduction and the categories are not mentioned until §20.[^46] But the question is more urgent than it might seem, because a careful answer will shed some more light on what Kant means by 'logical form of judgement' and help clarify in the next section the relation, for Kant, between the 'logical form of judgement' and the objective unity of apperception—a central issue that my reading of §19 must still address.

There are two angles from which to state the question. The first concerns the way that Kant understood the relation between his own category of substance and what Meier calls the 'connecting concept'. Might Kant have thought that the connecting concept is best understood as the category of substance? There are two related reasons for thinking that indeed he might. On the one hand, we know that Kant associated the logical form of categorical judgements with the category of substance, and we have just seen that, unless it is treated as the logical form of categorical judgements, the connecting concept may be unsalvageable. For another thing, once we take the connecting concept to be the logical form of categorical judgements, it is striking how well Kant's characterization of the category of substance applies to it. The second angle from which to ask our question concerns the relation between the logical form of categorical judgements and the category of substance itself. Are they one and the same thing? One would expect the answer to the question so stated to be affirmative if the answer to the question stated in terms of the connecting concept is affirmative. For the category of substance will be nothing more than the logical form of categorical judgements if indeed it offers the best hope of making sense of the connecting concept. But if the answer should be negative, then that will give us a window into what Kant thought was ultimately missing from Meier and Wolff and what was needed to make the logical theory of judgement fly.

Kant's comments about the categories in general are meagre and few. This is frustrating. More frustrating still is that his comments are scattered all over the place. For our purposes, it may be helpful to consider the remark he makes at B128 in the section whose title is 'Transition to the Transcendental Deduction of the Categories'. Let it be noted in passing that this remark is an addition of the B-edition. It reads, in part, as follows:

> Categories are concepts of an object as such whereby its[^47] intuition is regarded as determined with respect to one of the logical functions of judging. Thus the function of the categorical judgement was that of the relation of subject and predicate, e.g., all bodies are divisible. But, as far as the merely logical use of the understanding is concerned, it remained undetermined to which of the two concepts one wanted to give the function of the subject, and to which that of the predicate. For one can also say: some divisible things are bodies. But by means of the category of substance, when I bring the concept of a body under it, it is determined that its empirical intuition must always be considered in experience only as subject and never as mere predicate, and likewise for the other categories. (B128)

Neither the word 'form', nor the expression 'form of judgement' is found in this passage. But some kind of formal consideration must be in play. For Kant says that the categories are not concepts of any object in particular, like the concept Body or the concept Divisible.[^48] Rather they are all concepts of 'an object as such'. There are twelve of them altogether. There would only be one, if we could not distinguish them from one another somehow. But how can they be distinguished from one another, if they are all concepts of an 'object as such'? If not by a special object they each represent, it must be by some kind of formal consideration. Consider the category of substance. It too is the concept of 'an object as such'. By implication, it does not represent substance in the same way that the concept Dog represents dogs. The latter concept represents dogs by its marks and associated predicates which guide our thinking about a certain class of small carnivores domesticated by humans for hunting, herding, and companionship. Those marks and associated predicates give us the criteria an object must meet for it to count as a dog in our judgement. But if the category of substance is just the concept of an object as such, we should not expect it to give us any such criteria for determining which objects count as substances. To be sure, philosophers have sometimes pronounced judgements that make it seem as if it did, as when they say: substance is permanent. The surface grammar of this judgement is the same as the judgement that dogs are small carnivores. But the latter judgement indicates explicitly that Small Carnivore is a mark we are predicating of Dog, such that we will not count anything as a dog unless, at a minimum, it turns out to be a small carnivore. This is not the case with the philosophers' judgement about substance. Kant says that it is a tautology. 'Permanent' is just what we mean by 'substance'. We might just as well say that a substance is a substance (A185/B228).[^49] Since the category of substance is not the concept of substance in the same way that the concept Dog is a representation of dogs, we cannot expect it to figure in what Kant calls the matter of a judgement,[^50] as plainly the concept Dog can. Rather we infer, and Kant says in so many words at B128, that it determines the form of categorical judgements, i.e., it relates two other concepts, like—for example—Dog and Small Carnivore, as subject and predicate. Having said this much, it will seem natural to conclude that the category of substance is Kant's answer to Meier's connecting concept.

But it is not as simple as that, and it is a good deal more interesting too. To see this, though, we must address an unavoidable, but—I think—neglected problem of terminology. What does Kant mean by 'function'? He uses the word in the passage I quoted from B128. He applies it quite generally and consistently to the categories when he characterizes them, as he does here, as 'functions of judgement'. One could think that 'function' is just synonymous with 'form'. As we saw, many of the student transcripts report Kant as saying that the relation of subject and predicate is the form of a categorical judgement. Here at B128, he says that it is the function of a categorical judgement. Confirmation of the equivalence in meaning of 'form' and 'function' of judgement can apparently be found at A70/B95 at the beginning of §9—as numbered in the B-edition—which opens the discussion of the so-called 'guiding thread of the discovery of all pure concepts of the understanding' (Leitfaden). For there Kant writes: 'If we abstract from all content of a judgement as such and consider only the mere form of understanding, we find that the function of thinking in the same can be brought under four titles' (A70/B95). If we consider the 'mere form of understanding' and the 'function of thinking' turns up, surely form and function are the same thing. But, again, it is not as simple as that.

Since 'function' is an expression that Kant often uses in connection with the logical use of the categories, we would do well to consider what he means by it. This is tricky, because Kant himself claims in a passage found only in the A-version of the section on 'Phenomena and Noumena' that no general definition of the expression can be given without circularity, 'because the definition would indeed itself have to be a judgement and would therefore have to already contain these functions' (A244). He may well be exaggerating here, since the circle, if there were one, need not be vicious. But even if we cannot expect to find an explicit definition of the expression, we may yet uncover an implicit one—from the way Kant puts it to use. To start with, however, it will be helpful to step away from Kant and cast our nets beyond him. The Grimm Brothers' dictionary indicates that the German 'Funktion' comes from the Latin 'functio'. This should come as no surprise. But what does the Latin mean? 'Functio' is the noun derived from the deponent verb 'fungor', which means to carry out, discharge, fulfill, perform or execute: for example, a task or duty. It can mean 'observe', in the sense of observing customs, i.e., actually carrying them out; and fungor vitâ is a euphemism for dying, i.e., actually carrying out the business of living your life through to the end. Consequently, the noun 'functio' does not so much designate the task or duty a person performs, as rather the act of getting whatever it is done. Cicero uses the word in this sense in the Tusculan Disputations: 'Labor est functio quaedam vel animi vel corporis gravoris operis et muneris . . . ' (II.35).[^51] He is offering a definition or characterization of 'work' that we might translate into English as follows: 'Work is a certain carrying out [functio], on the part of either the mind or the body, of a more onerous task [opus] or duty [munus].' So if I were assigned the task of washing the dishes, my task would be to wash the dishes, but my 'functio' would be the actual housework I would have to do in order to complete the task. It would be odd, from the Latin point of view, to speak as we do in contemporary English when we say things like the following: 'Canadian postal workers conscientiously carry out their functions.' For the Latinist will take this to mean that Canadian postal workers conscientiously carry out their carrying out, which is just weird. Judging from the representative passages cited by the Grimm Brothers', the German 'Funktion' was largely parasitic in meaning on the Latin up to the end of the nineteenth century (the fourth volume of their dictionary—the one with this entry—was published in 1878). A German synonym is 'Verrichtung', which itself means a carrying out or executing of something. Some of the passages selected by the Grimm Brothers' for their entry use 'Verrichtung' and 'Funktion' interchangeably.

There are places in the Critique where Kant seems to use the German 'Funktion' in the sense of the Latin 'functio'. No passage documents this better than the notorious paragraph in §10—as numbered in the B-edition—that is thought to enunciate the principle of the 'guiding thread for the discovery of all the pure concepts of the understanding'. The passage—as much of it as we need—reads as follows: 'The same function [Funktion] that gives unity to the different representations in a judgement also gives unity to the mere synthesis of different representations in an intuition, which synthesis, when expressed universally, is called pure concept of the understanding' (A79/B104). The word 'function' here clearly means a certain doing, or carrying out, or executing. It is work carried out by the understanding. If we ask what kind of work it is, we will find ourselves characterizing it in terms of its aim or outcome. The outcome is twofold; in both cases it is unity. The first case of unity is the one of special interest to us, because it involves the logical use of the understanding, and we easily recognize it, in light of everything we saw in the previous section, as the form of a judgement. For Kant characterizes it as 'the unity of the different representations in a judgement'. The 'different representations' at issue here are surely the matter of a judgement: the different cognitions or given concepts out of which the judgement is made. Not every kind of unity will produce a judgement out of our representations. But surely the unity mentioned at A79/B104 does, just because Kant has explicitly used the word 'judgement' in connection with it. That is enough to indicate that the form of a judgement as such is the outcome of the work carried out by the understanding. The task of the understanding is to bring about this form, and its function—according to our passage at A79/B104—is just whatever it does in order to get the job done.[^52]

Other passages seem to bear out this construal of the word 'function'.[^53] Consequently, we find that there is a certain relation, according to Kant, between the logical form of judgement and the logical function of judgement. The logical function of judgement is just that work that brings about the logical form of a judgement.[^54] This makes good sense of the opening paragraph I quoted earlier from §9 at A70/B95. As should be clear now, Kant's words in that passage do not indicate that 'form' and 'function' are equivalent in meaning, as it initially seemed. On the contrary, they indicate just the relation I have suggested. For what Kant is saying there boils down to this: we know all the forms of judgement from studying logic, but if we investigate these forms in the right way we will discover the functions that bring them about. These functions are the categories.

The categories are thus the housework of the understanding. §20 of the B-Deduction confirms this, because we find the following two points clearly enunciated there: first that a logical function of judgement is a certain doing or a certain carrying out, and second that this doing or carrying out is a category. In Kant's own words, the first point is stated as follows: 'That operation of the understanding through which the manifold of given representations is brought under an apperception as such is the logical function of judgement' (B144). Notice the deliberate characterization of the logical function of judgement as an 'operation' (Handlung). 'Operation' is another word that conveys the idea of a carrying out, a bringing about or a discharging of something.[^55] There is no explicit mention of unity being the outcome of this operation or function in the passage I just quoted from B144. But it is surely implied by the elliptical mention of bringing the manifold 'under an apperception as such'. Then Kant states the second point as follows: 'The categories are nothing other than these functions of judging' (B144). They are not themselves forms of judgement, but rather the functions of judging that bestow logical form on the manifold. Indeed, precisely this idea is invoked as the principle of the 'guiding thread' for the derivation of the table of categories from the table of judgements (A70/B95, A79/B104).

We must consider one last, simple detail to complete the picture: it remains to characterize more precisely the task or duty discharged by the understanding through the categories or logical functions of judgement. The passages we have just considered indicate what the outcome is. As I suggested earlier, it is natural to cite the outcome to characterize the associated task. If I exhaust myself in drudgery for an hour and the outcome is snow-free steps leading to the entrance of my walk-up apartment, it will be reasonable to say that the task I undertook and completed was that of shovelling the snow. In the case at hand, the outcome is the logical form of judgement. But that does not give us the most perspicuous characterization of the relevant task, because the outcome itself has not yet been characterized completely. There is something much simpler and more useful we can say: the task is just to form a judgement, e.g., that bodies are divisible or that dogs are small carnivores. This is the task the understanding undertakes when its logical functions of judgement bestow on a given manifold the logical form of judgement.

The point is both terminological and—as we will discover—philosophical. But first the terminology. When we use the English verbal noun 'carrying out' or 'discharging', we feel the need to clarify what is being discharged or carried out. We provide the clarification by specifying the relevant task or duty. Cicero feels the same need when he uses the word 'functio' in Latin, which is why the word is usually followed by a word for 'task' or 'duty' in the genitive: functio operis or functio muneris. Kant's German follows the same pattern. In the Critique, 'Funktion' is usually followed by a word for judgement or judging, either in the genitive or in a prepositional phrase: e.g., 'logische Funktion der Urteile' (B144), 'logische Funktion zu urteilen' (B144), 'logische Funktion in Urteilen' (A70/B95). The categories are logical functions of judgement. Kant never says that they are logical functions of forms of judgement. I do not think that his reason for avoiding this admittedly tortuous circumlocution is stylistic. I think he deliberately uses the shorter expression, just because it is more natural, more concrete, and more perspicuous. The task is not to get us from a manifold to a judgement as such or to the necessary conditions of judging in the first place! The task is to get us to a specific judgement—about bodies, dogs, roses, or red-hot iron.

The point is not merely terminological; it is also philosophical. It concerns a problem associated with the distinction between matter and form in logic. There are many problems associated with this distinction. An obvious one is why introduce it at all; another is how to draw it precisely (both of which I tried to address in the previous section). But assuming we have a reason to embrace it and can say what counts as matter and what counts as form, there is a further problem, namely to say something enlightening about the composite of matter and form. That will take us a step beyond the distinction itself. The matter must be of the appropriate kind, otherwise it will not admit the form. But it cannot determine all by itself how the form will be impressed on it. Nor can the form determine this either. It takes some kind of operation for this to happen. If we are going to say anything at all about the composite of matter and form, this operation—whatever it is—must be accounted for. Suppose we stand before a bronze statue of a rider on a horse. We know something about the statue when we find out that it is bronze. We know something else about it by observing that it is in the shape of a horse and rider. But it remains to understand how that bronze was crafted into that shape. That will involve understanding a certain operation or function, namely how the art of sculpting goes to work on bronze. In the case at hand, the composite of matter and form is a judgement, like Kant's favourite example: bodies are divisible. We know that the matter of this judgement is supposed to be the constitutive concepts: Body and Divisible. We know too that the form of this affirmative categorical judgement is the relation of subject and predicate. But we do not yet understand how Kant's favourite example judgement comes to be out of its matter and form. Neither the matter nor the form all by itself is enough to explain the composite—an important point that Kant demonstrates in the part of the passage from B128, quoted earlier, that I have not yet discussed.

This is where Kant says:

> The function of the categorical judgement was that of the relation of subject to predicate, e.g., all bodies are divisible. But, as far as the merely logical use of the understanding is concerned, it remained undetermined to which of the two concepts one wanted to give the function of the subject, and to which that of the predicate. For one can also say: some divisible things are bodies. (B128)

Kant distinguishes in the first sentence of this passage between the function of the categorical judgement and the relation of subject to predicate in a categorical judgement, i.e., its form.[^56] I take him to associate this form with the 'merely logical use of the understanding'; by the end of the passage, it is clear that he associates the function with the category of substance. Now suppose we are given two concepts, one of greater extent (Umfang) than the other: e.g., Body and Divisible. Then, as far as the 'merely logical use of the understanding' is concerned, we have two possibilities and no way of deciding between them: either Body can carry out the role of subject and Divisible that of predicate, or the other way around. That is because the two possibilities are 'convertible' (provided we use the right quantifiers). I can say either that (all) bodies are divisible or that (some) divisible things are bodies. Aristotle used this kind of conversion to demonstrate the validity of syllogisms in the second and third figures. Kant uses it at B128 to argue that the form of a categorical judgement does not determine, all by itself, how it will be impressed on two concepts that might serve as the matter of such a judgement. Of course, a person, i.e., an individual judge, will have to decide which way to go. If I am the judge, I will elect Body as the subject if I have a special (non-logical) reason to do so. One reason might be the focus of a discussion I am having with somebody else: perhaps the two of us are less interested in the question which of the many different kinds of things may count as divisible (bodies, lines, plane surfaces, poems, symphonies, etc.) than we are in the question what all bodies have in common. Still my decision as such to elect Body as subject will not get me an affirmative categorical judgement about bodies and what they all have in common any more than the mere decision to cast a statue in bronze will get me a bronze statue. Something must yet be done to impress form on matter. In the case of the statue, I will have to get to work casting bronze in the appropriate way, or I will have to commission a sculptor to do the work for me. The case of my judgement that bodies are divisible will require the function of categorical judgements, i.e., the category of substance.

As Kant puts it in the continuation of our passage, 'But through the category of substance, when I bring the concept Body under it, it will be determined that its empirical intuition must always be regarded in experience as subject, never as mere predicate.' I must bring the concept Body under the category of substance, if I am to form a judgement whose subject is Body. But what does this mean? It cannot mean that I must implicitly make the judgement that bodies are substances in order to make the judgement that bodies are divisible. Otherwise, I would contradict myself if I should ever 'convert' my judgement and make Divisible the subject. For the converted judgement would, by parity of reasoning, rest on the implicit judgement that the divisible must always be regarded as subject and never as mere predicate. The converted judgement—or rather its implicit background judgement—would thus imply that bodies are indeed mere predicates or accidents of a substance and hence that they are not substances in their own right. This would imply, in turn, that the Aristotelian conversion rule, invoked in our passage at B128, must be rejected: you could not use it without contradicting yourself. We know from our passage at B128 that Kant accepted the conversion rule. This goes to show, once more, that the category of substance is not itself a constitutive element in the matter of any judgement—not even in these implicit, background judgements that we have just been considering. It is something else. There is no better way to characterize it than by using Kant's own words: it is the function of categorical judgements, i.e., it is the implementation, by the understanding, of my decision to make the concept Body the subject of a certain judgement and not the concept Divisible. By bringing the concept Body under the category of substance, I am not making any background judgement, as I have just argued; rather, I get the category of substance to carry out the function of impressing the form of categorical judgements on my two concepts by making Body the subject and Divisible the predicate. I do this in something like the way I would get the art of sculpting to cast a statue of a horse and rider in bronze: the category is, as it were, the art of judging, just as sculpting is the art of casting my bronze statue.

Here is the upshot of this subsection. It started with a twofold question. Is the category of substance Kant's answer to what Meier calls the 'connecting concept'? Is the category of substance the logical form of categorical judgements? The answer is a twofold 'no' that tells us something interesting about Kant's relation to Meier, Wolff, and the other logicians and his conception of what underlies the logical theory of judgement. As we saw in the previous section, Kant must have thought that Meier and friends had good reason to introduce the 'connecting concept' in order to account for affirmative categorical judgements, but also that it had to be treated as the logical form of such judgements—otherwise it would not be workable. But he must also have thought that the idea of a logical form of judgement was deficient on its own: it had to be supplemented, namely by the idea of a logical function, without which our account of judgement would be incomplete. The Kantian doctrine of the categories is thus intended to make good on the promise of the logicians' theory of judgement. Let me just add that I have encountered neither the term 'function' (either in German or in Latin) nor the corresponding notion in the writings of the logicians I have read on this topic.

#### §2e Judgement, categories, and apperception

We now have a fuller understanding of how Kant accounts for affirmative categorical judgements. To complete the picture, we would have to consider what he would say about the other forms of judgement. But our focus all along has been §19 of the B-Deduction. We have been following Kant's lead there by treating affirmative categorical judgements as a representative case. We hope to infer from this case what applies to them all. The question that is left for us to consider is whether the things we have learned about judgement—in light of our representative case—can bring to life the argument of §19 by helping us understand its fundamental claim: judgement is always an expression of the objective unity of pure apperception.

The simple and useful thing to observe first of all is that judgement is a species of synthesis. A certain manifold is combined in an act of self-activity on the part of a judge who—in the nature of the case—possesses understanding. The manifold comprises a bunch of concepts. The result of their synthesis is that the judge grasps them together in a certain way: they exhibit unity—their logical form—and may therefore count as a single cognition (with a truth value), i.e., a judgement. It might seem odd to speak of a 'manifold of concepts', because Kant so often speaks of 'the manifold of an intuition' and because he denies that this manifold is generated by the understanding (as concepts are). But no combination or synthesis can take place unless something manifold is on offer. Even if Kant wishes to reserve the word 'manifold' for that which is given to us in intuition, he acknowledges (in passing) in §15 that there can be combination and synthesis not only of 'the manifold of intuition' (das Mannigfaltige der Anschauung), but also of 'a number of concepts' (mancherlei Begriffe) (B130). He acknowledges the same thing in §20.[^57] The only reason not to refer to a 'number of concepts' as a manifold is caution: lest it encourage us to think that the line between sensibility and the understanding can be blurred. But once we have delivered ourselves from this temptation, it should be clear that the matter of a judgement is just a manifold of concepts (it will always take more than one) and that a judgement is what we get when that matter has been combined, i.e., when it has been worked over and then subjected to the relevant category from which it receives the appropriate logical form. This observation can serve as another example 'in concreto' of synthesis—like those we considered in §2b of Chapter Two.

In Chapter Two, I characterized synthesis as an activity that involves construing the manifold. The case of empirical pattern recognition was one of the examples I took from Kant himself: when a person observes cloud formations, she apprehends a manifold of whiter and greyer patches against a bluish background as they coalesce and come apart. The person does not just passively take in the scene: she actively perceives different parts of it as grouped together in a certain way; and she probably considers a number of different groupings. This active grouping is a way of unifying a certain manifold. By unifying it first one way and then another, the person may be struck by the resemblance of the cloud formation to human figures or different animals. Maybe she will report seeing first a duck and then a rabbit. This is an experience familiar to any of us who have day dreamed. It seems right to characterize it as the act of construing a manifold; it counts as a kind of synthesis.

Now Kant recognizes that there are different kinds of synthesis. One important factor that will distinguish them is the manifold itself, because it is reasonable to think that different kinds of manifolds cannot be synthesized in the same way. A 'number of concepts' cannot be synthesized in the same way as a manifold of whiter and greyer patches against a bluish background. Moreover, the outcome will be different. In the case we now want to consider, the synthesis will yield no images: no one will ever report seeing a duck or a rabbit in the logical hierarchy of higher and lower concepts. It will yield a judgement with a logical structure that images do not have. But the two kinds of synthesis have something in common. Even in the case of judgement, synthesis will involve grouping parts of the manifold with each other in different ways, i.e., it will group concepts and thereby construe them as matter for different judgements—just as a day-dreamer might group the same parts of a cloud formation in different ways and then construe them as the matter for different images.

The categories will play a certain role in the kind of synthesis we are now considering. They will produce the judgements corresponding to the different groupings of concepts by giving them the relevant logical structure. But they themselves are fundamentally neutral with respect to how a given clutch of concepts should be grouped, precisely because more than one grouping may be possible for a given manifold under a given category. As a result, the groupings as such will have to precede the work of the categories. Here is an example that will direct us ultimately to the synthetic unity of pure apperception and its role in all this.

In the passage at B128 we considered in the previous section, Kant took the simplest possible case relevant for our purposes, namely that in which we have two concepts: Body and Divisible. The significance of the Aristotelian conversion rule Kant alludes to in that passage is to show that some kind of grouping of the two concepts has to have been brought about so that a judgement can be made: either concept could be the subject of a judgement; either could be the predicate. But we saw that the category of substance does not itself determine which grouping we will go for. After all, it does not give us marks and criteria by which to recognize what is truly substance and to distinguish it from what is not truly substance. To be sure, Kant says that 'through the category of substance, when I bring the concept Body under it, it will be determined that its empirical intuition must always be regarded in experience as subject, never as mere predicate' (B128). But the Aristotelian conversion rule gives me just as much right to bring the concept Divisible under the category of substance—so long as I get the quantifiers right. If I do so, it will be determined in that case that the empirical intuition of the concept Divisible 'must always be regarded in experience as subject and never as mere predicate'. In short, the category under consideration gives no object of cognition any special claim to be regarded as a substance, and thus it gives no concept any special claim to be treated as the subject of judgements we make in whose matter it is included. As a result, which way we group Body and Divisible is entirely up to us. All the category can do is codify our preferred grouping by officially making Body the subject of our judgement, if our wish is to elect Body to this position rather than Divisible. The passage at B128 places emphasis on the work done by the category, which is just what we should expect, since Kant opens the passage by announcing that he is going to offer us an explanation of the categories. But as a result, the priority of grouping or construing concepts over judgement proper retreats into the background. Reflecting, as we just did (and in the previous section), on the significance of the Aristotelian conversion rule in play at B128 may be enough to remedy things.

But it may not quite do the trick, because the case under consideration at B128 is so simple. For perhaps one will object that deciding which of our two concepts—Body or Divisible—will be subject and which will be predicate is already to judge that bodies are divisible and hence that the act of judging and what I call 'grouping concepts' are one and the same thing. They are, nevertheless, distinct, assuming it makes sense to distinguish between the matter and form of a judgement at all. The act of judging consists in deploying the category and thereby impressing a certain form on a certain matter. Grouping concepts is a preliminary assessment of the matter to see how many different ways it might receive the relevant logical form and which of these different ways of receiving it we prefer given our interest at the moment. Concept grouping has to happen before the category can come into play. By implication, the synthesis that is in fact our judgement that bodies are divisible is itself built on a prior synthesis—a 'proto-judgement', as I like to think of it. One might now object that the compounding of synthesis by synthesis is a sure sign that either Kant's account or my account of his account is flawed. But I cannot find a better way of reading what Kant says, and I believe that what Kant says is reasonable on its own terms, i.e., given his logical hylomorphism. There are different degrees of actuality: a sculptor who has been commissioned to cast in bronze a statue of a horse and rider must evaluate the bronze available and take certain preliminary steps before the commission can be fully carried out: that may well involve making a mock-up. It may be easier to see how this consideration applies to Kant's theory of judgement if we consider cases more complicated than the one Kant himself considers at B128.

Suppose, then, that we are presented with a number of concepts greater than two. Suppose that our problem is to make a single affirmative categorical judgement out of this manifold. The form of our judgement will ultimately be the relation of subject to predicate. We cannot begin to solve our problem until we have determined which of the concepts will be the subject and which will be the predicate. But now suppose that we have figured that one out. That will leave n–2 concepts to deal with. What do we do with them? The answer is that we will have to sort them into as many as two different classes: some of them (if not all) we will use attributively as modifiers of the subject concept and some of them (all of them if we have used none for the subject) we will use attributively as modifiers of the predicate concept.[^58] How this is to be done will depend on what matters to us (and nothing else). Here is a simple case of the problem.

Suppose we have five concepts to work with: Speckled, Nocturnal, Vocal, Endangered, Amphibian. Suppose we elect the concept Endangered as subject and the concept Vocal as predicate. Then we must figure out to which of these two concepts we wish to assign the other three concepts as modifiers. Maybe the assignments will work out so that the judgement is this: Some amphibian endangered things are speckled, nocturnal, vocal things. But other assignments would be possible too. Another one would yield the judgement: Some nocturnal endangered things are speckled, amphibian, vocal things. The different assignments are obviously constrained by the logical form of categorical judgements. They cannot actually take the form of such judgements unless one of the given concepts has been brought under the category of substance. But unless the assignment of modifying concepts has also been made, we will just have a bunch of free-floating concepts and not a judgement, or perhaps we will have the judgement: some endangered things are vocal, and a trio of leftover concepts that float freely with respect to each other and with respect to both subject and predicate. Either way we would have to admit that we had failed to solve our problem, which was to form a single categorical judgement out of all five concepts. But if we do solve the problem, it will be by virtue of having carried out a set of assignments. Each set of assignments may be regarded as a different grouping. Every grouping may count as a synthesis in Kant's sense.

Thus the complex cases show pretty clearly that a judgement is a synthesis that is, in some sense, preceded by another synthesis, i.e., a proto-judgement that groups concepts in certain ways. In the case of categorical judgements, the grouping involves not only a determination of which concept will be subject and which will be predicate, but also which other concepts will be used attributively to modify the subject and/or predicate. The proto-judgement is not itself the judgement, but without it the judgement would not be possible. When we ask how the proto-judgement is possible, we are led back to the synthetic unity of pure apperception. The way to see this is to reconsider the final paragraph of §15 of the B-Deduction, because that is where Kant briefly alludes to the proto-judgement and explicitly asks after its condition of possibility.

He opens the paragraph with a quick analysis of the concept of combination. He says it includes not only the concept of the manifold and its synthesis, but also that of its unity. He also says that the concept of unity has a certain priority. It is not derivable from that of combination, but rather the concept of combination presupposes it: 'Thus the representation of this unity cannot arise from combination; rather it makes the concept of combination possible in the first place by being added to the representation of the manifold' (B131). This is abstract, but it is not hard to understand: unless some kind of unity is added to a manifold, we will just have a manifold, but not a combination of its parts: no image combined out of whiter and greyer patches of cloud; no judgement combined out of a number of concepts. But this simple thought leads Kant to raise a question: whether the concept of unity at issue here is not itself a category—more specifically, the category of unity. In fact, the temptation to associate the sought after concept of unity with the category of unity might just as well extend to all of the categories, since the effect of bringing concepts and cognitions under any one of them will be to add the unity of a certain logical form to them and hence to produce a synthesis that will count as some kind of judgement. But it turns out that none of the categories—not even the category of unity—is the concept of unity we are looking for here. On reflection, however, that is what we should expect. Though the category of unity and indeed all the other categories add unity to concepts and cognitions and thereby combine them synthetically, they themselves are less fundamental than the concept of unity Kant has called to our attention here. Indeed, they seem to presuppose it as the condition of their possibility.

Kant explains as follows:

> For all categories rest on logical functions in judgements, but combination is already in them [sc. in the logical functions in judgements—AL],[^59] and hence unity of given concepts is already thought in them. Thus the category already presupposes combination.[^60] Thus we must seek this unity . . . still higher, namely in that which itself contains the ground of unity of different concepts in judgements and hence that of the possibility of the understanding, even in its logical use. (B131)

The point of interest is that categories, as logical functions in judgements, already presuppose a combination. That could seem like a perverse thing to say. How can a category presuppose combination if it produces combination whenever it produces a judgement? It could sound as if Kant has placed his cart before the horse. But he has not, because the combination presupposed by the category is not the one the category produces: it is prior to that. Which one is it? It is surely the grouping of concepts or proto-judgement that has to be in place before the category can impose on these concepts the logical structure needed to form my judgement proper. The assignment of Amphibian to Endangered (as subject) and that of Nocturnal and Speckled to Vocal (as predicate) has to be in place so that the category of substance can carry out the combination that yields the categorical judgement: Some endangered amphibians are nocturnal, speckled, vocal things. But if that assignment counts as a combination in its own right (and why shouldn't it?), then it has its own unity, which is prior to the unity added to our five concepts by the category of substance when it produces a single categorical judgement out of them. The unity of that combination does not come from the category, since the combination itself is the product of some kind of activity—namely concept assignation—prior to the work of the category. The unity in question and the activity it is associated with must be sought 'higher still', namely 'in that which itself contains the ground of unity of different concepts in judgements and hence that of the possibility of the understanding, even in its logical use'. It is to be sought in the synthetic unity of pure apperception, as Kant goes on to argue in §16.

Thus judgement depends on the synthetic unity of pure apperception, because the synthetic unity of pure apperception makes possible the proto-judgement without which judgement itself would not be possible. Does it follow that this unity is objective? It must, because judgements have to relate to objects. If they did not, they could not claim to be true. This claim will sometimes prove to be without merit. But even a false judgement has to relate to an object. Otherwise, it would not make sense to say that it got the story wrong. Without some kind of relation to some kind of an object, storytelling cannot even get started. We would not have to be Kant to think something like this. But once we start saying with Kant that judgements as such depend on the synthetic unity of pure apperception, we will infer that this unity is objective.

As we saw in Chapter Two, Kant argues in §17 of the B-Deduction that concepts too depend on the synthetic unity of pure apperception and that they too relate to objects by virtue of this. A central theme in this section of the current chapter has been the distinction between concepts and judgements. It might be wondered if the dependence of judgements and concepts alike on the objective unity of apperception erases or blurs the distinction between them. Here I think the answer must be 'no'. For judgements relate to objects by virtue of the way that their form relates concepts and sometimes other judgements to one another. A single, free-floating concept can relate to an object without also relating to another concept in such a way as to yield a judgement. Here it might be worried that such a concept is more naturally associated with the subjective unity of apperception. This worry will assert itself if the passage I discussed above in §2c from §289 in Wolff's Vernünftige Gedancken is fresh in our memory. That is the passage in which Wolff distinguishes between concepts and judgements by imagining a person on a visit to a blacksmith in his forge. The visitor entertains a 'mere concept' of red-hot iron, when he reports what he sees: 'I see red-hot iron'. The visitor forms a judgement when he says: 'I think that the iron is red hot'. The judgement has what Kant would call 'objective validity', because it makes a claim about the iron whose truth value is the same for all of us. But the solution to this problem is surely to recall Kant's point at the beginning of the section on the Leitfaden: concepts are all 'predicates of possible judgements' (A69/B94). Judging is not the only use we make of concepts, as Wolff's example indicates; as we saw in §1 of this chapter, they are also used in the association of ideas. But the passage just quoted from the Leitfaden indicates that the special vocation of concepts is to serve as the matter of judgements. This suggests that, when used in a judgement, concepts somehow fully realize the objective quality of the synthetic unity of pure apperception that constitutes them as such.

### §3 The Concluding Argument of Step One of the B-Deduction

The first step of the B-Deduction is completed in §20 of the B-Deduction. The first premise of the argument was supplied in §17: 'the manifold given in a sensible intuition belongs necessarily under the original synthetic unity of apperception, because through this alone is the unity of intuition possible' (B143). But we have just seen in §19 that the action that, in fact, carries out this job is a logical function of judgement. We now understand what is meant by function in this context: it is just whatever the understanding has to do to put its concepts to work in a way that expresses their relation to an object as a truth value. Kant himself states the premise as follows: 'That action/operation [Handlung] of the understanding through which the manifold of given representations (whether they be intuitions or concepts) is brought under an apperception as such is the logical function of judgement' (B143). But the logical functions of judgement are supposed to be categories. Therefore the categories are just those operations or carryings-on of the understanding that determine the manifold in a given intuition in such a way as to be taken up objectively by the synthetic unity of pure apperception into a judgement.
