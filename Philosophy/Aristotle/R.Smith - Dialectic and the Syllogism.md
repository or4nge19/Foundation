## I. The Problem: Dialectic and the *Prior Analytics*

Aristotle says more than once that the difference between dialectical arguments and demonstrations is a matter of their premises. The premises of dialectical arguments must be *ἔνδοξα*, ‘accepted’ premises believed by an appropriately chosen class¹ of people. However, they need not be true: it is a hallmark of dialectic that dialectical arguments may be developed both for and against the same conclusion (dialectic ‘deduces opposites’, as Aristotle says: *Rhet.* i 1, 1355a33–35). Demonstrations, by contrast, are scientific arguments which explain scientific truths. Their premises must therefore be true, like their conclusions: there is only one fact of the matter about any scientific question, and nothing can serve as an explanation which is not itself a fact. Beyond this, the premises of a demonstration must actually give the cause or explanation of its conclusion, and they must be genuine ‘starting points’ (ἀρχαί) or ‘first’ premises. Underneath these differences, however, demonstrations and dialectical arguments are both συλλογισμοί: discourses in which some conclusion necessarily follows from some premises. The difference between dialectical argument and demonstration thus appears to be a matter of content, not of form. Whether an argument is a demonstration or not will depend on various extralogical considerations: whether its premises are true, whether they are prior to and explanatory of its conclusion, etc. However, whether it is a συλλογισμός depends only on the purely logical question whether its conclusion follows from its premises. In the same way, whether an argument is dialectical depends on the extralogical question whether its premises are *ἔνδοξα*.

Accordingly, we may separate the question ‘Is this argument a συλλογισμός?’ from the questions² ‘Is this argument a demonstration?’ and ‘Is this argument a dialectical συλλογισμός?’ and make it the object of a more general study, the theory of συλλογισμοί. A theory of what follows from what, abstracted from considerations of content, can tolerably well be called a logical theory. Aristotle gives us just such a theory in *Prior Analytics* i 1–22.³ Now, the long tradition of Aristotelian interpretation from antiquity forward universally supposed that this theory (often called ‘the syllogistic’) is the underlying logical theory presupposed by both the *Topics* and the *Posterior Analytics* (which is why those treatises follow the *Prior Analytics* in the ordering of the *Organon*). In the present century, however, attention has been focussed on a troublesome detail. Despite the highly general nature of Aristotle’s definition of συλλογισμός, the range of arguments actually considered in the *Prior Analytics* is relatively narrow. They must consist only of categorical sentences, as they are traditionally called: sentences having one of the four forms ‘Every/No/Not every/Some F is a G’, without any propositional connectives, many-place predicates, or other logical devices as we have come to understand them since Frege and Russell. Aristotle’s account of validity appears to be complete⁴ if we restrict ourselves to this limited language, but it is hardly adequate by our standards as a theory of validity in general since it is not even capable of expressing the propositional calculus. Accordingly, we must ask: did Aristotle think that he was giving an account of validity in general (so that συλλογισμός means ‘valid argument’), or did he intend only to give an account of validity for what he knew to be a

There is no mistaking Aristotle’s view. He says quite clearly in the *Prior Analytics* (i 23, 40b17–22; i 29, 45b36–46a2; ii 23, 68b8–14) that the theory he develops in that work is the theory of inference, giving all the possible valid argument forms for all types of argument whatsoever. In modern terminology, Aristotle believed that every valid argument is, or at any rate can be transformed into, a categorical syllogism.⁵ Since this is a critical point, let me take the time to clarify some matters of terminology and restate it. According to the definition Aristotle gives several times (*Top.* i 1, 100a25–27; *APr.* i 1, 24b18–20; *SE* 1, 164b27–165a2), a συλλογισμός is just an argument in which some premises imply a conclusion. However, Aristotle believes that he has established in the *Prior Analytics* the substantive claim that every such συλλογισμός—i.e., every argument in which the premises imply the conclusion—can be transformed⁶ into an argument consisting entirely of inferences from among the forms he establishes in *APr.* i 4–22. Now, if we call these forms ‘syllogisms’, as is traditional, and also translate συλλογισμός as ‘syllogism’, then we reduce this significant and by no means trivial claim to the vapid ‘Aristotle believed that every syllogism is a syllogism’. To avoid this and other confusions, I prefer not to use the word ‘syllogism’ at all. Instead, following a tradition established by Corcoran, I will use the term ‘deduction’ to translate συλλογισμός. I also need a term to indicate the argumentative forms Aristotle studies in *APr.* i 4–7. He usually calls these ‘arguments in the figures’; accordingly, I shall use the expression ‘figured argument’ in place of ‘syllogism’ in its traditional use, and I shall say ‘theory of the figures’ instead of ‘syllogistic’. Aristotle’s thesis, then, is that every deduction can be reduced to a figured argument, or a series of such arguments.

From the standpoint of modern logic, this claim is false.⁷ That in itself is not enough to show that Aristotle did not believe it, of course. However, it may encourage scholars to search for evidence that he did not, or at least not always.

## II. Developmental Explanations

One explanation is developmental. There are a number of other reasons for thinking the *Topics* to be an early work. It may therefore antedate Aristotle’s discovery of the figures and reflect an earlier state of his understanding of logic. Bocheński (1961, 43–44) articulates such a view, dividing Aristotle’s logical works into a ‘first logic’ represented by the *Topics*, *Sophistical Refutations*, and *Categories*, a ‘second logic’ represented by the theory of figures, and a ‘third logic’ represented by the modal theory of *APr.* i 8–22. On this developmental account, the ‘first’ logic is an unsystematic collection of logical rules with no overall organization or theory, amounting in many ways to a compilation of logical data or observations. The ‘second’ logic, by contrast, is theoretically much more sophisticated and, within its constraints, is a ‘flawless’ system, while the ‘third’ logic was an attempt to extend the second which Aristotle probably never completed. Something approximately like Bocheński’s view, at least including his distinction between an unsystematic but inclusive collection of logical rules in the *Topics* and a systematic but more limited theory in the *Prior Analytics*, has become widely accepted. Thus, the Kneales say that the *Topics* ‘contains a logical theory in solution’ from which the theory of figures subsequently ‘crystallized’ (1962, 33). The critical feature of this picture for our present purposes is that it supposes Aristotle to have taken a narrower view of logical form as his logic developed. Since the *Topics* has no systematic theory, Aristotle is willing to list all manner of valid rules of inference. ## III. Non-Developmental Explanations

To avoid these problems and the intrinsic difficulties attaching to any sort of developmental view, we may instead try to find some way to undermine the troubling presupposition on which these views rest, to wit, that there is a conflict between the logical theories of the *Topics* and the *Analytics*. One way to do this is to propose that the two treatises are simply about different kinds of arguments and thus give different theories for them. Dialectical arguments are supposed to concern what seems to be the case, or what is believed to be the case, whereas demonstrations are about what actually is (and even what must be) the case; dialectic aims at victory in debate or (by way of its close relative rhetoric) persuasion, whereas demonstration at explanation. If different forms of inference are appropriate to these two contexts, then why should not the theory of inference found in one treatise be different from the theory found in the other?

There are two ways such a view might be developed. One way is to make Aristotle a kind of logical relativist, allowing that the validity of an argument is relative to its subject matter or its context. There are some positions within the philosophy of logic which would find such a relativism welcome. But a major (and I would say fatal) obstacle to reading him in this way is his persistence in saying that a συλλογισμός is a συλλογισμός no matter what its subject matter. Nowhere does he say that necessary consequence varies with context. Indeed, precisely where we might most expect him to appeal to it, in discussing rhetorical arguments, he goes out of his way to say that the right way to study rhetorical arguments is simply to study arguments, then study how to cast them into appropriate language (*Rhet.* i 1, 1355a3–14). Again, his treatment of fallacies in the *Sophistical Refutations* displays a clear grip on the distinction between what appears to be valid and what actually is valid (see esp. ch. 6): Aristotle is very far from supposing that validity can be reduced to persuasiveness or to convention. A second alternative is to suppose that in dialectic, what

A third approach would be to interpret the difference between dialectical argument and demonstration as analogous to the difference between inductive and deductive arguments, in our terminology. Empirical generalizations rest on a kind of inference which is deductively invalid but nevertheless of great philosophical and scientific importance. But whatever the merits of such proposals, they do not help with our problem at all. Even if Aristotle thinks that the first principles of the sciences are established in some way that makes use of dialectic and is different from demonstration, he clearly thinks that there is such a thing as a dialectical συλλογισμός, and he clearly thinks that he has given us recipes for concocting lots of them in the *Topics*. The issue is not whether dialectic uses deductions in some different way from demonstration, but whether it analyzes them differently. For instance, if we hypothesize that dialectic is able to establish the first principles by a process of testing them that involves deducing consequences from them and examining these for internal contradictions, the deductions themselves must have some sort of logical structure. And here, we again confront Aristotle’s repeated definition of συλλογισμός in the *Topics* and *Analytics* as an argument in which the premises necessitate the conclusion, and his strong declaration that there is only a single correct theory of validity and that it applies to all arguments whatsoever (*APr.* ii 23, 68b9–14). If dialectic really does use different inferential procedures from demonstration, rather than putting the same procedures to different uses, then Aristotle contradicts this claim.

In view of their costs, none of these approaches is particularly satisfactory. If there is an alternative that lets us preserve something like Aristotle’s own understanding of his position, avoids invoking troublesome developmental hypotheses, and accommodates at least the bulk of what he says, it should be preferred. It is important to note that my position is neutral with respect to developmental theories. Or, he could have written the *Topics* in the full knowledge of the entire theory of figures in its most mature form without seeing any particular reason to mention that theory. In fact, I think the first of these is probably closer to the truth than the second, but nothing in my interpretation requires any answer at all to the question which came first.

## IV. What Is a Theory of Inference, and What Are They Good For?

Before I start ransacking the *Topics* and *Posterior Analytics* to see what sort of theory of inference we can uncover in each, we ought to ask some basic questions about what a theory of inference is and why Aristotle would want one. It is important to remember that our perspective is not his. We have been accustomed to the notion of a logical theory all our intellectual lives; but this is just exactly a part of our inheritance (in fact, our inheritance from Aristotle). Aristotle, by contrast, had no model to influence him. Therefore, it is worth our asking what could have motivated him to make inference an object of study. The answer to this last question seems obvious, at first. A theory of inference is a theory of validity; its goal is to give an account of which arguments are valid and which are invalid. A valid argument, in turn, is an argument in which the conclusion follows from the premises, that is, in which the conclusion must be true if the premises are true. To put it briefly, the purpose of logical theory is to explain which arguments are valid and which are not. But although this may be an adequate account of logical theory in itself, it does not address the question why anyone would pursue such a theory in the first place. One possible reason, of course, is pure theoretical curiosity. However, logical theory might be pursued for ulterior reasons in connection with some superordinate theory, or even for practical purposes. In fact, the question whether logical theory is a self-sufficient theory or merely an adjunct to other theories has a long history, descending from the controversy in Hellenistic times between the Stoics, who viewed logic as a ‘part of philosophy’ (i.e. as an area of theoretical inquiry in its own right), and the Peripatetic commentators on Aristotle, who thought of it only as an ‘instrument’ of philosophy.¹⁶

The relevant point for my present purposes is that one’s reason for pursuing logical theory may very well affect the way in which that theory is pursued. An example from modern logic will help. Gödel’s proof of the existence of formally undecidable propositions in first-order arithmetic is the end process of a line of inquiry beginning with Hilbert’s proposals for a ‘proof theory’ that explores what propositions can and cannot be obtained as theorems in a formal system. It is against that background that questions about the completeness and decidability of systems became important to logicians. In other words, they pursued the results which would contribute to answering the overall questions which gave rise to their theorizing in the first place. What comparable environment existed for Aristotle’s logical theorizing, and what were the problems and puzzles which motivated him?

The best way to approach this question, I suggest, is to go at it in reverse order. We possess his actual logical theory, namely, the theory of the figures as contained in the *Prior Analytics*. Therefore, we should ask: what work did this theory do for him? What puzzles did it answer? Our evidence for this should be the actual uses to which he puts his theory, whether in the *Prior Analytics* or elsewhere, and the details of his presentation and development of this theory. ## V. The Theory of the Figures and the *Posterior Analytics*

Aristotle’s *Analytics*—*Prior* and *Posterior* together—comprise a single treatise with a single overriding purpose: to give an account of demonstration (ἀπόδειξις). He says in its first sentence that he will first discuss deduction (συλλογισμός) in general because a demonstration is a kind of deduction. This is more than a trivial remark of method: if Aristotle’s purpose in discussing deduction at all is the fact that demonstrations are deductions, then we might expect that discussion to emphasize those features of deductions that are most important, most useful for the subsequent account of demonstrations.

I have argued elsewhere (1989, 1991) that there is a close interdependence between the results established in the *Prior Analytics* and the overall argument of the *Posterior Analytics*. The theory of figured deductions is not developed for its own sake, in lofty isolation from applications. Instead, the results Aristotle seeks to prove, and the ways in which he establishes them, strongly suggest that the program for his investigations was set by the needs of the theory of demonstration. This is not merely a general claim based on the fact that Aristotle says a demonstration is a kind of συλλογισμός: it is reflected in the detailed structure of the two works. The problems Aristotle wishes to solve in the *Posterior Analytics* set the agenda for the *Prior*; the results established in the *Prior* are put to use in the *Posterior*. Indeed, except for the uses he makes of the theory of the figures in the *Posterior Analytics* itself, Aristotle has hardly anything to say about the contents of the *Prior Analytics* in his other treatises.

The proof as well as the explication of my claim lies in the details. Here, I will consider only one especially important connection: the role played by *Prior Analytics* results in Aristotle’s arguments against two alternative views about the nature of demonstration presented in *Posterior Analytics* i 3. Aristotle’s own position is that a demonstration must rest on premises which are not themselves demonstrated, or indeed capable of demonstration. He describes, and rejects, two alternatives. The first of these denies that science is really possible at all on the basis of two claims: (1) the only way to scientific knowledge is demonstration; (2) the premises of a demonstration must be matters of scientific knowledge. The argument them proceeds as follows: either the premises are demonstrated or they are not. If they are demonstrated, then there must be other premises from which they are demonstrated, others from which those are demonstrated, and so on *ad infinitum*. But then there is no starting point to the demonstrations, and so nothing is demonstrated. On the other hand, if they are not demonstrated, then by (1) they are not objects of scientific knowledge. Now, Aristotle’s response to the first of these positions is actually quite elaborate. To explain it, let me introduce some non-Aristotelian terminology. Suppose P is a set of true propositions. I define a *premise set* for P as a set P' of true propositions obtained in the following way: take some member *p* of P and replace it with some set {q ... r} of true propositions not including *p* which imply *p*. Thus, P' is a premise set for P if and only if P' consists of all the propositions in P save one together with propositions from which that proposition follows. In the limiting case where P has only one member *p*, I say that any premise set P' for P is a premise set for the proposition *p*. I define a *premise regress* for a proposition *p* as a series of the form *p, P₁, ..., Pₙ*, where P₁ is a premise set for *p* and each successive Pᵢ is a premise set for its predecessor. Call a premise regress *circular* if there is some proposition *q* which is in one of its premise sets Pᵢ, not in Pᵢ₊₁, and again in some later set Pᵢ₊ⱼ. Intuitively, a premise regress is just the result of expanding a demonstration by replacing its premises with premises from which they follow, replacing those premises with premises from which they follow, etc., and a circular regress is one in which a proposition that is ‘demonstrated’ at one stage reappears as a premise at a later stage.

I have argued elsewhere (Smith 1984, 1986; see also 1991) that Aristotle tries to establish in the *Posterior Analytics* that no non-circular premise regress is infinite.¹⁷ This means that if we undertake to deduce a given true proposition from other true propositions and iterate the process, we will eventually come to a stop with true propositions that do not follow from *any other true propositions*. This result is strongly dependent on the thesis that the theory of the figures is the correct theory of inference: indeed, such a result can only be obtained in a relatively weak logic such as Aristotle’s. In any system that takes even the propositional calculus as part of its underlying logic, there can be no true propositions that do not follow from any other true propositions.¹⁸ Aristotle calls these non-inferable truths ‘unmiddled’ or ‘immediate’ (ἄμεσος), a term which reflects his own theory of inference: in the theory of the figures, any deduction must have a middle term, and therefore a proposition for which no such middle term exists (i.e. no term which can serve as the middle of a deduction with true premises) is ‘unmiddled’. I will not pursue the further consequences for these results here. What is important is that the results on which they rest are established in the *Prior Analytics*. Moreover, if we look carefully at the way those results are established, we can see that they are obtained with this use in mind. In order to show that infinite regresses are impossible for *all* propositions, Aristotle must argue that they are impossible for all types of propositions. An efficient way to do this is to classify propositions according to syntactical types; he does this by arguing that every proposition is in one of the four categorical forms (i 23). Then, it will be sufficient to argue that premise regresses for each type of proposition must terminate. To argue for this, Aristotle must be able to say what possible structures a deduction for each type of proposition may have. In the *Prior Analytics*, he establishes exactly this. His results determine all the possible valid forms of argument with categorical premises, and he takes care to note how many forms there are with each type of proposition as conclusion. Therefore, he can say (as he does in *APo.* i 21) that there are exactly three ways of proving a universal negative proposition.

There are even stronger indications that the theory of figured arguments is intended as a proof-theoretic tool. Here is a particularly telling example. Aristotle first proves that the four first-figure forms alone are sufficient and then shows (in *APr.* i 7) that two of those may also be dispensed with. Now, if his concern were to give a manual for constructing or for evaluating arguments, there would seem to be no point in this: the resulting arguments are significantly more complex and less perspicuous. However, dispensing with two forms leads to a deeper theoretical simplification, since it will follow that whatever can be deduced at all can be deduced relying only on those two forms. Therefore, if we establish that something cannot be true of deductions depending only on the universal forms, then we have shown that it cannot be true of any deduction. In a proof-theoretic context, this advantage may justify the increased complexity in actual deductions which results from using a smaller number of basic forms. For a modern comparison, parsimonious Fitch-style natural deduction systems produce deductions which are less perspicuous at a certain intuitive level (and harder for beginning students to find) than relatively profligate Copi-style systems with rules chosen for their intuitive appeal; however, it is much easier to study the metatheoretical properties of Fitch-style systems.

## VI. The Goal of the *Topics* and Aristotle’s Dialectical Art

Aristotle begins the *Topics* with an explicit statement of the overall goals of his treatise:

What we propose to do in our study is to find a procedure by means of which we will be able to construct deductions from accepted premises concerning any problem that is proposed

144

and, when we submit to argument ourselves, will not say anything of the contrary sort. (100a18–21)

In order to understand what Aristotle is after here, we need a clear picture of what a dialectical argument is and what would be required for success in such arguments. Here, I will rely on a picture for which I have argued elsewhere (Smith 1993). Briefly, dialectical argument is essentially argument directed at another person and proceeding through questions: the person arguing puts forward premises for acceptance or rejection to a respondent, who either accepts or rejects them. This general characterization can be stretched to include many different argumentative contexts, so that any sort of argument advanced through questioning may be called dialectical; however, in much of the *Topics* Aristotle has in mind a specific form of exchange (which presumably originated in the Academy) in which an answerer undertakes to defend a thesis and a questioner to refute it. To judge by *Topics* viii, these exchanges were governed by various rules and perhaps evaluated by judges.

Now, in any sort of dialectical argument, the questioner must construct an argument out of the answerer’s responses. Success in this endeavor requires that the questioner put forward premises which satisfy two criteria: (1) the answerer must accept them, and (2) they must actually imply the desired conclusion. I think Aristotle gives a method in the *Topics* for doing just this. In outline, the method rests on two different systems for classifying premises. First, we are to assemble collections of premises which various types of person believe: premises everyone accepts, premises most people accept, premises wise people (i.e. philosophers) accept. These are organized by subject matter, so that we can quickly recall, say, the various things everyone believes about the good, or the various things Democritus believes about the stars. Aristotle refers to such premises as *ἔνδοξα*: ‘accepted’ premises, i.e., things which people (of some sort or other) believe.¹⁹ The construction of these inventories of *ἔνδοξα* comprises the first component of Aristotle’s art.

Its second component consists of the τόποι, ‘locations’, from which the treatise takes its name. Each of these is, as its name suggests, a location for the storage (in memory) of premises, or at any rate premise patterns. Alexander quotes Theophrastus’ definition of a τόπος as ‘a starting point or element from which we can obtain the particular starting points, definite in outline but indefinite as regards the particulars’ (ἀρχή τις ἢ στοιχεῖον, ἀφ’ οὗ λαμβάνομεν τὰς περὶ ἕκαστον ἀρχάς, τῇ περιγραφῇ μὲν ὡρισμένος, τοῖς δὲ καθ’ ἕκαστα ἀόριστος: *In Top.* 126.13–15, cf. 5.21–26). ## VII. The Systems of the *Topics* and *Analytics* Compared

From the preceding remarks, it is evident that the theory of demonstration and the art of dialectic make demands on their respective underlying accounts of inference that are in many respects diametrically opposed. The proof-theoretic concerns of the *Analytics* require a genuine theory that encompasses every possible valid argument. Since Aristotle’s real goal is the establishing of results about deductions, the actual deductive theory produces relatively cumbersome and unintuitive deductions and even replaces natural language with an artificial canonical idiom. As a practical manual for the construction of persuasive arguments to be used in stand-up debate, the *Prior Analytics* is a disaster; as a treatise on logical theory intended to serve as the foundation for a study of proof, it is brilliant.

By contrast, the practical concerns of the *Topics* demand only a theory of arguments that will find the premises needed to get the job done. Completeness is irrelevant to such a project: it might be nice to have techniques that will find a wide range of different useful arguments, but there is no particular need to get them all. Neither is there any need for an actual theory that explains or even comprehensively classifies all the valid arguments. The τόποι are really just indexes, locations under which to file premises with a common structure. The dialectician *qua* dialectician need have no interest in the theoretical question whether such general premises explain the validity of the arguments classified under them: all that the dialectician must be able to do is come up with them when they are needed. The entire mechanism is classificatory rather than explanatory, a complex heuristic for retrieving premises from memory. Of course, Aristotle supposes that the dialectician will use only valid arguments. However, it does not follow that the dialectician as dialectician will require a

***

148

theory of validity, merely a stock of valid ways to argue. Compare the relationship of the theoretical physicist to the engineer.

Now, suppose that Aristotle undertook to write an ‘art of dialectic’ after having finished the *Prior Analytics*. Would he have included an exposition of the theory of the figures? If he had, he would have needlessly complicated his subject: it would be rather like incorporating a treatment of first-order theory into an elementary text on informal logic, or a debate manual. What the *Analytics* needs is the general result that it is *possible* to reduce every argument to a figured deduction, even if it is sometimes a cumbersome matter actually to carry out the reduction. To take a modern comparison: Gödel’s incompleteness theorem requires that it be *possible* to assign a number to each expression of the language in such a way that predicates like ‘is a theorem’ can be represented as arithmetic functions. The fact that it would be monstrously impractical to use that representation as a means of actually deciding what is or is not a theorem is, for Gödel’s purposes, totally irrelevant.

Less dramatically, many logicians have believed that first-order predicate logic is adequate for the expression of the logical form of any natural-language argument. But that hardly leads to the conclusion that in order to construct useful arguments, one *must* make explicit the logical form of every argument. Similarly, Aristotle’s thesis of universal reducibility to the figures is important only as a theoretical possibility: in practice, he could with a good conscience ignore it.

In a sense, then, my view is a traditional one. There is no real inconsistency between the *Topics* and the *Prior Analytics* because the theoretical purposes for which the syllogistic was developed are simply not important to the *Topics*. On the other hand, my view is also consistent with developmental interpretations. Indeed, it is even predisposed to one sort of developmental view: since I regard the syllogistic as Aristotle’s proof-theoretic tool for establishing the conditions under which demonstrative science is possible, I must regard it as having come later in his intellectual development than at least the general outlines of his theory of science. If indeed the *Topics* was written from the start by an Aristotle already in possession of the mature form of that theory, then given the manifest pride he takes in his achievement in the *Prior Analytics*, it is odd that we find no mention of it at all in the *Topics*; therefore, it is *prima facie* likely that the *Topics* reflects an earlier stage of his thought than the *Prior Analytics*. However, any number of other factors might explain this same circumstance. For instance, if the *Topics* is intended as a manual for relative newcomers to philosophy, or if it is directed at a very wide audience (including those quite ignorant of technical questions about the possibility of science), then Aristotle may have wanted to avoid bringing in a theory which had no special practical value in his method. In any event, it seems clear that Aristotle’s associates and successors (including Theophrastus) continued to write about both dialectic and demonstration and to take the *Topics* seriously.
