# Immediate Propositions and Aristotle’s Proof Theory

Robin Smith
*Ancient Philosophy* 6 (1986), 47–68

***

Recent researchers using the techniques of mathematical logic in interpreting Aristotle’s logical theories have suggested, not simply that Aristotle developed a theory of deduction, but also that he investigated the same types of questions about his logical system as are characteristic of modern logical research. The first to make such a case in detail was John Corcoran, who found evidence that ‘Aristotle was self-consciously studying interrelations among deductions—exactly as is done in Hilbert’s “proof theory”’ (Corcoran 1974, 116) and included a significant number of ‘fairly intricate metamathematical results’ among the fundamental achievements he ascribes to Aristotle as logician (Corcoran 1974, 122–123). More recently, Jonathan Lear has said:

> . . . Aristotle shares with contemporary logicians a primary interest in metalogic. He uses the syllogistic as modern logicians use the predicate calculus; not as a tool with which one actually formalizes mathematical proofs, but rather as an object about which one can reason in order to gain insight into the structure of proof. (Lear 1980, 13)

I would like to pursue the parallel suggested by these authors. Specifically, I will argue that Aristotle developed the theory of deduction in the *Prior Analytics* precisely in order to carry out proof-theoretic investigations. This leads to an important result concerning the perennial question of the relationship of the *Posterior Analytics* to the *Prior*.¹ The argument of this paper points towards a complex relationship of mutual dependency: the theory of science of the *Posterior Analytics* is not simply the temporal predecessor of the syllogistic, it is also its inspiration. As a result, the two parts of the *Analytics* correspond to two theoretical enterprises which are interactively linked, each influencing the development of the other, much as logic and the philosophy of science have done in our own century. Several lines of evidence point to this conclusion: the results derived in the *Prior Analytics*, the form in which those results are presented, the uses to which the syllogistic is put in the *Posterior*, and certain paradoxical things Aristotle is led to say by his theory.

## I

The principal subject of the first book of the *Posterior Analytics* is the demonstration (ἀπόδειξις), which Aristotle defines as ‘a syllogism which produces knowledge’ (συλλογισμὸς ἀποδεικτικός). Now, the use of the word συλλογισμός does not of itself

presuppose the syllogistic theory of the *Prior Analytics*, since Aristotle’s official definition of ‘syllogism’ at *An. Pr.* i 1, 24b18–20 and *Top.* i 1, 100a25–27 is broad enough to embrace any valid argument whatever.² It is therefore a legitimate question whether Aristotle developed the theory of demonstration before or after the theory of the syllogism. Barnes³ has been the most recent to argue that ‘the doctrine expounded in the *Posterior Analytics* is entirely—or almost entirely—independent of the Syllogism’ (Barnes 1981, 8).⁴ It is with this claim that I shall begin.

Aristotle holds that in order to bring about knowledge of its conclusion, a deduction must rest on premises satisfying two sorts of conditions: first, they must have certain properties in and of themselves, and second, they must stand in certain relationships to the conclusion which they support. In and of themselves, Aristotle tells us, they must be true, ‘primary’ (πρῶτος), and ‘immediate’ (ἄμεσος); they must also be ‘better known’ (γνωριμώτερος) than the conclusion, ‘prior’ (πρότερος) to it, and provide the cause or explanation (αἴτιον) of it. Barnes’s case is simply that none of these conditions has any essential dependence on the syllogistic of the *Prior Analytics*. Let us consider each condition in turn.

The requirement of truth is obviously separable from the syllogistic, and whether the premises are ‘better known’ than the conclusion is presumably a matter of epistemic status.⁵ Priority to the conclusion and primacy are less easy to explicate, but nothing about them suggests any appeal to a particular theory of deduction. There is certainly no difficulty in interpreting the requirement that the premises give the cause of the conclusion non-syllogistically. We are left, then, with immediacy.

A tradition of some antiquity treats immediacy also as an epistemic term: ‘known without the mediation of anything else’. So interpreted, it would clearly have no syllogistic dependency. There is some textual support for such an interpretation. *Topics* i 1 gives an abbreviated form of the definition of demonstration in *An. Post.* i 2, saying only that the premises of a demonstration must be ‘true and primary’ and explicating that in turn as follows:

> Those things are true and primary which carry their conviction not through other things but through themselves. For in the principles of sciences, one ought not to search for the reason why, but each of the principles ought in and of itself to be worthy of belief.⁶ (100a30–b21)

Unfortunately, although this passage defines a clearly epistemic notion, it does not define (or even mention) immediacy. However, there is a brief explication of immediacy later in the *Posterior Analytics*:

> For I call the same thing ‘primary’ and ‘principle’. A principle is an immediate premise of a demonstration; and an immediate premise is one to which no other is prior. (72a6–8)

Since a premise to which no other is prior must be primary (πρῶτος is the superlative of πρότερος), this seems to equate primacy and immediacy; and, taken together with the *Topics* account, this might seem enough to show that immediacy is an epistemic term, perhaps equivalent to *per se gnotum*.

Other texts, however, support a different interpretation. After listing the above six requirements in *An. Post.* i 2, Aristotle comments briefly on each, and when he reaches the position appropriate to ‘primary’ he says,

> [A syllogism must be] from primary and indemonstrable premises, because otherwise one will not know without having a demonstration of them. For to know that of which there is a demonstration (except incidentally) is just to have a demonstration. (71b26–29)

This passage substitutes ‘indemonstrable’ (ἀπόδεικτος) for ‘immediate’ (ἄμεσος), and the ancient commentators⁷ accordingly treat the two terms as equivalent. This gives an alternative interpretation of immediacy. An Aristotelian syllogism must have a middle term; therefore, a demonstrated proposition must be demonstrated through the use of a middle term, and an ‘indemonstrable’ proposition could also be called ‘immediate’. On this interpretation, immediacy is intrinsically linked to the syllogistic, for it is only in that system that it makes sense to talk about a proposition ‘having a middle’. It is also the sense we find in *An. Post.* i 15–17, where it is used interchangeably with ‘indivisibly belonging’ (ἀτόμως ὑπάρχειν), defined at 79a34–35 as ‘not having a middle term’. The evidence seems quite unequivocally to support a syllogistic interpretation of immediacy.

Even so, it may be argued that the link between the definition of demonstration and the syllogism is not deep. It could be that immediacy is merely the syllogistic way of describing a more general requirement and that in consequence the term ‘immediate’ is a later, comparatively superficial, modification to an originally non-syllogistic theory. Its omission from the *Topics* version of the definition of demonstration, together with its conjunction with ‘primary’ in 71b26–27,⁸ might suggest that immediacy is simply a specification, in syllogistic terms, of primacy. We may most conveniently understand this as a question about the epistemic content of ‘immediate’ and ‘indemonstrable’. Three possible senses of ἀπόδεικτος may be distinguished:

(A) Not in need of demonstration.
(B) Not demonstrable from any other propositions.
(C) Not deducible from any other true⁹ propositions.

Sense (A) is purely epistemic. Sense (B) also has epistemic content if we employ Aristotle’s notion of demonstration: since a proposition can only be demonstrated from others prior to it, and since nothing can be prior to what is primary, no deduction of a primary proposition from anything can be a demonstration. Sense (C), however, has no epistemic content and no intrinsic connection with primacy. Now, analogous senses of ‘immediate’ can be defined in obvious ways corresponding to (A), (B), and (C). On either (A) or (B), Aristotle really adds nothing to his definition by specifying that the premises must be immediate, for primacy entails (or perhaps is equivalent to) indemonstrability, and ‘immediate’ is just a syllogistically oriented way of saying ‘indemonstrable’. Interpretation (C), however, makes immediacy an independent component of the definition. For this reason alone, it might appear to be preferable to the alternatives.

and says that of two deductions involving the same terms, one may fail to be a demonstration while the other succeeds. The cases he has in mind involve ‘counterpredicable’ (ἀντικατηγορούμενα) or ‘convertible’ (ἀντιστρέφοντα) terms, i. e., terms universally true of one another, as are ‘what does not twinkle’ and ‘what is near’ in the following two deductions:

> Thus Aristotle can only say that [a proposition] is immediate if there are no prior propositions from which it is Syllogistically derivable ... i. e., if it is Syllogistically primitive. (Barnes 1981, 31.)

Barnes accordingly regards immediacy as simply the syllogistic specification of primitiveness, so that once again the core of the *Posterior Analytics* doctrine is separable from the theory of the syllogism.

Barnes may well be right in claiming that the theory of demonstration originated in independence of the syllogistic. However, as I shall try to show, the links between the two go much deeper than his position indicates. In particular, I claim, we cannot fully understand why Aristotle developed one of his most familiar—or notorious—doctrines without considering the syllogistic: his view that there is ‘a unique natural route to knowledge’, as Lear puts it.¹⁰ Aristotle’s comparison of the acquisition of science with moral education is instructive here: just as our goal in becoming good men is to make what is in itself good also good to us, so in science we strive to make what is in itself more known or intelligible also more known to us (*Meta.* vii 4.1029b4–12). Thus, for Aristotle what really *is* a demonstration might fail to produce knowledge in one lacking any proper knowledge of the principles.¹¹ In addition, Aristotle appears to hold that there is exactly one correct way to demonstrate anything demonstrable. Such views grate harshly on our modern sensibilities. They appear to be linked to a concept of intuitive self-evidence and an aprioristic view of science. One traditional line of interpretation of the function of νοῦς in *An. Post.* ii 19 is as providing such an intuitive sort of cognition; and while that interpretation has been strongly challenged by Lesher 1973,¹² it cannot be denied that Aristotle is far more confident than we of the uniqueness and specificity of the principles of sciences. Our historical experience has taught us how troublesome such views can be to maintain, and accordingly we should ask what it is that inclines Aristotle towards them.

The answer lies in something which commentators have generally ignored: the problems Aristotle tries to solve using the concept of immediacy. We must accordingly shift our attention from those parts of *Posterior Analytics* i in which immediacy is only mentioned to those in which it is used. As a first step, let us examine more carefully the relationship of the syllogistic to that work.

## II

Although Barnes believes that the theory of science was not originally tied to the syllogistic, he nevertheless does accept the traditional view that ‘the *Posterior Analytics* time and again presupposes a knowledge of the *Prior Analytics*’ (Barnes 1981, 28). In my 1982a, I argued instead that only a limited amount of knowledge about the syllogistic is presupposed by the *Posterior* and that in places Aristotle even seems to contradict views expressed in the *Prior*. In general, with the exception of certain texts (principally, book 1, chapters 16 and 17 and the latter part of 3), Aristotle actually makes use of a more primitive ancestor of the syllogistic which knows only the general properties of the universal moods (*Barbara, Celarent, Camestres, Cesare*). Moreover, those texts which do give evidence of greater knowledge of the syllogistic have the appearance of later developments not well integrated into their surroundings. However, although I rejected the claim that the *Posterior Analytics* relies to any great extent on the syllogistic theory expressed in the *Prior*, I nevertheless concluded that the treatise does rely throughout on some syllogistic theory, even if a simpler ‘proto-syllogistic’.

I now think that this conclusion must be modified. It is not the case that the whole of book 1 depends on even a weakened syllogistic: the dependency is essentially confined to chapters 3 and 14–29. Indeed, a resolute defender of Barnes’s position could accomodate my arguments by the simple supposition that all the syllogistically influenced sections are products of a later revision (this is in effect what Barnes does). Such a defense is not as arbitrary as it sounds, for if we extract these (admittedly extensive) sections, a very large portion of Aristotle’s doctrine remains untouched: the definition of demonstration; the discussions of universality and necessity; the differentiation of definitions, axioms, hypotheses; the division of principles into ‘common’ and ‘proper’ and the concomitant rejection of a universal science; the distinction between ‘knowing that’ and ‘knowing why’, and the differentiations of scientific knowledge from perception and opinion—all these would remain. Even the central subject of chapter 3, the claim that demonstration is possible only if there are principles which can be known without demonstration, can be preserved, for it is largely adumbrated in chapter 1 and in the definitions of chapter 2. Thus, quite a viable Aristotelian theory of science can be found in a sort of stump of the *Posterior Analytics* in total independence of any sort of syllogistic, quite as Barnes wishes to maintain.

We seem thus to arrive at an impasse, if our concern is to determine how much, if any, of the syllogistic Aristotle knew when he first put forth a recognizable form of his theory of demonstrative science. I suggest that we turn our attention instead to a different question: what difference does the syllogistic make to the doctrines of the *Posterior Analytics*? The answer to this is obviously to be found by studying the contents of those sections of *Posterior Analytics* i which do have syllogistic links. Here we discover one striking piece of evidence: all of them bear more or less directly on the question raised in the third chapter concerning the knowability of the first principles of sciences. Aristotle’s goal there is to resolve a dispute concerning demonstrative knowledge which was apparently current in his time.¹³ He portrays for us the positions of two groups of his opponents, as well as his own response. One group, whom I will name the agnostics, held that no proposition can be demonstrated; another,

***

**52**

whom we may call the circular demonstrators, responded that all the propositions of a science can be demonstrated. In order to understand the subsequent argument of *An. Post.* i, it will be helpful to set out schematically the various theses accepted (or rejected) by the parties to this controversy.

The agnostics, the circular demonstrators, and Aristotle all accept the following:
(1) Whatever is deduced from known propositions is known.

The agnostics and circular demonstrators also accept the converse of (1):
(2) Only what is deduced from known propositions is known.

Aristotle, however, denies (2).

In order to explain the remaining theses at work here, I will introduce the term *premiss regress*: a premiss regress is a sequence of sets of propositions P₁, P₂, ... Pₙ such that, for any Pᵢ, if there is a next member Pᵢ₊₁ then it results from Pᵢ by replacing some proposition with premisses from which it follows. For convenience, we may suppose that the first member of the series, P₁, contains a single proposition. It is assumed that a series may not be continued by replacing a proposition with premisses which include it and also that all propositions in the members of the premiss regress must be true. Now, a premiss regress can be extended only if some proposition in its last member Pₙ can be deduced from others not already in Pₙ; thus, if no such propositions are available, then the regress cannot be extended. Let us say that a regress of the latter sort *terminates* while one which can always be extended further¹⁴ does not terminate.

The agnostics’ position then continues with the following obvious dilemma:
(3) For any premiss regress, either it terminates or it does not.

The agnostics then deduced the impossibility of knowledge by appealing to the regress of premisses which would necessarily ensue if every premiss, in order to be known, must itself be deduced from others. The first horn of the dilemma is the only one usually noticed: obviously, if the regress never ends, then the demonstration never begins. However, Aristotle also allows for the possibility that the regress stops:

> [The agnostics] think that it goes on into infinity so that one will not know the posterior things through prior things of which there is no first ... But if it comes to a stop at some time and there are principles, these will be unknowable since there is no demonstration of them (which they say is the only sort of knowledge). (72b8–13)

The circular demonstrators in effect introduce another distinction. A non-terminating regress may be either a ‘straight-line’ regress which always introduces premisses that have not occurred in it before or a circular regress in which premisses eliminated at one point are reintroduced later on. In the latter case, it would be possible for a non-terminating regress to contain only finitely many distinct propositions.¹⁵ We may state this briefly as:
(4) Every non-terminating regress is either a straight-line regress (including infinitely many distinct propositions)

***

**53**

or a circular regress (possibly including only a finite number of propositions).

Aristotle approves of the agnostics’ response to the infinite-regress horn of the dilemma, remarking that ‘it is impossible to go through infinitely many things’ (72b10–11). From a modern perspective, this may seem to be simply an assertion of the requirement that a deduction cannot have infinitely many steps.¹⁶ However, Aristotle probably has also in mind the epistemic point that no one could ever mentally ‘go through’ an infinite proof. There is of course an acceptable Aristotelian sense in which someone can be said to know infinitely many propositions: anyone who knows that the square of an even number is even thereby knows, of each of infinitely many even numbers, that it has an even square. In the present case, however, ‘going through’ a proof requires actually taking cognizance of each of its steps, and thus the completion of an actually infinite sequence of acts. With a little liberty, we may formulate this as:
(5) No one can actually know infinitely many propositions.

We may then see the agnostics as arguing that no premiss regress can ever lead to a proof. For if the regress terminates, it must terminate in unknown propositions (by (2)), whereas if it does not terminate its constituent propositions cannot be known (by (5)). The circular demonstrators take advantage of the second clause of (4) and maintain something like:
(6) A circular deduction is a demonstration of all the propositions in it.

Exactly how they thought this would work we do not know, but one possibility is that they used a bifurcated notion of demonstration, with circular demonstration serving to prove the principles and all other propositions demonstrated from those principles.

It is this debate among the positions of the agnostics, the circular demonstrators, and Aristotle himself that is the fundamental point of entry of the syllogistic into *Posterior Analytics* i. To understand the relationship of the syllogistic to Aristotle’s theory of science, we must understand its relationship to that debate.

## III

Let us begin with a closer look at the interrelations among the positions in the controversy. The agnostics maintain that *nothing* can be demonstrated. It is surely not enough to respond, rather abstractly, that there might be *something* which can be demonstrated: a full response would instead need to show that the truths of the sciences are demonstrable. The circular demonstrators thus would have to do more than envision a proof-structure which allowed for the demonstration of everything: they would have to provide some account of how circular demonstration could provide proofs in an actual case, and indeed how an entire science could be organized into proofs in this circular fashion. The same holds for Aristotle’s position. It is not enough for him to deny (2) and say that at any rate something is known without demonstration: he must show that those things which are so known are also adequate as a foundation for the demonstration of everything else. If we look at Aristotle’s response, we see that he recognizes this:

> But we say that not all knowledge is demonstrative, but rather that knowledge of the immediates is indemonstrable. And that this is necessary is obvious. For if it is necessary to know those prior things

***

**54**

> on which the demonstration rests, and if the middles come to a stop at some time, these are necessarily indemonstrable.¹⁷ (72b18–22)

Aristotle thus responds to the agnostics’ dilemma (3) by following the other horn from that followed by the circular demonstrators: they tried to say that there could still be knowledge in a non-terminating regress, while Aristotle says that there can still be knowledge in a terminating one. The agnostics would of course agree, as Barnes 1975, 108 points out,¹⁸ that if there is knowledge of immediates then it cannot be demonstrative. It is up to Aristotle to show both that such knowledge—knowledge of the immediate propositions in which regresses must terminate—is possible, and that just that knowledge will be sufficient to provide a foundation for the sciences.

We may express his position in two theses:
(7) Every immediate proposition can be known without demonstration.
(8) Every proposition is either immediate or deducible from immediates.

In addition, if Aristotle wishes to rule out the circular demonstrators’ position, he needs to establish the following:
(9) A circular demonstration is not a demonstration.

In fact, as we shall see below, Aristotle thinks he can also establish the further thesis:
(10) Circular demonstration cannot provide a deduction of everything from principles.

Now, I wish to argue in what follows that Aristotle has a line of attack on these problems which can properly be described as proof-theoretic. It rests on the following thesis:
(11) Every premiss regress terminates.

This is sufficient to eliminate at once both the circular demonstrators’ position and the infinite-regress arm of the agnostics’ position. Moreover, since a regress that terminates is always reversible to give a deduction of its starting point, this suffices to establish (8) (if a regress terminates it can only terminate in immediate propositions).

What the syllogistic does for the theory of science is to establish (11). In brief, Aristotle uses the syllogistic to give a classification of all possible deductive structures of proofs and then argues that for each of these possibilities, an infinite regress is impossible. Thus, he rests a major claim about demonstrative sciences on a theory of the structure of deductions. This is strongly reminiscent—or anticipatory—of the role played by proof theory in modern formal logic, and I do not think we are unjustified in describing Aristotle’s own method as proof-theoretic.

Throughout these arguments, it is only the non-epistemic sense (C) of immediacy—undeducibility from other true propositions—that is important. Establishing (11) does not establish that there is non-demonstrative knowledge of the principles, but it does show that there is demonstrative knowledge *if and only if* there is non-demonstrative knowledge of those same principles. Aristotle’s argument thus has a simple overall structure: he first argues that every science must contain a certain set of propositions which stand in a certain deductive relationship to the remainder (they are underivable from others, whereas all others are derivable from them), and then he argues that the propositions of that set have a further epistemic status (they can be known without being demonstrated). The second phase of this is, it must be admitted, not fully clear

***

**55**

in the text of the *Posterior Analytics*. Its realization is found in the notoriously difficult chapter ii 19, which gives us an account, not of how immediate propositions are known, but of how concepts are formed. However, the difficulty of finding a link between this chapter’s doctrines and the theory of book 1 is one faced by any interpretation of the *Posterior Analytics*, not just by mine, and accordingly I will demur from any attempt at resolving this issue here. It is in any event clear from Aristotle’s opening statement at 99b20–26 that he intends ii 19 to explain how knowledge of immediate propositions is possible. But if we suppose that immediacy is by definition epistemic primacy, then Aristotle’s elaborate syllogistically based arguments to defend (11) are unintelligible. These arguments cannot show that every premiss regress eventually comes upon premisses with a certain epistemic status, only that it terminates in propositions with a certain deductive status. As I have just said, Aristotle must offer us a separate argument that these propositions in which regresses terminate also possess the required epistemic priority. But this is just to separate immediacy in sense (C) from an epistemic concept of immediacy: the proof Aristotle offers us makes unavoidable use of sense (C) of immediacy. Moreover, if the criterion for identifying an immediate proposition is its possession of a certain epistemic status, then Aristotle owes us an argument that the propositions which possess this status are also sufficient as the deductive basis of a science; and this he certainly never does.

For these reasons, I conclude that sense (C) is the primary sense of immediacy in the *Posterior Analytics*: the assertion that the immediate propositions of a science are epistemically prior to all others is, we might say, a synthetic proposition, not a definitional truth. To defend this, I must show in some detail how Aristotle’s arguments for thesis (11) work. This will be the task of the following section.

## IV

In order to appreciate the role of immediate propositions in deductive systems having the syllogistic as their underlying logic,¹⁹ we must note how such systems differ from the richer systems familiar from modern mathematical logic. In a word, immediate propositions are possible in the former but not in the latter. In fact, any system which includes conjunction in its language can easily be shown to contain no immediate propositions whatever. For suppose that we have a science containing the propositions ‘p’ and ‘q’. Then, since these together entail ‘p and q’, the science must also include that proposition. Now, ‘p and q’ is not immediate, since it follows from other propositions; however, neither is ‘p’ or ‘q’, since each of them follows from their conjunction. This argument can be applied to any pair of propositions, or even to a single proposition, so that no system containing conjunction can contain any immediate propositions at all. The exact conditions under which a deductive system can contain immediate propositions need not concern us here,²⁰ but it should be noted that any system that allows sentential composition along the lines of standard propositional logic will be susceptible to the argument just given. According to the interpretation I follow,²¹ however, the syllogistic is a much weaker system, and deductive sciences relying on it can easily contain propositions underivable from others. A simple example is the system containing the following nine propositions:²²
*Aab, Abc, Aac, Iab, Iba, Iac, Ica, Ibc, Icb*

***

**56**

It is easy to verify that this set is deductively closed and that all the other propositions in it can be deduced from the set {*Aab, Abc*}. However, neither of these two propositions can be deduced from any others in the set. Consequently, these two are immediate, and since everything else in the system can be deduced from them, our example satisfies (8). It also satisfies (11), as an examination of all possible ways²³ of constructing a regress for any premiss will show.

A significant portion of the text of *Posterior Analytics* i is devoted to establishing thesis (11). In chapter 19, Aristotle raises a series of questions concerning what I will call *predication chains*: sequences of terms linked by *A* propositions. A sequence *a₁*, *a₂*, ... *aₙ* in which each term after the first is universally true of its predecessor (that is, *Aₐᵢₐᵢ₊₁* for every *i*) is an ascending chain, while one in which the terms are conversely related (that is, *Aₐᵢ₊₁ₐᵢ*) is a descending chain. His first questions concern the possibility of infinite ascending or descending chains:

> Let C be a term such that it does not belong to anything else but B belongs to it first, with no other term between. And again let E belong to F in the same way, and this to B. Must this necessarily come to a stop, or can it go on into infinity? And again, if nothing is predicated of itself of A, but A belongs first to G, and G to H, and this to B, does this also necessarily come to a stop, or can it go on into infinity?²⁴ (81b30–37)

Aristotle distinguishes the two cases here, corresponding to ascending and descending predication chains, as proceeding respectively ‘upwards’ (ἐπὶ τὸ ἄνω) and ‘downwards’ (ἐπὶ τὸ κάτω). He then distinguishes a third possible case:

> Again, can those in between be infinite, when the extremes are fixed? I mean, for example, if A belongs to C and B is a middle for them, and something else is for A and B, and something else for those, can these go on into infinity, or is that impossible? (82a2–6)

Here Aristotle pursues the resemblance²⁵ between predication chains and series of points. Suppose that *Aca*; that there is a middle term *b* ‘between’ these with which to prove this, so that *Acb* and *Aba*; that there is a middle term for each of these; and so on for each new term introduced. We have, then, three possible types of infinite regress: upwards, downwards, and ‘in between’. Aristotle makes it clear what the purpose of this inquiry is:²⁶

> Investigating this is the same as investigating whether demonstrations go into infinity, and whether there is demonstration of everything, or whether, instead, they are limited with respect to each other. (82a6–8)

Aristotle here equates his questions about whether certain sequences of terms can be infinite with the earlier problem whether premiss regresses can be infinite. His answer, which is subtle and difficult to follow, is contained in the next three chapters (20–22). Since these have recently been the subjects of some scholarly attention,²⁷ I will forego the tedious business of an analysis of Aristotle’s arguments in favor of a summary of his results. The arguments of chapters 19–21 establish the following theses:
(12) If every ascending or descending chain is finite, then

***

**57**

> there cannot be infinitely many middle terms between any two fixed terms, and so every premiss regress for every *A* proposition must terminate. (chapter 20)

(13) If every ascending or descending chain is finite, then every premiss regress for every *E* proposition must terminate.²⁸ (chapter 21)

(14) Every ascending or descending chain is finite. (chapter 22)

From these propositions Aristotle can at once infer (11), and thus (8).²⁹ In order to prove these results, he must make use of quite specific claims about the syllogistic, in particular concerning the possible forms of premises from which a conclusion of a given type may be deduced: an *A* proposition follows only from two *A*’s in *Barbara*, while an *E* follows in three different ways³⁰ from one *A* and one *E* premise. His results are thus proof-theoretic in that they rest on an explicit theory of the possible structures of proofs.

The intimate involvement of these sections with the syllogistic is manifest from this analysis: they are not just phrased in syllogistic terms but also depend essentially on facts about the deductive structure of syllogistic systems. Thus, these sections are not the syllogisticized form of a more general doctrine that Aristotle may have elaborated earlier, for there is no such thing as the non-syllogistic form of these arguments. A proof-theoretic argument must rest on a systematic theory of deduction, and before the development of the syllogistic Aristotle had no such theory.

The syllogistic portions of the *Posterior Analytics* were in consequence syllogistic from their birth. Nevertheless, as I argued in detail in my 1982a, there are major differences between the syllogistic as presupposed by the *Posterior Analytics* and the syllogistic as developed in the *Prior*, and these differences invariably amount to shortcomings of the former with respect to the latter. The only credible conclusion to be drawn from this is that the *Prior Analytics* known to us was composed, or at least brought into final form, after Aristotle had finished the *Posterior Analytics* i known to us. Now, there is nothing intrinsically implausible about this: the level of interdependence between the two *Analytics* is great, and Aristotle could well have used some earlier and cruder version of *Prior Analytics* i to buttress his proof-theoretic arguments and then subsequently polished and improved his exposition of the syllogistic. In fact, it is pointless to speculate on the details of the relative compositional histories of two such interconnected treatises. One fact, however, should be noted: it is only in *Posterior Analytics* i that Aristotle makes any significant use of the syllogistic at all. This is quite in accordance with Aristotle’s opening assertion in the *Prior Analytics* that his purpose in studying the syllogistic is from the beginning its role in his theory of demonstrative science. What we have just seen is that those needs are highly specific and technically sophisticated: the role of the syllogistic is to provide him with a proof-theoretic solution to the difficulty about knowledge of the principles raised by the agnostics and the circular demonstrators. My principal contention in this paper is that these proof-theoretic needs inform his exposition of the syllogistic in *Prior Analytics* i throughout; and it is to the establishment of this reverse dependency relation that I now turn.

## V

There is considerable evidence in the *Prior Analytics* that Aristotle had proof-theoretic applications in mind as he developed the syllogistic. *Prior Analytics* i is throughout a study, not of how to tell if an argument is valid, but of what structures arguments for certain conclusions may have. The initial presentation of the theory of deduction (chapters 4–7) is designed as an exhaustive survey of all possible combinations of premises: such comprehensiveness is essential to his proof-theoretic claims. In his summaries at the ends of the treatments of each figure (26b26–33, 28a1–9, 29a11–18), he invariably notes what types of propositions can be deduced in each figure. The remarkably modern metatheoretical result proved in chapter 7, viz., that the system resting on all four first-figure rules is equivalent to and derivable from the system resting on only the two universal rules, is exactly the sort of result a proof theorist would want, for it simplifies the possible structures of proofs one must take account of. Exactly the opposite motivation would influence someone who wanted to construct deductions or mimic in a formal system the workings of everyday arguments: it is both difficult and unnatural to make do with few rules when many can be provided. The same motive may explain Aristotle’s interest in the ‘perfection’ of syllogisms.³¹ That is, by showing that all other deductions can be reduced to first-figure deductions, Aristotle greatly reduces the types of proof structures of which he must take account. Aristotle’s concern to show that every argument can be turned into a syllogism in his standard form is from the present standpoint not simply a desire to show the comprehensiveness of his theory but an absolute requirement: a theory of proof structures is no good unless it embraces all possible proofs.

Aristotle’s occasional summaries of what he has done and what he has left to do also indicate his concern with the sorts of results needed to do proof theory. At the beginning of chapter 23 he summarizes the point he has there reached in his plan:

> It is evident from what has been said that all the syllogisms in these figures are perfected through the universal syllogisms in the first figure and reducible to these. But that absolutely every syllogism is likewise will now be clear when it has been shown that each comes about through some one of these figures. (40b17–22)

We thus advance from proving the adequacy of the perfect syllogisms to perform all deductions ‘in the figures’ to a proof of their adequacy for every deduction by showing that every deduction can be reduced to a deduction in ‘one of the figures’. Aristotle undertakes to prove this in the chapters which follow: first that every ‘direct’ argument can so be reduced (23), then that every argument using *reductio ad impossibile* can (24). He adds a few results concerning which categorical propositions can be deduced in each figure, observing that only in the first figure can all types be deduced and that *A* propositions can be deduced in the fewest ways (only in *Barbara*), *O* propositions in the most (all figures). We have already observed that this interest is what would be expected from a proof-theoretic standpoint.

Aristotle then gives a more comprehensive sketch of his goals:

> It is evident from what has been said how each syllogism comes about, through how many terms and premises and with what relations to each other, and moreover what sort of problem is proved

> in each figure, and what sort in more and what sort in fewer. It remains now to say how we ourselves can always be supplied with syllogisms concerning whatever is proposed and through what route we may grasp the principles of anything. For one probably ought not only study the origin of syllogisms but also have the power to produce them. (43a16–24)

The emphasis on being ‘supplied with syllogisms’ here may seem to contradict my claim that the orientation of the *Prior Analytics* is proof-theoretic. However, if we look at what follows in chapters 27 and 28, we see that the real stress is on ‘the route’ through which ‘we may grasp the principles of anything’. Aristotle undertakes to provide a method (traditionally called the *pons asinorum*) for determining whether a given proposition can be proved from a given body of facts. His method is constructive: he either shows how to construct a deduction, if one is possible, or appeals to his earlier results to show that none is possible. Significantly, he relies only on a few moods (*Barbara, Celarent, Camestres, Darapti, Felapton*), thus again indicating a concern with a simplified proof theory. ‘Grasping the principles of anything’ is a procedure which would be essential in constructing an Aristotelian science, but it is extremely difficult (though perhaps not impossible) to construe it as a recipe for constructing arguments to be used in controversy. Since the goal of this procedure is a method for producing a proof of a proposition from a body of propositions whenever such a proof is possible, and thus also of determining when a proof is not possible, I do not think it unreasonable to compare it with a solution of the *Entscheidungsproblem* for his system.

Aristotle’s most general summary of his project comes at the beginning of chapter 32:

> It is evident from what has been said, then, both from what demonstrations come to be, and how, and what one should look to with respect to each problem. And how we may reduce syllogisms to the figures mentioned before would be next to be discussed after those things, for it is what still remains of the investigation. For if we study the origin of syllogisms, and have the ability to find them, and moreover can resolve already existing ones into the figures already mentioned, the original project will have reached its goal. (46b38–47a5)

What is ‘the original project’ (ἡ ἐξ ἀρχῆς πρόθεσις)? At the beginning of the *Analytics*, Aristotle said that his subject was ‘demonstration and demonstrative science’.³² Read in this light, this summary describes a clear project: the elaboration of a theory of the structures of proofs, together with a demonstration of its adequacy by showing that all proofs can be put into its forms. The *Prior Analytics* was written, not simply from abstract curiosity about deduction, but with a conscious goal of providing a proof theory for demonstrative sciences. In this regard, Aristotle closely resembles the founders of modern logic, who were primarily concerned with providing a foundation for the demonstrative science *par excellence*, mathematics.

## VI

One aspect of Aristotle’s use of proof-theoretic techniques remains to be discussed: his response to the circular demonstrators. This appears explicitly in the latter part

***

**60**

of *An. Post.* i 3, and its structure is noteworthy. Aristotle gives three arguments. The first relies only on the requirement that in a demonstration the premises must be better known than the conclusion, together with the relatively trivial point that two propositions cannot simultaneously be better known than each other. But if *p* were used as a premise for demonstrating *q* and *q* in turn as a premise for *p*, exactly this absurdity would result. Given Aristotle’s definition of a demonstration, this is an inescapable argument. His second argument is somewhat more difficult to follow, but its intent is that circular demonstration is a disguised form of begging the question—‘and in that way’, says Aristotle, ‘it would be easy to prove anything’. His meaning, of course, is that if we can use a proposition in its own proof, then absolutely anything is provable. Neither of these arguments depends on the syllogistic or any other theory of inference.³³

Aristotle’s third argument is quite a different matter. He begins with a correction to his previous claim:

> Even this [sc. ‘proving everything’] is not possible except for such as follow each other, as properties (ἴδια) do. For it has been shown that with one thing posited it is never necessary for something else to be ... two assumptions are the first or least number from which it is possible . . . . (73a6–10)

The analysis of 72b32–73a6, which seemed to assume that a circular deduction might involve deriving premises and conclusion from each other, is here rejected in the light of the *Prior Analytics*’ result that every argument has at least two premises.³⁴ Aristotle then makes an explicit appeal to a result established in *An. Pr.* ii 5–7: the only approximations to circular proof possible in syllogistic systems must all involve cases in which two terms are ‘convertible’ (ἀντιστρέφουσι) or ‘follow each other’ (ἀλλήλοις ἕπεται), that is, are coextensive. The connection of these last chapters to the circular demonstrators’ argument is not as straightforward as is sometimes supposed. The explicit subject of those chapters is in fact something a bit different: ‘proving reciprocally and in a circle’ (τὸ κύκλῳ καὶ ἐξ ἀλλήλων δείκνυσθαι), which Aristotle defines as deducing one premise of a syllogism from the conclusion and the converse of the other premise. With a little imagination, I think we can guess how this process is related to circular demonstration.³⁵ Since all syllogistic inferences require (at least) two distinct premises,³⁶ a syllogistic circular deduction could not deduce its premises from its conclusion. Suppose, then, that we have a deduction of *p* from premises *q* and *r*. What other circular-like possibilities can we imagine?

At least, we would want the conclusion *p* to be used as a premise in deducing one of the other premises. The simplest case would be a deduction in which each premise could also be deduced from the conclusion and the other premise. However, a survey of the syllogistic moods quickly shows that no deduction satisfies this. As a plausible next step, then, one might look for small transformations of the premises or conclusion which would yield a circular result. For example, one might ask: if *p* can be deduced from *q* and *r*, then how can we transform *q* to get something which, together with *p*, yields *r*? If *p* is an *A* proposition, this question has an elegant answer: we need the converse of *q*. Let our original deduction be:
*Aab, Abc*; therefore, *Aac*

***

**61**

The ‘circular’ deduction would then be:
*Aac, Acb*; therefore, *Aab*

We could equally well have chosen to deduce *Abc*, using the converse of the other premise:
*Aba, Aac*; therefore, *Abc*

At this point, a simplifying observation might be made. The two premises added here (*Acb* and *Aba*) together entail the converse of the conclusion, so that we might add a deduction of it. Even more simply, if we start with our original premises and the converse of the conclusion, then we can construct deductions of all six *A* propositions possible with these three terms. We have thus reached the case Aristotle discusses in *An. Pr.* ii 5, and it is, as he argues, the only possible case of anything like a circular deduction in the syllogistic: we must have, in his word, terms which ‘convert’ (ἀντιστρέφουσιν), as the three terms in this example do. In summarizing his results, Aristotle indicates the link with the argument of *An. Post.* i 3:

> In these cases it turns out that what is proved is used in the demonstration. For C is proved of B, and B of A, by supposing C to be said of A, but C is proved of A through just these premises, so that we must use the conclusion in the proof. (58a15–20)

This answers well to the structure of the argument in 72b37–73a6, since we find that (for example) we must use the premise *Aac* to deduce both *Aab* and *Abc*, which are the very premises required for deducing *Aac* in turn: Aristotle could quite readily describe this as showing ‘that if A is so, then A is so’ (73a5).

## VII

His results do not end here, however. In the remainder of *Prior Analytics* ii 5–7, Aristotle completes the investigation of circular proof for other syllogistic moods, simply trying out in each case the two combinations of conclusion and one converted premise to see when the other premise follows. His results are that this works only when two conditions are met: the premise whose converse is to be used must be an *A* proposition, and the remaining premise must be of the same categorical type as the conclusion.³⁷ Aristotle calls attention to the first of these conditions in particular in the close of his argument against the circular demonstrators, substituting ‘counterpredicable’ (ἀντικατηγορούμενα) for the equivalent ‘convertible’:

> It is not possible in any way to prove in a circle those which are not counterpredicable. Therefore, since such things are rare in demonstrations, it is evident that it is both empty and impossible to hold that demonstration can be reciprocal and that for this reason there can be demonstration of everything. (73a16–20)

Aristotle’s final answer contains a truly surprising move which commentators have universally missed. He rejects circular demonstration on two grounds: it is *empty* and it is *impossible*. The emptiness is epistemic: any circular demonstration must prove something from itself, and Aristotle has just shown both that this violates his priority-of-premises requirement and that it would trivialize demonstration into the deduction of a proposition from itself. The impossibility is another matter. What Aristotle has shown is that circular demonstrations must always involve convertible terms,

***

**62**

and he now claims that such terms are rare (ὀλίγα) in demonstrations. Deceived perhaps by the reference to ‘properties’ (ἴδια) at 73a7, interpreters have failed to see that what Aristotle says here would apply to all pairs of coextensive terms, including a *definiens* and its *definiendum*: and such propositions, far from being rare in proofs, are regarded by Aristotle as the foundations of sciences.

It is significant that terms of this sort should be what gives him trouble, for they are also involved in a major gap in his argument for the termination of all premiss regresses. The circular demonstrators in effect offered an alternative to the straight-line infinite regress and the terminating regress: the circular regress, which is ‘infinite’ in that it has no limit and yet contains only a finite number of distinct propositions, thus avoiding the agnostics’ appeal to thesis (5). Now, in a system of convertible *A* propositions such as those discussed above, there are no immediate propositions and the regress never terminates: this is an obvious difficulty for Aristotle’s claim. He also has an answer to it. Near the beginning of his arguments for the termination of all regresses, Aristotle says:

> It is not the same with those which convert. For among them there is no one of which any other is predicated first or last: for all are in the same relationship to all in this case, both if those predicated of [the subject] are infinite and if the terms under discussion are infinite in both ways.³⁸ (82a15–19)

What Aristotle does here is to deny that any relationships of priority exist among convertible terms: ‘all are in the same relationship to all’, i.e., any one of them is universally true of any other. Since the point is to block a possible non-terminating regress, Aristotle must mean that propositions involving convertible terms are somehow disqualified because of the lack of any priority. Both this and the remark that convertible terms are rare in demonstrations are contradicted by the discussion in *An. Post.* i 13, to which Barnes appealed in rejecting the concept of immediacy which I have been favoring: but it is Aristotle who is contradicting Aristotle here, and he is doing it rather consistently.

The problem is that convertible terms are simply disastrous for the entire project of refuting the agnostics and the circular demonstrators on proof-theoretic grounds, as Aristotle wants to do. Aristotle knows this: he tries to argue around them in one place, and in another he denies that they ever come up, as we have seen. There is one other passage in which he appears to embrace a similar drastic solution. In *An. Pr.* ii 16, Aristotle discusses *petitio principii*, distinguishing two forms which, following Lear,³⁹ I will call trivial and non-trivial. Trivial *petitio* is using a proposition as a premise in its own attempted proof, while non-trivial *petitio* is using, in the proof of a proposition, ‘other things which would naturally be proved by it’ (64b40). Aristotle criticizes an instance of the former, concluding with words reminiscent of his censure of circular demonstration at 72b34–35:

> Thus, it comes about that those who reason in this way are saying that a thing is so if it is so; but in that way, everything would be knowable (γνωστόν) through itself, which is impossible. (65a7–9)

The argument which follows (65a10–37) is exceptionally difficult. Aristotle has in view a deduction of *Aca* from *Acb* and *Aba*. If *Aca* and *Aba* are ‘equally nonevident’ (ὁμοίως ἄδηλον), this will fail to be a demonstration on epistemic grounds and so will be a

***

**63**

non-trivial *petitio*. However, he tells us, if ‘b is so related to c as to be the same, or if it is evident that they convert or that one belongs to the other’, then the argument is actually a trivial *petitio*. If *b* and *c* are identical then Aristotle’s meaning is clear: in this case, the conclusion is identical to one premise. What is surprising is that he says the same thing about merely convertible terms: he must mean that if *b* and *c* are convertible, then the propositions *Aba* and *Aca* are the same proposition. Such a remarkable view would make nonsense of the discussion in *An. Post.* i 13. Nevertheless, Aristotle’s summary description in 65a26–37 recapitulates the references to the identity of terms, distinguishing the case of ‘the same thing belonging to the same things’ (ταὐτὸν τοῖς αὐτοῖς) from that of ‘the same things belonging to the same thing’ (ταὐτὰ τῷ αὐτῷ). It is impossible to be sure about the correct interpretation of this passage,⁴⁰ but we can at least understand what would drive him to treat convertible terms as identical: his proof theory made him do it.

## VIII

To complete his response to the circular demonstrators and the agnostics, what Aristotle needed to establish was Theses (6) and (7): that every proposition is either immediate or deducible from immediates, and that every immediate proposition can be known without demonstration. The reconstruction I have given here deals only with (7). Aristotle’s treatment of (6) in the *Analytics* is found, of course, in *An. Post.* ii 19. A separate study would be required to determine the relationship of that chapter to the arguments we have considered, although one thing emerges clearly: what Aristotle owes us is a proof that those very immediate propositions in which all regresses terminate are also those which can be known without demonstration. Whether he pays this debt, and to what extent, remain to be investigated.

My picture of the *Analytics* is thus of a work in many ways unfinished. Aristotle’s desire to resolve problems about demonstrative sciences led him to study the structures of proofs, so that the theory of science provided the inspiration and goal for the syllogistic. However, the theory of inference which resulted brought its own problems, and these appear to have led Aristotle to consider some rather drastic modifications of his views on demonstrative science. We have no idea how he finally resolved the problem of fitting convertible terms into sciences: it is possible that he abandoned them in despair, or that he was confident they could be resolved eventually, or that he simply lost interest in the whole enterprise when its difficulties became too great. One thing which does emerge clearly, however, is the thoroughgoing interdependence between the *Prior* and the *Posterior Analytics*, between Aristotle’s theory of deduction and his philosophy of science. The relationship is one of continued reciprocal influence, each area of inquiry generating its own problems as it undertakes to resolve the problems set for it by the other. Such a situation recalls in an almost uncanny way the interplay between logical theory and the philosophy of science and of mathematics in the twentieth century. Comparisons of Aristotle with later periods of the history of philosophy are always as dangerous as they are tempting; but if the arguments presented here are accepted, then modern logicians may see still more of a likeness in their great predecessor than has already been recognized.⁴¹
