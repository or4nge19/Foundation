# Predication and Deduction in Aristotle: Aspirations to Completeness

Robin Smith

*Topoi* 10: 43–52, 1991.

***

One of my principal goals here is to give an account of the concerns which led Aristotle to develop the views he did about predication. Accordingly, it is in order for me first to say what those views are. But the more I work at a simple and systematic exposition of an Aristotelian theory of predication, the less I know what I want to say. It seems to me that two questions need to be answered: first, what is Aristotle's theory of predication a theory of? Next, what does he say about it? It would be a fine thing if I could answer at least the first of these questions in a neutral way, not committed to any particular interpretative line. But unfortunately no such neutral account is possible. To begin with, we must decide whether Aristotle's theory of predication is concerned with the interpretation of a class of statements, or with certain ontological relations, or with both of these, possibly in ways that Aristotle himself did not fully realize or even understand. In addition, there are major uncertainties about the extent to which his views changed over time (so that even the subject of his theory of predication, as well as its content, would have varied).

I will therefore begin with something much more modest: a set of sketches of aspects of Aristotelian views on predication. Out of these, as I hope, a question will emerge to which I want to suggest an answer: what are the purposes which the theory of predication was intended to serve? Among those purposes, as I intend to argue, was the support of a proof-theoretic argument basic to Aristotle's theory of scientific understanding.

### 1. Some Aristotelian views about predication

#### 1.1 Predications themselves

Predication (*katēgoreisthai*), whatever else it is, is something expressed by saying something. Aristotle's attention here is directed to statements in which one thing is predicated of another. In non-philosophical usage, the verb ‘predicate’ (that is, Aristotle's Greek verb *katēgorein*) means ‘accuse’ (as in a legal indictment). There are two components to an accusation: the person accused and the offense of which that person is accused. Similarly, in a predication there are a subject of predication (*hupokeimenon*) and a predicate. The sorts of things which function as subjects of predication for Aristotle are represented by proper names (‘Socrates’), demonstratives (‘this here’), and general terms (‘human’). The sorts of things which function as predicates are general terms (‘human’). Thus, typical expressions of predications are ‘Socrates is human,’ ‘This is an animal,’ ‘A human is an animal.’ Aristotle does allow that there are sentences expressing predication in which e.g. proper names function as predicates and general terms as subjects (‘This white thing is Socrates,’ expressed in Greek as *touto to leukon Sōkratēs*). However, he regards these as unnatural and generally parasitic on ‘natural’ predications in the sense that whenever A is truly predicated of B in this way, there is a corresponding true ‘natural’ predication in which B is predicated of A (in the example, ‘Socrates is white’).

It will be useful to compare predication in Aristotle with the modern (i.e. Fregean) understanding of predication. On the modern understanding, predicate and subject are of distinct logical type: in the paradigm case, the subject is an individual while the predicate is a universal. To put this more precisely, let us distinguish between the utterance expressing a predication and the semantic situation which it expresses. Syntactically, a simple predication consists of a predicate sign (a general term) and a sign for an individual (a proper name, a singular term) combined as dictated by the syntax of the language in question. For instance, a paradigmatic predication in a familiar language for first-order logic might be ‘Fa’, where ‘F’ is a predicate symbol, ‘a’ an individual constant, and the appropriate means of combination is concatenation with predicate symbol

For instance, in the familiar domain semantics, some member of the domain would be assigned to ‘a’ and some subset of the domain to ‘F’, and ‘Fa’ would be true or false according as that member is or is not a member of that subset. The elements of this analysis — syntactic categorization of expressions as general or singular, semantic interpretation — can then be extended to statements in natural language, given appropriate translation procedures (for example, mapping proper names like ‘Socrates’ onto individual constants and open sentences like ‘___ is pot-bellied’ onto predicate symbols).

Now, Aristotle is capable of making several of the distinctions embedded in this analysis. He knows the difference between a thing and a word (or, more precisely, the difference between talking about a thing and talking about its name), and he has devices available for making it clear which he is doing (e.g. using the neuter definite article as a sort of nominalizing quote, or just saying outright what he means). He also knows the difference between a universal and a particular (in fact, we could say that we really owe the first articulation of that distinction to him). He is even aware that there may be a variety of ways to express predications in natural language, and he makes some use of a canonical (and unnatural) formulation as a standard. In the *Prior Analytics*, for instance, he adopts the convention of writing a predication predicating A of B as ‘A belongs to B’ (*to A huparchei tōi B*), a purely artificial construction not found in the everyday language of his contemporaries, and he discusses at some length (in *Prior Analytics* I.32–44) the process of bending real-life arguments into canonical form.

But although he is capable of these distinctions, he does not systematically use them: at times, it is sufficiently unclear whether his remarks are intended to apply to expressions, or to the things which they signify, or perhaps to both.

#### 1.2. The theory of categories

In a number of places (for instance, the treatise called *Categories*; *Topics* I.9; *SE* 22; *Posterior Analytics* I.22; *De Anima* I.1; *Physics* I.7, III.2, V.1; *Metaphysics* VI.2, VII.1, VII.4) Aristotle tells us that the ‘things that are’ (*ta onta*) are partitioned into a number of classes. He calls these ‘the kinds of predications’ or ‘the kinds of predicates’ (*ta genē tōn katēgoriōn*); given Aristotle's tendency to shorten, sometimes to cryptic dimensions, a phrase he uses repeatedly, this becomes simply ‘the predicates’ (*katēgoriai*), which we transliterate rather than translate as ‘categories.’ Though scholarly disagreement persists, it seems fairly clear that Aristotle regards his list of categories as a list of kinds of thing, even though he also regards it as a list of types of expression: there is a certain number of types of things in the world, and there is a corresponding number of types of predicate expression. The number of categories Aristotle gives varies from text to text (only two, the *Categories* and the *Topics*, list the canonical ten), but a category of ‘substance’ or ‘entity’ (*ousia*) always appears with pride of place. Among the other categories, ‘quantity’ or ‘how much’ (*poson*), ‘quality’ or ‘what sort’ (*poion*), ‘relation’ or ‘with respect to something’ (*pros ti*) recur most commonly.

#### 1.3. The predicables

According to *Topics* I.4, there are four (or five) relationships possible between the predicate and the subject of a predication: the predicate may be definition, peculiarity (*idion*), genus or accident (*sumbebēkos*) of the subject. (A fifth predicable, difference or *differentia* [*diaphora*], is regularly included by later authors, e.g. Porphyry's *The Five Expressions*, but Aristotle is somewhat ambivalent about its inclusion: see the remarks below.) Evidently, Aristotle takes this division to be both comprehensive and orthogonal to the partition into categories: anything in any category may have a definition, etc.

In defining the predicables, Aristotle makes use of the notion of ‘counterpredication’ (*antikatēgoreisthai*): two predicates A and B counterpredicate if A is true of B and B is true of A. (There are some serious difficulties in the way of making sense of that account: for the present, we may say simply that counterpredicating predicates are coextensive, or that they are interchangeable with one another *salva veritate* in some appropriate range of contexts.)

A second distinction used in defining the predicables is that into ‘essential’ (*en tōi ti esti*, ‘in the what it is’) and

non-essential predications. Very roughly, ‘A is B’ counts as an essential predication if ‘B’ counts as an answer to the question ‘What is A?’ For instance, ‘Socrates is a man’ is an essential predication because ‘A man’ is a correct answer to the question ‘What is Socrates?’, or to the question ‘What is this?’ accompanied by an indication of Socrates. Similarly, ‘A dog is an animal’ because the question ‘What is a dog?’ is correctly answered by saying ‘An animal.’ By contrast, ‘Socrates is pot-bellied’ is not an essential predication because ‘Pot-bellied’ is not an answer to the question ‘What is Socrates?’

According to the *Topics*, both definitions and peculiarities counterpredicate with their subjects. However, a definition is ‘an account signifying what it is to be something’ or ‘an account signifying the essence’ (*logos ho to ti ēn einai sēmainōn*), while a peculiarity is a counterpredicate which does not ‘signify the essence.’ Thus, as it appears, definitions and peculiarities are, respectively, essential and non-essential counterpredicates. A genus is an essential predicate wider than the subject. (Aristotle actually says ‘something predicated essentially of several things different in species,’ which makes the notion of genus absolute, not relative to a subject of predication.) An accident is (1) none of the above; (2) ‘what can possibly belong as well as not belong to one and the same thing.’ Aristotle treats these last two definitions as equivalent, despite the fact that it is by no means evident that they are.

The *Topics* offers us no definition of ‘difference,’ but Aristotle does sometimes (e.g. 103b15–16) take it for granted that a definition consists of two parts, viz., genus and difference.

Aristotle gives other accounts of essential predication elsewhere: the one most significantly different from that in the *Topics* is in *Posterior Analytics* I.4, which says that A is essentially predicated of B if A is predicated of B and either A is in the definition of B or B is in the definition of A (in this latter sense, ‘odd’ is an essential predicate of ‘number’).

#### 1.4. Predications and categorical sentences

In what might be seen either as an understandable assimilation or (with Peter Geach²) as a disastrous conflation, Aristotle takes his theory of predication to embrace equally well two kinds of statements: those in which the subject is a particular (signified by a singular term such as ‘Socrates’) and those in which the subject is a universal (signified by a general term such as ‘Greek’). He does not treat the latter expressions as fundamentally different in logical form from the former, though he does recognize that they have different features. Most important, for our present purposes, are their relationships of contradiction with one another. A basic tenet of Aristotle's theory is that every ‘declarative sentence’ or ‘statement-making sentence’ (*logos apophantikos*), i.e. every sentence with a truth value, is either the affirmation or the denial of one thing of another: thus, every statement is a predication. Aristotle calls the pair consisting of an affirmation and its associated denial a contradiction (*antiphasis*): he also holds that each statement is one or the other part of exactly one contradiction, and (with certain exceptions that need not concern us here) that exactly one member of any contradiction is true.

In the case of statements with universal subjects, Aristotle notes that another element enters the picture: the predicate may be affirmed (or denied) either of the whole of the subject (*katholou*, ‘universally’) or of a part of it (*kata meros*, ‘particularly’). This distinction permits the correct determination of affirmation/denial pairs for such statements: a universal affirmation and the corresponding particular denial form a contradiction pair, as do a universal denial and the corresponding particular affirmation. (These results are included in the traditional ‘square of opposition.’) This analysis leads to a canonical recognition of what we call the categorical sentences: the universal affirmative (of the form ‘Every B is an A,’ ‘A belongs to every B’: symbolically, I write AaB), universal negative (‘No B is an A’/‘A belongs to no B’: AeB), particular affirmative (‘Some B is an A’/‘A belongs to some B’: AiB), particular negative (for which Aristotle notes two forms: ‘Not every B is an A’/‘A does not belong to every B’ and ‘Some B is not an A’/‘A does not belong to some B’; I formalize both as AoB).

Much of Aristotle's logical theory requires the supposition that these four categorical sentence types are the only forms of simple sentence. It is only on this assumption, for instance, that he can maintain (as he does in *Prior Analytics* I) that the syllogistic, which is the theory of deduction for the categorical sentences, is the theory of deduction *simpliciter*. Aristotle has no theory of sentential composition at all: he does, in a few places (most notably *On Interpretation* 4, 17a9, 15–16), recognize ‘compound’ (*sunthetos*) statements, but apparently he takes these to be equivalent to sets of statements or extended discourses (for instance, the

These remarks are intended only as a broad overview (with perhaps a few idiosyncratic points of emphasis) of Aristotle's concept of predication and its principal relationships to other aspects of his philosophical thought. I have not undertaken to defend, or even thoroughly to explain, any of it, for that would be the work of much more than this paper. But I do not think that it is, in general, an especially controversial picture. Now, I would like to return to my intitial question: what are the purposes which this theory was constructed to serve? Let me put the question more precisely: What philosophical work does the thesis that every statement is a predication do for Aristotle?

One familiar answer is that it is linked to his metaphysical view that the world is composed of substances having attributes. Another, and at first sight a much humbler, consequence is that (as noted above) it supports his thesis that the theory of deductions (syllogisms) he gives in *Prior Analytics* I is the entire theory of inference. What I want to do here is to show that this last point is not nearly as humble as it first appears, and indeed that it is related to much larger projects: it is a critical component in a large edifice that comprises Aristotle's epistemology and philosophy of science. Moreover, I shall offer a few reasons for believing that this particular connection has much to do with Aristotle's particular shaping of his theory of predication and with his choice to treat categorical sentences as predications.

### 2. Aristotelian sciences

There is a complex argument, scattered through much of the *Organon*, which Aristotle uses to elaborate and defend his position on epistemology (or more precisely his view of what ‘scientific understanding’ is and how we can achieve it). I will call this structure ‘Aristotle's epistemological argument.’ Aristotle's theory of predication (and particularly his view that his theory of predication is a complete theory of logical form) is a critical part of this argument. Naturally enough, then, the structure of the epistemological argument is shaped by the content of the theory of predication. But I also claim that there is an influence in the reverse direction: the needs of the epistemological argument shape Aristotle's views on predication, just as they shape his theory of deduction.

Let us begin with a reasonably precise statement of Aristotle's position on knowledge through demonstration. What he claims, and believes he shows, in the *Analytics*³ is the following:

(1) The truths concerning any subject matter are of two types, to wit, those which can, and those which cannot, be derived from other truths. Following Aristotle, we may call the former demonstrable and the latter indemonstrable truths.
(2) Every demonstrable truth can be proved by derivation solely from indemonstrable truths.
(3) There is a veridical epistemic state which has as its object just exactly the indemonstrable truths.

Although statement (1) appears to be a matter of definition, it actually depends on a substantive fact about Aristotle's theory of deduction. Since his logic has metalogical properties that are not always appreciated in this connection, it will be useful if I first sketch a general framework for viewing demonstrative sciences and then explain the peculiarities of Aristotle's deductive theory in comparison to familiar modern theories.

#### Deductive sciences

It is customary to define a deductive science as the deductive closure of some set of propositions. Of course, this definition must be interpreted *modulo* a certain language, in which those propositions are expressed, and a certain theory of inference. In customary fashion, call these the language and the underlying logic of the science. Now, the logical theories most studied by modern mathematical logic (e.g. the propositional calculus and the first-order predicate calculus) have certain properties which allow us to say a few things about any science for which they serve as underlying logic. For one thing, there are arbitrarily many logical truths, of arbitrary length, with respect to these logics. Since logical truths are consequences of any set, any deductive science must include them. In addition, different sets of propositions may have the same science as their closure (from which it follows, of course, that if S and T are such sets, then S's closure includes T and conversely). It is even the case that disjoint sets may have the same

closure, or for that matter (with certain reservations) that there may be arbitrarily many distinct sets all with the same closure. One reason for this last point is that, generally, the closure of a set under either of these logics is infinite. This, in turn, results from the facts that the languages in question permit arbitrarily long sentences to be constructed through sentential composition and that the underlying logics entail that arbitrarily many statements are equivalent to any given statement. For instance, since *p* and *p&p* are equivalent (and thus consequences of one another), any set containing *p* must contain *p&p* and all its consequences. By iterations of this same process, we obtain an infinite series *p*, *p&p*, (*p&p*) & (*p&p*), ..., all the members of which must be contained in any closure which contains any of its members. Or again, any (finite) set is deductively equivalent to the singleton of the conjunction of its members; thus, given any finite set, we can construct another disjoint set with the same consequences.

Since a science is by definition closed, we may without loss of generality define a science as a deductively closed set. A basis for a science is a set which has the science as its closure. Two bases are *equivalent* if they are bases of the same science. A basis of a science is *minimal* if no proper subset of it is a basis of that science. Now, it is not difficult to find cases of disjoint minimal bases for the same science (given any such basis containing at least two propositions, for instance, we can easily construct such a disjoint equivalent minimal basis by taking the singleton of the conjunction of all its members). Note that the question whether or not a given basis is minimal amounts to the question whether its members are all independent. The answering of such questions is, in some cases, a matter of celebrated difficulty.

One final result is worth noting. Take any science S, having classical propositional logic as part of its underlying logic, and any proposition *p* in S. If we eliminate *p* from S and close the resulting set S - {p}, we will get S back again: that is, any proposition in a science is derivable from the remaining propositions in that science. Thus, no proposition of a science is independent of the whole science. One quick proof of this is from the fact, noted above, that (given the underlying logic as described) any proposition is logically equivalent to arbitrarily many others. Therefore, if S contains *p*, then S must contain some other proposition *q* equivalent to *p*, and thus S - {p} also contains *q*. (Less generally: if S contains *p*, then S, and also S - {p}, contains *p&p*.)

Note that this holds even for propositions which are members of a minimal basis for the science: in other words, independence as an axiom does not imply independence in the theory as a whole.

#### Aristotelian science

When we turn to Aristotelian sciences — that is, sciences expressed in the language of categorical propositions and with the syllogistic as their underlying logic — much of this is changed. Sentential composition is absent from this language, so that sentences of arbitrary length are impossible. It is at least unclear whether there are logical truths: there are, to be sure, relations of logical consequence, but the weak expressive power of the language does not allow for the representation of these as conditionals or other sentential forms. Equivalences do exist (universal negative and particular affirmative sentences are each equivalent to their converses), but it is not in general the case that every sentence is equivalent to some distinct sentence, let alone infinitely many of them. Furthermore, the closure of a finite set of sentences is always finite (unless we allow that there are some logical truths and make certain other assumptions⁴ about the extent of the language itself).

A further, and for our purposes here more crucial, feature of Aristotelian sciences concerns independence. The poverty of equivalences and the limited nature of the consequence relation make it a relatively simple matter to construct closed sets in which no proposition can be derived from the remainder — in effect, sets which are their own minimal bases. As an example, take the set {AoB, BoC, CoD}. More generally, sciences can easily contain independent propositions, even when they have bases that are proper parts of themselves. For instance, the closed set {AaB, BaC, AaC, AiB, BiA, AiC, CiA, BiC, CiB} has as a minimal basis {AaB, BaC}, and each of the members of this basis is independent in the whole science. Suppose now that a science S contains a set of propositions P = {p₁, p₂.... pₙ} each of which is independent in S. By the nature of the consequence relation, it follows that each pᵢ is independent in any subset of S, and therefore in any basis of S; thus, P is a subset of any basis of S. If in addition P is itself a basis, then it must be a minimal basis, and indeed the unique minimal basis, of S. Let us call such a basis an *indemonstrable basis*.

these last possibilities have striking consequences for a theory of proof, consequences of which I believe Aristotle was aware. A proof, or demonstration, is at the least a deduction having premises with a certain epistemic status: minimally stated, they are the objects of a veridical epistemic state (e.g. they are known to be true). If we also suppose that derivation transmits this status, a proof may then be said to confer the epistemic status of its premises onto its conclusion. In general, a demonstrative science is a system of proofs conferring the appropriate epistemic status on all the truths of a science. This is accomplished by first designating a privileged set of principles, each of which has (primitively or by assumption) the requisite status, and then by specifying how all other truths are derived from these principles. From what has been said, it is evident that the set of principles must constitute a basis for the science.

Now, epistemic status is generally taken to be relative to an epistemic subject, so that strictly speaking a proof is a proof for a given subject (and perhaps at a given time). But Aristotelian indemonstrable propositions cannot be deduced from anything except themselves; therefore, no deduction can confer on them an epistemic status they do not already have. Therefore, the indemonstrable propositions of a science are necessarily part of any basis for that science: they must count among the principles, if all the truths of the science are either principles or derivable from principles. Here, then, is one point in which the epistemic status Aristotle wants for his principles must be determined absolutely. But if, on the other hand, a science should turn out to have an indemonstrable basis, then any demonstrable proposition in the science is derivable with a deduction having only indemonstrable propositions as premises. To put this another way: since an indemonstrable basis for an Aristotelian science is necessarily the unique minimal basis for that science, it follows that if the indemonstrable propositions of a science are sufficient as a basis, then it is redundant to include any further propositions in the privileged set. Therefore, the presence in a science of an indemonstrable basis would confer to some extent a non-relative character on the notion of demonstration. The proof-theoretic structure of such a science would on its own isolate the only possible candidate for a set of principles: proof theory would tell us in such a case exactly what propositions need to receive the privileged epistemic status. The problem of showing that demonstrative science is possible would then become enormously more precise: one would need to show that just exactly the indemonstrable propositions possessed the requisite status.⁵

All the results I have stated are conditioned on the assumption that the science in question does contain an indemonstrable basis. It is here that the peculiarities of Aristotle's system of deduction are critical: no science resting on the propositional or the predicate calculus can contain such a basis, whereas Aristotelian sciences may.

#### 3. Aristotle's epistemological argument

We are now in a position to state the epistemological argument which, I claim, is the backbone of Aristotle's *Analytics*. First, let me introduce the technical terms *premise regress* and *predicate regress*. Let a *premise set* P for a proposition *p* be a set of statements *q₁ ... qₘ* such that (*q₁ ... qₘ, P*) is a deduction (in other words, P contains premises for an immediate deduction of *p*). A *premise regress* in a demonstrative science is a series *P₁ ... Pₙ* of sets of propositions of that science such that (1) *P₁* is a premise set for *p*; (2) for each *i < n*, *Pᵢ* is obtained from *Pᵢ₋₁* by replacing one of its members *q* with the elements of a premise set Q for *q*. (Note that, since the members of a premise regress are subsets of a science, they consist of true propositions.) A *predicate regress* is a series BaA, CaB, DaC,... of propositions of a science in which the predicate of each proposition is the subject of its successor. (Again, note that, since these are propositions from a science, they are all true.)

In *Posterior Analytics* I.19–21, Aristotle gives arguments which he believes establish the following:

(1) If all predicate regresses terminate, then all premise regresses terminate.

*Posterior Analytics* I.22 then argues for the antecedent of (1):

(2) All predicate regresses terminate.

But the conditional (1) itself depends on the entire edifice of the theory of deduction given in the *Prior Analytics*. In arguing for it, Aristotle must show in particular that the only type of regress he need worry about is a straight-line one that never loops back on itself in circular fashion.⁶ Now, these proof-theoretic arguments for (1) are effective only if every deduction really is, or in some way can be replaced by, a syllogistic deduction. Two theses, each of which Aristotle believes, provide support for this:

(9) Things can be defined (i.e., essences are knowable).

#### 4. Aristotelian proof theory

The role of the theory of deduction in this argument is, I have argued elsewhere, analogous in certain ways to the role of proof theory in Hilbert's program: it is not so much an end in itself as a tool for resolving issues about the overall character of a deductive science. In this case, Aristotle's objective is not Hilbert's of resolving outstanding problems but instead establishing that demonstrative science is indeed possible. I believe, neverthe-

less, that the similarities in strategy warrant us in saying that Aristotle's project amounts to the development of proof theory. Aristotle appeals to results he has established about the structures of deductive systems which, he believes, make circular proof impossible except in a trivial sense. He also relies on features of possible proof-structures to show that an infinite regress of premises would entail an infinite predication-regress, which he holds to be metaphysically impossible. The result, in a sort of proof-theoretic fashion, is that the only deductive structure possible for a science is as a set of demonstrations from unprovable first principles.

Claims of comprehensiveness or ‘completeness’ (in a non-technical sense) are essential for this project. Aristotle must be able to claim that the syllogistic is the only theory of deduction. This claim reduces for him to the pair of claims: (1) the syllogistic is the theory of deduction for categorical sentences; (2) every statement is really a categorical sentence. Most of *Prior Analytics* I is concerned with (1). Aristotle does give a sort of argument for (2) in I.23, and parts of I.32–44 are concerned with revealing (as Aristotle might put it) the categorical nature of various apparently non-categorical sentences and arguments. However, the more serious argument for (2) is in *On Interpretation*. Thus, one completeness result requires another.

The example of the *Analytics* suggests a point about the relationships of Aristotle's treatises here. The *Posterior Analytics* defines the theoretical concerns which give a point to the investigations in the *Prior*; in a phrase, we might say, of the *Prior* vis-à-vis the *Posterior*, ‘*propter hoc, ergo post hoc*.’⁷ The moral, for the present, is that some aspects of Aristotle's logical theories may be tailored to suit the further philosophical purposes to which those theories are put. Thus, the goals of reduction in number of argumentative forms and proof of exhaustiveness of classification, both so prominent in the *Prior Analytics*, make little sense (are indeed perverse) if Aristotle wants to give practical guidance in the construction and evaluation of arguments, but are exactly the sorts of things one would do in proof theory (simplification of metatheory, canonical translation).

### 5. Influences on the theory of predication

How, then, do the requirements of Aristotle's epistemological argument, with its proof-theoretic foundation, shape his theory of predication? Several important points emerge from the preceding discussion. To begin with, a comparison of the types of arguments and statements Aristotle discusses in the *Topics* and *Sophistical Refutations* with those studied in the *Analytics* and *On Interpretation* makes it clear that, as his theoretical understanding sharpened, the richness of linguistic forms with which he dealt greatly decreased.⁸ This holds for sentential form as well as argumentative structure. The *Topics* abounds with sentences that do not in any obvious way appear to be predications (for instance expressions like ‘A is more B than C is D’) and often discusses patterns of inference which rest on sentential features hard to capture in a subject-predicate syntax, e.g. arguing from ‘Justice (*dikaiosunē*) is praiseworthy’ to ‘A just person (*ho dikaios*) is praiseworthy’ or ‘A just act (*to dikaion*) is praiseworthy.’ Indeed, in the *Analytics*, Aristotle makes it a project to rewrite sentences where possible into subject-predicate form as part of the project of working arguments into the canonical forms of the syllogistic. We have seen that the needs of the epistemological argument would explain this assumption of the primacy of predication. In a few words, Aristotle needs a complete characterization of all possible forms of argument in order to carry out his proof-theoretic project, and he argues that he has one by arguing for the translatability of all forms into a small set of canonical forms the proof-theoretic characteristics of which he can fully determine.

A much more important distortion of Aristotle's theory of predication is his assimilation of quantified predications to the form of simple ones. As noted above, the argument in *An. Post.* I.22 with which Aristotle links predicate regresses and premise regresses in order to establish the termination of the latter depends on precisely this conflation. The argument rests on a notion that genuine (as opposed to ‘incidental’) predication is asymmetrical: in a true predication, the predicate is a natural predicate and the subject is a natural subject. This claim borrows much of its force from the notion of simple predication, in which the predicate and subject are logically distinct in character: the predicate is general, the subject individual. But Aristotle extends its force to predications in which both subject and predicate are universals. My suggestion, then, is that Aristotle had what amount to theoretical motives — motives arising out of the goals of his epistemology — for this ‘fall’ of treating general predications as parallel to simple ones.

I believe we see some further evidence of this in the

curious doctrine of *Prior Analytics* I.27 (43a25–43) which classifies all things into three groups: those which are only subjects (‘what is individual and perceptible,’ he says), those which are only predicates (evidently, the categories), and those which are both predicates and subjects. Aristotle remarks that ‘arguments and inquiries are almost always chiefly concerned’ with the last of these groups. Some commentators⁹ explain these remarks as motivated by the fact that Aristotle's syllogistic theory makes important use of conversion inferences, in which the subject and predicate of a proposition are interchanged: terms which could not function as both subject and predicate would accordingly be blocked from full use in the syllogistic. But, in this very passage, Aristotle takes care to note (43a36–37) that he will ‘explain later’ that the series of terms also ‘comes to a stop at some point’ in the upwards direction — that is, that there are eventually predicates which are not also subjects. The reference is surely to the argument for the termination of regresses in *Posterior Analytics* I.22 (see in particular 84a30–b2).

The appearance of this passage here, then, is puzzling. Why should Aristotle take the trouble to distinguish the three classes of entities only to leave the subject in silence almost at once? An answer emerges, I believe, if we consider the project of *Prior Analytics* I.27–28. What Aristotle gives us there is a method for the discovery of the principles of sciences. The method, which assumes that all truths are expressible in categorical sentences and that (consequently) all proofs are syllogistic, begins with the collection of all the truths of a science. Aristotle then explains how we may, for a given proposition of that collection, either: (1) discover a deduction of it from other propositions, if one is possible, or (2) determine that no such deduction is possible (in which case the proposition must be an indemonstrable first principle). The details of the method need not concern us here, but they are straightforward consequences of Aristotle's study of the syllogistic.¹⁰

Now, my suggestion is that the real importance of the tripartite division in 45a20–43 is in providing a foundation for the subsequent argument in *Posterior Analytics* I.22. It does nothing to advance the theory of *Prior Analytics* I at all. In fact, the presence of terms which are subjects but not predicates (or conversely) would raise a certain number of problems for Aristotle in this context.

In this brief paper, I have tried only to make it plausible that Aristotle's theory of predication is strongly influenced by the needs of his epistemological argument, just as his theory of deduction is designed to serve as part of his proof-theoretic enterprise in support of the same goal. A full defense of my claim would require a detailed study of his theory of predication, especially the modifications it undergoes in the transition from the *Topics* to the *Analytics*. I hope to complete such a study soon.¹¹
