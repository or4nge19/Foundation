# Aristotle’s Regress Argument

**ROBIN SMITH**

1. Aristotle's *Posterior Analytics* is historically important because it is the earliest account of deductive sciences. A deductive science is a system of proofs; a proof is a derivation from premises established or known to be true. We can therefore define a deductive science as a system of truths together with proofs for them. Mathematics, in particular, has typically been developed as deductive sciences; this association also begins in ancient Greece. Thus, an important historical question is the relationship between actual Greek mathematics and Aristotle's own account of deductive science. Our knowledge of the earliest centuries of Greek mathematics (before the time of Euclid) is very fragmentary, and therefore we can only answer this question with comparatively tentative inferences from the evidence. Nevertheless, many scholars do hold that Euclid's organization of the proofs in the *Elements* (especially the notions of postulate and common notion) show traces of Aristotelian influence. Beyond this, there is no question that later philosophers have often been heavily influenced by Aristotle's views on demonstrative science.

2. Against this background it is important to realize that the notion of a deductive science is not something Aristotle invented. Plato makes it clear that mathematical proof was a familiar activity before Aristotle; there is also evidence that the organization of proofs of propositions into a systematic whole, with fundamental propositions serving as the foundations from which others were established, was already under way. For instance, Proclus tells us that an early 'Elements' was used in the Early Academy. Beyond this, the *Posterior Analytics* itself makes it clear that debates about the proper organization of deductive systems were in progress from early in Aristotle's career. A good deal of the technical vocabulary of the *Posterior Analytics* is also introduced by Aristotle in a way that strongly suggests it was already current, not his coinage. This includes the central concept of the entire work: the demonstration (ἀπόδειξις). At least this much seems to have been common currency in Aristotle's time: a demonstration is a deduction from premises established in some appropriate way; a deduction is an argument the premises of which logically imply its conclusion; a deductive or demonstrative science is

a collection of demonstrations about a common subject matter and employing at least a good many common basic premises.

3. There is, of course, much that Aristotle adds to this. He links demonstration with knowledge of the reason why something must be as it is; thus, he holds that what is demonstrated must itself be incapable of being otherwise than it is, and he holds that the premises of demonstrations must not merely imply their conclusions but must also give their causes or explanations. He also has a number of important views on the relationship between demonstrations and empirical evidence (inductive argument), on the role of definitions in sciences, on the nature of the objects of science, etc. However, all these developments are in the details, not in the overall conception of deductive science. If we concentrate only on these aspects of the *Posterior Analytics*, Aristotle appears to have been one among many contributors to a field of research: an important contributor, perhaps, but not a seminal innovator.

4. But there is another field in which Aristotle is, by his own estimation and by ours as well, a remarkable innovator: he discovered logic. That is to say, Aristotle produced the first systematic theory of validity. This of course does not mean that he was the first to argue logically, nor even that he was the first to study what it takes to succeed in argument. He tells us himself that his predecessors taught a kind of knack for arguing. However, he says that they never made it into an art or science: his predecessors taught argumentation rather like someone who professes to teach how to cure foot troubles by providing his students with a large collection of shoes of all sizes. That is to say, they gave their students arguments to memorize and trot out whenever an appropriate occasion arose. By contrast, Aristotle claims to have been the first to invent an actual art of discovering arguments for a given conclusion, and there is every reason to respect his claim.

5. In fact, Aristotle's explicit claim of precedence (in *SE* 34) applies to the dialectical art in the *Topics*, not directly to the theory of validity in the *Analytics*. But that only makes Aristotle's position still more remarkable. The *Topics* cannot be said to contain a full-fledged theory of validity, but it does take the first step by introducing syntactic categories for classifying propositions and arguments and then recognizing that these can be used to define valid *forms* of argument. What the *Topics* does not do is attempt to give a systematic treatment of *all* valid forms of argument. Now, there is something of a consensus among scholars that the *Topics* was originally composed by an Aristotle who had not

yet made his most famous logical discovery, the theory of inference in the *Prior Analytics*. That theory does in fact purport to be a systematic theory of validity *tout court*. If Aristotle's own assessment in the *SE* is correct that he had no predecessor in constructing any kind of systematic account of valid forms at all, then it is still more remarkable that he was able, not only to invent logic, but to discover logical theory.

6. There is, however, a problem with his achievement. Aristotle's theory of validity (which I will for convenience call the syllogistic) only applies to a certain narrow class of arguments, viz., arguments constructed of categorical sentences. It has been shown that, properly interpreted, his theory is a complete natural deduction system for such sentences, and Smiley has even argued recently that Aristotle came close to giving a completeness proof for his system. Within its limits, then, the syllogistic is a remarkable achievement. The difficulty is that Aristotle does not recognize these limitations: he seems to think that this is the only system of logic there is. And that difficulty is a special difficulty for modern readers of the *Posterior Analytics*, for Aristotle supposes that a demonstration is not merely a deduction, but a *syllogistic* deduction, of its conclusion.

7. To us in the post-Fregean world, this is a bit of an embarrassment. No one (hardly anyone, anyway) can take the syllogistic seriously as the basis for any real deductive sciences. To bring the point closer to home, Mueller and others have made clear the hopelessness of treating actual Greek mathematical proofs as syllogistic. It is therefore tempting to try to save the *Posterior Analytics* by pruning away the syllogistic parts and keeping the views that result only from some very general notion of demonstration. All that Aristotle really needs—so goes the reasoning—is a general notion of logical consequence in order to say lots of interesting things about demonstrations; let us therefore put to one side all the remarks that are tied to the syllogistic and concentrate on the epistemology and philosophy of science we are left with. Thus, everything concerned with the area in which Aristotle was an undisputed innovator is ignored, and attention is focused instead on those views which may not do much to distinguish him from all the other contributors to debate about proof whose works, and even names, we have lost.

8. A great deal of recent interpretation of the *Posterior Analytics* takes just this tack. The unfortunate result is that it has led to a sort of divorce between interpretation of the *Prior Analytics* and interpretation of the *Posterior*. I think

this is a bad idea. If we try to read the *Posterior Analytics* with one eye closed to Aristotle's position that deduction is identically syllogistic deduction, then we will miss its most brilliant achievement—or rather, its most brilliant failure. In my view, the most interesting thing about the *Posterior Analytics*, and about Aristotle's logical theory in general, is that he was able to think about proof in a way that none of his successors could until the present century. What he tried to do with his theory ultimately was a failure, because the logical system with which he was working was not up to the task; but the conception of the task itself was an even more remarkable achievement.

9. I will try to explain these remarks by discussing what I have called 'Aristotle's Regress Argument'. This is an argument that appears in *Posterior Analytics* I.19-22. What is genuinely striking about it is the link it reveals between Aristotle's logical theory and his approach to some fundamental problems about proof.

10. In order to say what the Regress Argument tries to prove, we need first to see what occasions it. The occasion occurs near the beginning of the *Posterior Analytics*. In I.3, Aristotle records the positions of parties to a controversy about proofs or demonstrations. One party objected to the entire notion, claiming that if demonstrations establish their conclusions by deriving them from established results, and if the way in which results must be established is through proof, then proof is impossible because the premises of any proof will have to be proved from prior premises already proved, generating an infinite regress. Another party tried to respond to this objection by contending that it is possible to have proofs for everything if the proofs are somehow circular, so that propositions are proved reciprocally, or from one another. Aristotle's own view is that the circular position is incoherent and that the agnostic position is correct on one point: if everything proved must be proved from things already proved, then there is no proof. However, Aristotle took this to show not that proof is impossible but to give a necessary condition for the possibility of proof: proof is possible only if there are first premises, starting points, which need not be proved.

11. Here is a non-Aristotelian way to interpret this. It is a familiar point (and probably one familiar because it is in Aristotle) that any deductive system must start with axioms or postulates which are not proved in the system. This is all that Aristotle has maintained. A deductive system therefore begins with the act of assuming, postulating, positing, or whatever, certain axioms: these are

***

Aristotle's Regress Argument 25

unquestionable within the system, the rules defining the game. To ask, within the system, why they are true, or indeed whether they are true, is illegitimate: the whole point of the deductive system is to deduce consequences from the axioms. There may be various external reasons for choosing some particular set of axioms, but that is not relevant to the construction of the system itself.

12. Another more traditional assessment—and, as I think, an equally non-Aristotelian one—is after the manner of many Hellenistic philosophers and mathematicians, going something like this: the point of proof is to establish results as true by deducing them from established truths. For this to be possible, there must be certain first truths which are so secure, so certain, and so evident that they require no argument to establish them. They are, instead, self-evident, or self-justifying, or intuitively certain. Since these axioms need no proof, they are a secure foundation on which to build a system of proofs. To find the foundations of the system, we must have appeal to some source of knowledge outside the system.

13. These two pictures have two things in common. First, neither of them is particularly concerned about the *details* of what follows from what. Mathematicians are generally content just to *give* proofs, without wondering about just how it is that we get from one step to the next. They may know about logic, but they tend to regard it as not worth talking about in real-life cases. Second, each of them supposes that, from a purely logical point of view, it is not really settled what the ultimate axioms or starting-points are. To figure out where to start, we have to appeal to some mysterious thing like intuition, or (in a more modern-sounding spirit) we have to quit worrying about that and just pick the axioms for the fun of it. There may even be all sorts of different ways to axiomatize the same theory, that is, various axiom sets which are provably equivalent. Ultimately, the choice among such sets may be a matter of aesthetics.

14. But here is a third way of describing proofs. Let us suppose that there are various truths about some subject—the truths about the natural numbers, say (a convenient name for this set of truths is 'arithmetic'). How can we characterize these truths? There are a lot of them, indeed infinitely many. We do not seem to be responsible for inventing them; instead, we discover them. For instance, there may or there may not be odd perfect numbers, or there may or there may not be non-trivial solutions to $x^n + y^n = z^n$ for $n > 2$, or the Goldbach conjecture may or may not be true. We are often able to determine that some proposition of

***

26 Robin Smith

arithmetic is true, or false, by means of a proof. In that proof, we take some basic axioms which we suppose to characterize the numbers and derive some further proposition from it. But can we be sure that everything that is true about the numbers can be so proved from the axioms we have chosen? And can we be sure that everything which can be so proved is true? In other words, is our chosen axiomatic system an adequate characterization of the numbers?

15. This way of asking the question is a characteristically twentieth-century answer. Even to ask it, let alone to try to answer it, we must be able to separate (at least in thought) the truths of mathematics from a formal system which expresses those truths, and we must be able to think of proofs as formal objects, sequences of sentences defined by some set of rules. This way of approaching the concept of proof has been enormously fruitful. It has been the main driving force behind modern mathematical logic since Frege. Logical theory is the *ancilla* of the foundations of mathematics.

16. Now, I claim that Aristotle's Regress Argument shows that he was capable of just this sort of abstraction. His syllogistic was constructed *primarily* as a proof-theoretic device. Aristotle made proofs themselves into objects of formal study in order to see what structure a demonstrative science could have.

17. The Regress Argument attaches to the argument of *Posterior Analytics* I.3. Aristotle's concern is what we may call a 'premise regress.' Suppose $p$ is a true proposition. If there are other true propositions $P$ distinct from $p$ from which $p$ can be deduced, then $P$ is a set of *premises* for $p$. Consider now some proposition $q$ in $P$. If $q$ has a premise set, say $P'$, then we can find a larger premise set for $P$ by replacing $q$ with $P'$ (that is, forming $P \setminus \{q\} \cup P'$). We may continue the procedure by replacing each member of $P$ for which a premise set exists with its premise set, thus getting larger and larger premise sets for $p$ (and obviously, the process can be applied to each premise introduced by the process itself, to premises introduced by those applications, etc.). I will call any such series of expanding premise sets for a proposition a *premise regress* for that proposition. How far can such a regress continue? The obvious answer is: so long as we can keep finding a premise set for some proposition in the last set in the regress. Conversely, if we ever were to reach a set consisting of propositions *none* of which could be derived from other true propositions, then the regress would come to a stop.

18. Aristotle's Regress Argument is an attempt to prove that *every premise regress terminates*. That is, if we start with any true proposition and construct a regress, no matter how we construct it, so long as we always replace a true proposition with true propositions which imply it, we will after finitely many steps reach a set consisting of propositions none of which can be derived from any true propositions distinct from itself. In order to defend this, Aristotle needs to rule out two possibilities. First, he must somehow eliminate the possibility of an endless cycle through finitely many propositions. Second, he must rule out the more straightforward infinite series of premises without repetition. Let us look first at the structures of his arguments for these results, then at the larger significance of the results themselves for Aristotle.

19. The simpler case is that of endless cyclical regresses. The core of Aristotle's argument against this possibility is found in one of his replies (the third one, in fact) to the circular-demonstration party in I.3. Aristotle first shows—correctly—that the only such cycles possible in syllogistic systems will involve 'convertible' terms (terms universally true of each other). It follows that if such terms cannot occur in a demonstrative science, then neither can cyclical regresses. (There are some minor technical complications with this which Aristotle does not address.) Can such terms be excluded from a deductive science? There is evidence that Aristotle contemplated the following argument: within a science, convertible terms are substitutable for one another *salva veritate*. Therefore, we should really regard them as *identical*. But in that case, a set of cyclical terms is really one term, and the regress is not a real regress.

20. To rule out infinite non-cyclical regresses, Aristotle constructs a brilliant argument which I think it is fair to say uses proof-theoretic and model-theoretic devices. We find its outlines in *Posterior Analytics* I.19-22. At its core are two devices. First, Aristotle investigates different possible types of regress by considering different possible forms of conclusion and corresponding different possible forms of deduction. He is able to use this strategy because (as he believes) he has established that there are only certain possible forms of conclusion (i.e. the categorical sentence types *a, e, i, o*) and, for each, only certain possible forms of deduction (i.e. the syllogistic forms of *Prior Analytics* I.4-6). A premise set for a given proposition, then, can only take one of a very limited number of forms, determined by its categorical type. For instance, a conclusion 'A belongs to every B' can only be deduced from premises of the forms 'A belongs to every C', 'C belongs to every B'. More diverse possibilities arise for the other categorical types, but the diversity is still very

limited. Moreover, since the premises introduced are also categorical sentences, continuation of the regress also can take only certain possible forms for each premise. Therefore, Aristotle can reduce the possible forms of premise regress to a manageable number of structures.

21. Aristotle can use this strategy only because he *knows* that a given type of conclusion can be deduced only from certain types of premise combinations; he knows this only because he has made deductions themselves the objects of formal study. In other words, the theory of the *Prior Analytics* provides the necessary foundation for this argument of the *Posterior*. In fact, I think this dependence is almost causal: Aristotle was inspired to carry out the formal explorations in the *Prior Analytics* largely because he wanted to be able to give proof-theoretic arguments. I have argued elsewhere that the manner in which he establishes and presents results in the *Prior Analytics* gives further evidence for this.

22. Classifying the possible structures of premise regresses only sets the stage for an argument; the argument itself must somehow show that no possible combination of structures can be extended forever. To do this, Aristotle introduces a second technique, one which I do not think it amiss to call model-theoretic. He distinguishes between the proposition 'A belongs to every B' and the state of affairs which makes it true. That state of affairs he describes in terms of an abstract structure of terms organized into 'chains'. In a chain, terms are 'above' and 'below' one another: if A is above B in a chain, then 'A belongs to every B' is true. Chains can be developed into a semantic theory for categorical sentences (including truth-conditions for the other categorical sentence types: I omit the details here). Since a premise regress is a regress from a *true* conclusion to *true* premises which imply it, we can infer a chain-structure from the structure of a premise regress. Aristotle uses this technique to argue that an infinite premise regress is possible if and only if an infinite ascending or descending chain is possible. He then argues that no such infinite chains are possible. It is thus a condition on the possible models for his theory that is the ultimate basis of his rejection of infinite regresses.

23. A sketch of his arguments will help show how this works. Take first the case of an $a$ proposition 'A belongs to every B' (I write '$AaB$'). If this is true and provable, then A is over B in a chain and there is some term $C$ between them:

(Aristotle's argument on this score includes what seems to be a sophisticated response to a sophisticated objector who proposes that the series of middle terms may be dense at some point and approach this as a limit: Aristotle shows that this does not matter.)

26. Finally, Aristotle argues (*An. Post.* I.22) that there cannot be infinite chains. To do this, he again invokes a distinction between sentences and what makes them true, now distinguishing between 'accidental' and genuine predications. 28. These results approach the problem of demonstration in a way totally different in spirit from the epistemologically dominated approach we find if we simply abstract from the actual properties of Aristotle's logical system. Aristotle thinks that he can show that *whatever* the truths are, there must be some truths which are indemonstrable because unmiddled. These truths would have that status in virtue of their own relationships to others, quite apart from any knowledge we might or might not have of them. Thus, their indemonstrability is in no way the consequence of epistemological factors: they are indemonstrable no matter what one tries to demonstrate them from. But this

***

Aristotle's Regress Argument 31

status has epistemological consequences, most fundamentally that if these specific truths are known, *then* they cannot be known by means of demonstration.

29. This is a very different answer from one that is often attributed to Aristotle. Interpreters sometimes read *An. Post.* I.3 as if it gave the whole story on the structures of sciences. This may be attractive to those repelled by the details of his logical system: Aristotle's entire claim will then rest merely on the generalized notion of logical consequence. We can, if we like, appropriate this claim for our own use and say that Aristotle is right, knowledge cannot be produced by demonstration unless there are indemonstrables. Or, we can reject his position and maintain instead that deduction has its own powers of making known, so that something like the circular position is acceptable. The issue seems in this case to rest entirely on questions about epistemology, specifically, whether there are things we know to be true without argumentative justification, whether justification always flows downhill in a deduction, etc. Logic, of course, will not help us much with these issues.

30. Aristotle's approach to the issue stands to this roughly as Gödel's approach to provability in arithmetic does to the question 'Do Peano's axioms look true enough to you?' Let us for the moment suppose that Aristotle's arguments all work as he thinks they do (there are serious problems with this, but set them to one side). What they show, then, is that there are fundamental *logical* constraints on any deductive system, constraints quite independent of any epistemological views one might have about which first principles are obvious or familiar or how these principles become obvious, etc. Aristotle's grand metalogical thesis is that in any science, there is a unique set of axioms, no one of which can be derived from any other true propositions in the science, and which every other true proposition in the science can be derived from. Thus, he thinks that on purely logical grounds, there is only one possible choice of axioms.

31. This is a purely logical thesis with major epistemological consequences. Aristotle seems to be very much aware of these: they give his epistemology a surprising twist. If any science must contain indemonstrable first principles, then how can demonstrations work? Aristotle's answer is that it is both a necessary and a sufficient condition for this that there be some non-demonstrative means of knowing *just exactly the indemonstrables*. He could then piously hope, or arbitrarily postulate, that some such cognitive faculty is

innate in us. I think, however, that it is more likely that he took seriously the parallel he draws several times between moral education and the process of becoming scientifically wise. Aristotle holds that our dispositions to feel pleasure and pain serve as criteria, not of whether a given action is virtuous, but of whether we are virtuous: virtuous people take pleasure in virtuous actions. Thus, Aristotle need not presuppose an innate capacity to recognize or know just exactly the indemonstrables, only a capacity to become cognitively habituated to them.

32. I have been claiming that Aristotle was a modern metalogician before his time. Why didn't it catch on, then? Though any response here is sheer speculation, I have two speculations to offer. First, Aristotle's logic just does not do the job of representing the structures of mathematical proofs. The point hardly needs arguing at a theoretical level: the syllogistic is far weaker than first-order predicate logic and incapable of representing the forms of any but a limited class of valid arguments. Those few attempts in the history of mathematics to turn geometrical proofs into 'syllogisms' show, in the attempt, just how implausible it is to think that every inference is a categorical syllogism. It is hard not to suppose that some of Aristotle's contemporary mathematicians noticed this, if any of them ever tried rewriting their proofs in syllogistic form. Second, the fact is that mathematicians throughout history have more or less taken inference for granted. Studying proofs themselves, rather than proving results, is widely perceived as boring. Even Hilbert's proposals for a proof theory were motivated by the aim of solving *other* major mathematical problems by discovering whether their solutions could be proved in certain formal systems.
