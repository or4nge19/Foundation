# The Syllogism in *Posterior Analytics* I*

**by Robin Smith (Manhattan)**

At the beginning of the *Analytics*, Aristotle tells us that his subject is ‘demonstration and demonstrative science’ (24a 10–11). This presents us with an over-arching plan for the entire treatise. The *Prior Analytics* develops a theory of syllogism in general, while the *Posterior* applies this theory to demonstrative science. As a description of the plan of the *Analytics*, this is obviously correct. However, questions have been raised about the history of the various parts of the treatise. Applying Jaeger’s methodology, Solmsen¹ argued that the *Posterior Analytics* was actually written first and that the plan announced in *An. Pr.* I. 1 was only added later when Aristotle had developed a comprehensive theory of syllogism; the original theory of demonstration (which Solmsen calls ‘apodeictic’) rested on a system of *Eide-Ketten*, ‘Form-chains’, rather than on the syllogistic. Ross² takes some pains to refute Solmsen’s position in his edition of the *Analytics*, maintaining instead that the works were probably written in the order in which they appear in the corpus.

Now, to the extent that this controversy turns on resolving the relative dates of the *Prior* and the *Posterior Analytics*, it is probably not susceptible of a meaningful resolution. The most natural interpretation of the various cross-references between the parts of the *Analytics* is that Aristotle revised and edited the work as a whole over some period

### I

A full study of the passages in *Posterior Analytics* I which appear to refer to the *Prior Analytics* or presuppose its doctrines would make this paper excessively long; in the interests of brevity, I will simply note the results of such a study here.⁴ In general, what we find is that Aristotle’s knowledge of the syllogistic is (as Solmsen had claimed) restricted to the four universal moods *Barbara, Celarent, Camestres, Cesare*.⁵ Of course, this presupposes the basic terminology of syllogisms, with the nomenclature of major, middle and minor terms and some sort of classification into figures. However, with few exceptions (all discussed below) Aristotle does not indicate a knowledge of moods with particular conclusions. More significantly, he never alludes to the doctrine of ‘perfecting’ second- and third-figure syllogisms as developed in *An. Pr.* I. 4–22. The general claims Aristotle does make about syllogisms⁶ could all be made on the basis of the universal mood system proposed above. Furthermore, those texts which do involve a more intimate knowledge (73a6–20, cc14, 16, 17) are in no cases necessary to any further claims made in *An. Post.* I and in at least one case (73a6–20) badly suited to the surrounding text.

Let me now consider some apparent exceptions to these claims. First, in 73a6–20 there is an unmistakeable reference (ὡς δέδεικται ἐν τοῖς περὶ συλλογισμοῦ) to the discussion in *An. Pr.* II. 5–7 of “proving in a circle and reciprocally” (τὸ κύκλῳ καὶ ἐξ ἀλλήλων δείκνυσθαι). ROSS correctly notes that the argument for a major point in the *Posterior Analytics* — the impossibility of a ‘circular’ demonstration of first principles — here appeals to a technical point of the syllogistic.

But let us take a closer look. In fact, as Barnes (pp. 108, 111–112) has shown, there is a significant difference between the subject of *An. Post.* I. 3 and that of *An. Pr.* II. 5–7. The argument in the *Posterior Analytics* aims at showing the following: suppose we have a circular demonstration of certain principles, i.e., we demonstrate B from the assumption that A and A from the assumption that B. Although the precise interpretation of this passage is not clear, Aristotle appears to rely on the transitivity of entailment to argue that such a circle amounts to proof that A is true if A is true. In *Prior Analytics* II. 5–7, by contrast, Aristotle’s concern is a process of “proving one premiss through the conclusion and the other premiss taken in the reverse order of predication” (57b18–20).

### II

I have argued that the treatment of the syllogism in *Posterior Analytics* I seems to belong to an earlier stage in Aristotle’s development than that represented by *Prior Analytics* I. In this section, I argue that the treatment of *per impossible* proof in *An. Post.* I is similarly inferior both to the account of *An. Pr.* II. 11–13 and to the understanding of his technique implicit in *An. Pr.* I. 4–22. The immediate consequence is that, at least with respect to this one point, *An. Post.* I can hardly have been written after the syllogistic was fully developed. Furthermore, Aristotle’s treatment of a universal affirmative proposition and the corresponding universal negative as if they were exhaustive alternatives leads us to consider what sort of model for arguments he might have had in mind. I consider the implications of this question in the following section.

According to Aristotle’s own remarks, the theory of demonstration is a special case of the syllogistic: it results from the application of the syllogistic to the restricted domain of scientific propositions. This fact is sometimes cited to explain Aristotle’s not infrequent failure to specify the quantity of a proposition in *An. Post.* I: all propositions are presumed to be universals. Now Aristotle, in fact, never says this; commentators have rather supplied this explanation. Moreover, there are some textual problems which cannot be resolved simply by supposing that all propositions are universal unless otherwise specified. The difficulty is that Aristotle sometimes seems to treat a (universal) affirmative proposition and its corresponding (universal) negative as in effect contradictories. The most striking such case is c. 26, a discussion of proof *per impossibile*:

If it be required to prove that A does not belong to B, it should be taken to belong, and B to C, so that it follows that A belongs to C. But let it be recognized and agreed that the latter is impossible. Then A cannot belong to B. Thus, if B is agreed to belong to C, it is impossible for A to belong to B. (87a7–12)

Literally interpreted, the proof given here is invalid if we interpret all the propositions as universal. Aristotle would then be saying: to prove *AeB*, suppose *AaB* and *BaC*, and let *AaC* be agreed impossible; from the assumptions, *AaC* follows; since this is impossible, *AaB* is false; therefore *AeB*. The first thing wrong with this is that the wrong hypothesis is made: if we want to prove *AeB* indirectly, we should use *AiB* as hypothesis. What Aristotle gives us is rather an indirect proof of *AoB*.

### III

My aim in this section is to show that Aristotle’s theory of demonstration appears to have been constructed against the background of a certain theoretical structure of terms which serves as its model. Briefly, the theory of demonstration is the theory of the structure of a system of terms arranged in ordered ‘chains’ (συστοιχίαι) meeting certain constraints. As we shall see, some important concepts in the *Posterior Analytics*, most notably the concept of an immediate proposition, have their home in this structure. Nevertheless, I also argue that Aristotle was himself undecided about certain details of its structure and that the *An. Post.* I. we know does not contail a finished version of this theory. Moreover, I suggest some reasons why the theory cannot be brought to a satisfactory state of completion and, in view of this last fact, argue that it probably was developed before the syllogistic of *An. Pr.* I had been finished.

Since I make use of certain concepts of formal semantics or model theory in what follows, let me begin with a brief sketch of the concept of a model. A model or interpretation for a set of sentences is a set of functions assigning truth values (truth and falsehood) to each of those sentences. Typically, a model maps some constituent elements of sentences (e.g. terms) onto elements of some system of things and assigns truth values on the basis of correlations between sentences and relations of the things in the system. For instance, a familiar model for Aristotelian categorical sentences is a system of non-empty classes. In this model, terms are mapped onto classes and truth values are assigned to particular sentences based on the relations of the classes to which terms in

So far, we have considered a very general notion of a model for a set of sentences as simply a means of assigning truth-values to them. If we wish certain logical relations such as entailment or inconsistency to obtain among some of these sentences, or if we wish some of them to count as necessarily true, then it will be necessary to add constraints to any model that is to reflect these further characteristics adequately.

To return to the example of the categorical sentences, we can interpret the syllogistic as asserting relations of entailment between pairs of sentences (premisses) and sentences (conclusions). For example, if ‘A belongs to all B’ and ‘B belongs to all C’ are true in a model, then ‘A belongs to all C’ must also be true in that model. It is easy to see that the model indicated above employing non-empty classes is adequate for the syllogistic: If we interpret ‘A’, ‘B’ and ‘C’ with the classes α, β and γ respectively, then ‘A belongs to all B’ and ‘B belongs to all C’ will be true if and only if α includes β and β includes γ; but since class inclusion is transitive, under these circumstances α will include γ, and thus ‘A belongs to all C’ will also be true. It is not difficult to show that if we assign the proper set-theoretic relation to each of the four categorical relations, then the premisses of any Aristotelian syllogism will be true in a model only if the conclusion is also true in that model.

Different models may be adequate for the interpretation of the same system of sentences, and it may be that two such models have divergences that make it impossible to correlate them. For instance, consider two models for the syllogistic, one of which (Model 1) consists of a set of non-empty classes in which each class is either included in or includes any other class, and the other one of which (Model 2) consists of a set of non-empty classes no one of which includes any other. Both are adequate; but in Model 1 all *e*-propositions are false, whereas in Model 2 all *a*-sentences are false. These further properties of these models are not reflected in the structure of the syllogistic itself; any model which satisfies certain minimum conditions will be adequate for the syllogistic, even if it includes further features not expressed in the syllogistic.

The model presupposed by *Posterior Analytics* I is a structure of terms (ὅροι) arranged in ordered series called συστοιχίαι (for convenience I render this as ‘chains’). A chain is a series of terms with a linear order: if A and B are terms in the same chain, then one of them precedes the other in that order. Aristotle’s expressions for the order relation are simple: one term is said to be under (ὑπό) another, and the series has an upwards (ἄνω) and a downwards (κάτω) direction. Now if A is over B in a chain, then the proposition *AaB* is true: this is the first rule of interpretation allowing us to correlate categorical sentences and terms in chains. We may note at once that the converse also holds (if *AaB* is true then A is over B in a chain). The nature of order

***

relations guarantees that the syllogistic mood *Barbara* will be valid in this model: if *AaB* and *BaC* are true, then A is over B and B is over C, whence by the transitivity of any order relation we have that A is over C; but if A is over C, then *AaC* is true.

An additional detail of structure concerns the number of terms in a given segment of a chain. First, let us introduce some terminology. If a term is the upper end of a chain (that is, if nothing else is above it), Aristotle calls it a ‘primary’ (πρῶτον); if it is at the bottom of a chain (so that nothing is below it), he calls it an ‘ultimate’ (ὕστατον). He also uses the expression ‘between’ (μεταξύ) in an obvious way, saying that A is between B and C if A is under B and over C or under C and over B. Note that betweeness obtains only among triples of terms in the same chain; if A and B are in different chains, no term can meaningfully be described as between them.

Aristotle also speaks of the interval (διάστημα) between two terms. Frequently, this expression seems to be an equivalent for ‘proposition’ (πρότασις), but its mathematical senses (it means both ‘distance’ and ‘musical interval’; the last, of course, is for the Greeks equivalent to ‘ratio’) recall the fact that the word for ‘term’, ὅρος, can mean both ‘boundary’ and ‘term’ of a ratio or proportion. Thus, Aristotle’s terminology suggests, if nothing else, a mathematical model for the structure of term in which terms are endpoints of lines or intervals.

This last point takes on particular importance in connection with the concept of immediate (ἄμεσος) propositions or intervals. Aristotle’s remarks on this subject concern a fundamental issue in the *Posterior Analytics*, to wit, the possibility of knowledge through demonstration. Early in the work, he rejects two views: the view that there is no knowledge because it is impossible to demonstrate the principles of a demonstration, demonstrate the principles of that demonstration, and so on *ad infinitum*; and the view that it is possible to demonstrate even the principles by way of ‘circular’ demonstrations. Aristotle’s own view is that not every scientific proposition is demonstrable but that nevertheless demonstrative knowledge is possible: demonstrations ultimately rest on ‘indemonstrable’ first principles. He calls these indemonstrables ‘immediate’, and a long-standing tradition interpreted both these terms epistemologically. According to this tradition, indemonstrables are propositions not needing proof because of their own self-evidence; they are immediate because they are known to be true without the mediation of any other propositions. Textual confirmation of this view seems to be found in the definition of ‘immediate proposition’ in 72a8 as one “than which no other is prior”.

As important as these epistemological notions may have been to later Greek thought, especially mathematics, it seems to me unlikely that this represents the process whereby Aristotle came to develop his own theory. The plain meaning of ἄμεσος is ‘without a middle’, and from this standpoint the definition at 72a8 seems to reflect a consequence of Aristotle’s theory: since any demonstration must be by way of a middle term, therefore a proposition for which there is no middle term cannot be demonstrated (is ‘indemonstrable’, ἀναπόδεικτος), and consequently no proposition can be prior, i.e., logically antecedent, to it. In this picture, a proposition is indemonstrable, *i.e.* incapable of demonstration, because it is immediate. This view contrasts with a picture sometimes seen in Aristotle (by Philoponus and Alexander, for instance) according to which the principles are ‘indemonstrable’ in the sense of not needing demonstration. Thus, on the latter view it is self-evidence that characterizes immediate propositions.

Some support might be gleaned for this from the assertion in *Topics* I. 1 that “those things are true and primary which have their belief not through others but through themselves” (τὰ μὴ δι’ ἑτέρων ἀλλὰ δι’ αὐτῶν ἔχοντα τὴν πίστιν, 100b18–19). However, the closest to this view we have in *An. Post.* I. is the claim that principles are “more knowable in themselves” (γνωριμώτερα αὐτοῖς). Now, the concept of self-evidence is an epistemological notion which might be appealed to as an explanation of how we *come to know* a given proposition. But the question how we might get knowledge of an indemonstrable proposition is quite distinct from (even if it may be bound up with) the question whether there *are* such propositions. If Aristotle gives an answer to the former question in the *Analytics*, it is the brief theory of νοῦς in *An. Post.* II. 19. Nothing at all is said about this question in *An. Post.* I., and in particular Aristotle does not appeal to self-evidence. The question of the *existence* of indemonstrables, however, is dealt with at some length, principally in cc. 15 and 19–22. In these chapters, Aristotle’s aim is not to show that there are certain propositions not needing proof but rather to show that there are certain propositions not susceptible of proof. Thus, the property of indemonstrability is a consequence of the property of immediacy, and the latter property is not epistemological but rather a matter of the relationship of the terms in a proposition.¹²

We may begin by distinguishing three separate chain models for categorical propositions. Each of these models after the first results from its predecessor when a constraint is dropped; thus, the order in which they are presented is the order of increasing generality. It is also, as a matter of fact, the order of decreasing frequency of appearance in *An. Post.* I, the third model actually appearing perhaps only once. The models are as follows:

*Model I*: A system of chains of terms in which no chains ever intersect. In more precise terms, we may state this as two conditions: (1) if some term A is under each of two other B and C then either B is over C or C is over B; (2) if some term is over each of two other terms B and C, then either B is over C or C is over B.

*Model II*: A system of chains containing branches but otherwise like Model I. Such a model is adequately characterized by retaining condition (1) above and dropping condition (2).

*Model III*: A system of chains with no constraints (except finiteness). This results if both conditions (1) and (2) are dropped. We have seen evidence above that Aristotle frequently relies on both conditions (1) and (2) in his discussions of immediate propositions and related matters; thus, Model I is the most commonly used model. In a few places, we find rejections of condition (2), which would give us Model II. In the one place in which condition (1) is rejected (c. 29), a branch is also assumed (which amounts to a denial of condition (2)), so that in at least one place Model III is assumed. Model III, however, is adequately described only by the fully general syllogistic, so that this no longer holds: we may only say that either *AaB* or *AoB* is true, and likewise either *AeB* or *AiB*.

This point leads to another property of significance distinguishing Models I and II. To explain its importance, let us return to the discussion of *per impossibile* proof in *An. Post.* I. 26. What Aristotle contrasts there is two arguments in which “the terms are similarly arranged” (οἱ ὅροι ὁμοίως τάττονται), but there is a difference as to which of two propositions is better known. The “arrangement of terms” is as follows: we suppose that *AeB, BaC*, and (consequently) *AeC* are true. Aristotle contrasts a ‘direct’ (δεικτική) proof of *AeC* via *Celarent* here with a *per impossibile* proof of *AeB*. In this proof, we suppose *AeB* false. Now, in either Model I or Model II, this entails that either *AaB* or *BaA*. The first of these, together with the premiss *BaC* (common to both arguments), entails *AaC*, contradicting *AeC*, which is supposed ‘impossible’ in this proof. But note that the second possibility is also inconsistent with *AeC*. If *BaA* and *BaC*, then in Model I A and C must be in the same chain; but we have supposed *AeC* as part of our proof. Therefore, in slightly revised language, we might restate Aristotle’s proof as follows:

From the premises *AeC, BaC* the conclusion *AeB* follows. For suppose not. Then, if *AeB* is false, A and B must be in the same chain. But, given that *BaC*, B and C are in the same chain, whence A and C are in the same chain. But this contradicts our assumption that *AeC*. Therefore, if *AeC* and *BaC*, it follows that *AeB*.

The validity of this argument depends on the transitivity of the relation of being in the same chain: if A and B are in the same chain, and if B and C are, then A and C are. This holds in Model I, since all chains are

A look at the arrangement of terms presupposed in this case shows that B is above C in a chain here, so that *AeB* is (if not itself immediate) closer to the immediate *e*-proposition on which the proof ultimately depends. That is, there must be a term D such that it is in a chain above B and such that *AeD* and such that there is no other term above D such that *AeD*. *AeD* is not necessarily immediate in such a case (there may be another term E above A such that *EeD*), but in a fairly natural sense *AeB* is closer to this immediate proposition and hence prior to *AeC*: B is higher up the chain towards D.

Thus, although we cannot actually make c. 26 into a coherent account of *per impossibile* proof, we can make it at least plausible if we suppose it to have been originally developed against a background of Model I. Since textual evidence otherwise suggests that this model is most frequently the one used in *An. Post.* I, it seems plausible that Aristotle developed his theory of demonstration while supposing terms to be arranged in mutually exclusive chains. The fact that Aristotle only infrequently uses features of Models II or (still less frequently) III suggests that he only came to consider these less restricted structures as possibilities after he had worked out the general outlines of his theory of demonstration. This is confirmed to a degree by the fact that the one passage in which Model III is clearly presupposed is obviously in an unfinished state.

IV

In summary, what I have tried to show is that the treatment of the syllogism presupposed by *Posterior Analytics* I is quite different from the exposition of that subject in the *Prior Analytics*. Now, speaking very generally, we can imagine two ways in which to account for this. Either the theory of demonstration was elaborated before the doctrines of the *Prior Analytics* had been settled, or Aristotle was led to constrain and modify the general theory of syllogisms for the sake of *Posterior Analytics* I.

If we adopt the latter position, we must then explain what these restraints are; the traditional answer has been that Aristotle confines himself to universal propositions, since it is with these that demonstrative science is properly concerned. I have tried to show above the inadequacy of this hypothesis: to mention only one difficulty, we must suppose that Aristotle, after having acquired the sort of understanding of *reductio ad impossibile* reflected in *An. Pr.* II. 11–14 and in the use of this method in reducing imperfect moods, somehow produced the very unsatisfactory treatment of this subject in *An. Post.* I. 26. Besides, there is no direct textual evidence that Aristotle had any such motive in mind. He nowhere says that any restrictions (let alone this specific one) must be placed on the theory of syllogisms. (The argument in c. 14 is not an exception to this, as I have argued above.)

On the other hand, if we suppose that the theory of demonstration was developed before the theory of syllogisms had been completed, then we must explain why *Posterior Analytics* I continues to reflect this incomplete theory. While it is implausible that Aristotle would write accounts reflecting an inferior understanding of a subject after he had already demonstrated a superior understanding of it, it is not as unlikely that he might have let an inferior account already written stand even after his understanding of the subject advanced. Thus, he may simply not have gotten around to revising all aspects of *Posterior Analytics* I in the light of the completed theory of syllogisms; or, he may not have realized the flaws in some of his discussion in that treatise. Here, the hypothesis of a restriction to universal propositions might well be revived. For instance, the extension of the theory of immediate propositions to cover particular as well as universal propositions raises significant difficulties. Possibly, Aristotle encountered some of these difficulties but did not pursue their resolution because the theory of demonstration would not need to consider particular propositions. The picture of the evolution of the *Analytics* which I have tried to draw is, I think, inherently a plausible one, and in outline it conforms to a view expressed by other scholars (e.g., Barnes, p. xiv). Very generally, I suppose that Aristotle was engaged in developing a theory of demonstration and in studying the deductive structure of arguments at about the same time. At some point, his concept of the syllogism became sufficiently developed for him to use it in an articulate theory of demonstrative science; this is generally what we have recorded in *Posterior Analytics* I. However, he continued to study deductive arguments, eventually producing the much more detailed theory in the *Prior Analytics*. The *Analytics* as we know them are simply Aristotle's attempt at presenting all his researches in these areas in a reasonably logical and coherent manner. It seems to me that this picture is on the face of it much more plausible than the supposition that Aristotle somehow confined himself to the study of syllogisms in general until he had completed the doctrines of the *Prior Analytics* and only then turned his attention to the application of this theory to demonstration. Among the many implausible features of such a view, I will note only that the systematic deductive presentation of mathematics (which had already begun among Academic mathematicians in Aristotle's youth) forms an obvious paradigm for a demonstrative science, whereas the study of the syllogism was something which Aristotle had to invent for himself. What I have tried to do is not simply argue for this picture but add some details to it by indicating just at what stage in the development of the theory of syllogisms Aristotle consolidated his views on demonstration. If my views be accepted, then we might next wish to ask what can be determined about the origins of this earlier theory and its course of development into the finished product seen in the *Prior Analytics*. These questions must be reserved for a subsequent work.¹⁴
